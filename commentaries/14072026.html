(1 Corinthians VI, 20-VII, 12)
1 Corinthians VI, 20 — 'ἐν τῷ σώματι' — 'en to somati' - in the body; by body. Both variants are possible, but translations mostly give the first. The first emphasizes the body as a place of glorification, as a temple of God, while the second indicates onto the body as a method of glorification and more corresponds to the ideal of holiness and deification. In many ancient texts there are no words about the spirit and that they are God's (in a similar meaning as a temple is called God's, although built by people). Also, the first emphasizes the body as a component of human nature, and the second — that it must be a sanctuary and by this glorify God. The words about the spirit along with the body are absent in many ancient manuscripts.

1 Corinthians VII, 5 — 'ἐκ συμφώνου' — 'ek symphonou' - by agreement [from agreement], consonantly, harmoniously. ‘συμφωνος’ — ‘symphonos’ — symphonic, harmonious, consonant, sounding similarly together; conveys by means of a musical sound image the concept of agreement, like-mindedness. See also:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/26072024.html

1 Corinthians VII, 7 — 'ἐκ θεοῦ' — 'ek theou' - from God. The preposition 'ek' here emphasizes the deepest significance, the highest degree of significance of the state of a person for themselves. This is the most trusting relationship with God — to trust all of oneself in the most secret and deep components of one's being to God. By virtue of such depth and trustfulness, many people need labor and certain efforts for acceptance, and often even for the awareness and recognition of God's design regarding what one or another person should be. Next, I will dwell in detail on the verse 1 Corinthians VII, 4. 'ἡ γυνὴ τοῦ ἰδίου σώματος οὐκ ἐξουσιάζει ἀλλὰ ὁ ἀνήρ· ὁμοίως δὲ καὶ ὁ ἀνὴρ τοῦ ἰδίου σώματος οὐк ἐξουσιάζει ἀλλὰ ἡ γυνή' — The wife has not power over her own body, but the husband: and likewise also the husband has not power over his own body, but the wife.

A rather mysterious passage that sheds light on many questions. But first things first.

I asked Google Gemini about the origin of the word 'exousia'. Here is the answer:

The word ἐξουσία (exousia) in the ancient Greek language (particularly in the Koine period) has a very clear origin that reveals its legal and philosophical meaning. It comes from the impersonal verb ἔξεστι(ν) (exesti), which translates as 'it is permitted', 'lawful' or 'possible'.
Etymological analysis
The word is formed by combining two elements:
The prefix ἐξ (ex) / ἐκ (ek): means 'out of', 'from', 'from the inside out'.
The root of the verb εἰμί (eimi): 'to be', 'to exist'. More precisely, it is directly related to the noun οὐσία (ousia) — 'essence', 'being', 'nature' (which, in turn, is formed from the present participle feminine ousa — 'the one that is').
Literally, exousia can be understood as that which 'comes out of the essence' — that is, a state where action flows from someone's nature, right, or property. Hence comes the idea that a person has grounds to do something.
Meaning in the Koine language
In the everyday language of the Hellenistic period (including the texts of the Septuagint and the New Testament), this word became established in the following meanings:
Right and freedom of action: The opportunity to freely choose and act, a privilege.
Lawful authority and powers: Authority that gives the right to rule, make decisions, or dispose.
Jurisdiction: The sphere of influence over which someone's power extends.
Power structures: In the plural (exousiai), it often denoted the rulers themselves, magistrates, or even spiritual entities.
A key nuance: Exousia vs. Dynamis
To understand exactly the essence of exousia, researchers often compare it with another Greek word — δύναμις (dynamis):
Dynamis — is raw strength, power, capability, internal energy (from this root come the words 'dynamics', 'dynamite'). It answers the question 'Can I do this physically?'.
Exousia — is authority, jurisdiction, and delegated right. It answers the question 'Do I have the legal right to do this?'.
For example, a robber has dynamis (strength) to break the door and enter the house, but only the owner has exousia (right) to enter there.
Would you like to consider the use of this word in some specific text, for example, in the works of philosophers or in the New Testament?

Then I directed the conversation directly to the verse. Here is what Google Gemini wrote:

This is an extremely apt example. In the 1st Epistle to the Corinthians 7:4, the apostle Paul uses precisely this concept, using the verb ἐξουσιάζει (exousiazei), which directly comes from the noun exousia.
In the Ukrainian translation (for example, of Ivan Ohienko), this text sounds like this:
«Дружина не володіє (не має влади — exousiazei) над своїм тілом, але чоловік; так само й чоловік не володіє своїм тілом, але дружина».
If we apply that understanding of the word exousia which we considered earlier (as a legal right, jurisdiction, and delegated powers, and not raw strength dynamis), this verse is revealed much deeper:

1. Transfer of jurisdiction, not the use of force
Paul does not say that the husband or the wife has dynamis (physical strength or coercion) over one another. The use of the verb exousiazei means that in marriage a voluntary transfer of 'property right' or 'jurisdiction' takes place. Each of the partners consciously renounces exclusive authority over themselves and transfers this right to the other. This is a legal-philosophical concept of mutual giving of rights.
2. Radical symmetry of powers
In the Greco-Roman world (as well as in the Jewish society of that time), the concept that a husband has exousia (lawful power) over his wife was absolutely standard and fixed in law (for example, Roman patria potestas or the power of paterfamilias).
However, the second part of the verse — 'and likewise also the husband has not power (exousiazei) over his own body, but the wife' — was revolutionary for ancient society. Paul takes a legal term that usually worked only in one direction (from top to bottom, from husband to wife) and makes it absolutely symmetrical. He endows the woman with an identical exousia over the man.
3. Essence (ousia) in relationships
Recalling the etymology (that which flows from the essence — ousia), we can say that Paul describes marriage as a state where two people become 'one flesh' (a shared essence), and therefore neither of them has any longer an isolated, sole right (exousia) to dispose of themselves without taking into account the other.
Consequently, in this context, exousia is not about dominance or dictate, but about a deep, mutual belonging and equal ownership in the marital union.

I then asked: Why is the genitive case used with respect to the body?. Google Gemini replied:

This is a very precise linguistic observation. In the original Greek text, the genitive case (Genitivus) is indeed used: τοῦ ἰδίου σώματος (tou idiou sōmatos — 'of one's own body').
The use of precisely the genitive case here is not accidental and is determined both by the rules of ancient Greek syntax and by the philosophical content of the concept of power. Here is why it works exactly like this:
4. Grammatical rule of verbs of ruling
In the ancient Greek language, verbs expressing the idea of management, leadership, dominion, or possession (for example, ἄρχω — to rule, κυριεύω — to be master, ἐξουσιάζω — to have right/power) govern the object in the genitive case. This syntactic construction is sometimes called Genitivus dominationis (genitive of ruling).
5. Focus on belonging, not action
In the Ukrainian language, we usually say 'to have power over someone/something' (using the instrumental case with a preposition). However, in the Greek language, the genitive case historically is responsible for expressing belonging, origin, and sphere of influence.
If Paul had used the accusative case (Accusativus), which denotes the direct object of action, it would sound as if a person performs some direct physical action on the body (for example, moves it, beats it, or treats it).
But the genitive case (Genitivus) shifts the emphasis from mechanical action to legal belonging. The use of ἐξουσιάζω along with the genitive case is literally closer to the meanings:
'to be the manager (of what?) of the body'
'to have property right to (what?) the body'
'to perform judgment/governance in the sphere (of what?) of the body'.
Consequently, the grammar emphasizes the same thought as the etymology of the word exousia: it is not about the use of force or manipulation to the object, but about determining in whose jurisdiction this object is. The genitive case here fixes not action, but status — the status of the one to whom the highest right to make decisions belongs.

I further noted: Here it speaks about equality, equal possession. But the apostle speaks about the absence of own possession over the body, which remains own. Google Gemini replied:

You caught the sharpest and deepest paradox of this verse. This is an extremely precise remark.
Indeed, the Greek text literally says: τοῦ ἰδίου σώματος οὐκ ἐξουσιάζει (tou idiou sōmatos ouk exousiazei).
The word ἴδιος (idios) means 'own', 'private', 'one's personal' (from this root comes the word 'idiom' or 'idiosyncrasy'). That is, the apostle Paul does not say that the body becomes foreign. It remains the person's own body, but the person loses exousia (sole right to dispose) over it.
Here is how linguistics and the law of that time help to untangle this paradox:
6. Separation of 'property' and 'jurisdiction'
Paul distinguishes ontological belonging and legal right.
Imagine this as transferring property to trust management. The house remains your property (idios), it is your home, but you voluntarily transferred the right to make decisions about it (exousia) to another person. Your body remains an inseparable part of your personality, but within the marriage covenant you renounce the status of a sovereign monarch over it.
7. Context of asceticism and mutual duties
To understand why Paul formulates this so radically, one must look at the context of the 7th chapter. The Corinthians, captivated by a false spiritual radicalism, believed that the highest good was the refusal of intimate relationships in marriage (7:1 — 'it is good for a man not to touch a woman').
Someone in Corinth could say: 'This is my own body, and I have the right (exousia) to decide to dedicate it exclusively to spiritual practices, refusing my husband/wife'.
Paul responds to this with strict legal logic: 'Yes, the body is yours (idios). But you no longer have the right (exousia) to make such decisions solely, because during the conclusion of marriage you transferred the right of veto to your partner'.
8. Kenosis (self-emptying) in marriage
This paradox — 'to have one's own, but not to have power over it' — ideally reflects the broader Christian concept of love. This is voluntary vulnerability. A person has something most valuable and private (their body), but consciously and voluntarily surrenders, giving the management of it to another, expecting that the other will do the same for them. This is a radical mutual renunciation of egoistic autonomy.
That is, equality here is built not on the fact that everyone protects their rights (as in a modern contract), but on the fact that both partners are symmetrically deprived of sole rights before each other.

When Cleopas spoke about Christ, he said: mighty in deed and word (Luke XXIV, 19). The Pharisees, when they asked Christ about His authority (exousia), also had in mind the realization of what Christ did, taught, and said.

True authority in the Biblical understanding is connected with realization. For example, when the people were astonished at Christ's teaching, for he taught them as one having authority, and not as their scribes, it is not so much about the authoritativeness of Christ, but about the fact that He Himself performed all this, everything was realized, His words were realized even in a miraculous way (Matthew VII, 29).

And here, regarding 1 Corinthians VII, 4, it is necessary to understand that the matter is not in control, the opportunity to dispose, but in realization. A husband cannot realize his body alone, likewise a wife. A person themselves cannot realize their body in fullness. Likewise, the body alone does not act, does not realize its capabilities. At the same time, a person governs their body.

When someone enters into extramarital sexual relations, they also do not realize the body, because they do not have God's blessing in their actions.

Doctors during a medical examination and treatment often have access to the depths of the body, to internal and external organs, but they do not realize the body, its capabilities.

If by body we understand not the sexual sphere, but the body in general, then this same thought becomes even more expanded. For example, parents, caring for children, especially small ones, can do more with respect to the child's body than strangers. Likewise, children (including when infants suckle the breast) can do more with respect to the bodies of parents. But neither parents nor children realize the body of the children or parents respectively in God's design.

Introduction. The Space of Freedom and Realization: the Dynamics of Dynamis and Exousia in the Theology of the Body
Any attempt to comprehend Christian anthropology and the mystery of relationships inevitably faces the necessity of careful terminological delicacy. This is felt especially sharply in the interpretation of the words of the apostle Paul about the mutual power of spouses over the body of each other: 'The wife has not power [ouk exousiazei] over her own body, but the husband…' (1 Cor. 7:4). To avoid a false understanding of this 'possession' as violence or legal enslavement, one should turn to the classical biblical distinction between two dimensions of power: δύναμις (dynamis) and ἐξουσία (exousia).

1. Power (Dynamis) vs. Authority (Exousia)
In Greek thought and the New Testament language, these concepts describe fundamentally different aspects of reality:
Δύναμις (dynamis) — is a natural, internal power, potential, energy, or physical ability to do something (hence come 'dynamite' or 'dynamics'). It is the ability to act by the right of the stronger or by the laws of nature.
Ἐξουσία (exousia) — is not simply power, but a legitimate right, authority, empowerment, or freedom of choice. It is power that operates in a legal, moral, or spiritual space. It is the right to act, resting on authority or voluntary consent.
2. The Example with a Door: the Boundary Between Coercion and Mystery
The difference between these two categories is most vividly illustrated by a simple real-life image — the situation with a closed door:
A person who has dynamis (physical strength) can simply break a closed door with their shoulder. They overcome the obstacle by force, destroying its structure.
In contrast, a person who has exousia (authority/right) — is the one who has the key to this door. They open it freely, naturally, and without any destruction, because they have the lawful empowerment and access to do so.
This metaphor is crucial for understanding marital relationships. One who acts on the level of dynamis (solely through selfish passion or physical dominance) brutally 'breaks the door' of another's corporeality, turning the other into an object and destroying the Encounter. Instead, Christian marriage is a voluntary exchange of 'keys'. The husband and the wife transfer to each other precisely exousia — the right to freely enter the space of another without coercion, in love and mutual consent.
3. From the Surrender of Authority — to the Power of Realization
However, giving this authority (exousia) over one's own body to another, a person does not lose their agency. After all, as we noted earlier, the heart of a person is not an instrument that can be transferred; it remains an inner closet, preserved by God for the very capacity to love.
Moreover, biblical exousia always leads to the realization of God's design. As Christ taught 'as one having authority' (hos exousian echon, Matt. 7:29), because His words were instantly realized by deed, so also marital authority is not an instrument of control, but a shared power of realization. A person by themselves, in their ontological loneliness, is not capable of realizing their own body in fullness. Only by giving exousia to the beloved person, do we allow our body to spread its wings, overcome the boundaries of biology, and become a mysterious space of Encounter.
