Liturgy:
(Acts XVII, 19-28)
Acts XVII, 19 — 'αὕτη ἡ ὑπὸ σοῦ λαλουμένη διδαχή' — 'aute he hypo sou laloumene didakhe' - this teaching spoken by you. The preposition 'hypo' here with the genitive case indicates the passive voice of the participle spoken.

Acts XVII, 20 — 'εἰσφέρεις εἰς τὰς ἀκοὰς ἡμῶν' — 'eisphereis eis tas akoas hemon' - you are bringing into our ears. The same word as in the Lord's Prayer lead us not into temptation/trial. See more at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/21022026.html
Here what is meant is: what teaching are you proposing to us, acquainting us with (in a sense similar to to table a proposal for consideration). The preposition 'eis' indicates here whither the words of the apostle Paul are directed.

Acts XVII, 21 — 'εἰς οὐδὲν ἕτερον' — 'eis ouden heteron' - for nothing else; to anything else. The preposition 'eis' indicates here the goal and desired result of the actions of these people.

Acts XVII, 22 — 'ἐν μέσῳ τοῦ Ἀρίου πάγου' — 'en meso tou Ariou pagou' - in the midst of the Areopagus. Where. The preposition forms part of a common expression.

Acts XVII, 23 — 'ἐν ᾧ' — 'en ho' - in which; where. Common expression.

Acts XVII, 24 — 'τὸν κόσμον καὶ πάντα τὰ ἐν αὐτῷ' — 'ton kosmon kai panta ta en auto' - the world and all things that are in it. What and where. 'οὐκ ἐν χειροποιήτοις ναοῖς κατοικεῖ' - 'ouk en kheiropoietois naois katoikei' - does not dwell in shrines made by human hands. Where. It is important to note that here the word used is not temple — for example, the Jerusalem one — but shrine, sanctuary, temple in the broad sense. It must be understood that here the apostle Paul refutes incorrect understandings of sacred places. The first thing he refutes is that the divine action, God's presence, His perceptibility, the possibility of meeting with Him is found only in sacred places. The second thing Paul refutes here is that the initiators of the encounter with God are people. In reality it is precisely God Who seeks people in order to bestow gifts upon them — for He created them for blessed eternal life with Him. Such a vision of God also makes it possible to bypass many possible questions — including ethical, political, cultural ones, and so on — in order to move directly to the essence of the preaching: concerning Christ and His Resurrection.

Acts XVII, 25 — 'οὐδὲ ὑπὸ χειρῶν ἀνθρωπίνων θεραπεύεται προσδεόμενός τινος' — 'oude hypo kheiron anthropinon therapevetai prosdeomenos tinos' - nor does He desire any care (concern, 'healing', service) done by human hands. That is, what is spoken of is the fact that it is not people who take care of and concern themselves about God, but He about people. The preposition 'hypo' here with the genitive case indicates that by which that care is made — care that God has no need of. The apostle also redirects the attention and mind of the hearers from a material to a spiritual understanding of God as the Perfect Spirit Who loves people and creation, rather than needing them for Himself, for one or another personal need.

Acts XVII, 26 — 'ἐποίησέν τε ἐξ ἑνὸς πᾶν ἔθνος ἀνθρώπων κατοικεῖν ἐπὶ παντὸς προσώπου τῆς γῆς' — 'epoiesen te ex henos pan ethnos anthropon katoikein epi pantos prosopou tes ges' - and created from one the whole human people that dwells on the face (surface) of the earth. The expression points to the direct origin of the people who lived in the then known territory from the one Adam, and also to the origin of people from the one Adam through Eve. Acts XVII, 27 — 'οὐ μακρὰν ἀπὸ ἑνὸς ἑκάστου ἡμῶν ὑπάρχοντα' — 'ou makran apo henos hekastou hemon hyparkhonta' - not far from each one of us he exists; not far from each one of us. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that what is spoken of is a distance.

Acts XVII, 28 — 'ἐν αὐτῷ' — 'en auto' - in Him. In Him we live — for He bestows life; we move — for He created space, time, the forms of motion of matter and all the like, and gives strength and other things; we exist — for thanks to Him we do not pass into non-being but are such that we exist (in particular, the soul of a person is immortal and eternal not of itself but by God's will and His care). One may see here also the transitions and interweaving of the most varied forms and spheres of human existence — of each person in particular, of groups, and of all humanity, as also of all the rest of creation.

The sermon of Paul in Athens, in the Areopagus. Rich in theology — I wrote earlier about one of its verses.

I shall note that Paul draws upon the culture of the people he is addressing, showing interest — even though their traditions and faith are connected with polytheism.

In general all of this is about God's Providence concerning all people, not only the faithful — God cares for all people. At the same time He has prepared salvation in Christ Jesus, which is already greater than God's Providence.
