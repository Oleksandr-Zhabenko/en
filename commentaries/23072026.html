Liturgy:
(1 Corinthians X, 28-XI, 7)
1 Corinthians X, 29 — 'ὑπὸ ἄλλης συνειδήσεως' — 'hypo alles syneideseos' - by another's conscience. Here the preposition 'hypo' is used with the genitive case, indicating the passive voice of the verb (was judged), emphasizing that this refers to judgment exercised by the conscience of another person. The Apostle's question in this sense is completely logical: for if it were indeed the opposite — a person's liberty judged by another's conscience as judge — then people would be judging one another instead of God, because their consciences would possess the authority to judge others. But in truth, in the absolute sense, only God can judge.

1 Corinthians X, 31 — 'εἰς δόξαν θεοῦ' — 'eis doxan theou' - to the glory of God; unto the glory of God. The preposition 'eis' emphasizes the dynamic orientation of people themselves and the ultimate destination of their actions — what they ought to be crowned with, where they ought to lead and press toward.

The reading centers on care for conscience, as well as various personal distinctions among Christians and how these particularities ought to be understood. These are highly concentrated and profound texts, often difficult to grasp.

1) Regarding meat sacrificed to idols: A continuation of earlier readings on this topic, explaining that one must avoid idol-sacrificed food not because of any inherent substance in it, nor because it is some exceptionally evil spiritual food or source of malice, but for the sake of other people's consciences — so as not to create stumbling blocks leading to paganism, idolatry, apostasy from Christ, and the like.

2) Regarding men and women: Paul writes about spiritual realities that we (our contemporaries) do not fully comprehend. For instance, it remains a mystery why Paul mentions angels and what exact weight this carries in this context. Nevertheless, difficulty in understanding a passage in Scripture is no reason to reject it.

Paul states that distinctions between the sexes are provided for by God, and that their existence is good, just as all that God created is good. Paul emphasizes that one sex does not exist independently without the other — that they are mutually essential. He also speaks of their distinct roles. Having written on this topic previously, I note here again:

Why God created man first and woman afterwards is a divine mystery. Nonetheless, Paul states that this carries significance for all human history; it was not accidental nor a mere historical sequence. Rather, it belongs to the divine design. In light of this design, Paul frames all the aforementioned considerations.

It should be noted that due to textual obscurity, many misinterpretations easily arise.

I will focus on one key insight, from which many others can be properly understood: The statement that woman was created from man / through man does not mean she is less important, less significant, or inferior. It refers to God creating Eve from Adam's rib, from his body. God's creative intention for humanity unfolded in Eve through Adam. Here we see a clear prefiguration of Christ and the Church as the Body of Christ. The creation of Eve from Adam's body prefigures the Church becoming the Body of Christ, Who gives Himself up for her salvation. When a man and a woman are united in Christ and the Church, this archetype of Christ and the Church is manifestly revealed. It must be remembered that Eve is not a simple extension or product of Adam, but God creates her from his body as a prefiguration of Christ and the Church. Conversely, there is also the complementary reality—the Son of God is born of the Virgin, receiving His human flesh by the Holy Ghost and of the Virgin Mary, as stated in the Creed. Overall, there is no implication that woman is lower or less significant than man. Adam was created from the dust of the earth, whereas Eve was created from Adam's living body. This shows that humanity is at once the crown of visible creation according to God's plan and called to become part of the Church as the Body of Christ. Although authority (exousia) is mentioned frequently here, there is no notion of a natural authority of man over woman. Furthermore, it is emphasized that a woman covers her head because of the angels, not strictly because of man — it touches upon nature and spiritual order, not raw dominance. (Matthew XVI, 24-28)
Matthew XVI, 24 — 'ἀπαρνησάσθω ἑαυτὸν' — 'aparnesastho heauton' - let him completely deny himself; let him renounce himself. The first word derives from 'apo' and 'arneomai', where the prefix 'ap-' emphasizes complete refusal, distancing, and renunciation.

Matthew XVI, 27 — 'ἐν τῇ δόξῃ τοῦ πατρὸς' — 'en te doxe tou patros' - in the glory of the Father. In what state, in what environment, and in the manifestation of what.

Matthew XVI, 28 — 'ἐν τῇ βασιλείᾳ αὐτοῦ' — 'en te basileia autou' - in His Kingdom. How, in what, in the manifestation and power of what. In both instances, this functions as a spiritual sphere or environment — not merely a spatial location, but a fundamental transformation of the mode of existence of the world. Much like how the Resurrection of Christ transformed the mode of existence of His human nature.

The reading speaks of following Christ, of His Second Coming, and of the glory of the Kingdom that may be revealed to certain faithful even during this earthly life (shortly thereafter, these words were fulfilled in a singular way for Peter, James, and John on Mount Tabor during the Transfiguration).
