Liturgy:
(Romans IV, 4-12)
Romans IV, 5, 9 — 'δικαιοῦντα' — 'dikaiounta' - makes righteous (this is precisely the meaning in this passage). That is, God transforms an ungodly person into a righteous one when they are converted and renewed by grace and cooperation with it. For such renewal and conversion, for such transformation into a righteous person, one must believe that this is possible, believe that it will come to pass, trust God in this, and carry out what He calls to. Therefore genuine repentance rests upon such faith. 'εἰς δικαιοσύνην' - 'eis dikaiosynen' - into righteousness; as righteousness. The preposition 'eis' indicates here what such faith becomes.

Romans IV, 6 — 'χωρὶς ἔργων' — 'khoris ergon' - apart from works; without works. Both translations are possible here, though most always give only the second. The first means that faith becomes a separate value — it is capable of changing a person's life, drawing other virtues in its wake. The second means that this value can be so weighty as to be capable of covering the lack of other virtues. For example, the wise robber on the cross repented and through faith — without other significant virtues (though with humility, but that was not as significant as the faith) — entered paradise first. This is not an encouragement to inaction and fruitlessness, but an affirmation of the extraordinary power and weight of faith. Genuine faith is capable of saving a person even when there is nowhere to expect salvation — in particular when (as yet) there are no good works; but this does not mean that if salvation is expected from faith without works whilst neglecting them, faith itself will bring salvation. Faith is a talent, a divine gift that can save and is capable of it — but a person can also bury the talent in the ground, the example and warning of which we see in the Gospel.

Romans IV, 10 — 'ἐν περιτομῇ' — 'en peritome' - in circumcision. 'ἐν ἀκροβυστίᾳ' - 'en akrobystia' - in uncircumcision. In both expressions what is spoken of is the state of the person.

Romans IV, 11 — 'ἐν τῇ ἀκροβυστίᾳ' — 'en te akrobystia' - in the uncircumcision (spoken of just now). State. 'εἰς τὸ εἶναι αὐτὸν πατέρα πάντων τῶν πιστευόντων' - 'eis to einai auton patera panton ton pisteuonton' - so that he might be the father of all who believe. The preposition 'eis' indicates here the purpose. 'εἰς τὸ λογισθῆναι αὐτοῖς δικαιοσύνην' - 'eis to logisthenai autois dikaiosynen' - so that righteousness might be reckoned to them. Here too it indicates purpose. See also at the link concerning the seal:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/13062025.html

Romans IV, 12 — 'τοῖς οὐκ ἐκ περιτομῆς μόνον' — 'tois ouk ek peritomes monon' - (father) not only of those from circumcision. That is, father not only of those who, like Abraham, have physical circumcision. 'ἀλλὰ καὶ τοῖς στοιχοῦσιν τοῖς ἴχνεσιν τῆς ἐν ἀκροβυστίᾳ πίστεως τοῦ πατρὸς ἡμῶν Ἀβραάμ' - 'alla kai tois stoikhousin tois ikhnesin tes en akrobystia pisteos tou patros hemon Abraam' - but also (father) of those who walk in ordered fashion (who proceed with purposeful, ordered, conscious intent towards the goal) in the footsteps of the faith of our father Abraham, which he had in uncircumcision. That is, Abraham was called righteous at a time still prior to circumcision — called through faith and not through circumcision. Therefore above all one must follow the faith of Abraham.

Verses Romans IV, 4-5 speak of two kinds of righteousness — or one may put it differently: of who can be set as an example of righteousness. The first is the good fulfilment of God's commandments and God's will (life without mortal sins) — one may call this classical righteousness. The second is more difficult to understand, but a substantial part of the reading is devoted to it and it requires understanding. The ungodly person in verse 5 is in reality… to a considerable degree the person themselves (probably many readers of the passage) who strives for righteousness but has not fulfilled (did not fulfil) God's commandments without mortal sins — who has sinned in some way. Any (even a single) mortal sin without God's forgiveness renders the person spiritually dead. As the apostle James says in another place: For whoever keeps the whole Law but fails in one point has become guilty of all of it (James II, 10). James II, 10 — 'ἐν ἑνί' — 'en heni' - in one; with one. In what or by means of what. 'ἔνοχος' - 'enokhos' - answerable for; implicated in. What is meant is NOT that the person violates all the commandments and is guilty of all of them, but that the Law of Moses reveals their guilt — shows that it exists not in vain, for the purpose of exposure, and implicates the person in a state of judgement. (See more at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/09012026.html
), and also compare with what is at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/08012026.html
https://www.instagram.com/oleksandr_zhabenko/p/DT6fitxAvd9/?img_index=1
Thus in the verse Romans IV, 5 what is spoken of is a person who has in some way sinned mortally — and whom the Lord has forgiven, in particular in connection with their faith. Faith here means not simply confidence that the Lord exists and forgives sins and leads to righteousness, but life directed towards fulfilling God's will and God's commandments — the faith about which the apostle speaks throughout the entire Epistle — and here also repentance, for a person who has sinned deeply in some way genuinely needs repentance. Faith in the One Who justifies (makes righteous) the ungodly — in effect and above all here faith in Jesus Christ Who came to seek and save what was lost — is a manifestation of the relationship with Him in repentance, in cooperation with God's grace, God's will, and God's action. Therefore faith here is a sign of this invisible bond with Christ. And the bond with Christ leads to the fact that a person, even having fallen into sin, is called to salvation — the Lord is powerful enough to raise them, even if at present they lack the strength. One may say that in the second sense the person is righteous potentially — through faith that directs their life and strength and actions towards Christ, towards salvation. The second righteousness is the righteousness of justification in the direct sense of the word — being set on the path of righteousness, becoming just and righteous. The righteous person here is not actually and manifestly so, but is in the process of becoming and moving, in cooperation with God's mercy and love of humanity.

Paul says that faith precedes works. A particular example of this is the story of Abraham. Righteousness was reckoned to him for his faith — not for circumcision — but he performed circumcision for the sake of faith.

Therefore genuine good faith is a kind of seed of good works, just as sincere and unhypocritical love is the root from which only good fruits will grow — as Blessed Augustine says (if I am not mistaken in the attribution).

In speaking of reckoning unto righteousness, what is spoken of is God's foreknowledge: even when Abraham had not yet performed works, God — having given him faith, which Abraham received and preserved — knew that works would subsequently come from it. We discussed this in more detail during Great Lent, when the story of Abraham from the book of Genesis was being read.

(Matthew VII, 15-21)
Matthew VII, 15 — 'Προσέχετε ἀπὸ τῶν ψευδοπροφητῶν' — 'Prosekhete apo ton pseudopropheton' - Be vigilant to avoid false prophets. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that they must be avoided entirely, wholly, completely. 'ἐν ἐνδύμασιν προβάτων' - 'en endymasin probaton' - in sheep's clothing. In what (they are clothed). Prophets were often shepherds (Moses, David, and others) — so what is spoken of is the outward imitation of genuine prophets.

Matthew VII, 16, 20 — 'ἀπὸ τῶν καρπῶν αὐτῶν' — 'apo ton karpon auton' - from their fruits. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that these fruits must be examined more objectively — one must not rely solely on one's personal opinion, views, or perception, which are often under the influence of various circumstances, events, or people (and even spirits) and can be very subjective. 'μήτι συλλέγουσιν ἀπὸ ἀκανθῶν σταφυλὰς ἢ ἀπὸ τριβόλων σῦκα;' - 'meti syllegousin apo akanthon staphylas e apo tribolon syka?' - do they gather grapes from thorn bushes or figs from thistles? Here Christ to a certain degree defends those who might be mistakenly perceived as false prophets — by those who understand His warning in an uncritical and entrenched manner. He does not say that genuine prophets will not come, but He does not say that one must listen to everyone who outwardly resembles a prophet. This means that there will be those who are genuinely similar to true prophets, and there will be those who are not. The former will bring wonderful fruits that must be received for peace and happiness, whilst the latter will bring evil. Discernment is needed. See also the fine citation from the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/13062023.html
Verses Matthew VII, 17-18 in Greek have literally this meaning: Every tree that is good in the broad sense — naturally, genuinely good — produces beautiful, noble, good, masterful fruits; whilst the damaged, bad, spoiled tree produces bad and harmful fruits. A tree that is good (in the broad sense, naturally, genuinely good) has no power (cannot, is not able) to produce bad and harmful fruits — any more than a damaged, spoiled, bad tree can produce beautiful, noble, good, masterful fruits. By fruits here what is meant is something new in general — something that did not exist before. Example: the author of a masterpiece in art cannot be an ignoramus or incapable of art. Fruits carry within them a higher degree of characterisation than their human creators. Hence everyone strives to have good and fruitful trees. And if a tree bears no beautiful fruits, its position in the garden is precarious — on inspection it may be uprooted.

Matthew VII, 19 — 'εἰς πῦρ' — 'eis pyr' - into the fire. Whither.

Matthew VII, 21 — 'εἰς τὴν βασιλείαν τῶν οὐρανῶν' — 'eis ten basileian ton ouranon' - into the Kingdom of Heaven. Whither. One of the meanings of this frequent expression in the Gospels is: already in this life to live in such a way as to fulfil God's will entirely — as will be the case in the eternal Kingdom of God after the general resurrection of the dead. 'ὁ ποιῶν τὸ θέλημα τοῦ πατρός μου τοῦ ἐν τοῖς οὐρανοῖς' - 'ho poion to thelema tou patros mou tou en tois ouranois' - the one who does the will of My Father Who is in the heavens. Where.

In the Sermon on the Mount Christ speaks of false prophets — or in modern terms: of false spirituality.

Concerning Matthew VII, 21 — it is not sufficient to be a Christian in name alone; it is not sufficient either to surround oneself with Christian traditions, customs, attributes, and symbols. What is important for salvation is the fulfilment of God's will. Though even the former can lead to salvation, if it is the fulfilment of God's will. For example, many martyrs gave their lives for confessing the very name of Christians.

In general the reading balances the Epistle reading. If the Epistle today speaks more of faith, then the Gospel speaks more of works. For salvation both are important. One may also put it thus: if you have works but do not have genuine faith — take care to know the truth and to obtain genuine faith. If you have genuine faith but do not have works — take care to obtain, through faith (in faith), good works.

Saint John Chrysostom also says that on the Day of the Bright Resurrection of Christ let no one grieve over their sins, for forgiveness has shone forth from the tomb. And speaking in memory of today's words of the Saviour, he says: Even if a wolf says to you: 'Christ is Risen!', answer: 'Truly he is Risen!' See on this at the link:

Of the Holy Bishop Cyril, Archbishop of Alexandria:
(Hebrews XIII, 7-16; Matthew V, 14-19)
Hebrews XIII, 8 — 'εἰς τοὺς αἰῶνας' — 'eis tous aionas' - for ever; unto the ages. Common expression.

Hebrews XIII, 9 — 'ἐν οἷς' — 'en hois' - in which; with which. The second translation is more likely. It means that foods are, what people in their spiritual life consider useful and nourishing, good, and life-giving.

Hebrews XIII, 10 — 'ἐξ οὗ' — 'ex hou' - from which; of which. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following 'hou' indicates here whence that comes. Many gifts were received by the faithful from the Sacrifice of Christ — which is what is spoken of here.

Hebrews XIII, 11 — 'εἰς τὰ ἅγια' — 'eis ta hagia' - into the holy (places); into the sanctuary. Literally plural. What is meant is the holiness of the temple — the all-holiness of the one Old Testament sanctuary of the true God.

Hebrews XIII, 10 — those who serve the tabernacle — Jews who did not believe in Christ. Paul here compares Christians with Jews after the building of the Temple in Jerusalem in the Promised Land — whilst the Jews themselves are compared with those who wandered through the desert with Moses, making their way to that Promised Land, carrying the tabernacle in a tent with them.

Figuratively he then speaks of the possible sufferings of the Christians themselves, speaking of their connection with the Sufferings of Jesus Christ. He also calls for praise to God and gratitude to Him (which is also directly connected here with the Eucharist).

Hebrews XIII, 16 — fellowship — 'κοινωνιας' — may also be translated as communion (participation in). What is meant in general is mutual inclusion in one another's lives, participation and involvement, communication and fellowship. The word is deeply theological, but due to limited space I shall not dwell on it in detail.

Matthew V, 15 — 'ὑπὸ τὸν μόδιον' — 'hypo ton modion' - under the basket (a certain measure for dry goods). The preposition 'hypo' here with the accusative. 'ἐν τῇ οἰκίᾳ' - 'en te oikia' - in the house. Where.

Matthew V, 16 — 'ἐν τοῖς οὐρανοῖς' — 'en tois ouranois' - in the heavens. Where.

Matthew V, 18 — 'ἀπὸ τοῦ νόμου' — 'apo tou nomou' - from the Law. The preposition 'apo' indicates that what is spoken of is complete disappearance and nullification — more precisely, since it is in fact negated here, what is spoken of is that they will not disappear. In other words, nothing in the Law is unimportant — everything has meaning and significance, even the diacriticals and the grammar.

Matthew V, 19 — 'ἐν τῇ βασιλείᾳ τῶν οὐρανῶν' — 'en te basileia ton ouranon' - in the Kingdom of Heaven. Where.

Concerning the iota (the smallest letter of the Greek alphabet in its written form, and in Hebrew the corresponding letter yod is also the smallest) — there is an example from Church history: in the 4th century, during the Arian controversies, as a compromise the doctrine of the like-in-substance of the Son to the Father arose in place of the correct doctrine of consubstantiality. The first word differs from the second in Greek by… one letter in the middle... And that letter is iota! Yes, the very same iota!

Historians note that the general understanding of the faith and the whole of Christianity depended upon the acceptance or rejection of one small letter.

One may see that in Scripture even what seems insignificant is important. All Scripture is God-breathed. And all of it matters — even if we may not understand why.

These words about the iota also speak of the fact that the Lord manifests His love for us even in such small things, that He cares for everyone — and such particular examples are also one of the innumerable confirmations of this.
