Liturgy:
(Romans VI, 11-17)
Romans VI, 11 — 'ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ' — 'en CHristo Iesou' - in Christ Jesus; with (by means of) Christ Jesus. Rather the first variant of translation, since it is more comprehensible.

Romans VI, 12 — 'ἐν τῷ θνητῷ ὑμῶν σώματι' — 'en to thneto hymon somati' - in the mortal body of you; with (by means of) your mortal body. In what or with what. Both variants of translation are possible. The first indicates onto that sin must not reign (rule, dominate) in the body, that is, the body must not be a means for the accomplishment of sin. The second indicates onto that one must not let sin use the weakness of the body for the enslavement of a person into its snare, for in such a case the body also becomes an instrument of sin, a means, a tool. 'εἰς τὸ ὑπακούειν' - 'eis to hypakouein' - unto obedience; for to obey. The preposition 'eis' indicates here onto the goal.

Romans VI, 13 — 'ἐκ νεκρῶν ζῶντας' — 'ek nekron zontas' - as [ones] alive out of [the] dead; living out of [the] dead. A parallel with the Resurrection of Christ. The preposition 'ek' indicates here from where the transition takes place — from the dead into the living. Therefore both variants are suitable; that one appears more expedient which is more eloquent, striking (if someone experienced a resurrection, this would be striking, although if one recalls the parable of the rich man and Lazarus, unbelief is capable even then to remain).

Romans VI, 14, 15 — 'οὐ γάρ ἐστε ὑπὸ νόμον ἀλλὰ ὑπὸ χάριν' — 'ou gar este hypo nomon alla hypo kharin' - for you are not under Law, but under grace. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the accusative and indicates here onto whom people deliver themselves into obedience, whom or what they obey.

Romans VI, 16 — 'εἰς ὑπακοήν' — 'eis hypakoen' - into obedience; unto obedience; for obedience. The preposition 'eis' emphasizes the gradualness of the manifestation of obedience — a person gradually, moving, delivers themselves into obedience. A very important statement regarding the development of spiritual life and also of various sinful dependencies, sinful passions. 'εἰς θάνατον' - 'eis thanaton' - into death; for death. 'εἰς δικαιοσύνην' - 'eis dikaiosynen' - into righteousness; for righteousness. Here in both cases the preposition 'eis' emphasizes the movement and the final point of destination of the movement, but can also indicate here also the goal (rather in relation into righteousness, for rarely do people truly desire eternal death for themselves).

Romans VI, 17 — 'ἐκ καρδίας' — 'ek kardias' - out of heart. That is, sincerely, entirely, personally and consciously. Such an expression is in one of the formulations of the two main commandments of love — into God and neighbour. 'εἰсь ὃν παρεδόθητε τύπον διδαχῆς' - 'eis on paredothete typon didakhes' - into which form of teaching you were delivered; unto which form of teaching you were delivered. The use of the pair of prepositions 'ek'-'eis' indicates onto the movement away out of — into. First there was a heartfelt decision, and then growth and perfection in Christian teaching, piety, faith, life, etc.

Paul uses the condition of slaves well-known to the people of that time. A slave does not do that into which they have an inclination, a natural leaning, an interest, but that which they are forced to do. Moreover, a slave has no way to get rid of this state by their own forces without violating the laws of that time; their masters could only free them, let them go.

For the state of a person who commits sin — the comparison with slavery to sin is very expressive — a person also commits in sin not that which is necessary for them, and cannot deliver themselves away out of sin (deliverance away out of sin signifies such a change in a person that they are not even tempted by it, a complete deliverance, sin becomes inactive, powerless), and sin (by definition) cannot let go or free a person away from itself by itself.

Further Paul extends the comparison already onto righteousness (today these words are not read, but at another time), and emphasizes that this is only a metaphor which fixes, voices that a person entrusts themselves completely into God, but this is not slavery in essence, but only a metaphor (I speak after the manner of people, — Paul speaks about that). A person who truly lives for God can say that their entire nature — truly is a good gift to their very self and others through them — out of God. A gift — is that which you accept away out of love, which in no way burdens you, which grants joy.

(Matthew VIII, 14-23)
Matthew VIII, 14 — 'εἰς τὴν οἰκίαν Πέτρου' — 'eis ten oikian Petrou' - into the house of Peter. Where to.

Matthew VIII, 18 — 'εἰς τὸ πέραν' — 'eis to peiran' - into the other side [unto the side beyond]. Where to. A set expression.

Matthew VIII, 23 — 'εἰς τὸ πλοῖον' — 'eis to ploion' - into the boat (to the boat). Where to.

I would like to dwell on the verses Matthew VIII, 19-22. They are often little understood. If you compare the parallel passage regarding the thrice-repeated denial of the apostle Peter, then the situation will become clear. In short — the Lord expects away out of disciples yes! into Him, but a yes! humble, not self-confident.

The first dialogue is about self-confidence. An unbaptized person is so confident in themselves that they openly speak that they will follow after Christ wheresoever He might go (compare Peter). The words of Christ show that His state is a state supernatural, not natural like with animals. And would that person follow after Christ into sufferings? And what if Christ begins to open into them the heavenly? And what if He opens into them that which shall be after two thousand (ten thousand, a million, a billion, etc., there can be so many or even more) years, will they believe? For what will remain out of their understanding in that distant time away out of us (and even in the present, what has remained)? And if He gives into them unseen until then revelations, will they believe? And if they believe, from where will they know that they have not erred? And that person thinks that they can by their nature, by their own forces be everywhere where Christ is. This is the first mistake. The answer is analogous to the answer into Peter. As a summary, no one contains the fullness of the Gospel except Jesus. People are weak, but in all times enough is opened so as to be saved. And the saints performed that, not self-confidently, but humbly.

The second dialogue is about the opposite extreme — the putting off of life according into the Gospel for later, until better times, after the pandemic, after the war, for tomorrow, when they grow up, etc. If a person wishes to live with Christ, and not to vegetate, or to wait for conversion like the spiritually dead, then it is necessary to begin without putting off, now, here and right now, not looking onto the circumstances, favorable or not.

As a summary of both — the Gospel is higher than the human, higher than the temporary, eternal.

End of quote.
