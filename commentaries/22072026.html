Liturgy:
(1 Corinthians X, 12-22)
1 Corinthians X, 14 — 'ἀπὸ τῆς εἰδωλολατρείας' — 'apo tes eidololatreias' - from idolatry; from the worship of idols. The preposition 'apo' emphasizes every effort to avoid possible service to idols, the struggle against it, and the absence of fellowship.

1 Corinthians X, 17 — 'ἐκ τοῦ ἑνὸς ἄρτου' — 'ek tou henos artou' - from the one bread; of the one bread. The preposition 'ek', which is omitted in many translations, actually carries a deep meaning — it points to the source of origin of the unity of Christians — to communion in the Body of Christ, in the Church. Through the Holy Communion of the Body and Blood of Christ, the Church is created as the Body of Christ, and the Holy Mysteries here are the source of Christian unity. See more at the links:

Paul states that the faithful are one in the Eucharist, forming one body, the mystical Body of Christ, the Church.

The cup which we… — Holy Communion.

At the same time, regarding idolatry, Paul explicitly testifies that these sacrifices are offered not to God, but to demons. Here it is necessary to recall that once in Athens Paul preached and mentioned the piety of the Athenians, who had an altar To the Unknown God, and Paul said that it was Him whom he was preaching (a missionary approach, one might say) (Acts XVII, 22-31). Why did Paul not characterize the sacrifices on that altar as idolatry or as sacrifices to demons, whereas the sacrifices of pagans to idols are sacrifices to demons? What is the difference?

This is not entirely a clear question, but the following can be seen. Before pagans began to offer sacrifices to idols, they either witnessed unusual manifestations that they understood as gods, or they invented those gods for various reasons. In both cases, the idols were a direct result of deception. And deception comes from the evil one, who is the father of lies and deception.

When people offered sacrifices on the altar to the Unknown God, it was a result of… truth. That truth that they lacked something in paganism, and they were seeking the true God. That truth that they did not know Him and humbly acknowledged this. That truth that this was a manifestation of their genuine piety (in this Unknown God they had to believe if, knowing nothing about Him, they still built an altar to Him, acknowledging their ignorance). And therefore, since the Source of truth is the True God, this altar was — to Him. But faith in the Unknown God is weak by virtue of the fact that they did not know how exactly to believe in Him correctly and how important it is.

(Matthew XVI, 20-24)
Matthew XVI, 21 — 'Ἀπὸ τότε' — 'apo tote' - from that time on; since then. A set expression. The preposition 'apo' can emphasize here that that moment had passed; Jesus awaited and knew about this confession in advance as God, hence He acted further. 'εἰς Ἱεροσόλυμα' - 'eis Hierosolyma' - into Jerusalem; to Jerusalem. Where to. 'ἀπὸ τῶν πρεσβυτέρων καὶ ἀρχιερέων καὶ γραμματέων' - 'apo ton presbyteron kai arkhiereon kai grammateon' - from the elders [presbyters; priests], and chief priests, and scribes. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that people are not the real cause of Christ's sufferings, but rather their sins.

Matthew XVI, 23 — 'ὕπαγε ὀπίσω' — 'hypage opiso' - get behind; step back behind; go behind. Jesus tells Peter not to stand in the way, on the path, to step back and not hinder. It emphasizes the complete and utter determination with which the Lord goes to Jerusalem for His voluntary Sufferings, Death, and Resurrection.

Matthew XVI, 24 — 'ἀπαρνησάσθω ἑαυτὸν' — 'aparnesastho heauton' - let him completely deny himself; let him renounce himself. The first word comes from 'apo' and 'arneomai', where the prefix 'ap' emphasizes refusal, distancing, renunciation.

The Evangelist writes not about Peter, but about Jesus. The main Person in both of these readings is not Peter, but Jesus (as in the entire Gospel). If we look at Jesus, in today's reading we see the absence of flattering people. Jesus does not try to appease anyone or gain someone's favor through praise, etc. Therefore, He can effectively respond to the sins of anyone, even those who are close to Him. Personal attitude and relationships do not obscure the truth from Him.

As for Peter, we see his (as well as the other disciples' at that time) weakness; he thought in purely human categories about divine matters.

Of Equal-to-the-Apostles Mary Magdalene, Holy Myrrh-bearer:
(1 Corinthians IX, 2-12; John XX, 11-18)
1 Corinthians IX, 2 — 'ἐν κυρίῳ' — 'en kyrio' - in the Lord.

1 Corinthians IX, 7 — 'ἐκ τοῦ γάλακτος τῆς ποίμνης' — 'ek tou galaktos tes poimnes' - from the milk of the flock. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that such physical support is entirely natural, justified, right, and logical.

1 Corinthians IX, 9 — 'ἐν γὰρ τῷ Μωϋσέως νόμῳ γέγραπται' — 'en gar to Moyseos nomo gegraptai' - for it is written in the Law of Moses. Where.

Apostle Paul speaks about the need to recognize the value of labor, even if it is carried out not by that person. The Apostles did not always meet the expectations of their listeners, but this does not diminish their labor. Moreover, they always made sure that possible objections would not hinder the preaching. One can see that Mary Magdalene is also equal-to-the-apostles, because such is her labor and ministry. Thus the Lord glorified her.

John XX, 11 — 'εἰς τὸ μνημεῖον' — 'eis to mnemeion' - into the tomb; to the tomb. Where to.

John XX, 12 — 'δύο ἀγγέλους ἐν λευκοῖς καθεζομένους' — 'duo angelous en leukois kathezomenous' - two angels in white [bright garments] sitting. In what clothing.

John XX, 14 — 'ἐστράφη εἰς τὰ ὀπίσω' — 'estraphe eis ta opiso' - turned back. Where to.

Here my beloved saint woman (besides the Mother of God), Mary Magdalene, meets the Risen Christ, among the very first. Having come to pay homage to the body of the Teacher, she does not find Him and thinks that the gardener took the body. Her polite and respectful address shows that she had humility, as well as readiness for service. The appearance of Christ also shows that one can see Him in other people, and indeed, such a thought is also clearly visible from the parable of the Last Judgment. Great joy to meet the Risen Christ!
