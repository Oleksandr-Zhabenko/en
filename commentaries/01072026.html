Liturgy:
(Romans XV, 7-16)
Romans XV, 7 — 'εἰς δόξαν τοῦ θεοῦ' — 'eis doxan tou theou' - into [the] glory of God. The preposition 'eis' emphasizes movement towards glory and the state, the moment of entering into it, that is, glorification.

Romans XV, 8 — 'εἰς τὸ βεβαιῶσαι' — 'eis to bebaiosai' - into confirmation; for to confirm. Here the preposition 'eis' indicates onto the goal of the action.

Romans XV, 9 — 'ἐν ἔθνεσιν' — 'en ethnesin' - among nations; among ethnoses; among Gentiles. All three variants of translation are correct, but each is partial because of the richness of meaning in the words. In the subsequent verses-prophecies, as well as in verse 16, the forms of this word have three meanings, all of which are correct at the same time, but in different senses.

Romans XV, 13 — 'ἐν τῷ πιστεύειн, εἰς τὸ περισσεύειν ὑμᾶς ἐν τῇ ἐλπίδι ἐν δυνάμει πνεύματος ἁγίου' — 'en to pisteuein eis to perisseuein hymas en te elpidi en dynamei pneumatos hagiou' - in the believing (as the space and time of faith) into the overflowing (for the overflowing; that you might overflow) of you in the hope in the Holy Spirit (with (by means of) hope with (by means of) the Holy Spirit; in hope with (by means of) the Holy Spirit; with (by means of) hope in the Holy Spirit). All variants of translation are correct, but carry different meaning. Perhaps it is expedient to consider the complete meaning as a combination of all variants.

Romans XV, 15 — 'ἀπὸ μέρους' — 'apo merous' - in part. This is a set phrase. 'ἀπὸ τοῦ θεοῦ' - 'apo tou theou' - away from God. Here 'apo' emphasizes that grace is God's gift into Paul. Granted to such an extent as to be entirely Paul's, without ceasing to be God's.

Romans XV, 16 — 'εἰς τὸ εἶναί με λειτουργὸν Χριστοῦ Ἰησοῦ εἰς τὰ ἔθνη' — 'eis to einai me leitourgon CHristou Iesou eis ta ethne' - for to be me a participant of a common cause (that is, the one who takes part in the cause of the salvation of mankind, the cause of the accomplishment of the entire Divine design regarding creation together with others) of Christ Jesus into the nations (Gentiles; ethnoses). The word used here has an important meaning for theology, since from it originates the name of the Liturgy, the common deed. Most translations omit a fuller translation of the key word 'leitourgon'; such an omission stands out and emphasizes the significance of Paul as a person, but diminishes the meaning. 'προσφορά' — 'prosphora' - offering (thus also is named the bread which is used for the performance of the Eucharist). 'εὐπρόσδεκτος, ἡγιασμένη ἐν πνεύματι ἁγίῳ' - 'euprosdektos, hegiasmene en pneumati hagio' - well-received, sanctified in the Holy Spirit; acceptable, sanctified in the Holy Spirit; well-received, sanctified with the Holy Spirit. The variants with a preposition in translation indicate onto that the Holy Spirit Himself sanctifies, as God, and with the instrumental case — that the Father and the Son sanctify through the Holy Spirit. In general — it concerns the Trinity explicitly or implicitly.

Paul writes about the unity of Christians out of the Gentiles and those away out of Israel. Also he speaks about his personal service among the Gentiles as the apostle of the Gentiles. Also here is emphasized the conciliar dimension of the Church, her liturgicty. The salvation of people, the fulfillment of the entire Divine design is a common deed of Christ and the faithful.

(Matthew XII, 38-45)
Matthew XII, 38 — 'θέλομεν ἀπὸ σοῦ σημεῖον ἰδεῖν' — 'thelomen apo sou semeion idein' - we desire, long, wish away from Thee a sign, a miracle to see, know, experience, deeply perceive. Actually, the words used here emphasize not only a desire for miracles, but more of a deep miraculous experience, feeling, the fullness of the miraculous not only from an objective, but also a subjective point of view. Moreover, the preposition 'apo' here emphasizes that they are not interested in the proper meaning of the miracle itself and the testimony about Jesus, not interested in Who He Himself is and what follows out of this, but are interested in the possibility to receive and to appropriate the experienced entirely miraculous thing.

Matthew XII, 40 — 'ἐν τῇ κοιλίᾳ τοῦ κήτους' — 'en te koilia tou ketous' - in the belly of the sea monster; in the belly of the great fish. Where. Different variants of translation are possible. See also the well-known story away out of modern times (the text is in Ukrainian):
https://parafia.org.ua/biblioteka/hrystyyanstvo-virovchennya-ta-tradytsiji/zakon-bozhyj
'ἐν τῇ καρδίᾳ τῆς γῆς τρεῖς ἡμέρας καὶ τρεῖς νύκτας' — 'en te kardia tes ges treis hemeras kai treis nyktas' — in the heart of the earth three days and three nights. Where. A passage which seems very simply to indicate onto the Sufferings, Death and Resurrection of Christ during three days. But if one begins to think into it, then very essential details become visible. Previously I wrote, quoting:
https://www.facebook.com/Oleksandr.S.Zhabenko/posts/
There exist different explanations, and the naive one, that it concerns Holy Friday, Saturday and the day of the Resurrection of Jesus Christ and our ordinary days and nights — is erroneous, although it seems the most logical. Erroneous, because Christ was dead out of the 15th hour of Friday until 00.00 (approximately, more generally — before the dawn) of Sunday — and this cannot be our three days and three nights (if a part is considered as a whole, then these are two days and two nights, regardless of how the counting of the beginning of the day is kept — according into the Biblical and liturgical manner — that is, away from the evening of the previous day, or according into our modern method — away from midnight). Even if one divides the night by midnight into two nights, each of which belongs into different modern days, then even under such an artificial construction we shall have only two days (still incomplete) and three nights. Therefore the naive interpretation obviously is incorrect, and only approximate.
In my opinion, the most correct is such a thought, that the heart of the earth is not hades, whither by the soul descended Christ after the death upon the cross, but this is a metaphor which generally signifies His voluntary sufferings, death, burial, descent into hades, Resurrection.
As the heart it is named because these events focus, concentrate all that which is chief in the life of people — all into which life became after the fall, all that briefly describes the temporary life in this season and the future resurrection, just as the heart most significantly characterises a person. The earth is the world in the understanding of the method of existence of all mankind.
And wherefrom are the days and nights? The eclipse of the sun during the sufferings of Christ according into prophecies is precisely the third night which came at daytime. And as days and nights alternate, then in the heart of the earth we have such three days and three nights:
1 day — Holy Friday before the onset of darkness during the crucifixion;
1 night — darkness during the crucifixion;
2 day — away from the cessation of darkness until the night away out of Friday into Saturday;
2 night — away out of Friday into Saturday;
3 day — Holy Saturday;
3 night — the Resurrection night.
This understanding is confirmed also by the mention by Matthew of the resurrection of many of the departed and their manifestation into many precisely after the crucifixion, and not after the Resurrection of Jesus, although logical is precisely the second.
Therefore here Christ speaks not about our days and nights and not about hades, but about that which is the focus of His ministry and the image of all our life.
I will say still a little regarding an evil and adulterous generation (Matthew XII, 39). Evil — wicked, if to say more precisely, not by nature, but by virtue of its committing of evil, effectively evil; adulterous — as a woman who betrays her husband, — Israel is named God's Bride, and subsequently in the same manner also the Church. The prophets compared with betrayal the turning away from God for the sake of creation.

End of quote.

Matthew XII, 41 — 'ἄνδρες Νινευεῖται ἀναστήσονται ἐν τῇ κρίσει μετὰ τῆς γενεᾶς ταύτης καὶ κατακρινοῦσιν αὐτήν' — 'andres Nineueitai anastesontai en te krisei meta tes geneas tautes kai katakrinousin auten' - the men of Nineveh shall rise up, stand, resurrect in the judgement with this generation and shall condemn it. That is, not simply shall stand, but shall rise up, turn out to be higher or resurrect in the judgement. It concerns both the general Resurrection of the dead, the Last Judgement, but also onto that that those Ninevites shall be exalted, magnified beside the Pharisees. 'εἰς τὸ κήρυγμα' - 'eis to kerygma' - into the preaching. There is emphasized the change in the Ninevites under the action of the preaching, one can say, their entering into the heard thing, their faith and conversion.

Matthew XII, 42 — 'βασίλισσα νότου ἐγερθήσεται ἐν τῇ κρίσει μετὰ τῆς γενεᾶς ταύτης καὶ κατακρινεῖ αὐτήν' — 'basilissa notou egerthesetai en te krisei meta tes geneas tautes kai katakrinei auten' - the queen of the South shall rise up (stand, resurrect) in the judgement with this generation and shall condemn it. See above, analogously. 'ἐκ τῶν περάτων τῆς γῆς' - 'ek ton peraton tes ges' - out of the ends of the earth. The preposition 'ek' indicates here onto that she originates out of a nation distant and foreign for the Israelites. These two examples of Old Testament history show that there cannot be a true justification for complete unrepentance and insensitivity, or even exasperation regarding Christ, neither the cultural difference which was in particular between the Ninevites and the Israelites, nor the geographical distances and boundaries which were between the queen of the South and Solomon.

Matthew XII, 43 — 'ἀπὸ τοῦ ἀνθρώπου' — 'apo tou anthropou' - from the person. The preposition 'apo' indicates here onto that it shall go out completely, fully, entirely.

Matthew XII, 44 — 'εἰς τὸν οἶκόν μου' — 'eis ton oikon mou' - into the house of me; to my house. Where to.
