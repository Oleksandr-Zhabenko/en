Liturgy:
(Romans VIII, 2-13)
Romans VIII, 2 — 'ἠλευθέρωσέν σε ἀπὸ τοῦ νόμου τῆς ἁμαρτίας καὶ τοῦ θανάτου' — 'eleutherosen se apo tou nomou tes amartias kai tou thanatou' — freed you from the law of the sin and of the death. The apostle begins to use the word law constantly; here in the passage we have the law of the mind, the Law of God, the law of the Spirit, the law of the sin, and the law of the death. And previously there was simply the Law. Actually, here it is that which is more durable, more enduring, stronger than all that can be changed by a simple effort of a person. The image of the desired good, named the law of the mind, cannot simply be arbitrarily changed by a person, for it attracts them, reflecting the depth of their personality; the Law of God is established by God; the law of the Spirit of the life in Christ — is a gift of grace, which is able to operate upon all; the law of the sin and of the death — inclinations to sin, passions, the capability to die, and death itself. See at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/16062026.html

Romans VIII, 3 — 'ἐν ᾧ ἠσθένει διὰ τῆς σαρκός' — 'en ho esthenei dia tes sarkos' - in which [it] was weak through the flesh. The law does not have a body (as one might think from some translations); instead, because of the weakness of human nature after the fall, the Law does not save a person. 'ἐν ὁμοιώματι σαρκὸς ἁμαρτίας καὶ περὶ ἁμαρτίας κατέκρινεν τὴν ἁμαρτίαν ἐν τῇ σαρκί' - 'en homoiomati sarkos hamartias kai peri amartias katekrinen ten amartian en te sarki' - in likeness of flesh of sin and concerning sin condemned the sin in the flesh. [He] condemned sin because it is sin, literally a miss, a failing to hit God's target.

Romans VIII, 4 — 'ἐν ἡμῖν' — 'en hemin' - in us. Where.

Romans VIII, 7 — 'ἔχθρα εἰς θεόν' — 'ekhthra eis theon' - enmity into God — that is, the preposition 'eis' indicates the direction of the enmity, namely against Whom — it is not always visible that the fleshly thought, the sinful thought, is enmity into God and His purpose, and often it does not come to the manifestation of this, but its meaning is precisely such — enmity into God, even if not manifest to such a degree as to be clearly discovered. 'οὐχ ὑποτάσσεται' - 'oukh hypotassetai' - [it] is not subjected; [it] does not place itself under the Law of God. Neither indeed can, that is, is not capable, has no power — for by virtue of the previously mentioned laws of sin and death, a person cannot in a state of sin obey the Law of God, this is mutually exclusive.

Romans VIII, 8 — 'οἱ δὲ ἐν σαρκὶ ὄντες' — 'hoi de en sarki ontes' - and the [ones] in flesh being. That is, those who themselves live as if they are only flesh, a purely material being.

Romans VIII, 9 — 'ὑμεῖς δὲ οὐκ ἐστὲ ἐν σαρκὶ ἀλλὰ ἐν πνεύματι' — 'hymeis de ouk este en sarki alla en pneumati' - but you are not in flesh but in Spirit. That is, you live as spiritual beings. 'εἴπερ πνεῦμα θεοῦ οἰκεῖ ἐν ὑμῖν' - 'eiper pneuma theou oikei en hymin' - if indeed [the] Spirit of God dwells in you. That is, the faithful are spiritual people if the Holy Spirit dwells in them. This is an explanation of what it means to be spiritual people.

Romans VIII, 10, 11 — 'ἐν ὑμῖν' — 'en hymin' - in you. Where. See also about perichoresis at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/10042026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/09042026.html

Romans VIII, 11 — 'εἰ δὲ τὸ πνεῦμα τοῦ ἐγείραντος τὸν Ἰησοῦν ἐκ νεκρῶν οἰκεῖ ἐν ὑμῖν' — 'ei de to pneuma tou egeirantos ton Iesoun ek nekron oikei en hymin' - but if the Spirit of the [One] having raised Jesus out of [the] dead dwells in you. That is, it refers at once to the entire Trinity — the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit. 'ὁ ἐγείρας ἐκ νεκρῶν Χριστὸν Ἰησοῦν' - 'ho egeiras ek nekron CHriston Iesoun' - the [One] having raised out of [the] dead Christ Jesus. The preposition 'ek' indicates here from where the transition took place — from the dead to the living. 'ζῳοποιήσει' - 'zoopoiesei' - shall make alive, shall create, like poetry, actively alive. I wrote about the meaning of the word earlier, see at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/03072024.html
Here it is meant that the life which the Spirit of God, the Holy Spirit, grants, will extend also to the body. If a person lives according to the Holy Spirit, then He will make alive even their body.

The Lord Jesus Christ accomplishes that for which the Law makes preparation — the victory over sin.

To defeat sin, one needs to be united with Christ in the Spirit.

In such a case, a person lives by the Spirit, meaning that the Holy Spirit grants them life not in sins and lusts, but in purity and faithfulness.

(Matthew X, 16-22)
Matthew X, 16 — 'ὡς πρόβατα ἐν μέσῳ λύκων' — 'hos probata en meso lykon' - as sheep in [the] midst of wolves. That is, surrounded by wolves. See more at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/18072025.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/25042026.html
Quoting:
The apostles had to expect that someone among their surroundings would be those wolves, meaning that vigilance and wisdom are needed by the apostles always, and especially among non-believers. At the same time, it is important to combine wisdom and innocence — be wise as serpents, and innocent as children (doves).

End of quote.

‘φρονιμοι’ — ‘phronimoi’ — prudent, circumspect, cautious, aware, forewarned.

Matthew X, 17 — 'ἀπὸ τῶν ἀνθρώπων' — 'apo ton anthropon' - away from the men. The use of the preposition 'apo' indicates separateness from people, meaning that the life of the apostles should not be completely transparent and predictable by people. In general, behavior should be open, inspiring trust, yet at the same time it should not be simple-minded, frivolous, thoughtless, inattentive, indifferent, etc. 'εἰς συνέδρια' - 'eis synedria' - into councils; for councils. Where to and for what. 'ἐν таῖς συναγωγαῖς αὐτῶν' - 'en tais synagogais auton' - in the synagogues of them. Where.

Matthew X, 18 — 'εἰς μαρτύριον αὐτοῖς καὶ τοῖς ἔθνεσιν' — 'eis martyrian autois kai tois ethnesin' - unto a testimony to them and to the nations (Gentiles). The preposition 'eis' indicates here the goal that the apostles and the faithful themselves must try to attain in this — to testify to others.

Matthew X, 19 — 'μεριμνήσητε' — 'merimnesete' - do not be anxious, do not be worried, do not trouble yourselves (beforehand). The same as in the Sermon on the Mount, compare:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/08062026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/Beauty.html
'ἐν ἐκείνῃ τῇ ὥρᾳ' — 'en ekeine te hora' — in that hour. That is, precisely at that time. It is also (among other things) about the timely use of time by the faithful.

Matthew X, 20 — a literal translation gives: for it is not you who are the [ones] speaking, but the Spirit of your Father is the [One] speaking in you (as the place of action). The Holy Spirit Himself speaks during the confession and martyrdom of the holy apostles (and, following their model, of many faithful) and therefore there is no need to trouble oneself about that matter beforehand. If a person troubles themselves beforehand, they will be susceptible to purely human anxieties, weaknesses, in particular ignorance, and hence also to sins, whereas if they fulfil this commandment, they will be in trust towards the Holy Spirit and He will give them what is needed for the confession of faith.

Matthew X, 21 — 'εἰς θάνατον' — 'eis thanaton' - into death; unto death. That is, to deliver for what — to destroy, to kill.

Matthew X, 22 — 'ὑπὸ πάντων διὰ τὸ ὄνομά μου' — 'hypo panton dia to onoma mou' - by all through (as a channel or means of action; here properly it is the cause of people's hatred) the name of Me. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates who will perform the action in question. People will hate the faithful, particularly the active ones, because of the name of Christ (here it does not necessarily mean hate, but at least love less or dislike). The name itself will be something that the world will not accept. Perhaps the world will accept the teaching of Jesus, the history, and other things, but not Himself as Christ, as Savior, as Lord and God, etc. The world will try not to enter into a relationship with Christ Himself (although this is impossible, all people are in a relationship with Christ one way or another, it is simply a question for each person — in what kind exactly). The situation is analogous to the conspiracy of the wicked vinedressers in the corresponding parable regarding the son-heir. 'εἰς τέλος' - 'eis telos' - unto [the] end. A set expression.

Luke XXI, 19 — 'ἐν τῇ ὑπομονῇ ὑμῶν κτήσασθε τὰς ψυχὰς ὑμῶν'. Here the aorist imperative middle 2nd person plural is used. To my question: How to understand the aorist imperative middle voice 2nd person plural in Luke 21:19? Gain [possess]? Google Gemini Pro-3.1 replied:

This is an excellent question that touches the very core of understanding the tense-aspect system of the Ancient Greek language. In Luke 21:19, it concerns the word κτήσασθε (ktēsasthe), which comes from the verb κτάομαι (ktaomai).
You have identified its form absolutely accurately. To understand the full depth of this call, let us break down each grammatical component separately, and then bring them together.
1. Middle Voice (Medium)
The verb κτάоμαι (to gain, buy, acquire into ownership) by its nature often acts as a deponent verb, but it retains a clear meaning of the middle voice. In the Ancient Greek language, the middle voice means that the subject performs the action for themselves or with a deep personal involvement in the result.
This is not just the acquisition of some abstract object. This means: acquire into your full ownership, make it your own inalienable possession.
2. Aorist Imperative
This is the most interesting part. In the indicative (the real mood), the aorist usually indicates a past time. But in the imperative mood, the aorist does not have a temporal meaning. It indicates exclusively the aspect of the action.
The present tense of the imperative would call for a continuous process: Continue constantly acquiring.
The aorist imperative views the action as a whole, completed act (punctiliar action). It often has a tone of decisiveness, urgency, or final achievement of the result.
Therefore, the aorist here is a categorical call: Do this decisively and bring the matter to an end!, Secure this as a fact!.
3. Lexical Context: To gain the soul
The phrase κτήσασθε τὰς ψυχὰς ὑμῶν (ktēsasthe tas psychas hymōn) sounds somewhat paradoxical: how can you gain your own soul (or life) if you already possess it?
In the context of the 21st chapter of the Gospel of Luke, Jesus warns of future persecutions, betrayal, and even death (verses 12-16). Yet immediately there sounds a promise of spiritual integrity: But there shall not an hair of your head perish (v. 18). Therefore, the verb κτάομαι here acquires the meaning of to save, to preserve from destruction, to win back.
You gain your soul when, passing through trials, you do not lose faith and thereby acquire yourself for eternity.
Synthesis: How to understand it?
If we combine all these nuances — the finality of the aorist, the for-oneself-action of the middle voice, and the call of the imperative — the literal sense of this verse can be unfolded thus:
Through your endurance, decisively win back and forever preserve for yourselves your souls.
Textual nuance:
In some Ukrainian translations (for example, in Ogienko's), you may see the future tense: By your patience you shall gain your souls. This is due to the fact that in some ancient manuscripts (specifically those underlying the Textus Receptus), instead of the aorist imperative κτήσασθε (ktēsasthe) stands the future indicative κτήσεσθε (ktēsesthe — the difference is only in one letter: α/ε).
However, most modern critical editions of the Greek text prefer precisely the aorist imperative, which you noticed. This variant is significantly stronger: it is not just a statement of a future fact, but a powerful call to a volitional effort.
