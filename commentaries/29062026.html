Liturgy:
(2 Corinthians XI, 21-XII, 9)
2 Corinthians XI, 21 — 'ἐν ᾧ' — 'en ho' - in what; with what. Both variants of translation are possible. 'ἐν ἀφροσύνῃ' - 'en aphrosyne' - in lack of sense; foolishly; madly. How. The apostle does not encourage to speak about oneself and one's achievements. Here he as if enters into a contest who is greater, which also Christ teaches to avoid, but does this for the salvation of the disciples. See more at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/07082025.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/20022023.html

2 Corinthians XI, 23 — 'ἐν κόποις περισσοτέρως' — 'en kopois perissoteros' - in labours more abundantly (that is, in a higher degree — in the passage several times). 'ἐν πληγαῖς περισσοτέрως' - 'en plegais perissoteros' - in stripes above measure [more abundantly, many]. 'ἐν φυλακαῖς ὑπερβαλλόντως' - 'en phylakais hyperballo' - in prisons more exceedingly [beyond measure, even more, many, which is difficult to hold all at once in operational memory, that is, more than 7-9 times]. 'ἐν θανάτοις πολλάκις' - 'en thanatois pollakis' - in deaths many times. The plural indicates onto the circumstances and state of affairs, that is, in circumstances when death is expected.

2 Corinthians XI, 24 — 'ὑπὸ Ἰουδαίων' — 'hypo Ioudaion' - by Jews. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates here onto the passive voice of receiving (as an unjust punishment for the preaching of Christ). 'πεντάκις τεσσεράκοντα παρὰ μίαн' - 'pentakis tesserakonta para mian' - five times forty beside one [forty save one]. That is, the maximal disciplinary punishments by the Jews. The preposition 'para' here is with the accusative and indicates here onto that something is lacking, namely — only one stroke until there should be exactly 40.

2 Corinthians XI, 25 — 'ἐν τῷ βυθῷ' — 'en to bytho' - in the deep sea. That is, in the open sea without a vessel, simply upon the waves in the water.

2 Corinthians XI, 26 — 'ἐκ γένους' — 'ek genous' - out of [my own] race (out of Jews). 'ἐξ ἐθνῶν' - 'ex ethnous' - out of nations (that is, out of Gentiles). That is, away from whom were perils. 'ἐν πόλει' - 'en polei' - in city. Where. 'ἐν ἐρημίᾳ' - 'en eremia' - in wilderness. Where. 'ἐν θαλάσσῃ' - 'en thalasse' - in sea. Where. 'ἐν ψευδαδέλφοις' - 'en pseudadelphois' - in [the midst of] false brethren. In what surroundings, among whom.

2 Corinthians XI, 27 — 'ἐν ἀγρυπνίαις πολλάκις, ἐν λιμῷ καὶ δίψει, ἐν νηστείαις πολλάκις, ἐν ψύχει καὶ γυμνότητι' — 'en agypniais pollakis, en limo kai dipsei, en nesteiais pollakis, en psykhei kai gymnoteti' - in watchings many times [without sleep], in hunger and thirst, in fastings [that is, without food several days and nights] many times [frequently], in cold and nakedness. How, in what, in which situations and states.

2 Corinthians XI, 31 — 'εἰς τοὺς αἰῶνας' — 'eis tous aionas' - into the ages; forever. A set expression.

2 Corinthians XI, 32 — 'ἐν Δαμασκῷ' — 'en Damasko' - in Damascus. Where.

2 Corinthians XI, 33 — 'ἐν σαργάνῃ' — 'en sargane' - in a basket; in a hamper. In what.

2 Corinthians XII, 1 — 'εἰς ὀπτασίας καὶ ἀποκαλύψεις κυρίου' — 'eis optasias kai apokalypseis kyriou' - to visions and revelations of [the] Lord. Here the preposition 'eis' indicates onto movement towards visions and revelations.

2 Corinthians XII, 2, 3 — 'ἐν Χριστῷ' — 'en CHristo' - in Christ. 'ἐν σώματι' - 'en somati' - in body; with body. Rather the first variant of translation, since in the second case the soul and spirit are meant implicitly, for they together with the body were there also in such a case.

2 Corinthians XII, 4 — 'εἰς τὸν παράδεισον' — 'eis ton paradeison' - into the paradise. The preposition 'eis' indicates here precisely where to Paul was caught up (it is logical and generally accepted to think that he writes about his own self).

2 Corinthians XII, 5, 9 — 'ἐν ταῖς ἀσθενείαις' — 'en tais astheneiais' - in the infirmities; with the infirmities. Both variants of translation are possible — the first indicates onto that these circumstances are as if a background for the action of God's grace, which is truly important, and the second — that the infirmities themselves become an occasion for glory and praise if they do not hinder or even help a person to be united with the grace of God, with God.

2 Corinthians XII, 6 — 'εἰς ἐμὲ' — 'eis eme' - into me. Here according into meaning into whom?. The accusative case here emphasizes that Paul wishes that people should evaluate him by his activity and its results (by fruits), and not by the attitude towards the personality of Paul. 'ἐξ ἐμοῦ' - 'ex emou' - out of me. The preposition 'ek' in its form before a subsequent vowel indicates from where a person hears the words of the apostle during communication with him.

2 Corinthians XII, 8 — 'ἀπ’ ἐμοῦ' — 'ap emou' - away from me. The preposition 'apo' in its form before a subsequent vowel indicates onto that the apostle requested that that messenger should completely leave him, altogether.

2 Corinthians XII, 9 — 'ἐν ἀσθενείᾳ' — 'en astheneia' - in infirmity; with infirmity. Rather the first variant of translation, since the second opposes God's power and human infirmity, at the same time uniting them into one whole — this is to a certain degree contradictory.

Concerning the labor of the apostle Paul out of his own lips. He boasts since this is necessary for the salvation of brethren and sisters, and not for his glory or honour from people. Some people recognize only experience, therefore for such Paul speaks about his experience in the Holy Spirit. Out of the XI chapter — about endurance and difficulties, sufferings and labor more fleshly; in the XII chapter — about spiritual labor and the revelations of God.

Also one can notice that Paul does not encourage anyone to boast in a similar manner, but speaks about himself from the third person, from the side; also in no other manner was it possible for those people to bring the Word of God.

Paul says that the power of God in infirmity is brought to completion (here is used a word that originates out of end, goal). That is, the infirmity of a person can serve as a means of overcoming all temptations through the action of God's power at the same time. One can say so, that on the example of Paul in this situation we see that for a person whom God strengthens and fortifies, to whom He gives strength, their own human infirmities can serve then as a means of victory over temptations (and consequently also sins). One can say even otherwise: if God fortifies a person, gives strength, then whether a strong person or a weak person — they can overcome temptations and sins through this Divine power. It is worth recalling that in the Old Testament there is a similar example — the Lord saved Israel away from the Midianites through Gideon and 300 people, by a small number, so that later the Israelites should not boast that they conquered by themselves, and not God conquered for them.

One can about these same words say still thus. God's power does not need something so as to be strong. But when through God's power the infirm becomes stronger, becomes strong, then truly is manifested both God's power as power, and also more clearly is visible human nature. This reminds even more of Jesus Christ, in Whom two natures were united so that their characteristics and properties are not erased, are not leveled, are not reduced, are not diminished in power, manifestation or quantity, but even more are manifested as such — Divine and human. Thus one can see in the sufferings of Christ, thus one can see also in the glory of Christ. The apostle here says about participation in such a manner in Christ, in His life. Since there are many reflections from the topic of the conflict between the apostles Peter and Paul, I must say here the following.

The conflict described in Scripture was not theological. All the apostles at that time had come onto an agreement regarding theology. Peter pleased people.
The greatest zealot of the Old Testament traditions was James, but he personally spoke in favour of a peaceful proposal. Peter followed the tradition until God's opening in questions of ritualism. But in the case of the conflict with Paul, he simply did not operate according into faith and reason, but was guided by pastoral considerations regarding some, forgetting about others.

Peter, judging out of everything, under the influence of Paul's reproach, corrected himself.

The apostles did not force after the Apostolic Council anyone to keep the entire Law of Moses. But besides the apostles in the Church there are different people. Such a same problem was also in the times of the apostles.

Concerning Simon Peter.

Matthew XVI, 14 — according into traditions and prophecies, precisely Elijah and Jeremiah had to come before the Coming of the Messiah-Christ. As Elijah precisely before the First Coming was John the Baptist, and it is a widespread opinion that also Elijah himself, who until now is still alive in heaven, shall return before the Second Coming.

Matthew XVI, 17 — son of Jonah — in other ancient manuscripts — son of John.

It is necessary to note that all the promises of Jesus into Peter relate into that if Peter shall act in Jesus Christ, in the Holy Spirit. This is visible also out of that that shortly Peter received a reproach away from Jesus when he began to think by his purely human imaginations and reasonings, and not by Divine ones.

A similar authority received also the other apostles, with such a same clarification. See also at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/01062026.html
It is necessary to note that in the verse Matthew XVIII, 18 stands the singular regarding heaven, and here the plural. The apostle Peter answered from the name of all the apostles, since Christ asked all the apostles; Simon expressed their common faith, stood out as the one who did this. Factually it concerns that Peter spoke from the name of the New Testament Church, as its voice and testimony, and therefore received an assurance regarding heavens in the plural, for the Church is called to lead people into the heavens, into all the mansions which the Father has prepared. Opened by the Father, experienced by the Church, confessed by Peter. Why does Christ not say that Peter spoke from the name of all the Church directly, but instead speaks about the building by Him of the Church upon the foundation of the rock of Peter? Christ addresses the faithful and expects faith which makes possible the acceptance of all the Divine Revelation, all the Divine will. If the apostles could not manifest such faith, then Christ would continue their preparation into this necessary step. Peter boldly manifested, and therefore received the naming of blessed (happy). I will note also that the Church built by Christ opposes the gates of hades. Hades does not have a Church as a building, it has only gates — there one can enter, but it is impossible to become a part of the edification, only one can begin to belong into it, remain in it. The Church is a living building, since it is built out of people and angels who become participants of the edification, a part of her very self. Peter receives a promise that Christ shall build upon living faith the Church, as upon a rock, for there is no living faith without a faithful person or angel, without the one who confesses faith, who lives by it.
