Liturgy:
(Acts XV, 5-34)
Acts XV, 5 — 'τινες τῶν ἀπὸ τῆς αἱρέσεως τῶν Φαρισαίων πεπιστευκότες' — 'tines ton apo tes aireseos ton Pharisaion pepisteukotes' - some of the party of the Pharisees who had believed. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that these Christians were not typical Pharisees who shared all the Pharisees' views — that is, they were some with their own particular vision, even though they were Christians. One may also understand this same preposition differently here — as pointing to the fact that they had managed to overcome the widespread prejudice against Christ and came out from that Pharisaism, having become Christians, though Pharisaic views continued to exert a certain influence upon them. Further in the text it will become evident (contrary to the widespread opinion which is in fact refuted by this text and others) that none of the apostles had imposed such demands upon the faithful. Acts XV, 7 — 'ἀφ' ἡμερῶν ἀρχαίων' — 'aph hemeron arkhaion' - from ancient days. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following 'he' indicates the starting point of the reckoning. 'ἐν ὑμῖν' - 'en hymin' - among you. Where — among which community.

Acts XV, 12 — 'ἐν τοῖς ἔθνεσιν' — 'en tois ethnesin' - among the nations; among the Gentiles. Where — among whom. In terms of meaning — the second translation is more apt.

Acts XV, 14 — 'ἐπεσκέψατο λαβεῖν ἐξ ἐθνῶν λαὸν τῷ ὀνόματι αὐτοῦ' — 'epeskepsato labein ex ethnon laon to onomati autou' - visited to take from the Gentiles a people for His name; graciously looked upon taking from the Gentiles a people for His name. Both translations are possible and complement one another. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that those former Gentiles are now representatives of their peoples before God and before the Church. For His name indicates here that God now receives the nations similarly to the way he received Israel. God's people properly is the Church, composed of representatives of various nations.

Acts XV, 18 — 'ἀπ' αἰῶνος' — 'ap aionos' - from eternity. Common expression. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following vowel indicates here the starting point of the reckoning.

Acts XV, 19 — 'μὴ παρενοχλεῖν τοῖς ἀπὸ τῶν ἐθνῶν ἐπιστρέφουσιν ἐπὶ τὸν θεόν' — 'me parenokhlein tois apo ton ethnon epistrephousin epi ton theon' - not to trouble those from the Gentiles who are turning (back, so as to see, to look towards, to look in the direction of; a state opposite to 'turning away from God') to God. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that those Gentiles converted to God become former Gentiles — that is, they now entirely cease to be Gentiles, becoming God's people, the Church, the community of the faithful.

Acts XV, 21 — 'ἐκ γενεῶν ἀρχαίων' — 'ek geneon arkhaion' - from ancient generations; from of old; from time immemorial. Common expression indicating here a tradition and continuity of this state from generation to generation regardless of what preceded Moses. 'ἐν ταῖς συναγωγαῖς' - 'en tais synagogais' - in the synagogues. Where.

Acts XV, 22, 30 — 'ἄνδρας ἐξ αὐτῶν' — 'andras ex auton' - men from among them. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that those chosen men became representatives of the Council for those to whom they were sent. 'εἰς Ἀντιόχειαν' - 'eis Antiokheian' - into Antioch. Whither. 'ἐν τοῖς ἀδελφοῖς' - 'en tois adelphois' - among the brothers; with the brothers. In terms of grammatical meaning the first manner of translation conveys it best.

Acts XV, 23 — 'τοῖς ἐξ ἐθνῶν' — 'tois ex ethnon' - those who are from the Gentiles. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that what is spoken of is the origin of those Christians who were originally Gentiles.

Acts XV, 24 — 'τινὲς ἐξ ἡμῶν' — 'tines ex hemon' - some from among us. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following 'he' indicates here that those people were participants of the community of the faithful, as whose representatives the apostles here name themselves. See also the comment above concerning verse Acts XV, 5.

Acts XV, 29 — 'ἐξ ὧν' — 'ex hon' - from which. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following 'ho' indicates here that all those sins must be guarded against and avoided — including for reasons of observance (one of the principal themes of the Apostolic Council in Jerusalem) — there being a parallel between their abandonment and resurrection from the dead.

Acts XV, 31 — literally they rejoiced over (upon) the encouragement. The decision of the Council of apostles was joyfully received by the Church as joy and encouragement of the Holy Spirit.

Acts XV, 33 — 'ἀπελύθησαν μετ' εἰρήνης ἀπὸ τῶν ἀδελφῶν' — 'apelythesan met eirenes apo ton adelphon' - they were dismissed in peace from the brothers. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that this was a bestowal of peace upon one another. See more concerning peace at the link:

Acts XV, 35 — 'ἐν Ἀντιοχείᾳ' — 'en Antiokheia' - in Antioch. Where.

The reading concerns the First Apostolic Council in Jerusalem in 51 AD. The reading is detailed, but the speeches are presented in summary form. The thoughts of representatives of various directions — of James (Jewish-Christian), of Paul and Barnabas (properly Christian), of Peter (those that unite within themselves the former and the latter — although Peter had not studied under them) — converged in the common decision of the Council. I wrote more concerning their understanding of the question in earlier works connected with readings before the beginning of Great Lent, and therefore I shall not dwell on this in detail now.

I shall note that in effect the necessity of the ritual components of the Old Testament for Christians from among the Gentiles was almost entirely lifted, whilst the ethical components and faith were preserved. As Christ says in the Gospels — judgement, mercy, and faith.

Not all the sins from which one must keep away and distance oneself are named, but those that were either very widespread (universal), or those that directly returned one to paganism, or those whose absence from the list of sins would sharply contradict Old Testament piety and spirituality (the prohibition on eating blood). For example, the prohibition on stealing is not in the decree not because it is permitted (no, it is not — this is a sin!), but because even the Gentiles recognised this commandment in its general form (without the details). Therefore one must understand that precisely these rules distinguished the Christians of those times from Gentiles and Jews most of all.

(John X, 17-28)
John X, 18 — 'οὐδεὶς αἴρει αὐτὴν ἀπ' ἐμοῦ, ἀλλ' ἐγὼ τίθημι αὐτὴν ἀπ' ἐμαυτοῦ' — 'oudeis airei auten ap emou, all ego tithemi auten ap emautou' - no one takes (lifts) it from Me, but I lay it down from Myself. What is spoken of is the self-sacrifice and freedom of the Lord Jesus Christ. The meaning of the expression is built upon the contrast of lifting up — laying down. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following vowel indicates first that no one can take the life from Christ, and then that He Himself sacrifices it and also bestows it. See more at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/11052026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/22042026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/10042026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/08052026.html
Here the important thought is that Christ, the Son of God, offers His life as a sacrifice voluntarily, Himself — no one compels Him to do this, no one sacrifices the Son of God (not even the Father), but He Himself acts thus. By this, among other things, the value of life is emphasised. Also this same preposition in the second instance indicates that the Sacrifice of Christ does not disappear but is bestowed. That is, it is similar to a gift of grace — it is bestowed upon the faithful no longer as a sacrifice as such, but as true life and resurrection. This is also important for the salvation of people — a person sinned themselves, no one sinned by means of them, but they themselves sinned. Therefore for their salvation the voluntary Sacrifice of Christ Himself is needed — not with Him. And it is also important that this points to the divine authority of Christ as God — for no one can take life from the Son (even according to His human nature) if He does not sacrifice it Himself.
'ταύτην τὴν ἐντολὴν ἔλαβον παρὰ τοῦ πατρός μου' — 'tauten ten entolen elabon para tou patros mou' — this commandment (I) received from beside My Father. Commandment here means what is thus fitting to do. The preposition 'para' here with the genitive case. This construction is used concerning the procession of the Holy Spirit from the Father and also concerning the human nature of Jesus Christ. See more at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/uk/commentaries/Pryjmennyky.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/02082025.html
Here it may indicate that what is spoken of is the human nature of Jesus Christ — that Jesus as a human being received this in particular through communion with the Father, through prayer. See also at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/09042026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/10022026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/17022026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/Cup.html
But it is more likely to think that in this passage what is spoken of is the Eternal Council of the Most Holy Trinity, the participation of the Holy Spirit in it, and the decision of the Trinity that if people should sin, the Son of God would become incarnate and offer Himself as a Sacrifice for the salvation of people. By the entire logic of it, the Son of God Himself wished to do this — but, not introducing the seeking of primacy within the Trinity, He says that He received it from beside the Father (where the Holy Spirit was also present).

John X, 19 — 'ἐν τοῖς Ἰουδαίοις' — 'en tois Ioudaiois' - among the Jews. Where — among whom.

John X, 20 — 'πολλοὶ ἐξ αὐτῶν' — 'polloi ex auton' - many of them. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that those Jews were generally of a similar opinion to many other Jews concerning Jesus.

John X, 22 — 'ενκανια' — 'enkania' — the feast of Renewal, of Dedication, of new consecration (in honour of the restoration of religious service after the Antiochian (Syrian) subjugation). 'ἐν τοῖς Ἱεροσολύμοις' — 'en tois Hierosolymois' - in Jerusalem. Where.

John X, 23 — 'ἐν τῷ ἱερῷ ἐν τῇ στοᾷ Σολομῶνος' — 'en to hiero en te stoa Solomonos' - in the temple, in the porch of Solomon (the covered courtyard of the temple, whose roof rested on massive columns). Where.

John X, 24 — 'παρρησια' — 'parresia' — plainly, openly, frankly, freely, boldly, with full utterance, confidently.

John X, 25 — 'ἐν τῷ ὀνόματι τοῦ πατρός μου' — 'en to onomati tou patros mou' - in the name of My Father; with the name of My Father. Both translations are possible. The first indicates that Christ performs those works as the Only-begotten Son of the Father, and the second — that He is the Representative of the Father and therefore performs these works as the Father — from Him.

John X, 26 — 'ὅτι οὐκ ἐστὲ ἐκ τῶν προβάτων τῶν ἐμῶν' — 'hoti ouk este ek ton probaton ton emon' - because (you) are not of My sheep. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that those Jews were not disciples of Christ.

John X, 28 — 'εἰς τὸν αἰῶνα' — 'eis ton aiona' - for ever. Common expression. 'ἐκ τῆς χειρός μου' - 'ek tes kheiros mou' - from My hand. Whence. There is no one stronger than Christ.

The answer of Jesus to the Jews refutes their desire to make Him guilty of the disputes among them concerning Him. Instead of seriously seeking to understand, they are governed by stereotypes (He has a demon, He is beside Himself).
