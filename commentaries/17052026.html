Liturgy:
(Acts XVI, 16-34)
Acts XVI, 16 — 'εἰς τὴν προσευχὴν' — 'eis ten preseukhen' - for elevated and inspired prayer; to the place of elevated and inspired prayer. Whither and for what purpose.

Acts XVI, 18 — 'ἐν ὀνόματι Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ' — 'en onomati Iesou CHristou' - in the name of Jesus Christ; by the name of Jesus Christ. Both translations are possible — the first indicates that Paul acts as a faithful follower of Christ, as a Christian and apostle, and the second — that Paul acts as a representative of Christ, in His name and Person. See also more at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/PowerOfHuman.html
'ἀπ' αὐτῆς' — 'ap autes' — from her. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that the apostle Paul commands the unclean spirit to completely leave that woman, to come out entirely, wholly, so as to trouble her no more.

Acts XVI, 19 — 'εἰς τὴν ἀγορὰν' — 'eis ten agoran' - into the agora (the gathering place for judgement, trade, and communication); into the marketplace. Whither.

Acts XVI, 23 — 'εἰς φυλακήν' — 'eis phylaken' - into prison. Whither. This was not part of the apostle Paul's plans — that is, he was placed there by force — but this does not mean that he offered resistance.

Acts XVI, 24 — 'εἰς τὴν ἐσωτέραν φυλακὴν' — 'eis ten esoteran phylaken' - into the inner prison (more securely guarded, in which interrogations and torture were also possible). Whither. 'εἰς τὸ ξύλον' - 'eis to xylon' - into the wood (into the wooden stocks). Whither.

Acts XVI, 32 — 'ἐλάλησαν αὐτῷ τὸν λόγον τοῦ κυρίου σὺν πᾶσιν τοῖς ἐν τῇ οἰκίᾳ αὐτοῦ' — 'elalesan auto ton logon tou kyriou syn pasin tois en te oikia autou' - they spoke to him the Word of the Lord together with all those in his house. That is, to all of them — the word order here is somewhat unusual for this.

Acts XVI, 33 — 'ἐν ἐκείνῃ τῇ ὥρᾳ τῆς νυκτὸς' — 'en ekeine te hora tes nyktos' - at that very hour of the night; at that time in the night. When. 'ἔλουσεν ἀπὸ τῶν πληγῶν' - 'elousen apo ton plegon' - washed from the wounds. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that the guard washed them diligently — so as to heal more quickly and for the wounds' effect to be as minimal as possible.

Acts XVI, 34 — 'εἰς τὸν οἶκον' — 'eis ton oikon' - into the house. Whither. They all believed in God — stated in the same verse — which means that God did this, and they thus believed in God.

It is worth noting that beginning a little earlier in chapter XVI, the book of Acts uses the word we — that is, the apostle and evangelist Luke, my beloved saint, joined Paul and Silas.

The story of the healing from possession of a young prophesying girl, and of the imprisonment of Paul and Silas, and of the miraculous conversion of the prison guard together with all his household. The events in the prison take place at night, quickly — and one may note what a responsible person the prison guard was, but his desire to commit suicide shows that responsibility can lead a person to evil if they do not have a correct attitude, a correct faith.

Concerning the prophesying girl I shall say that the apostles tolerated her prophesying — which testifies to the fact that they were not spiritual conquerors — but where the Lord granted it, they freed people from the power of evil. Which is indeed what happened in the events described in this reading.

(John IX, 1-38)
John IX, 1 — 'ἄνθρωπον τυφλὸν ἐκ γενετῆς' — 'anthropon typhlon ek genetes' - a man blind from birth. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that this was a congenital condition — he had been blind from the time of the development of sight in his mother's womb, that is, for as long as one can speak of a person's sight at all. Of course, at that time there were no such means of intrauterine diagnosis, but this detail is recorded through divine inspiration, since all things are known to God.

John IX, 3 — 'ἵνα φανερωθῇ τὰ ἔργα τοῦ θεοῦ ἐν αὐτῷ' — 'hina phanerothe ta erga tou theou en auto' - so that the works of God might be manifested (might become manifest, visible) in him (with him). That is, God permitted that illness from the womb in order later to grant a miracle and glorify Christ. The works of God — that is, properly speaking those of the Father — but it is Jesus Christ Who performs them, as in other places of the Gospel. The Father thus glorifies the Son, and the Son — the Father.

John IX, 5 — 'ἐν τῷ κόσμῳ' — 'en to kosmo' - in the world. Where.

John IX, 6 — 'πηλὸν ἐκ τοῦ πτύσματος' — 'pelon ek tou ptysmatos' - clay from saliva. The preposition 'ek' here is somewhat unusual — if what is spoken of is the material from which something is made, the typical usage is 'apo' instead; but in that case it would mean that the product differs substantially from the material (for example, a wooden article differs substantially from the wood from which it is made); whereas the preposition 'ek' indicates that this clay does not differ in any way in meaning and action from the pure saliva of Christ itself — that is, this miraculous clay did not acquire the properties of earth and mud, but has the properties of saliva; and that saliva is here healing — not of itself, but through the action of Christ Himself, as also of the Father and the Holy Spirit, the Trinity. See also:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/22042026.html

John IX, 7 — 'εἰς τὴν κολυμβήθραν τοῦ Σιλωάμ' — 'eis ten kolymbethran tou Siloam' - into the pool of Siloam. That is, it indicates where to go, not where to wash himself. Here the important point is that the water of Siloam had no effect on the miracle — only the saliva of Christ and His divine will. That is, go to the pool of Siloam and wash. From this it is evident that the washing was needed exclusively (only for the purpose) of washing away the dirt from the clay, which in no way contributed to the healing but was needed as an image of God's action in the world. John IX, 11 — 'ὕπαγε εἰς τὸν Σιλωὰμ καὶ νίψαι' — 'hypage eis ton Siloam kai nipsai' - go to Siloam and wash. Whither. See the explanation on verses 6 and 7 above.

John IX, 14 — 'ἐν ᾗ ἡμέρᾳ' — 'en he hemera' - on the day when. When. The healing was on the Sabbath — the day of the Lord for the Jews, the day of expectation of the coming of the Messiah, Christ.

John IX, 16 — 'ἐκ τῶν Φαρισαίων τινές' — 'ek ton Pharisaion tines' - some of the Pharisees. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that they were representatives of the Pharisees, fully sharing their views. 'οὐκ ἔστιν οὗτος παρὰ θεοῦ ὁ ἄνθρωπος' - 'ouk estin houtos para theou ho anthropos' - this man is not from beside God. The preposition 'para' here with the genitive case. This construction is used concerning the procession of the Holy Spirit from the Father and also concerning the human nature of Jesus Christ. See more at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/uk/commentaries/Pryjmennyky.html

Here this indicates that the Pharisees deny the evident action of the Holy Spirit in Christ, and deny Christ Himself as the awaited Messiah contrary to all available testimonies and confirmations — and do this without realising it, without understanding.

John IX, 16 — 'σχίσμα ἦν ἐν αὐτοῖς' — 'skhisma en en autois' - there was a division among them. That is, they were divided.

John IX, 24 — 'ἐκ δευτέρου' — 'ek deuterou' - a second time. Common expression.

John IX, 30 — 'ἐν τούτῳ γὰρ τὸ θαυμαστόν ἐστιν' — 'en touto gar to thaumaston estin' - for in this is the wonder; this is the wonder. In terms of meaning — the second variant.

John IX, 32 — 'ἐκ τοῦ αἰῶνος' — 'ek tou aionos' - from of old; since the world began. That is, never at any time had such a thing happened. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that this is a unique case — nothing like it had ever happened before.

John IX, 33 — 'εἰ μὴ ἦν οὗτος παρὰ θεοῦ, οὐκ ἠδύνατο ποιεῖν οὐδέν' — 'ei me en houtous para theou, ouk edynato poiein ouden' - if this One were not from beside God, He could do nothing. That is, He could not have performed such a miracle. The man born blind who was healed refutes the position of the Pharisees in verse 16 above. The preposition 'para' here with the genitive case. This construction is used concerning the procession of the Holy Spirit from the Father and also concerning the human nature of Jesus Christ. See more at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/uk/commentaries/Pryjmennyky.html

Here this man affirms under the action of the Holy Spirit that Christ is sent by the Father.

John IX, 34 — 'ἐν ἁμαρτίαις' — 'en hamartiais' - in sins. The Pharisees held that in matters of faith one may only trust the testimony of righteous people, whilst they regarded illness as evidence of sinfulness — and therefore concluded that they would not listen to this healed man's testimony about spiritual matters. They were mistaken in both opinions.

John IX, 35 — 'σὺ πιστεύεις εἰς τὸν υἱὸν τοῦ ἀνθρώπου;' — 'sy pisteueis eis ton hyion tou anthropou?' - do you believe in(to) the Son of Man? The preposition 'eis' indicates here that true faith leads to Christ, the Son of Man — that He is the goal and final point of faith.

John IX, 36 — 'ἵνα πιστεύσω εἰς αὐτόν;' — 'hina pisteuso eis auton?' - that (I) may believe in(to) Him? See the explanation above concerning verse 35.

The story of the healing of the man born blind. It has many important moments. In general one may notice a kind of movement — a spreading of the Gospel from the closest disciples of Jesus (closer to the feast of Pascha) and extending to people who come to know Jesus not immediately but gradually — among whom is the healed man born blind. The words of Christ about the glory of God concerning the blind man may also be understood as meaning that his illness too became for him an invitation to participate in the life of Christ — including in His sufferings. See more at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/renewal-of-Peter.html

Here one must also pay attention to verse 37. Christ says: 'καὶ ἑώρακας αὐτόν' — and (you) have seen Him. Here a different word is used than in verses 39-41 below. Taking into account the order and the events themselves, one may think that here had seen Him means that the blind man, while still blind, had seen Christ, had experienced, had encountered Christ in one way or another. He did not know who had addressed him, who had anointed his eyes — but he went to Siloam and washed. Here we see an example of how God implants and gives feelings and sensations beyond the organs of the senses. That is, the first thing that the man born blind saw was… Christ — Whom he did not see visually. Seeing nothing, the man born blind went to wash at the word of Christ, washed, and then began to see. But at the very beginning he had seen Christ. And then he saw with his eyes now opened. I am reminded of the love story of Tobit, described in the non-canonical book of the same name of the Old Testament. There Tobias, having listened to the archangel Raphael (not knowing who he was), loved Sara — whom he had not seen and did not know — but loved at the words of Raphael. God's word through Raphael bestowed upon Tobias love for his future wife (Tobit VI, 10-(17)18). Thus we see a foreshadowing of this healing by Christ, the Word of God. In this context the healing in the Gospel of John is indeed revealed not simply as a medical miracle but as an illustration of how God acts with a person. First the person 'blindly' receives the Word and follows it — and it is precisely this trust in the invisible that becomes the power which opens one's eyes to the visible. A beautiful paradox is constructed: the Pharisees, who possess βλέπω (physical sight), remain blind to the truth; the man born blind, lacking βλέπω, gains ὁράω (true spiritual contemplation) through obedience to the Word.

The dialogues with the Jews show their rigidity in their unbelief, the fearfulness of the parents — but also the open movement towards the truth of the healed man born blind. The readings examined at the link:
