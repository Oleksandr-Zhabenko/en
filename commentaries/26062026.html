Liturgy:
(Romans XI, 25-36)
Romans XI, 25 — 'παρ’ ἑαυτοῖς' — 'par heautois' - in the vicinity of yourselves (many other manuscripts contain instead 'en heautois' — in your own selves, that is here internally). The preposition 'para' in its form before the subsequent 'he' here is with the dative case and indicates onto a comparison with reality. The apostle Paul means that when the faithful try to form a self-assessment, to evaluate their own selves, then it shall be incorrect to consider themselves wise in the vicinity of themselves — that is, in comparison with themselves. The apostle does not wish that the thought about that God allowed sins to people, and then manifested mercy to all, should prompt anyone to consider themselves wiser, to be puffed up, to boast, to exalt themselves, etc. The apostle opens the mind for vision, but cautions away from self-absorption, particularly by the depth of thought. I say to you, that you may know, but do not be proud, do not be absorbed by your knowledge. One can think that Christ also can thus command to relate towards special, wise knowledge. 'ἀπὸ μέρους' - 'apo merous' - in part. A set expression.

Romans XI, 26 — 'ἐκ Σιὼν' — 'ek Sion' - out of Zion; away from Zion. Where from. The preposition 'ek' indicates here from where the Coming of the awaited Messiah, that is, Christ, must take place. Also Zion, besides the geographical meaning, has also a symbolic one — as a stronghold of resistance to sin, as a sign of human dignity. 'ἀπὸ Ἰακώβ' - 'apo Iakob' - away from Jacob. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that it concerns a complete deliverance away from ungodliness, iniquity, sin.

Romans XI, 27 — 'αὕτη αὐτοῖς ἡ παρ’ ἐμοῦ διαθήκη' — 'aute autois he par emou diatheke' - this is the covenant to them from beside Me. The preposition 'para' in its form before a subsequent vowel here is with the genitive case. The same construction is used regarding the procession of the Holy Spirit from the Father, regarding the Most Holy Trinity, regarding the entire Divine design and regarding the human nature of Jesus Christ. Romans XI, 32 — 'εἰς ἀπείθειαν' — 'eis apeitheian' - into unbelief; into disobedience. Where to. Again the apostle Paul, following after the Old Testament understanding, does not distinguish God's will and God's permission — God in reality allowed disobedience, unbelief to people, but where there is no complete faithfulness and responsibility of people, He manifests mercy into all. Romans XI, 36 — 'ἐξ αὐτοῦ καὶ δι’ αὐτοῦ καὶ εἰς αὐτὸν τὰ πάντα' — 'ex auton kai di autou kai eis ta panta' - literally: out of Him (as Source, Beginning), through Him (as by Power) and into Him (as the point of destination of movement, as final Goal) are all things. On all stages of development and perfection, God leads people to the good, to bliss, to Himself. 'εἰς τοὺς αἰῶνας· ἀμήν' - 'eis tous aionas amen' - into the ages. Amen. A set expression.

In general, this is a summary of several readings regarding the destiny of Israel and of Christians out of former Gentiles.

Paul balances the vision of both the ones and the others regarding their own selves and mutually.

Further I will add that God's gifts and calling — concerns supernatural gifts and Revelation, and not natural gifts. Natural gifts people and nations can receive, can also subsequently lose, they can be in a certain period of time exhausted or replaced onto new ones, but supernatural gifts — about them Paul says that they are irrevocable, that is, God, granting them, grants not for that to take them away later. Yes, people can reject these gifts, but the Lord Himself will not take them away.

As was spoken earlier, the Old Testament people did not distinguish God's will and God's permission, Paul, quoting them, also does not distinguish. Therefore that which God allowed, he describes in terms of shut up bound.

Further, finally, he shows that it is necessary to distinguish, — by words about the depths of God's understanding and wisdom, but setting the questions, he does not go further. Most likely of all, he wishes by this to engage the listeners themselves into active reflections and that they by the action of the Holy Spirit should come into distinction gradually, into the measure of growth in Christ.

(Matthew XII, 1-8)
Matthew XII, 1 — 'Ἐν ἐκείνῳ τῷ καιρῷ' — 'en ekeino to kairo' - in that season; at that time. When. A set expression.

Matthew XII, 2 — 'ἐν σαββάτῳ' — 'en sabbato' - in a sabbath. When.

Matthew XII, 4 — 'εἰς τὸν οἶκον τοῦ θεοῦ' — 'eis ton oikon tou theou' - into the house of God. Where to.

Matthew XII, 5 — 'ἐν τῷ νόμῳ' — 'en to nomo' - in the Law (of Moses, Divine). Where. 'ἐν τῷ ἱερῷ' - 'en to hiero' - in the temple. Where.

The key of understanding in this passage — Matthew XII, 7 — a quote out of the prophet Hosea VI, 6: For mercy I desire, and not sacrifice, and knowledge of God more than whole burnt offerings. Actually these words show what the Lord wishes that we should perform on a sabbath (Sunday, the Lord's Day, on feasts, if more generally).

Mercy and the knowledge of God.

The priests are blameless, for they perform service, and this is both the one and the other; David was not guilty, for by this was opened God's good pleasure into him; the apostles are not guilty, for they testify by that that they are with the Lord Jesus Christ. One can continue this list also further, in particular regarding the Jews themselves and their gatherings in synagogues on sabbaths.

Also these words have also a more general sense — for the good, time is always good. That is, for true good, time is not an obstacle.
