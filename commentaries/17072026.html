Liturgy:
(1 Corinthians VII, 35-VIII, 7)
1 Corinthians VII, 36 — 'υπεракμος' — 'hyperakmos' - 'hyper' + 'akme' — past the bloom [flowering] of youth, of mature age.

1 Corinthians VII, 37 — 'ἐν τῇ καρδίᾳ' — 'en te kardia' - in the heart. Where. 'ἐν τῇ ἰδίᾳ καρδίᾳ' - 'en te idia kardia' - in one's own heart. The verse speaks about a virgin and about a father who has a virgin daughter.

1 Corinthians VII, 39 — 'μόνον ἐν κυρίῳ' — 'monon en kyrio' - only in the Lord. How.

1 Corinthians VIII, 3 — 'ὑπ’ αὐτοῦ' — 'hyp autou' - by Him. The preposition 'hyp' (a shortened form of 'hypo' before a subsequent vowel) here is with the genitive case and indicates the passive voice of the participle known, it also emphasizes God's condescension to a person in love.

1 Corinthians VIII, 4 — 'ἐν κόσмῳ' — 'en kosmo' - in the world. Where.

1 Corinthians VIII, 5 — 'ἐν οὐρανῷ' — 'en ourano' - in heaven; on heaven. Where.

1 Corinthians VIII, 6 — 'ἐξ οὗ' — 'ex hou' - from Whom. 'ἡμεῖς εἰς αὐτόν' - 'hemeis eis auton' - we for Him. It concerns the purpose of the faithful to be children of God, about God's design regarding the participation of creation in the blessed eternal life in love. The words about the Holy Spirit are absent in some ancient manuscripts of the Epistle. The apostles did not always think of and name the Entire Trinity together when it concerns not Itself, but creation. That is, where it concerns God properly, there the entire Trinity is seen manifestly, It is named fully accordingly, because God is God. But where it concerns creation, there the apostles often, so as not to complicate understanding, in many cases do not mention the entire Trinity. Nevertheless, if it is a matter of God, then it is an apostolic tradition to mention and revere the Entire Trinity.

1 Corinthians VIII, 7 — 'ἐν πᾶσιν' — 'en pasin' - in all. In whom.

Two parts, the first of which is about the marriage of virgins, and about widows (generally about the state of those who are currently unmarried), and the second is about idol worship and the consumption of food offered to idols.

Regarding the first part, it is generally explained at the links below. Specifically, Paul puts pure virginity above marriage without explanations. Considering the explanations at the links below, this is his experience. But for other people, it may be otherwise. If one looks not at the experience of the apostle Paul, then virginity has only one truly essential advantage over marriage — virginity is more like the state of the future age, after the resurrection. Christ Himself says that after the Resurrection they neither marry nor are given in marriage, but are like angels in heaven. But this does not mean that marriage is somehow truly inferior to virginity, but this is because in the blessed resurrection there is no need for marriage as such (this is one of the mysteries of the future age). This is also clearly seen from the fact that the Mother of God was at the same time a Virgin and Married (as the hymns call Her, Bride Unwedded, meaning a Bride Who is not such as brides), and there was no need for Christ to marry, although the Church is mysteriously called His Bride, and He — the Bridegroom. Nevertheless, the saving and sanctifying actions of God, particularly in the Sacrament of Marriage, as well as the love of saved married couples, are preserved in a special way in eternity, for love never ends.

It is also necessary to mention the passage Matthew XIX, 1-12. There will be a separate reading about it, then, God willing, I will write in more detail. But I will dwell on the word receive [room/capacity for] and its forms and derivatives, which are used several times in this place. In the original Greek, the word literally means to prepare a room, that is, to give place, to accept. If we remember how the heart is spoken of in the context of faithfulness, also as a certain space, and also that there is virginity for the sake of the Kingdom of God, then it becomes clear that here Christ speaks in such a form about the degree of a person's dedication to the Kingdom of God. If compared with human relationships, it is natural to desire to be closer to the beloved person. So also here — if a person loves God, they want to draw closer to His Kingdom, and therefore, to give preference and significance also to what is connected with it — in particular, since virginity is more like the state of the future age, such a person wants to be unmarried in purity more than to marry.

I will also note that a widow or a widower can marry a second time — only in the Lord. But it is better to remain without a second marriage (I think, in view of the mystery of the heart and all the above).

(Matthew XV, 29-31)
Matthew XV, 29 — 'παρὰ τὴν θάλασσαν' — 'para ten thalassan' - to the proximity with the sea [by the sea]. The preposition 'para' here is with the accusative, indicating an approach to the sea shore. 'εἰς τὸ ὄρος' - 'eis to oros' - into the mountain [up on the mountain]. Where to.

Matthew XV, 31 — 'παρὰ τοὺς πόδας αὐτοῦ' — 'para tous podas autou' - to the proximity with His feet [at His feet]. The preposition 'para' here is with the accusative and indicates here that people came to Jesus and fell down at His feet, begging for healing.

A short reading, but if it is omitted, then a weighty component of Jesus' activity can be omitted.

In another place, Christ will mention these and similar healings as a sign of His messiahship, particularly before the disciples of John the Baptist, who precisely in God's action and power sought the Messiah.
