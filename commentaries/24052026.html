Matins:
(John XXI, 1-14)
John XXI, 2 — 'Ναθαναὴλ ὁ ἀπὸ Κανᾶ τῆς Γαλιλαίας' — 'Nathanael ho apo Kana tes Galilaias' - Nathanael, who is from Cana of Galilee. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that Nathanael left Cana of Galilee in order to be a disciple of Jesus, an apostle. 'ἄλλοι ἐκ τῶν μαθητῶν αὐτοῦ δύο' - 'alloi ek ton matheton autou duo' - two others of His (Christ's) disciples. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that those disciples were representatives of the disciples of Christ — of the apostles — among whom the named ones are also included. It is entirely possible that these were some two of the eleven apostles, but they may also be disciples from the seventy (seventy-two) apostles.

John XXI, 3 — 'εἰς τὸ πλοῖον' — 'eis to ploion' - into the boat; to the boat. Whither. 'ἐν ἐκείνῃ τῇ νυκτὶ' - 'en ekeine te nykti' - that night; during that night. When.

John XXI, 6 — 'εἰς τὰ δεξιὰ' — 'eis ta dexia' - to the right side; on the right. Whither. 'ἀπὸ τοῦ πλήθους τῶν ἰχθύων' - 'apo tou plethous ton ikhthyon' - from the fullness (great number, abundance) of the fish. The preposition 'apo' indicates here not the true cause of why they could not haul it in, but an accompanying cause that is not essential. We see such usage in other places as well. Further on, verse 11 states that although it was so large, they did haul it in (thus they were indeed able to), and also that the nets did not tear. Verse 11 thus refutes the idea that they truly could not haul it in (that is, lacked the strength, were unable). Also important is the fact that from the standpoint of faith, Christ performed the miracle maintaining measure — as God He knew what maximum capacity the apostles' nets could catch, and therefore gave them precisely that maximum quantity which required their effort. All of this indicates that the preposition points to the true cause of what was happening — the apostles had to overcome human weakness in order to haul in the nets with the fish, whilst at the same time having the strength to do so. This resonates with the forgiveness and calling of Peter, and also with the story of John the Theologian in the same place — later at the end of the chapter — which shows that all things are possible through grace. Also, since it was Peter who hauled the net to shore, he once again deeply realised his weakness as a human being — the lack of awareness of which had served as one of the substantial causes of his threefold denial.

John XXI, 7 — 'ἔβαλεν ἑαυτὸν εἰς τὴν θάλασσαν' — 'ebalen heauton eis ten thalassan' - he threw himself into the sea; he cast himself into the sea. Whither. The Greek text has a parallel with the prophet Jonah, who said to throw him into the sea in order to save the ship from the storm.

John XXI, 8 — 'οὐ γὰρ ἦσαν μακρὰν ἀπὸ τῆς γῆς ἀλλὰ ὡς ἀπὸ πηχῶν διακοσίων' — 'ou gar esan makran apo tes ges alla hos apo pekhon diakosion' - for they were not far from land, but about two hundred cubits. The preposition 'apo' forms part of a common expression concerning distance.

John XXI, 9, 11 — 'εἰς τὴν γῆν' — 'eis ten gen' - to the ground; onto the land. Whither. In terms of meaning — the second translation.

John XXI, 10 — 'ἐνέγκατε ἀπὸ τῶν ὀψαρίων' — 'enenkate apo ton opsarion' - bring (some) of the fish. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that a portion of the caught fish was to be separated for the meal with Christ.

John XXI, 14 — 'ἐγερθεὶς ἐκ νεκρῶν' — 'egertheis ek nekron' - having been raised from the dead. The preposition 'ek' indicates here whence the transition took place — from the dead to the living. The passive voice concerning the Resurrection of Christ is found in various other places of the New Testament, as is the active. In the case of the passive voice, what is spoken of is the Resurrection by the Father; and in the active — Christ Himself rose by the power of His own Divinity. In general such usage testifies to the fact that Christ rose and this was the work of the entire Most Holy Trinity.

Liturgy:
(Acts XX, 16-18, 28-36)
Acts XX, 16 — 'ἐν τῇ Ἀσίᾳ' — 'en te Asia' - in Asia. Where. 'γενέσθαι εἰς Ἱερουσαλήμ' - 'genesthai eis Hierousalem' - to arrive in(to) Jerusalem. Whither. In terms of meaning this is the appropriate translation here.

Acts XX, 17 — 'Ἀπὸ δὲ τῆς Μιλήτου πέμψας εἰς Ἔφεσον' — 'apo de tes Miletou pempsas eis Epheson' - but from Miletus he sent to Ephesus. Whence and whither — with movement, and the messengers completely left Miletus.

Acts XX, 18 — 'ἀπὸ πρώτης ἡμέρας ἀφ' ἧς ἐπέβην εἰς τὴν Ἀσίαν' — 'apo protes hemeras aph hes epeben eis ten Asian' - from the first day from the time I came into Asia. The preposition 'apo' twice in different forms indicates here the starting point from which important changes, events, and so on took place, whilst the preposition 'eis' indicates the direction of movement and the destination — whither.

Acts XX, 28 — 'ἐν ᾧ' — 'en ho' - in which. Where.

Acts XX, 29 — 'εἰς ὑμᾶς' — 'eis hymas' - among you; towards you. Whither.

Acts XX, 30 — 'ἐξ ὑμῶν αὐτῶν ἀναστήσονται ἄνδρες' — 'ex hymon auton anastesontai andres' - from among yourselves there will arise men. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following 'hy' indicates here that those men will be representatives of the communities — including clergy or presiders — who in general will be similar to the surrounding faithful (and therefore it will not be easy to distinguish them from the good and faithful).

Acts XX, 32 — 'δοῦναι τὴν κληρονομίαν ἐν τοῖς ἡγιασμένοις πᾶσιν' — 'dounai ten kleronomian en tois hegiasmenois pasin' - to give the inheritance among all the sanctified. Where. That is, participation in the community of the sanctified — indeed of the saints.

Acts XX, 28 — 'επισκοπους' — 'episkopous' — those who look over (the literal translation), overseers; bishops. In this reading this most likely refers to the first meaning rather than the second. That is, senior pastors for their communities (not necessarily bishops in the proper sense).

Acts XX, 30 — 'διεστραμμενα' — 'diestrammena' — twisted, distorted — from 'διαστρεφω' — 'diastrepho' — to twist, to damage, to distort, to direct towards decay and dissolution.

Acts XX, 36 — 'προσηύξατο' — 'proseuksato' - prayed in elevated and inspired prayer — from 'προσεύχομαι' - 'proseukhomai' — to pray in elevated and inspired prayer. Kneeling as a sign of humility and humility itself do not hinder praying in elevated, inspired, spiritual, or even sublime prayer. What matters is only that the elevation in prayer be from God. Paul is truly speaking of sublime things — in effect he prophesies — and therefore cannot but pray in elevated and inspired prayer at the same time. But if a person prophesies and prays, this does not mean that they discard the bounds of themselves and their nature. God acts therein, but the person also acts humbly and truthfully, so as not to fall into pride.

The apostle continues with his preaching and gives pastoral instructions. The reading is directly connected with today's commemoration of the holy fathers of the First Ecumenical Council at Nicaea in 325 AD. That is, by wolves and false teachers one may understand in particular the heresiarchs — and specifically Arius. Paul also speaks of his own particular example of zeal in gaining and bringing more people to salvation (in particular the fact that he, although he could have devoted himself entirely to preaching and ministry, also worked with his own hands so as to burden no one). I shall also note that the pastoral words do not make Paul a commander and the overseers and elders subordinates — for they pray together on their knees.

(John XVII, 1-13)
John XVII, 1 — 'εἰς τὸν οὐρανὸν' — 'eis ton ouranon' - into the heaven; to the heaven. That is, whither.

John XVII, 5 — 'παρὰ σεαυτῷ' — 'para seauto' - in the vicinity of Yourself. 'παρὰ σοί' - 'para soi' - in the vicinity of You. The preposition 'para' here in both cases with the dative case indicates the closeness of the Son of God to the Father as a consequence of Their consubstantiality, unity, and love.

John XVII, 6 — 'ἐκ τοῦ κόσμου' — 'ek tou kosmou' - from the world. The preposition 'ek' indicates here whence those disciples come — they are part of the world, of creation.

John XVII, 7 — 'παρὰ σοῦ εἰσίν' — 'para sou eisin' - they are from beside You. The preposition 'para' here with the genitive case. This construction is used concerning the procession of the Holy Spirit from the Father, concerning the Holy Trinity, concerning all God's design, and concerning the human nature of Jesus Christ. See more at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/uk/commentaries/Pryjmennyky.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/02082025.html
Here, similarly to what is written concerning an analogous passage at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/15052026.html
what is spoken of is not so much the human nature of Christ as the Most Holy Trinity — the Father and the Son are mentioned directly, whilst the Holy Spirit is present alongside through the preposition 'para' with the genitive case.

John XVII, 8 — 'παρὰ σοῦ ἐξῆλθον' — 'para sou exelthon' - I came out from beside You. Analogous to verse 7, but here also the Eternal Council of the Most Holy Trinity is spoken of, and the participation of the Holy Spirit. Again, as similarly stated at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/15052026.html
the active actions of the Son of God are emphasised, as well as the participation of the entire Trinity — in particular the Holy Spirit.

John XVII, 10 — 'ἐν αὐτοῖς' — 'en autois' - in them.

John XVII, 11-13 — 'ἐν τῷ κόσμῳ' — 'en to kosmo' - in the world. Christ carries out his sacrificial ministry with complete self-giving, whilst the disciples remain to live in the world. 'ἐν τῷ ὀνόματί σου' - 'en to onomati sou' - in Your name; with Your name. Here what is spoken of is the unity of the Father and the Son — in particular the ministry of the Son as sent by the Father and received by the disciples in particular for this reason. The apostles received the Son (which kept them faithful to God) — in particular also because they honour the Father Who sent Him.

John XVII, 12 — 'οὐδεὶς ἐξ αὐτῶν' — 'oudeis ex auton' - none of them. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following vowel indicates that what is spoken of is representatives of the disciples — or more simply here — the disciples themselves.

John XVII, 13 — 'ἐν ἑαυτοῖς' — 'en heautois' - in themselves.

Half of the chapter containing the so-called High-Priestly Prayer of Jesus Christ (as High Priest after the order of Melchizedek — as one may say drawing on the Epistle to the Hebrews). This is again closely connected with the commemoration of the holy fathers.

John XVII, 8 — 'ρεματα' — 'rhemata' — rhemata; that which is actually communicated concerning the subjects of conversation — not so much the very words as the content of what is said.

The Father brings people to the Son, and the Son to the Father. I wrote about this earlier — see at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/04052023.html

Concerning the traitor Judas — the Lord did not wish him to perish, but knew, as God, that this would come to pass. And Scripture contains prophecies concerning this.

In general one may see a distinctive quality of the relations within the Trinity — a mutual accountability in love. Yes, God is a model of accountability — of the Father before the Son and the Spirit, of the Son before the Father and the Spirit, of the Spirit before the Father and the Son. This is the accountability of mutual love, not of fear or obligation.
