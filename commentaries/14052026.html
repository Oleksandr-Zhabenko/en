Christians of the Western Rite celebrate today the Ascension of the Lord, and I congratulate them with the celebration. The Lord, ascending bodily and in soul (in His human nature) to heaven — to where He was already according to His divinity — thereby exalts and glorifies that nature. It is precisely this glorification that is needed, according to His words, in order to send the Holy Spirit, Who will also glorify the Son. Also closely connected with the Ascension are the words concerning the Second Coming in the Creed: He shall come again, with glory, to judge the quick and the dead; whose Kingdom shall have no end.

For the feast of the Ascension the greeting used is generally the same as throughout most of the year — in Ukraine this is Glory to Jesus Christ! Glory for ever!

Liturgy:
(Acts XIV, 20-27)
Acts XIV, 20 — 'εἰς τὴν πόλιν' — 'eis ten polin' - into the city (Lystra). Whither. 'εἰς Δέρβην' - 'eis Derben' - into Derbe. Whither.

Acts XIV, 21 — 'εἰς τὴν Λύστραν καὶ εἰς Ἰκόνιον καὶ εἰς Ἀντιόχειαν' — 'eis ten Lystran kai eis Ikonian kai eis Antiokheian' - into Lystra, and into Iconium, and into Antioch. Whither.

Acts XIV, 22 — 'εἰς τὴν βασιλείαν τοῦ θεοῦ' — 'eis ten basileian tou theou' - into the Kingdom of God. Whither. To enter the Kingdom of God can often mean in the New Testament even now — in this life — to live in a manner befitting the Kingdom of God, according to God's will.

Acts XIV, 23 — 'παρέθεντο αὐτοὺς τῷ κυρίῳ εἰς ὃν πεπιστεύκεισαν' — 'parethento autous to kyrio eis hon pepisteukeisan' - they committed them to the Lord, in Whom they had believed. The preposition 'eis' indicates here that true faith leads to Christ — He is its goal and final point.

Acts XIV, 24 — 'εἰς τὴν Παμφυλίαν' — 'eis ten Pamphylian' - into Pamphylia. Whither.

Acts XIV, 25 — 'εἰς τὴν Πέργην' — 'eis ten Pergen' - for Perga. The preposition 'eis' indicates here towards whom the words of the preaching were directed — to the inhabitants of which city. 'εἰς Ἀτταλίαν' - 'eis Attalian' - into Attalia. Whither.

Acts XIV, 26 — 'εἰς Ἀντιόχειαν' — 'eis Antiokheian' - into Antioch. Whither. 'εἰς τὸ ἔργον ὃ ἐπλήρωσαν' - 'eis to ergon ho eplerosan' - for the work which they had accomplished (completed, fully carried out). The preposition 'eis' indicates here the purpose.

The passage is omitted where Paul is stoned by enraged Jews for the faith of Christ. Instead, we read immediately of the miraculous recovery of Paul when the disciples gathered around him. Of all things — for every true teacher, the support of disciples is capable of working wonders — more often in the figurative (but real) sense, and here — manifestly — the Lord shows His mercy, so as to teach everyone to support teachers (and more broadly — encourages mutual support in general).

The words about many tribulations are encouragement on the way to the Kingdom of God. One may compare this to a sportsman whom a coach encourages with the words: You will win in a difficult and important competition if you put in great effort. These are not words meant to discourage the sportsman in preparation, but it is to be expected that on the contrary they can inspire to greater diligence and improve motivation. The apostle says that the faithful will enter the Kingdom of God by putting forth effort (compare Matthew XI, 12). Also here the question arises whether these words concern all the faithful, or only that particular situation. According to the general view, it is impossible to receive crowns without performing a feat and without exerting effort. But the richness of God's promises is so great that all the faithful should enter the Kingdom of God. The question ultimately is in what people themselves do. These words are not a predetermination of many tribulations for all, but an encouragement to go to the Lord regardless of tribulations, should such exist or arise.

(John IX, 39–X, 9)
John IX, 39 — 'εἰς κρίμα' — 'eis krima' - for judgement. The preposition 'eis' indicates here the purpose of the action. 'εἰς τὸν κόσμον' - 'eis ton kosmon' - into the world. Whither.

John IX, 40 — 'ἐκ τῶν Φαρισαίων οἱ μετ' αὐτοῦ ὄντες' — 'ek ton Pharisaion hoi met autou ontes' - of the Pharisees those who were with Him. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that they were representatives of the group of Pharisees.

John X, 1 — 'εἰς τὴν αὐλὴν τῶν προβάτων' — 'eis ten aulen ton probaton' - into the sheepfold; into the sheep pen. Whither.

John X, 5 — 'φεύξονται ἀπ' αὐτοῦ' — 'pheuxontai ap autou' - they will flee from him; they will run (away) from him. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that they will flee and run away entirely, so as not to be together with him.

Those without sight (the blind) here are those who have no possibility of seeing. This is a state not connected with a person's will, desires, or actions. And in such a case a person is not at fault for not seeing. But in the case of the Pharisees, their not seeing of Christ, their insensibility to and impenitence regarding their own sinfulness and errors, is the consequence not of their inability to see these things, but of their will not to see — their unwillingness to see. They have a will, they have the means that would allow them to see all of this, they have intelligence, they have Scripture and other things — but they, thinking that they are using all of these correctly, actually direct them not towards Christ, towards repentance, towards salvation, but in their own interests — often sinfully, and with regard to Christ — ultimately even criminally.

Doors are always set in places of narrowing — transitional places in which one is not meant to remain. One may be at the door, awaiting entry or exit, but there is no sense (unless it is a matter of earthquakes — but Christ did not have them in mind) in standing in the doorway. Therefore doors are naturally designed and used as an entrance and exit in a narrow place. Hence also the understanding of the passage: entry — that is, movement towards the narrowing — is an image of the renunciation of the old self, of conversion and repentance; exit — that is, movement from the narrowing towards the widening — is an image of the renewal of the person, salvation, transfiguration, resurrection, and the becoming of a pure sheep and child of God by grace.

A door is a stronger call to movement than a mere signpost, because the direction of movement according to a signpost can be modified — whereas a narrow door makes it impossible to pass through it, to teleport past it — and so too there is no other way to God except through Jesus.

Doors also carry the significance of certain boundaries that give something. The doors themselves fulfil certain important and useful functions, but by virtue of their importance and their use at significant events and occasions, they also carry symbolic meaning. The doors themselves give nothing beyond their functions, but the person who opens or closes them may include this in the giving of something. Also important here is the following point: if ordinary doors are transitional — almost always a person must be before them or already through them having passed — then in the case of Christ as the door to the sheep, a person does not pass through Christ arriving in a space beyond Him. Christ is not transitional — not a stage for further movement. The entering through Christ as the Door and the going out to the Pasture is in reality an image of life in Christ Himself — not past Him and not beyond Him. The door here functions as an image of the restoration of the person, of their conversion, of the renewal of unity with the Source of life and of good — God; whilst the pasture is an image of life in unity with God, when a person aligns their life with the Source of life.

The pasture is also an image of the Kingdom of God — both that which is within you and that final, eschatological one which will come at the end of the world.
