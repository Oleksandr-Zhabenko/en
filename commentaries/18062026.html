Liturgy:
(Romans VIII, 22-27)
Romans VIII, 23, 26 — 'ἀπαρχὴν τοῦ πνεύματος' — 'aparkhen tou pneumatos' - first fruits of the Spirit. 'ἐν ἑαυτοῖς' - 'en heautois' - in themselves. A set expression.

In verses 24-25 we see how virtues give birth to one another, pass into one another, so that the later fulfillment of God's promises serves the gradual growth at once in several virtues for the faithful. We see here one of the reasons for God's long-suffering for the salvation of people and for history to have meaning.

Romans VIII, 27 — 'κατὰ θεὸν' — 'kata theon' - according to God. God — here is the Father, and the Holy Spirit operates in the world according to His common will with the Father and the Son. He resembles the image of caring hands with which someone embraces and holds close, taking care of their child. To a certain extent, the Holy Spirit represents the faithful, in particular the saints, before the Father. And if the request of a man is the request of a man, a creation, then the request of the Holy Spirit is the request of God with all the consequences. Also here it is well visible that the Holy Spirit is a Personality, because He intercedes. Intercede, however, can only a conscious person. Consequently, He is a Personality, a Hypostasis of the Divine nature. Therefore often in prayer the faithful say: Intercede us, O God, by Thy grace!. In the verse Romans VIII, 27 is used the mind [thought] of the Spirit (that which He thinks). Here it is clearly visible that the Holy Spirit also thinks, is intelligent [rational]. Here is an important moment for theology: the Father knows the mind of the Spirit, and in another place the apostle Paul says that the Spirit knows all things, even the deep things of God (consequently, the thoughts of the Father). Thinking is a manifestation of the nature, and the latter is common in the Trinity, but the Hypostases think, realizing the powers and freedom of the nature. And it is important that each Hypostasis of the Trinity knows what the Other thinks. This also is a testimony of the perichoresis of the Hypostases of the Trinity, and also of the fact that God's knowledge does not have a certain finite volume of knowledge, meaning it actually is boundless. For in the case of a finite volume of knowledge of the Hypostases, each would have to know all that He knows Himself, but also all that the Two Others know, and at the same time know that which He knows Himself. This tripling is impossible for a finite volume of knowledge, therefore God's knowledge has an infinite volume, is boundless. And also it is important to emphasize that the possession by each Hypostasis of the fullness of the Divine nature in no way becomes an obstacle for Them Each to perfectly know the thoughts and will of One Another. Full and perfect possession of the Divine nature does not prevent the Hypostases from fully and perfectly knowing One Another. These are not Three private Hypostases, but these are Three Loving and Open for Communion Hypostases. See also the good works at the links:

Concerning hope, Paul means that in the very definition of hope there is an indication that hope relates more not to that which is obvious, but to that which is unapparent. In modern language we use this word in various cases, even also when it concerns something obvious, but Paul used this word in relation to the unapparent. One can put it this way, that when something becomes obvious and present, existing, manifest, then hope gives place to knowledge and certainty. But the faithful in Christ, even until the time of the coming of this manifestation, until the Kingdom of God, wait willingly and patiently with hope. Hope extends into the invisible, so as to be a going beyond the boundaries of the visible.

Also this is an important passage for Trinitarian theology, since it clearly shows that there is no complete general correspondence between the manifestations of the Trinity in creation and what relationships are between the Hypostases in the Godhead.

(Matthew X, 23-31)
Matthew X, 23 — 'ἐν τῇ πόλει ταύτῃ' — 'en te polei taute' - in this city. Where. 'εἰς τὴν ἑτέραν' - 'eis ten heteran' - into the other. Where to.

Matthew X, 27 — 'ἐν τῇ σκοτίᾳ' — 'en te skotia' - in the darkness. Where. 'ἐν τῷ φωτί' - 'en to photi' - in the light. Where, under what conditions, how. 'ὃ εἰς τὸ οὖς ἀκούετε' - 'ho eis to ous akouete' - what into the ear you hear. That is, where the words enter, it is meant in the secret of the heart, inaudibly to those around, internal speech, voice.

Matthew X, 28 — 'μὴ φοβεῖσθε ἀπὸ τῶν ἀποκτεννόντων τὸ σῶμα' — 'me phobeisthe apo ton apoktennonton to soma' - do not fear away from the [ones] killing the body. That is, death from persecutors, the very possible persecutors, oppressors. 'ἀπολέσαι ἐν γεέννῃ' - 'apolesai en geenne' - to destroy in Gehenna. Where. See in detail and more at the link:

It is necessary to distinguish God's will and God's permission [allowance], that which God wishes, and that which He does not wish, but allows for the sake of the freedom of creation. In the Old Testament almost no one did this, and following it, especially when quoting, and in some places of the New Testament they also do not distinguish. But still it is necessary. See in detail at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/renewal-of-Peter.html

The necessity of such a distinction is well visible regarding the passage about the birds. Its translations are very eloquent. Many Slavic ones, in particular the Ukrainian one, as can be seen also in the figure above, add the word will…
https://www.facebook.com/groups/mereza/posts/3791128591185725/?comment_id=3791837754448142
The word will in the original is not there… Instead there is the Father Himself. But as Who? A participant? A judge? The one who kills? Creator? Provider [Provider of all]? Almighty [Ruler of all]? Witness? Co-sufferer? The word will is added, but it narrows all possible meanings to their subgroup where the Father either kills the birds intentionally, or wishes for them to become dead… But precisely this God does not wish.
