As for the coals on the head, Paul is literally quoting from Proverbs XXV, 21-22. In the context of Paul's words, it is not about making the enemy more painful, but about the fact that 'coal on the head' is called a real good that the person (enemy) begins to realise and see against all odds. After all, the person can often feel and have hostility, prejudice (bias), anger and enmity because he or she does not realise that the other one is a Christian and what this really means. In this case, such a person, having seen, or better to say, having deeply felt the love of a Christian, will fall into a state of deep emotion or even shock, which is called 'coal'. Once upon a time, it was the charcoal from under the altar with which the lips of Isaiah were cleansed by the Seraphim. This is a purifying coal, the coal of love, and Paul says that it is better for a person to experience such a difficult and dramatic insight and enlightenment than to continue to hold on to evil, hostility, anger, or the like. If such a person is humane, he or she will cease to be an enemy and may even become a friend (Paul himself is a vivid example), and if not, he or she will at least try to change the relationship. If he or she does not do this, then he or she is really affected by sin, mastered by passions, blinded by them, and will have an even deeper insight and enlightenment later. See also: 1 Corinthians XIV, 22-25.
