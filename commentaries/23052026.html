Liturgy:
(Acts XX, 7-12)
Acts XX, 7 — 'Ἐν δὲ τῇ μιᾷ τῶν σαββάτων' — 'en de te mia ton sabbaton' - on the first day after the Sabbath. As ChatGPT notes, this is a Semitic expression conveyed in Greek with this meaning. When. Google Gemini Pro provides the following details:

ἐν δὲ τῇ μιᾷ (en de tē mia) — 'but on the first [day]'. The numeral 'one/first' (μιᾷ) stands in the feminine gender, and so the word 'day' (ἡμέρᾳ), which in Greek is also feminine, is simply implied.
τῶν σαββάτων (tōn sabbatōn) — literally 'of Sabbaths' (in the genitive plural).
Why does 'Sabbaths' mean 'week'?
This is a classic Hebraism (the influence of Jewish thinking) in the Greek of the New Testament (Koine).
In Jewish tradition the days had no proper names of their own (such as Monday, Tuesday, and so on) but were counted relative to the principal day — the Shabbat. Therefore the Greek word σάββατον (sabbaton) began to mean not only the Sabbath itself but also the entire interval of time from Sabbath to Sabbath — that is, a week.
Accordingly, the idiom 'mia tōn sabbatōn' is translated as 'the first [day] of the week'. Since the Jewish week ended on the Sabbath, the first day of the new week logically became Sunday.

Acts XX, 9 — youth — in the original young man (may be up to approximately 40 years of age). 'κατενεχθεὶς ἀπὸ τοῦ ὕπνου ἔπεσεν ἀπὸ τοῦ τριστέγου κάτω' — 'katenekhtheis apo tou hypnou epesen apo tou tristegou kato' - having been overcome by sleep he fell from the third storey down. The first preposition 'apo' indicates here that sleep was not the true cause of his being overcome — the true cause was actually fatigue from listening, or inattentiveness, or incomplete engagement, which leads to the onset of tiredness and monotonous reception, which leads to relaxation. Acts XX, 12 — what is spoken of is the boy or the servant. 'παρεκλήθησαν' — 'pareklethesan' — they were comforted.

The story of the miraculous recovery or resurrection of Eutychus.

It is worth noting that the first day of the week — Sunday — is named as the day on which the faithful gathered for the Eucharist (the Sunday Eucharist is an apostolic tradition).

The miracle is deferred in time — for Eutychus rose or at least recovered from the severe fall after Paul's departure, but according to his words and faith. If one asks whether he was truly dead and this was a resurrection, it is not entirely clear. I think that since Paul said his soul is in him, he was not truly dead — but people initially perceived it so, because he showed no actual signs of life.

In any case, the miracle shows that the Lord can also perform miracles at a divine service (Paul's long sermon was part of the divine service, the Liturgy).

(John XIV, 10-21)
John XIV, 10, 11 — 'ἐγὼ ἐν τῷ πατρὶ καὶ ὁ πατὴρ ἐν ἐμοί (ἐστιν)' — 'ego en to patri kai ho pater en emoi (estin)' - I am in the Father and the Father is in Me. This concerns the perichoresis of the Hypostases of the Most Holy Trinity — their mutual abiding in One Another. 'ἐν ἐμοὶ' - 'en emoi' - in Me.

John XIV, 12 — 'ὁ πιστεύων εἰς ἐμέ' — 'ho pisteuon eis eme' - the one who believes in(to) Me. See at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/08042026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/16042025.html

John XIV, 13, 14 — 'ἐν τῷ ὀνόματί μου' — 'en to onomati mou' - in My name; with My name. Various translations are possible, which may either complement one another or stand separately. The first indicates that the disciples will ask precisely as Christians, as disciples of Christ — that is, as bearers of His name. The second — that they will ask on the basis of the fact that they are faithful disciples of Christ, Christians. Here two different meanings, united into one general one, may be seen at once. The faithful ask both as servants of Christ and as co-heirs of glory — that is, as children of God. The first more emphasises agreement with God's will, which servants carry out or are to carry out, and the second — a close relationship with God, including friendship. 'ὁ πατὴρ ἐν τῷ υἱῷ' - 'ho pater en to hyio' - the Father in the Son; the Father with (by means of) the Son. Both translations are grammatically possible, but the first has the advantage in terms of meaning. The second presents the Son as if He were less important than the Father — as if He were an instrument of the Father — which is not so: all three divine Hypostases are equal in honour, equal in glory, co-eternal, and consubstantial. The second also reduces the activity of the Son of God in creation, presenting everything as if the Son does not so much actively carry out God's will as something connected with Him is directed by the Father towards good. This last is also incorrect — and therefore indeed the first translation is better and conveys the general thought of the passage.

John XIV, 16 — 'εἰς τὸν αἰῶνα' — 'eis ton aiona' - for ever. Common expression. See above.

John XIV, 17 — 'παρ' ὑμῖν μένει καὶ ἐν ὑμῖν ἔσται' — 'par hymin menei kai en hymin estai' - He abides in the vicinity of you and will be in you. The preposition 'para' in its form before the following 'hy' here with the dative case indicates that the Holy Spirit also cares for the disciples — that is, the disciples are close to Him. The future tense is spoken of in regard to Pentecost. What is meant is that at that moment — the moment of the Last Supper — the disciples could not fully receive the Holy Spirit in the proper manner, whereas subsequently at Pentecost they did receive Him. The apostles were at that time not in the Spirit but in the vicinity of Him — in the classical expression not by place but by state.

John XIV, 20 — 'ἐν ἐκείνῃ τῇ ἡμέρᾳ' — 'en ekeine te hemera' - on that day; in that day. 'ἐγὼ ἐν τῷ πατρί μου καὶ ὑμεῖς ἐν ἐμοὶ κἀγὼ ἐν ὑμῖν' - 'ego en to patri mou kai hymeis en emoi kago en hymin' - I am in My Father, and you in Me, and I also in you. Here too there is perichoresis (see above concerning verses 10, 11), but additionally what is spoken of is the consubstantiality of the Son with the Father and the Spirit in divinity, and with people in human nature. All people share in common with Christ by nature — even those who are hostile to Christ — but perichoresis, the hypostatic mutual-abiding with God, belongs only to the faithful. It must be understood that God can be everywhere in three different ways: by existence and power (1), by grace (2), and by participation and communion (3). Perichoresis concerns precisely the third way — communion — and it is to this that the faithful are called most of all. God can act by grace even upon the hearts of sinners, manifesting the action of grace wherever He wills, and existence and power — everywhere without exception. God calls the faithful and all people to communion with Him. And here Christ affirms that such communion exists in the faithful. It is important to say that the faithful know not only God's participation in their needs, but also communion and unity with God — which is considerably more than the first.

John XIV, 21 — 'ἀγαπηθήσεται ὑπὸ τοῦ πατρός μου' — 'agapethesetai hypo tou patros mou' - will be beloved by My Father. The preposition 'hypo' here with the genitive case indicates the passive voice of the participle will be beloved.

The Lord Jesus Christ at the Last Supper speaks of His passage to the Father, and also of the Holy Spirit Whom He will send to the disciples, and of the eternal life of the faithful. The Holy Spirit is called the Comforter and the Spirit of Truth — these are His most commonly used names, apart from the Holy Spirit itself and the Spirit of the Lord and the Spirit of Christ. Comforter — Paraclete — is not only a Comforter but also an Advocate, and therefore also a Defender.

If anyone truly comes to know how much good God bestows upon people, they may willingly come to love God — and this means fulfilling His commandments.

Concerning prayer — several important points. Jesus promises that one may pray in His name, that such prayer is pleasing to God, and that one may also pray to Him directly. It is not mentioned explicitly here but it is implied that if a prayer is offered in the Holy Spirit, and the Holy Spirit Himself wishes not only to assist in prayer, to prompt it, to strengthen and teach it — which is already valuable in itself — but also to bring to fulfilment that for which He gives the capacity to pray, then the Lord will hear such a prayer and fulfil it. Therefore it is also important to pray concerning the Holy Spirit in connection with what we ask. The apostle Paul reasons analogously in another place concerning Christ: Who shall bring a charge against God's elect? It is God who justifies. Who is he who condemns? It is Christ Jesus who died, and furthermore is also risen, who is even at the right hand of God, who also makes intercession for us (Romans VIII, 33-34).
