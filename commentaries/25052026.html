Matins:
(Luke VII, 17-30)
Luke VII, 17 — 'ἐν ὅλῃ τῇ Ἰουδαίᾳ' — 'en hole te Ioudaia' - in all Judaea.

Luke VII, 21 — 'ἐν ἐκείνῃ τῇ ὥρᾳ' — 'en ekeine te hora' - in that hour; at that time. 'ἀπὸ νόσων καὶ μαστίγων καὶ πνευμάτων πονηρῶν' - 'apo noson kai mastigon kai pneumaton poneron' - from illnesses, and grievous afflictions, and evil spirits. The preposition 'apo' indicates that the healing was complete and total.

Luke VII, 23 — 'ἐν ἐμοί' — 'en emoi' - in Me.

Luke VII, 24 — 'εἰς τὴν ἔρημον' — 'eis ten eremon' - into the desert. 'ὑπὸ ἀνέμου' - 'hypo anemou' - by the wind. The preposition 'hypo' here with the genitive case indicates the middle or passive voice of the participle shaken/being shaken [involving itself more in the action] and explains by what. There is also here an indirect possible pointer from Christ that a person who doubts often inclines themselves to doubt yet more — amplifying and surrendering to doubts increasingly.

Luke VII, 25 — 'ἐν μαλακοῖς ἱματίοις' — 'en malakois himatiois' - in fine clothing. 'ἐν ἱματισμῷ' - 'en himatismo' - in garments. 'ἐν τοῖς βασιλείοις' - 'en tois basileiois' - in the (royal) palaces.

Luke VII, 28 — 'ἐν γεννητοῖς' — 'en gennetois' - among those born. 'ἐν τῇ βασιλείᾳ' - 'en te basileia' - in the Kingdom.

Luke VII, 30 — 'εἰς ἑαυτούς' — 'eis heautous' - in themselves; for themselves. Both translations are possible. 'ὑπ' αὐτοῦ' - 'hyp autou' - by him. The preposition 'hypo' here in its form before the following vowel with the genitive case indicates the passive voice of the participle rendered as were baptised, answering the question by whom.

After the Baptism of Jesus, John testified concerning Him to others and had Revelation from God concerning Jesus. Therefore it is considered that he sent his disciples so that they too might be convinced of Who Jesus is.

The answer of Jesus to them is closely connected with the fact that John — being a great prophet — nevertheless performed not a single miracle. Whereas Christ, after him, performed many wondrous miracles with many meanings — for the salvation and instruction of people, for the glory of God, and otherwise.

The testimony of Jesus confirms the fulfilment of many Old Testament prophecies concerning John.

Liturgy:
(Acts XXI, 8-14)
Acts XXI, 8 — 'εἰς Καισαρίαν' — 'eis Kaisarian' - into Caesarea. Whither. 'εἰς τὸν οἶκον Φιλίππου τοῦ εὐαγγελιστοῦ ὄντος ἐκ τῶν ἑπτὰ' - 'eis ton oikon Philippou tou euangelistou ontos ek ton hepta' - into the house of Philip the evangelist, one of the seven (first deacons; see more concerning him in chapter VIII). Whither. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that Philip is a representative of the first deacons, similar to the others. 'παρ' αὐτῷ' - 'par auto' - in the vicinity of him; with him. The preposition 'para' in its form before the following vowel here with the dative case indicates residing close to him — that is, as close friends, welcome guests.

Acts XXI, 10 — 'ἀπὸ τῆς Ἰουδαίας' — 'apo tes Ioudaias' - from Judaea. Whence. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that Agabus left Judaea in the process.

Acts XXI, 11 — 'ἐν Ἱερουσαλὴμ' — 'en Hierousalem' - in Jerusalem. Where. 'εἰς χεῖρας ἐθνῶν' - 'eis kheiras ethnon' - into the hands of the Gentiles. Whither. The preposition 'eis' forms part of a common expression here.

Acts XXI, 12 — 'εἰς Ἱερουσαλήμ' — 'eis Hierousalem' - into Jerusalem. Whither.

Acts XXI, 13 — 'ἀποθανεῖν εἰς Ἱερουσαλὴμ ἑτοίμως ἔχω' — 'apothanein eis Hierousalem etoimos ekho' - to die? (I go) to Jerusalem! I have readiness (I am ready)! The preposition 'eis' indicates here the decisiveness of Paul to go and preach Christ, even if this may lead to martyrdom. The words of Agabus were indeed from the Holy Spirit, but so also was the decisiveness of Paul. The ministry of apostleship and the preaching of Christ are so important and so commanded by Christ that even spiritual warnings are not the determining factor with regard to whether to preach or not.

Acts XXI, 12 — 'παρεκαλουμεν' — 'parekaloumen' — here they began to beg, to entreat, to implore.

Paul in response speaks of the loud weeping and the fact that it is breaking his heart. He expresses his resolve not only to suffer but even to die for the name of Jesus Christ.

Luke and the other fellow workers of Paul entrusted him and themselves to God's will.

God's will did not consist in the death of Paul, but in his preaching. Paul is prepared even to die for the sake of God's will. See also:

(John XIV, 27–XV, 7)
John XIV, 30 — 'ἐν ἐμοὶ' — 'en emoi' - in Me. Here what is meant is that Christ is pure from sin, sinless.

John XV, 2 — 'ἐν ἐμοὶ' — 'en emoi' - in Me; in Mine. Here the second variant is more likely, signifying possession — the belonging of the branch. If the first, then what is spoken of is a person who was with Christ and then departed from Him.

John XV, 4, 6, 7 — 'μείνατε ἐν ἐμοί, κἀγὼ ἐν ὑμῖν' — 'meinate en emoi, kago en hymin' - abide in Me, and I also in you. See concerning John XIV, 20 at the links:

'ἀφ' ἑαυτοῦ' — 'aph heautou' - from itself; of itself. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following 'he' indicates here that those fruits are a gift for others — that is, they define one's relations with others. 'ἐν τῇ ἀμπέλῳ' - 'en te ampelo' - in (as part of) the vine. That is, where. 'ἐν ἐμοὶ' - 'en emoi' - in Me. In whom.

John XV, 5 — 'ὁ μένων ἐν ἐμοὶ κἀγὼ ἐν αὐτῷ' — 'ho menon en emoi kago en auto' - the one who abides in Me and I in them. See above.

John XV, 6 — 'εἰς τὸ πῦρ βάλλουσιν' — 'eis to pyr ballousin' - they throw into the fire. That is, whither.

John XV, 7 — 'τὰ ῥήματά μου ἐν ὑμῖν μείνῃ' — 'ta rhemata mou en hymin meine' - (if) My words (deeds) abide in you. That is, upon this condition. See also more at the link:

In textual criticism there is a widespread view that chapters XV-XVII were added to the original text later (either by John himself or by his disciples). Nevertheless, there are no real differences between these chapters and all the others in terms of content or style, and therefore their authenticity is the same as that of all the others. Google Gemini Flash-Lite notes that these chapters may be seen as a kind of summit of the theological thought of the Gospel. It also proposes the following way of viewing the text (in the form of a question):

The perception of these chapters as 'added' is a consequence of the modern habit of linear-logical presentation, whereas the structure of the Johannine text functions rather as concentric circles, where each subsequent section does not simply continue the narrative but deepens the preceding theses (a spiral development of thought).

The image of the vine, the vinedresser, and the branches is very eloquent — I think it requires no further explanation beyond what has already been said.

My Father is greater than I — rather profound words to explain. The predominant view is that what is spoken of is the fact that the Son of God is begotten of the Father, Who is the Root of the Divinity in that sense; and also that these words point to the human nature of Jesus — the Father is greater than the human nature, and the words themselves speak of the glorification of the human nature of Jesus. These words do NOT mean that the Father is greater than the Son in the sense of greater fullness of Divinity (no — all Three Hypostases of the Trinity are equal in Divinity, which is One for all Three).
