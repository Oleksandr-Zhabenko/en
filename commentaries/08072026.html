Liturgy:
(1 Corinthians II, 9-III, 8)
1 Corinthians II, 9 — 'ανεбη' — 'anebe' - (into where the heart of a person has not) ascended.

1 Corinthians II, 11 — 'ἐν αὐτῷ' — 'en auto' - in him.

1 Corinthians II, 12, 16 — 'τὸ πνεῦμα τὸ ἐκ τοῦ θεοῦ' — 'to pneuma to ek tou theou' - that Spirit Who (proceedeth, is) from God. 'εἰδῶμεν τὰ ὑπὸ τοῦ θεоῦ χαρισθέντα ἡμῖν' - 'eidomen ta hypo tou theou kharisthenta hemin' - (that we might) know about that which from God is granted to us; (that we might) know about that which by God is granted to us. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates onto the passive state of the participle granted. Here an important distinction is present between the prepositions which both are translated into Ukrainian, Slavonic, and many other languages by one and the same word, but in Greek by different words convey different meanings. See in more detail at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/uk/commentaries/Pryjmennyky.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/uk/commentaries/vystup-2025-hypo-genitive-Romans-XIII_1.pdf
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/02082025.html
The Holy Spirit, about Whom it is spoken at the beginning, is otherwise from God (the Father) than that which is granted into us. The preposition 'ek' here emphasizes the essential procession of the Holy Spirit as a Hypostasis of the Most Holy Trinity from the Father, a procession about which in ancient Koine Greek it is spoken as 'para tou patros'. Consequently, these are two different realities — the gift of the Holy Spirit, the Third Hypostasis of the Most Holy Trinity, and all other gifts of God. In the first there is no limit and He can lift a person higher than the entire creation onto divinisation, onto infinity, into God. If one compares also with the verse 1 Corinthians I, 3, it is clear that here the apostle Paul emphasizes that they are granted by Whom — precisely by God, in particular both as spiritual gifts of grace (which immediately unite with God) and that which God wished to grant also — God's benefits, as well as the creations of God, in particular also the very people around, which all together manifests great love of God. The Holy Spirit is an infinite and endless Way, and God's gifts — that which happens and is met onto this path (such an imperfect comparison). This part of the reading also is a vivid testimony onto that the Holy Spirit is a Hypostasis, a Personality, God. The Holy Spirit knows the things of God.

1 Corinthians II, 13 — 'ἐν διδακτοῖς ἀνθρωπίνης σοφίας λόγοις' — 'en didaktois anthropines sophias logois' - in the taught words of human wisdom. 'ἐν διδαкτοῖς πνεύματος' - 'en didiktois pneumatos' - literally in the taught [words] of the Spirit. It concerns that these are the words of the Holy Spirit which the apostles learned, which they accepted and which brought forth fruit.

1 Corinthians II, 14 — 'μωρια' — 'moria' - foolishness, madness, absurdity.

See a more detailed explanation of this word and its shades at the link:
https://www.facebook.com/Oleksandr.S.Zhabenko/posts/

1 Corinthians II, 15 — 'αὐτὸς δὲ ὑπ’ οὐδενὸς ἀνακρίνεται' — 'autos de hyp oudenos anakrinetai' - yet he himself [the spiritual person] by no one is judged. The preposition 'hypo' in its form before a subsequent vowel here is with the genitive case and indicates onto the passive voice of the participle is judged. That is, although also various people can judge a spiritual person by the permission of God, yet properly the final and righteous judgement belongs to God, and such a person God expectedly will mercy and glorify.

1 Corinthians III, 1 — 'ἐν Χριστῷ' — 'en CHristo' - in Christ.

1 Corinthians III, 3 — 'ἐν ὑμῖν' — 'en hymin' - in you; among you. Where, among whom.

(Matthew XIII, 31-36)
Matthew XIII, 24, 31 — 'ἐν τῷ ἀγρῷ' — 'en to agro' - in the field. Where.

Matthew XIII, 32 — 'ἐν τοῖς κλάδοις' — 'en tois kladois' - in the branches. Where.

Matthew XIII, 33 — 'εἰς ἀλεύρου σάта τρία' — 'eis aleurou sata tria' - into three measures of meal. Where to.

Matthew XIII, 34, 35 — 'ἐν παραβολαῖς' — 'en parabolais' - in parables; with (by means of) parables. Both variants are correct, although somewhat different by content. The first means that Jesus used a parable as a form of conveying the content which teaches, and the second — that the parable itself also by its immediate literal content also teaches, is a teaching means. The second variant more fully describes the situation, therefore is better. 'χωρὶς παραβολῆς' - 'khoris paraboles' - without a parable; apart from a parable. The second variant of translation says that Jesus did not speak by lectures, but always adduced wonderful illustrations and parables as a form of teaching and remembering. The first emphasizes that parables were added organically and constantly.

Matthew XIII, 35 — 'ἀπὸ καταβολῆς' — 'apo kataboles' - from the foundation; from the beginning. The preposition 'apo' indicates here onto that it concerns not the secrets of God in the Trinity Most Holy, in the Godhead (pre-eternal, eternal), but the secrets regarding creation, the world, people, etc. (which have a beginning in time). That is, important secrets which are relevant in all times. In other words universal (from the word Universe), ecumenical (also from it, only in Greek). See also about this important distinction and explanation at the links:

Matthew X, 39 — 'ὁ εὑρὼν τὴν ψυχὴν αὐτοῦ' — 'ho heuron ten psykhen autou' - he that findeth [hence the word Eureka!] his soul [life]. 'ὁ ἀπολέσας τὴν ψυχὴν αὐτοῦ ἕνεκен ἐμοῦ εὑρήσει αὐτήν' - 'o apolesas ten psykhen autou heneken Emou heuresei auten' - he that loseth his soul [life] for My sake will find it. Here it concerns the life of a person as a find, that is, about a creative, actualised, rich life — a person who does not co-create life with Christ will waste and lose it, and that one who co-creates with Christ, for His sake, so that they are ready also to lose — in reality shall find that which they seek — a full, creative, actualised, and also eternal life.

Matthew X, 40 — 'ἀποστείλαντά με' — 'aposteilanta Me' - sent Me. The prefix 'apo' emphasizes that the Son of God entered into the world, as if leaving the Father and the Spirit, that is, became as if distant, took onto Himself the form of a servant, as Paul says about that in another place. At the same time He did not cease to be God, and also did not leave the Father and the Spirit, being inseparable from Them.

Matthew X, 41, 42 — 'εἰς ὄνομα' — 'eis onoma' - into the name of; for the name of. Both variants of translation are possible, which by meaning are identical. A set expression which emphasizes a movement towards confession.

Matthew X, 1 — 'ἐν ταῖς πόλεσιν αὐτῶν' — 'en tais polesin auton' — in their cities. Where.

Here Christ says that whosoever shall seek to have (receive, find, build, etc.) their life outside the Gospel, like the prodigal son in the parable, then such a person will lose their life. If, however, anyone for the sake of the fulfillment of God's will, for the sake of the Gospel, for the sake of Christ shall lose something from their own, such a person will preserve it in eternity in the best understanding, about which it is spoken further.

That which a person does for the sake of God, they shall never lose. God will repay a hundredfold for every true good done in His name, according to the Gospel. Even if a person later refuses, the good done in God will not vanish and will not be rejected, but God will reward the person in one or another method. This, as I wrote earlier, does not signify that the person necessarily will be saved, but this signifies that God will not forget and will not despise that real good. For Matthew, who several times in the Gospel speaks onto the topic of Heavenly property and reward, treasures, this was personally relevant as for a former worker of the financial sphere (in modern language).
