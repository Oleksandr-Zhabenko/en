Matins:
(Luke IV, 22-30)
Luke IV, 22 — 'ἐκ τοῦ στόματος' — 'ek tou stomatos' - out of the mouth. Wherefrom.

Luke IV, 23, 24 — 'εἰς τὴν Καφαρναοὺμ' — 'eis ten Kapharnaoum' - in Capernaum. Here 'eis' indicates location in the context of rumors spreading. Rumors of Jesus' miracles were spreading in various places, hence the movable 'in' is used. 'ἐν τῇ πατρίδι' - 'en te patridi' - in the Homeland [in His own country]. Here, properly, is the location of the people themselves who address Jesus.

Luke IV, 25 — 'ἐν ταῖς ἡμέραις Ἡλείου ἐν τῷ Ἰσραήλ' — 'en tais hemerais Heleiou en to Israel' - in the days of Elijah in Israel. When and where.

Luke IV, 26 — 'εἰς Σάρεπτα' — 'eis Sarepta' - to Sarepta.

Luke IV, 27 — 'ἐν τῷ Ἰσραὴλ' — 'en to Israel' - in Israel. Where. Analogous to verse 25.

Luke IV, 28 — 'ἐν τῇ συναγωγῇ' — 'en to synagoge' - in the synagogue. Where.

Liturgy:
(1 Corinthians IX, 13-18)
1 Corinthians IX, 13 — 'ἐκ τοῦ ἱεροῦ' — 'ek tou hierou' - from the temple. The preposition 'ek' emphasizes that the food for the ministers of the (Jewish) temple originates from their service, that is, the service feeds them.

1 Corinthians IX, 14 — 'ἐκ τοῦ εὐαγγελίου' — 'ek tou euangeliou' - from the Gospel / preaching. Since the word Gospel in the New Testament denotes both the Gospel itself and the act of preaching, the translation is polysemantic. How and what a person preaches in Christ is meant to feed them.

1 Corinthians IX, 15 — 'ἐν ἐμοί' — 'en emoi' - in me. In modern language, it can be translated generally as in my case. Paul's words additionally emphasize that this is not merely situational behavior on the part of Paul and those around him, but his and their overall state.

1 Corinthians IX, 18 — 'εἰς τὸ μὴ καταχρήσασθαι τῇ ἐξουσίᾳ μου ἐν τῷ εὐαγγελίῳ' — 'eis to me katakhresasthai te exousia mou en to euangelio' - not to abuse [make full use of / consume] my authority in the Gospel/preaching. God's commandment and right generate the authority and prestige of the Apostle Paul, generating spiritual authority in general. But Paul, by personal example, tries not to make full use of the authority and right he has according to God's commandment, so as not to push away those who have doubts about the truth of the commandment.

Paul writes about preachers of the Gospel who have completely dedicated themselves to this work, effectively entrusting themselves into God's hands, Christ's hands. And the Lord takes care of such people in a special way, particularly through a commandment. Paul himself, although he could rely on this commandment, did not do so, in order not to place additional obstacles to the preaching and acceptance of Christ on the part of those who would then have to take care of Paul. This was his personal stance, but also a kind of zeal that he valued.

Also, here he distinguishes between voluntary and reluctant preaching of the Gospel. Preaching the Gospel is called a reward for those who do it willingly, because it is an opportunity to completely dedicate oneself to a beloved and blessed work; whereas it is called a duty for those who, being called to it, do not rejoice in it and labor reluctantly. Thus, to a large extent, it depends on the person themselves whether they will preach the Gospel willingly or not, whether it will be their beloved task; while the Lord, for His part, has given such an opportunity — to completely dedicate oneself to a beloved task — the preaching of the Gospel.

(Matthew XVI, 1-6)
Matthew XVI, 1 — 'ἐκ τοῦ οὐρανοῦ' — 'ek tou ouranou' - from the heaven. The preposition 'ek' indicates here the origin of the sign.

Matthew XVI, 4 — literally an evil [by the results of its actions] and adulterous generation seeks a sign.

Matthew XVI, 5 — 'εἰς τὸ πέραν' — 'eis to peran' - to the other side. A set expression with this meaning. Where to.

Matthew XVI, 6 — 'προσέχετε ἀπὸ τῆς ζύμης τῶν Φαρισαίων καὶ Σαδδουκαίων' — 'prosekhete apo tes zymes ton Pharisaion kai Saddoukaion' - beware [keep away] from the leaven of the Pharisees and Sadducees. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that one must be careful to stay away from the position of the Pharisees (blind zealous adherents of human traditions who are convinced of their own righteousness) and the Sadducees (believers who have actually lost faith in the efficacy of God's power and are occupied only with the earthly). When people attach themselves to the earthly, the vain, and sometimes the wrong, while being certain of their own righteousness, then without changing this attitude it leads to decline, and sometimes even to opposition to God, as happened with the Pharisees and Sadducees who became hostile toward Christ. Of course, one must be careful, because a person can be tempted similarly, even while being a Christian.

Regarding the question of the Pharisees and Sadducees, it is worth looking deeper to understand better. Christ's answer reveals the logic of the Pharisees and Sadducees, which is worth examining more deeply.

A sign is a signal to action, specifically to conversion, repentance, and transformation. That is, a person who is not established in good (and often inclined to evil), who sometimes turns away from God (from the Gospel commandments), may realize that something is wrong with them, but wants to have a signal, an impulse, for real change. But if a person realizes that they need to repent, what signal do they need for this? After all, the time for repentance is always relevant until the Kingdom of God comes; God is always ready to accept a person in repentance, at any time. There is no need to look for a suitable time for conversion and repentance — it is always now. The same applies to circumstances — any circumstances are suitable for conversion and repentance. Therefore, there is simply no real logic in searching for a signal for conversion, a signal for repentance.

If a person does not realize that they need repentance, then what should the signal testify to them? Will they not perceive it as a sign… of their own righteousness (of which they are already convinced)? Or even worse — as a sign that they can control God, receiving from Him the desired signals?

Therefore, Christ does not want to accommodate the logic of the Pharisees, because it is erroneous. However, He does not reject signals themselves. But He wants people to see special manifestations of God's love and perceive them correctly. Therefore, as an example of this, He cites His future Resurrection (the sign of Jonah). Indeed, all the perfections of Jesus' Resurrection far exceed those of any other signs. Truly, the Resurrection of Jesus Christ is so powerful, life-giving, glorious, bright, holy, wondrous, triumphant, joyful, good, full of such love, granting such hope, and strengthening in faith, that one could go on and on — like no other sign.

Further, Christ warns against the leaven of the Pharisees, that is, the just-described mindset, feelings, will, desires, direction, etc.

Jesus' words about the weather are absent in some ancient Gospel manuscripts.

Of holy Prophet Elijah:
(James V, 10-20; Luke IV, 22-30)
James V, 10 — 'ἐν τῷ ὀνόματι κυρίου' — 'en to onomati kyriou' - in the name of the Lord; by the name of the Lord. Both translation options are possible. The first indicates that the prophets spoke to the people conveying the words of the Lord, functioning as the mouth of the Lord for the people, as they are often called; the second indicates that the prophets represented the Lord to the people and were His witnesses. See also at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/24042026.html

James V, 12 — 'ὑπὸ κρίσιν' — 'hypo krisin' - under judgment. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the accusative case and indicates that through violating the Gospel commandment not to swear, people may fall under God's judgment. See more about Christ's commandment at the links:

James V, 13, 14, 19 — 'τις ἐν ὑμῖν' — 'tis en hymin' - anyone among you; someone among you. That is, if any of the faithful find themselves in the circumstances spoken of.

James V, 14 — 'ἐν τῷ ὀνόματι κυρίου' — 'en to onomati kyriou' - in the name of the Lord; by the name of the Lord. Both translation options are possible. The first indicates that the elders of the Church (often priests, although the apostle did not necessarily mean only them; these could also be bishops, as well as other Church elders, particularly prophets and teachers) act from the Lord, meaning the Lord performs the Sacrament (later, this passage and the corresponding practice became the basis for the Sacrament of Unction / Anointing of the Sick, which is performed specifically by clergy — and almost always by a council of priests). The second option indicates that the elders act here as God's representatives, witnesses of the Sacrament.

James V, 19 — 'ἀπὸ τῆς ἀληθείας' — 'apo tes aletheias' - from the truth (distancing and separation are emphasized).

James V, 20 — 'ἐκ πλάνης' — 'ek planes' - out of wandering [error]. 'ἐκ θανάτου' - 'ek thanatou' - out of death. The outward movement from wandering and death is emphasized.
