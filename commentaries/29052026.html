Liturgy:
(Acts XXVII, 1-44)
Acts XXVII, 1, 6 — 'εἰς τὴν Ἰταλίαν' — 'eis ten Italian' - into Italy; to Italy. Whither.

Acts XXVII, 2 — 'εἰς τοὺς κατὰ τὴν Ἀσίαν τόπους' — 'eis tous kata ten Asian topous' - to the places along Asia. Whither.

Acts XXVII, 3 — 'εἰς Σιδῶνα' — 'eis Sidona' - to Sidon. Whither.

Acts XXVII, 5 — 'εἰς Μύρρα τῆς Λυκίας' — 'eis Myrra tes Lykias' - into Myra of Lycia. Whither (a city later made famous through the ministry of Saint Nicholas, Archbishop of that city).

Acts XXVII, 6 — 'εἰς αὐτό' — 'eis auto' - into it. Whither.

Acts XXVII, 7 — 'ἐν ἱκαναῖς δὲ ἡμέραις' — 'en hikanais de hemerais' - over many days. When.

Acts XXVII, 8 — 'εἰς τόπον τινὰ' — 'eis topon tina' - to a certain (particular, some) place. Whither.

Acts XXVII, 11 — 'ἢ τοῖς ὑπὸ Παύλου λεγομένοις' — 'e tois hypo Paulou legomenois' - the things said by Paul. The preposition 'hypo' here with the genitive case indicates the passive voice of the participle said (by whom).

Acts XXVII, 12 — 'εἰς Φοίνικα' — 'eis Phoinika' - into Phenice; to Phenice. Whither.

Acts XXVII, 17 — 'εἰς τὴν Σύρτιν' — 'eis ten Syrtin' - into Syrtis; into the quicksands. Whither.

Acts XXVII, 21 — 'ἐν μέσῳ αὐτῶν' — 'en meso auton' - in their midst. Where. Common expression. 'ἀπὸ τῆς Κρήτης' - 'apo tes Kretes' - from Crete; away from Crete. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that Paul had counselled them not to leave Crete — but they left.

Acts XXVII, 22 — 'ἐξ ὑμῶν' — 'ex hymon' - from among you. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following 'hy' indicates here that all the participants of the voyage in general are meant — since it includes all the travellers, including Paul himself, who is among them.

Acts XXVII, 26 — 'εἰς νῆσον δέ τινα' — 'eis neson de tina' - but onto a certain island. Whither.

Acts XXVII, 27 — 'ἐν τῷ Ἀδρίᾳ' — 'en to Adria' - in the Adriatic Sea. Where.

Acts XXVII, 29 — 'ἐκ πρύμνης' — 'ek prymnes' - from the stern. Whence. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that the anchors were cast from the stern — without further specification.

Acts XXVII, 30 — 'ἐκ τοῦ πλοίου' — 'ek tou ploiou' - from the ship. Whence. 'εἰς τὴν θάλασσαν' - 'eis ten thalassan' - into the sea; towards the sea. Whither. 'ἐκ πρῴρης' - 'ek prores' - from the bow of the ship (from the front part of the vessel). Whence.

Acts XXVII, 31, 37 — 'ἐν τῷ πλοίῳ' — 'en to ploio' - in the ship; on the ship. Where.

Acts XXVII, 34 — 'ἀπὸ τῆς κεφαλῆς' — 'apo tes kephales' - from the head. Whence. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that what is spoken of is the loss of those hairs — and in context, since a negation follows, that they will not be lost. A parallel to the Gospel words of the Saviour to the apostles. It points to the fact that God can manifest particular care not only for faithful servants. See also:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/01032025.html
https://www.facebook.com/Oleksandr.S.Zhabenko/posts/
Here the Lord cares in a particular way not only for the faithful apostle Paul and his faithful companions, but also for those who were granted to him. This points to the fact that God does not cease to care for those He has granted to someone, but also often (at least as we see here) cares for them in a particular way together with those for whom He has shown such prior concern.

Acts XXVII, 38, 40 — 'εἰς τὴν θάλασσαν' — 'eis ten thalassan' - into the sea. Whither.

Acts XXVII, 39 — 'εἰς ὃν' — 'eis hon' - to which.

Acts XXVII, 40 — 'εἰς τὸν αἰγιαλόν' — 'eis ton aigialon' - to the shore. Whither.

Acts XXVII, 41 — 'εἰς τόπον' — 'eis topon' - into a place. Whither. Common expression. 'ὑπὸ τῆς βίας' - 'hypo tes bias' - under the force (of the waves); by the force of the waves. The preposition 'hypo' here with the genitive case indicates that through which the stern was being destroyed — that is, the means of destruction through which this was taking place.

Acts XXVII, 44 — 'ἀπὸ τοῦ πλοίου' — 'apo tou ploiou' - from the ship. Whence. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that they completely left the ship in the process, thereby reaching solid ground.

Part of the story of Paul's difficult journey on the way to his trial in Rome. Here too, in difficult circumstances, the Lord manifests His mercy and help — and through Paul, as once through the prophets, reveals His will, saving people from calamity. Also worthy of note is the mutual support between Paul and the centurion Julius, his guard, who was conveying him to trial.

I shall also draw attention to the mention of the city of Myra in Lycia, where subsequently by God's will Saint Nicholas the Wonderworker was glorified — also known for his help to travellers and in particular to seafarers.

The description of the voyage is very detailed — perhaps it is a parallel to the difficult sea voyages of the Mediterranean and other seas well known to ancient mariners, such as the Odyssey.

(John XVII, 18-26)
John XVII, 18 — 'εἰς τὸν κόσμον' — 'eis ton kosmon' - into the world. Whither.

John XVII, 19 — 'ἡγιασμένοι ἐν ἀληθείᾳ' — 'hegiasmenoi en aletheia' - sanctified with truth; sanctified in truth. Both translations are possible. The first indicates that truth sanctifies people, since it is impossible to be in truth without being in grace — which is what properly bestows sanctification. The second indicates that being in truth is important for attaining holiness and growing in it (being sanctified).

John XVII, 20 — 'περὶ τῶν πιστευόντων διὰ τοῦ λόγου αὐτῶν εἰς ἐμέ' — 'peri ton pisteuonton dia tou logou auton eis eme' - concerning those who believe through their word in(to) Me. See concerning John XIV, 1 at the links:

John XVII, 23 — 'ἐγὼ ἐν αὐτοῖς καὶ σὺ ἐν ἐμοί' — 'ego en autois kai sy en emoi' - I in them and you in Me. 'ἵνα ὦσιν τετελειωμένοι εἰς ἕν' - 'hina osin teteleiomenoi eis hen' - that they may be perfected (accomplished) into one. That is, so that their perfection may be unity rather than divergence and discord, and also that they may truly be so perfected.

John XVII, 26 — 'ἵνα ἡ ἀγάπη ἣν ἠγάπησάς με ἐν αὐτοῖς ᾖ κἀγὼ ἐν αὐτοῖς' — 'hina he agape hen egapesas me en autois e kago en autois' - that the love with which you loved Me may be in them, and I also in them. See above concerning perichoresis, and also concerning God's love at the links:

John XVII, 21, 22 — 'εν' — 'hen' — one, single (there is no entirely satisfactory equivalent for translation; in the original it is an adjective in the singular deriving from the numeral one and signifying to be one, single).

John XVII, 23 — the same adjective in the accusative case, but in terms of meaning closer to the concept of unity.

A continuation of the so-called High-Priestly Prayer of Jesus Christ at the Last Supper — in effect its conclusion. Concerning the beginning of the prayer I wrote at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/24052026.html

The Lord prays that among the disciples there may be a unity similar to the unity in the Holy Trinity. Through such unity the world also is to understand the disciples and through them come to know God. Words of great importance for the Church, which is One and Single (and it is precisely the above-mentioned adjective that is meant here).
