Liturgy:
(Acts XXV, 13-19)
Acts XXV, 13 — 'εἰς Καισάριαν' — 'eis Kaisarian' - into Caesarea. Whither.

Acts XXV, 14 — 'ἀνήρ τίς ἐστιν καταλελειμμένος ὑπὸ Φήλικος δέσμιος' — 'aner tis estin kataleleimmenos hypo Phelikos desmios' - there is a certain man left by Felix as a prisoner. The preposition 'hypo' here with the genitive case indicates the passive voice of the participle left (by whom).

Acts XXV, 15 — 'γενομένου μου εἰς Ἱεροσόλυμα' — 'genomenou mou eis Hierosolyma' - when I came to Jerusalem. Whither. In terms of meaning this is the appropriate translation here. The preposition 'eis' indicates here that Festus had recently become governor in Jerusalem, having been transferred there not long before the events he mentions.

The testimony of a Gentile — a ruler and a judge — concerning Paul and the accusations against the latter and the Christians. It is important in the sense that it reveals the legal groundlessness of the persecution of Christians — which was then taking place and continued in later times as well. Also the words of the ruler show what Christians expected from the authorities when they were brought to trial: the opportunity to defend themselves in court, and for the trial to be impartial towards them. Unfortunately this wish of the Christians was often not fulfilled. But here in this case (with Paul) it was realised, and Luke writes of it.

(John XVI, 23-33)
John XVI, 23, 24, 26 — 'ἐν ἐκείνῃ τῇ ἡμέρᾳ' — 'en ekeine te hemera' — on that day; in that day. When. 'ἐν τῷ ὀνόματί μου' - 'en to onomati mou' - in My name; with My name. Various translations are possible, which may either complement one another or stand separately. The first indicates that the disciples will ask precisely as Christians, as disciples of Christ — that is, as bearers of His name. The second — that they will ask on the basis of the fact that they are faithful disciples of Christ, Christians. See at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/PowerOfHuman.html

John XVI, 25 — 'ἐν παροιμίαις' — 'en paroimiais' - in parables; with parabolic speech. A fuller translation gives the following meaning: in closed, dark, unclear, enigmatic, and allegorical language — the language of a parable; with closed, dark, unclear, enigmatic, and allegorical language, the language of parables. Both translations are possible. The first indicates that Christ is not here speaking in the form of parables but is teaching openly and manifestly. The second indicates that Christ here does not construct His teaching in such a way as to contain various immediate and additional meanings, as parables do.

John XVI, 27 — 'αὐτὸς γὰρ ὁ πατὴρ φιλεῖ ὑμᾶς, ὅτι ὑμεῖς ἐμὲ πεφιλήκατε καὶ πεπιστεύκατε ὅτι ἐγὼ παρὰ τοῦ θεοῦ ἐξῆλθον' — 'autos gar ho pater philei hymas, hoti hymeis eme pephilekate kai pepisteukate hoti ego para tou theou exelthon' — for the Father Himself loves you, because you have loved Me and have believed that I came out from beside God (from God). Here is the first of the three verses I draw particular attention to today. Both the preposition 'para' and the further prefix of the verb 'ex-' ('ek-') are present. As I wrote at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/15052026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/16052026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/24052026.html
concerning John X, 18, 32, XVII, 7 — here this points not to the human nature of Christ but to the entire Most Holy Trinity. The Father and the Son are mentioned directly and explicitly, whilst the Holy Spirit is present implicitly through the use of the preposition 'para' with the genitive case and of the verb with the prefix 'ex-' ('ek-'), analogous to that also used concerning the procession of the Holy Spirit from the Father. This indicates that Christ trusts in the disciples — trusts in their faith — that He is the Son of God and not merely a human being. The disciples, however, then show their incomplete understanding. One may say that Christ speaks of the entire Most Holy Trinity, of the entire divine design, of the fullness — whilst the disciples at this very moment understand only partially. Why then does Christ speak the truth? Because the love of the disciples bestows upon them the possibility of remaining with Christ, with God — of remaining direct participants in the divine design, even though they themselves do not yet fully see, understand, or comprehend it. But Christ accepts their imperfection and leads them towards knowledge of God, of truth, and of the divine design. Concerning love I shall write separately below.
John XVI, 28 — 'ἐξῆλθον ἐκ τοῦ πατρὸς καὶ ἐλήλυθα εἰς τὸν κόσμον' — 'exelthon ek tou patros kai alelytha eis ton kosmon' - I came out from the Father and have come into the world. The typical pair of prepositions 'ek'-'eis' speaks here of movement from whence and whither — from the Father into the world (the meaning for the world is also present, but recedes to the background owing to the grammar of the prepositional pair) — that is, what is spoken of here is the Incarnation as a component of the divine design for the salvation of people and for the Kingdom of God.
'πάλιν ἀφίημι τὸν κόσμον καὶ πορεύομαι πρὸς τὸν πατέρα' — 'palin aphiemi ton kosmon kai poreuomai pros ton patera' - again I am leaving the world and I am going to the Father. The preposition 'pros' here with the accusative indicates movement towards the closest of relationships, towards the greatest closeness. Indeed God's love becomes manifest in the matter of the sufferings, death, Resurrection, and Ascension of Jesus Christ, and also the coming Kingdom of God. The love of the Father for the Son does not become greater (being perfect and full) but overflows upon the faithful through Christ. John XVI, 29 — 'ἐν παρρησίᾳ' — 'en parresia' - freely; openly; manifestly; confidently; with full utterance. Various translations are possible. The apostles mean that they now understand Christ better in general — but in reality their understanding is still partial and incomplete. The Lord receives them with their imperfect understanding.

John XVI, 30 — 'νῦν οἴδαμεν ὅτι οἶδας πάντα καὶ οὐ χρείαν ἔχεις ἵνα τίς σε ἐρωτᾷ· ἐν τούτῳ πιστεύομεν ὅτι ἀπὸ θεοῦ ἐξῆλθες' — 'nyn oidamen hoti oidas panta kai ou khreian ekheis hina tis se erota en touto pisteuomen hoti apo theou exelthes' - now we know that You know all things and have no need for anyone to question you. Therefore we believe that You came out from God. The disciples use here a different preposition — 'apo' — concerning Jesus and His relationship with the Father. Here this has many meanings. In particular it means:
1) Jesus received something from the Father and is now separately revealing it to the disciples;
2) this Coming of Christ from the Father is a divine gift — in particular for the apostles themselves;
3) the apostles here do not understand the Trinity, the divine design, or all that Christ says — they understand only what He says directly and openly (the very fact of directness and openness), and also they see that He knows their desire to question Him about plans that they understood in human terms as a possible seizure of power in Jerusalem and beyond (Messiah-Christ as an earthly ruler).
Here the preposition 'apo' appears with many meanings. In particular it emphasises the leaving of the Father by Jesus, the gift to the apostles, the visible cause that is not the primary one (see more at the links:

This passage is, in a significant measure, the summit of the difficulty of human understanding of God — whilst at the same time being the summit of God's long-suffering and love to the end.

John XVI, 32 — 'εἰς τὰ ἴδια' — 'eis ta idia' - to their own. That is, each will scatter to their private interests — losing the unity of the disciples, in effect ceasing to be disciples, even though the Lord did not deprive them of that gift.

John XVI, 33 — 'ἐν ἐμοὶ' — 'en emoi' - in Me. In Whom. 'ἐν τῷ κόσμῳ' - 'en to kosmo' - in the world. Where.

And here I shall write further about love. Christ uses the word 'philei' here — which is used earlier concerning the love of the Father for the Son, and also has important significance for the forgiveness of Peter. It is evident that the other disciples (it is unclear concerning John the Theologian, who nonetheless did not leave Christ and was present at the cross) — as also Peter — loved Christ in this way.
