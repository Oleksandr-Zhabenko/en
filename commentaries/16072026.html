Liturgy:
(1 Corinthians VII, 12-24)
1 Corinthians VII, 14 — 'ἐν τῇ γυναικί' — 'en te gynaiki' - in the wife. The true and Only Source of sanctification is God through the grace He provides, but this grace can operate jointly with someone, particularly with a wife or a husband, which is what is spoken of here. 'ἐν τῷ ἀδελφῷ' - 'en to adelpho' - in the brother (meaning 'a brother in Christ for the readers or listeners', and this is the husband for the wife herself). Analogously to the wife just above.

1 Corinthians VII, 15 — 'ἐν τοῖς τοιούτοις' — 'en tois toioutois' - in such cases. A set expression. When, under what circumstances. 'ἐν δὲ εἰρήνῃ' - 'en de eirene' - but in peace. God calls people to Himself not by fighting them, but by peace as His gift to people.

1 Corinthians VII, 17 — 'ἐν ταῖς ἐκκλησίαις πάσαις' — 'en tais ekklesiais pasais' - in all the Churches. Where.

1 Corinthians VII, 18 — 'ἐν ἀκροβυστίᾳ' — 'en akrobystia' - an expression meaning being uncircumcised.

1 Corinthians VII, 20 — 'ἕκαστος ἐν τῇ κλήσει ᾗ ἐκλήθη ἐν ταύτῃ μενέτω' — 'hekastos en te klesei he eklethe en taute meneto' - literally let each one remain in that same calling in which he was called.

1 Corinthians VII, 21 — 'δοῦλος ἐκλήθης; μή σοι μελέτω· ἀλλ’ εἰ καὶ δύνασαι ἐλεύθερος γενέσθαι, μᾶλλον χρῆσαι' — 'doulos eklethes? me soi meleto all ei kai dynasai eleutheros genesthai, mallon khresai' - Were you called as a slave? Do not worry about it; but if you can also become free, rather use [it]. That is, one should not worry about whether this state hinders the action of grace — and precisely on this account. That is, no, it does not hinder, for further comes the explanation of why it does not hinder — calling is a great action of grace; if the state of slavery did not prevent it, then slavery itself will not prevent grace from operating in the life of a male or female slave. God operates where He wishes. At the same time, right here Paul continues his thought further, looking at the situation and slavery through the eyes of God, of Christ. Christ Himself, by virtue of the infirmity of the listeners, did not speak about this as Paul writes here, but now, by the action of the Holy Spirit, Paul witnesses on behalf of Christ. Here Paul (the Holy Spirit through him, Christ through him) compares human slavery and the state of freedom among people and advises to become free people. This has advantages, meaning it is better. The very posing of the question, although not very frequent, speaks of the apostle's (Christ's) non-indifference to the issue of slavery and social order. What does you have the power (ability) mean? That is, if it is within your real capabilities that do not require something extraordinary. For example, if the master of a male or female slave releases them, then they can be free (and Paul says to do so, to become free). Or if there is an opportunity, by performing a certain task, to become free as a reward — analogously. At the same time, a violent way of deliverance from slavery is denied — the apostle does not say to start a rebellion to get rid of slavery. A person is strong when they do what is in their power. In the next two verses, an explanation is given as to why Christ (God) relates to this so. See also the detailed analysis at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/14072026.html

1 Corinthians VII, 22 — 'ὁ γὰр ἐν κυρίῳ κληθεὶς δοῦλος ἀπελεύθερος κυρίου ἐστίν· ὁμοίως ὁ ἐλεύθερος κληθεὶς δοῦλός ἐστιν Χριστοῦ' — 'ho gar en kyrio kletheis doulos apeleutheros kyriou estin, homoios ho eleutheros kletheis doulos estin CHristou' - for he who is called in the Lord as a slave is a freedman of the Lord; likewise he who is called as a free man is a slave of Christ. At first glance, it concerns that God looks at people differently than people, than society. But in reality, it concerns that when the Lord operates by grace, it grants freedom to a person, makes them completely free. Someone will note that it concerns a change of personal belonging, devotion — before conversion to Christ, a slave of people depends on these people personally; conversion frees him (her) in Christ, while at the same time they begin to diligently serve Christ. But in reality, it concerns devotion to Christ — the more a person is devoted to Christ, the less slavery can harm them spiritually. Someone will say that it is only about a change of the form of human bondage. But this is not so. Slaves of Christ are not actually slaves, but followers, disciples, brothers and sisters of Christ, friends, children of God. A person does not seek their own (often sinful) will, but seeks God's will; they accept it as their own. This only externally looks like bondage, but in reality, it is liberation and realization of who a person really is and can become. This grants not only present benefits, but also future ones, as well as God's mercy, bounties, glory, the dignity of God's beloved children, the joy of resurrection and victory, and much else, etc. I am the Lord Who brought you out of the house of slavery!, says Christ, therefore, Obey Me as a free person!.

1 Corinthians VII, 23 — 'τιμῆς ἠγοράσθητε· μὴ γίνεσθε δοῦλοι ἀνθρώπων' — 'times egorasthete me ginesthe douloi anthropon' - You were bought with a price; do not become slaves of men. Here is the continuation of the explanation of why slavery is not Christian, why slave-owning should be abolished by Christians, why slavery to people can harm Christian life. This completes the explanation of why it is better to be free.

1 Corinthians VII, 24 — 'ἐν ᾧ ἐκλήθη, ἀδελφοί, ἐν τούτῳ μενέτω παρὰ θεῷ' — 'en o eklethe, adelphoi, en touto meneto para theo' - In what [state] he was called, brethren, in that let him remain in the vicinity of God. The preposition 'para' here is with the dative case and indicates here that if God called a person in a certain state that is not a sin, then this state is not sinful, does not contradict God's will, and does not harm closeness to God or holiness.

The reading consists of two parts — the first speaks of peace in marriage — through peace and agreement in good, even unbelieving people can become believers, and children can grow up so as to believe and be good faithful.

The second part concerns the state of a person in life with God. These reflections can be generalized to almost any case regarding how a person should relate to various issues.

It must be said that a literally exact translation gives approximately: more, in a greater degree use or another variant — better use — in both cases the first word is an adverb, that is, a characteristic of action.

One can see that the perspective of faith gives a person more internal freedom and flexibility — they can see measures differently. Flexibility allows focusing more on life with God.

Therefore, one can generally paraphrase this part so that Be flexible where there is no need to be principled. It is principled to fulfill God's commandments. But if something is not directly connected with their fulfillment or violation, then a person in Christ receives immense actual freedom.

Some researchers have already noted that such flexibility (which they may call by other words) served well the development of modern science and culture.

(1 Corinthians VII, 24-35)
1 Corinthians VII, 24 — 'ἐν ᾧ ἐκλήθη, ἀδελφοί, ἐν τούτῳ μενέτω παρὰ θεῷ' — 'en o eklethe, adelphoi, en touto meneto para theo' - In what [state] he was called, brethren, in that let him remain in the vicinity of God. The preposition 'para' here is with the dative case and indicates here that if God called a person in a certain state that is not a sin, then this state is not sinful, does not contradict God's will, and does not harm closeness to God or holiness. See also the extensive explanation above regarding yesterday's reading in detail.
Much like a temple not only does not prevent a person from being with God, but is called to such being. If the calling of a person by God in a certain state became possible and actual, then this state can remind of a temple, with which the state has the just-mentioned similarity.

Further, Paul, as earlier in the Epistle, gives advice as one who has obtained mercy to be faithful. See about that at the link:

1 Corinthians VII, 25 — 'ὡς ἠλεημένος ὑπὸ κυρίου πιστὸς εἶναι' — 'hos eleemenos hypo kyriou pistos einai' - as one who has obtained mercy from the Lord to be faithful. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates the passive voice of the participle has obtained mercy, it also emphasizes God's majesty and Paul's humility.

1 Corinthians VII, 26 — 'λέλυσαι ἀπὸ γυναικός' — 'lelysai apo gynaikos' - Are you loosed from a wife?. The preposition 'apo' indicates here onto complete loosing, that is, divorce, complete loss of connection. See more about that at the links:

In the reading, Paul speaks about psychology.

Since people can live, love, think, marry, and do other things differently, Paul's reflections can be both consonant and close to every person in Christ, or quite uncharacteristic. If a person lived before marriage completely and fully desiring to please only God, and married so that pleasing God and pleasing the wife / husband now distracts, then Paul's words are precisely about that. If, however, a person received the marriage itself from God as a blessing (much like, for example, it was with the Old Testament Isaac, Tobit, Moses, etc.), then marriage will not only not be an obstacle, but will also contribute to pleasing God. After all, if we speak of the integrity and undividedness of a person, the best example is… the Virgin Mary... Who was... married. And this did not prevent Her from entirely pleasing God. Therefore, Paul warns, teaches, instructs, but his words do not necessarily have to take place in people's lives. One can also say that this is a certain experience of spiritual life, but it may differ in others.

(Matthew XIV, 35-XV, 11)
Matthew XIV, 35 — 'εἰς ὅλην τὴν περίχωρον' — 'eis holen ten perikhoron' - into all that surrounding region. The use of the preposition 'eis' regarding the spread of information indicates the movement of information flows.

Matthew XIV, 35 — 'ἀπὸ Ἱεροσολύμων' — 'apo Ierosolymon' - from Jerusalem. The preposition 'apo' emphasizes here that the Pharisees and scribes went outside the city, left Jerusalem. There is a certain symbolism in this, if we remember that Jerusalem is called the city of peace.

Matthew XV, 5 — 'ἐξ ἐμοῦ' — 'ex emou' - from me. The preposition 'ex' (a derived form of 'ek' before a subsequent vowel) indicates the attachment of a person to the material, which they do not want to give.

Matthew XV, 8 — 'ἀπ’ ἐμοῦ' — 'ap emou' - from Me. The preposition 'ap' as a form of 'apo' before a subsequent vowel indicates the distance and estrangement of such people from God because of their unrepentance and sinfulness.

Matthew XV, 11 — 'εἰς τὸ στόμα' — 'ἐκ τοῦ στόματος' - 'eis to stoma' - 'ek tou stomatos' - into the mouth - out of the mouth. An antonymic pair of prepositions in this context, which emphasizes the thought.

The question of the Pharisees puts a hygiene rule on the level of God's commandments. I wrote earlier that this is fundamentally wrong and freezes any state in the course of development.

Christ therefore explains their error with an example from another sphere. He adduces an example where cunning manipulations in thoughts give a person permission not to fulfill God's commandment (in the opinion of the Pharisees, which, of course, in no way gives such permission in reality). Researchers note that it concerns that among the Pharisees, it was considered the norm that it was enough only mentally to gift something to the temple — and these things were already considered a gift to God. They could still be used by the owners, but they could not be given to anyone else, because it is already God's, and not theirs.

There was nothing like this in the Law, so the example shows how, by putting on the same level some less important things and God's commandments, one can arrive at the actual violation of these very commandments.

Matthew XV, 4 — the translation speaks evil of can be more precisely translated as bitterly insults with words. And the continuation — let him die the death. That is, this is not a wish to such a person, but a testimony to the fact that they are on the path of destruction, the end of which is death, and of which therefore one should beware.

Further, Christ explains what defiles a person. Christ means that defilement is a spiritual concept, not a physical one. It is not matter itself that defiles a person, but evil thoughts and sins.

Therefore, the Lord draws attention to such questions so that people can live a pure and godly life.

(Matthew XV, 12-21)
Matthew XV, 14 — 'εἰς βόθυνον' — 'eis bathynon' - into a pit. Where to.

Matthew XV, 17 — 'εἰς τὸ στόμα εἰς τὴν κοιλίαν χωρεῖ καὶ εἰς ἀφεδρῶνα' — 'eis to stoma eis ten koilian khorei kai eis aphedrona' - into the mouth, goes into the stomach, and is cast out into the draught. Here is a description of the biological process of the movement of food and drink through the digestive system without additional details. Everywhere here is the direction of movement, which does not linger anywhere.

Matthew XV, 18 — 'ἐκ τοῦ στόματος' — 'ek tou stomatos' - out of the mouth. 'ἐκ τῆς καρδίας' - 'ek tes kardias' - out of the heart. The preposition 'ek' indicates the source of origin, wherefrom?.

Matthew XV, 19 — 'ἐκ γὰρ τῆς καρδίας' — 'ek gar tes kardias' - for out of the heart. Wherefrom, the heart here is as a source (the same thought is, in particular, in the Proverbs of Solomon, but less explicitly and directly).

Matthew XV, 21 — 'εἰς τὰ μέρη Τύρου καὶ Σιδῶνος' — 'eis ta mere Tyrou kai Sidonos' - into the regions of Tyre and Sidon. Where to.

Between the parable and its explanation, it is spoken about the Pharisees. Christ's words regarding them show the state of the Pharisees from different sides — both their blindness and lack of understanding, and their conviction of their correctness, and the desire to lead others, while they do not see the essence of the matter. Regarding the pit — a physical example — and quite eloquent — first, logically, the blind guide, the leader, must fall into the pit, and then the one led by such a leader will also fall into the same pit. These words of Jesus were fulfilled, in particular, when the Pharisees came forward to crucify Him.

Regarding plants — the Lord means that the truth must not be stopped by someone's disagreement with it. At least in the Kingdom of God, the truth will be manifest to everyone, so there is no sense in compromising it for the sake of someone's (erroneous) opinion. For example, scientists constantly face this issue — new knowledge to some extent changes previous ideas, which have to be abandoned. If not, there is no sense in science itself as such.

Regarding the origin of sins from the heart of a person — here is meant the core of the personality, which manifests who a person is as such. This makes a person the source of what comes from within themselves, from their heart. This capability — to be such a source, such a cause — is directly connected with freedom. See more at the links:
