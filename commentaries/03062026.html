Liturgy:
(Romans I, 18-27)
Romans I, 18 — 'ἀπ' οὐρανοῦ' — 'ap ouranou' - from heaven. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that the wrath of God points in particular to the fact that a person is living in a manner entirely unworthy of heaven — which is why the person does not live in heaven, and why God's justice descends to people; but through their rootedness in impenitence, people receive it as God's wrath.. 'ἐπὶ πᾶσαν ἀσέβειαν καὶ ἀδικίαν ἀνθρώπων τῶν τὴν ἀλήθειαν ἐν ἀδικίᾳ κατεχόντων' - 'epi pasan asebeian kai adikian anthropon ton ten aletheian en adikia katekhonton' - upon all ungodliness and unrighteousness of people who hold down (suppress) the truth in unrighteousness (through unrighteousness). Various translations are possible. By truth what is meant is conformity to God's design, and also the truths of faith; hold down (suppress) means that those people, existing through God's truth as God's creatures, nonetheless distance themselves from and resist God's will and God's truth through their impenitent unrighteousness — on account of which even other people (or those same people) begin to blame God for the consequences of sins and unrighteousness.

Romans I, 19 — 'διότι τὸ γνωστὸν τοῦ θεοῦ φανερόν ἐστιν ἐν αὐτοῖς· ὁ θεὸς γὰρ αὐτοῖς ἐφανέρωσεν' — 'dioti to gnoston tou theou phaneron estin en autois ho theos gar autois ephanerosen' - because that which is knowable of God is manifested in them, for God has made it manifested to them. Here are two important assertions: people know about the divine and about God, because God has revealed and manifested it — this is God's revelation (it is impossible to know something about God or the divine without God's revelation, disclosure, uncovering, manifestation) — and also that what is spoken of is not something secret, mysterious, incomprehensible, or beyond understanding, but rather something amenable to logic, to human reflection, understanding, reception, and experience — that is, it is manifest, not hidden.

Romans I, 20 — 'τὰ γὰρ ἀόρατα αὐτοῦ ἀπὸ κτίσεως κόσμου τοῖς ποιήμασιν νοούμενα καθορᾶται, ἥ τε ἀΐδιος αὐτοῦ δύναμις καὶ θειότης, εἰς τὸ εἶναι αὐτοὺς ἀναπολογήτους' — 'ta gar aorata autou apo ktiseos kosmou tois poiemasin nooumena kathoratai, he te aidios autou dynamis kai theiotes, eis to einai autous anapologetous' - for His invisible things from the creation of the world, being knowable (such as can be understood, known, where one may exercise one's reason and thinking) through what is made, are clearly (plainly, distinctly) visible (perceived) — both His eternal power and Godhead — so that they are without excuse. What is meant is that God has manifested to people His invisible designs and will, His power, and has revealed Himself (this concerns in particular the Old Testament divine manifestations, but one may say the same of the New Testament ones), testifying thereby what is good and what is not — and now people cannot say they simply did not know, and are therefore innocent. It must be understood that the existence of divine mysteries and the Mysteries does not prevent people from recognising and coming to know what God has openly revealed and made accessible to understanding.

Romans I, 21 — 'ἀλλ' ἐματαιώθησαν ἐν τοῖς διαλογισμοῖς αὐτῶν' — 'all emataiothesan en tois dialogismois auton' - but they became futile (worthless, foolish, wrong, and so on) in their reasonings (doubts, conversations, imaginings, disputes, debates); but they became futile through their doubts. That is, instead of rising upward towards God and a high calling, they began to fall downward and to decay.

Romans I, 23 — 'ἤλλαξαν τὴν δόξαν τοῦ ἀφθάρτου θεοῦ ἐν ὁμοιώματι εἰκόνος φθαρτοῦ ἀνθρώπου καὶ πετεινῶν καὶ τετραπόδων καὶ ἑρπετῶν' — 'ellaxan ten doxan tou aphthartou theou en omoiomati eikonos phthartou anthropou kai peteinon kai tetrapodon kai herpeton' - they exchanged the glory of the incorruptible God for a likeness in the image of corruptible man, and of birds, and of four-footed animals, and of reptiles. Here two different understandings are possible, leading to different interpretations. The first consists in linking the human being in this verse with the subsequent creatures in the list — in which case what is spoken of is idolatry, in that people, instead of the true God, began to worship corruptible people and animals, ascribing divine glory to them. The second consists in linking the human being with God and the beginning of the phrase, whilst the rest of the verse does not carry this connection (this understanding is suggested by the difference in number — the human being is in the singular whilst the other creatures are in the plural, even though the pagans venerated many gods in human form). In that case what is spoken of is the fact that the human being is created truly as a likeness of the image of God — though being corruptible and, after sin, subject to sufferings, illnesses, and death — whilst the animals further experienced the influence of the human fall. This second understanding is generally in much better consonance with the entire Epistle thematically, and is therefore better and fuller than the first — though both carry meaning here.

Romans I, 24 — 'ἐν ταῖς ἐπιθυμίαις τῶν καρδιῶν αὐτῶν' — 'en tais epithymiais ton kardion auton' - in the lusts of their hearts. Only this translation here. 'εἰς ἀκαθαρσίαν' - 'eis akatharsian' - into uncleanness; into impurity. Whither. That is, God permitted people who had turned away from true life and true worship to fall into the lusts of their hearts and further — into bodily and spiritual uncleanness. 'τοῦ ἀτιμάζεσθαι τὰ σώματα αὐτῶν ἐν αὐτοῖς' - 'tou atimazesthai ta somata auton en autois' - in the dishonour (disgrace) of their bodies among themselves (by themselves). That is, the path of unbelief led people all the way to the dishonour and disgrace of their own bodies.

Romans I, 25 — 'ἐν τῷ ψεύδει' — 'en to pseudei' - in falsehood; for falsehood. That is, they exchanged God's truth for delusion. 'ἐλάτρευσαν τῇ κτίσει παρὰ τὸν κτίσαντα' - 'elatreusan te ktisei para ton ktisanta' - they worshipped (religiously, as God) the creature alongside the Creator; they worshipped the creature in a manner like the Creator; they worshipped the creature as though the Creator; they worshipped the creature as one worships (as one ought to worship) the Creator. A prepositional phrase of very manifold meaning. The preposition 'para' here with the accusative indicates that the creature is elevated to one degree or another to the same level as God the Creator. Moreover, the apostle may also be pointing to the fact that devotion to one's religion or faith (the latter may be more relevant in the world today, since idolatry can take various forms and not only the literal one) may be no less than the devotion owed to the true God. 'ὅς ἐστιν εὐλογητὸς εἰς τοὺς αἰῶνας· ἀμήν' - 'hos estin eulogetos eis tous aionas. amen' - who is blessed for ever. Amen. The preposition 'eis' forms part of a common expression.

Romans I, 26 — 'εἰς πάθη ἀτιμίας' — 'eis pathe atimias' - into dishonourable passions (which have no honour, respect, or value — shameful ones). 'φυσικὴν χρῆσιν εἰς τὴν παρὰ φύσιν' - 'physiken khresin eis ten para physin' - the natural use into that which is to the vicinity of nature; the natural use into that which is alongside nature. The preposition 'eis' indicates here what they exchanged it for. The preposition 'para' here with the accusative has many different meanings. A common translation is against nature (possible in particular on the grounds of the Gospel, where it is used in the same construction with this meaning of contrast — and the venerable Maximus the Confessor is also mentioned here in connection with this). One may understand that the second translation variant appears more precise in the sense that people replaced the natural use of sexual union with a person of the opposite sex with what is connected (in this sense alongside) with nature — which is also a sexual union with a person — but has no connection with the nature of people and the character of these relationships.

Romans I, 27 — 'ἐξεκαύθησαν ἐν τῇ ὀρέξει αὐτῶν εἰς ἀλλήλους' — 'exekauthesan en te orexei auton eis allelous' - they were deeply inflamed in their desire (excitement) of mind towards one another. 'ἄρρενες ἐν ἄρρεσιν' - 'arrenes en arresin' - men in men. What is spoken of is directly sexual relations. 'ἐν ἑαυτοῖς' - 'en heautois' - in themselves; through themselves. Here the verse concerns physiology — a description (here of male, but one may think similarly of female) homosexuality.

Romans I, 21 — 'εματαιωθησαν' — 'emataiothesan' — from 'ματαιοω' — 'mataioo' — to be vain, foolish, empty.

Romans I, 22 — 'εμωρανθησαν' — 'emoranthesan' — from 'μωραινω' — 'moraino' — to become foolish; to become insipid; to lose flavour.

1 Corinthians I, 20 — 'εμωρανεν' — 'emoranen' — from 'μωρος' — 'moros' — 'foolish; stale, insipid; one who has lost flavour; credulous; trusting, naïve'. That is, human wisdom before God 'has lost its flavour' — people have become 'credulous' (believing what is easy to believe). God's faith is different.

In general one may say that the knowledge of God is here effectively called coming out of idolatry, and its absence — when it is possible and expected — is the cause in particular of active homosexuality. One must not understand the knowledge of God exclusively and solely in relation to God. For this knowledge itself takes place in particular through the knowledge of creation; and the relationships that the apostle cites as an example of the darkening of the mind (of some — and at that time, in those days, quite many) pagans are called contrary to nature (that is, their erroneousness is visible not only in view of God's commandment and will, but also in view of created nature). The cause of active passionate homosexuality is in fact that people, instead of striving towards God, devoted themselves to the veneration of creation — instead of ardent love for God they gave themselves over to a tepid veneration of creatures and animals. Therefore passion received a wrong direction and realisation, whilst the mind did not hinder it in this.

One may say that if a person truly loves God with the whole heart, they will be able to value the natural union rather than the unnatural one, and will be able to overcome possible inclinations. This is similar to the struggle against pests in a garden or a field. They may be present, but this must not prevent a good harvest.

If one speaks of sexual relations directly, this passage implies that desire may arise from good relationships and not a priori — that is, the value of God's marriage and of the relationships within it is to a considerable degree progressive: it gradually reveals itself and does not necessarily have to be extraordinarily palpable from the beginning. The apostle is thinking of the natural union in marriage, which largely evokes desire and love not through passion and infatuation but through cherishing and the gift of love — which presupposes effort in this direction. From the standpoint of the apostle Paul, homosexuals see the value of their practice primarily in passion rather than in overall value; and the passion itself originates from the actions of the person rather than from a natural inclination according to God's design. The person takes natural inclinations and, through the darkening of their mind, directs them towards a passion contrary to nature. At the same time the apostle Paul does not say that people do this consciously, but says that they were given over — that is, this is a consequence, permitted by God, of their distancing from Him.

Concerning divine Revelation I shall say that it is necessary for knowing God. But what is important is that God has granted it to people.

It is important to emphasise that there is a certain distance between ceasing to seek God and giving oneself over to passions and sins. Thus not all who ceased to seek God immediately became gravely sinful — but on the other hand, all such people came under the influence of this tendency towards decline.

Paul means that even when it may be difficult for a person to discern God's action in what is in their life, they are nonetheless entirely free to seek that action, and nothing must stand in the way of this. And this must be done by each person.

(Matthew V, 20-26)
Matthew V, 20 — 'εἰς τὴν βασιλείαν τῶν οὐρανῶν' — 'eis ten basileian ton ouranon' - into the Kingdom of God. Whither. The expression in the Gospels has several meanings — in particular already in this life, now, to live according to God's will, in the manner in which the saved will live in the Kingdom of God.

Matthew V, 22 — 'ἔνοχος ἔσται εἰς τὴν γέενναν τοῦ πυρός' — 'enokhos estai eis ten geennan tou pyros' - is liable (guilty and is in danger of) the Gehenna of fire. That is, moving whither — into the Gehenna of fire. I wrote earlier — I shall cite now:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/07062023.html
Anger makes a person a participant in God's judgement (but in no way a judge there!); insulting someone makes them subject to God's judgement or the Highest Judgement (in the Church this is the judgement of an Ecumenical Council); proud contempt makes the proud person spiritually dead. The degrees of falling into the sin of malice, anger, condemnation, insult, harassment, bullying, and contempt are clearly visible. Many translations add the word without cause, but in the original it is absent.

Matthew V, 23-24 — contain no prepositions that I examine, but are important. In particular, important as a testimony that the absence of reconciliation with a person is an obstacle to Communion. Yes, it is good to receive the Holy Gifts and Mysteries of the Body and Blood of Christ, and it is His commandment — but if a person is not at peace with brothers and sisters in faith, and if the absence of peace is not occasioned by the necessity of fulfilling God's commandments and God's will (that is, when a person is not actually guilty of what a brother or sister accuses them of), then such lack of peace is a Gospel obstacle for the clergy to the celebration of the Eucharist, and for the faithful — an obstacle to Communion (and if read more literally — even to participation in the Liturgy of the faithful).

Matthew V, 25 — 'ἐν τῇ ὁδῷ' — 'en te hodo' - on the road; along the way. Where. 'εἰς φυλακὴν' - 'eis phylaken' - into prison; to prison. Whither.

Someone will say: How then is one to struggle against others' sins? One may say: as God struggles — righteously. Not to sin oneself and to be a good example in this. If you see that someone is genuinely insulting another, do not insult — but do good, which may also mean: protect. In a similar way soldiers and defenders — in particular in Ukraine — do good when they protect peaceful people from the attack of invaders. But if rage, proud contempt, and the like were to seize the defenders, they would be guilty according to the words of the Gospel.

Hence the need to be at peace wherever possible — as far as it depends on you, as Paul says in the Epistles.
