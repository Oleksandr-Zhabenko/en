Liturgy:
(Romans II, 10-16)
Romans II, 11 — 'Οὐ γάρ ἐστιν προσωπολημψία παрὰ τῷ θεῷ' — 'Ou gar estin prosopolempsia para to theo' - for there is no respect of persons in the vicinity of God. The preposition 'para' here is with the dative case and indicates here that drawing near to God diminishes the differences between various social statuses, wherefore it can be said that the Lord Himself does not at all look at them in relationships with people. But on the contrary, He turns His attention onto the person themselves, and not onto what people think or say about them.

Romans II, 12 — 'ἐν νόμῳ' — 'en nomo' - in Law. That is, accepting the Law of Moses and trying to keep it — here it is strictly about the internal convictions of a person, their conscience, and not about belonging to a certain religious or political group. See verse 11.

Romans II, 13 — 'παρὰ τῷ θεῷ' — 'para to theo' - in the vicinity of God. The preposition 'para' here is with the dative case and indicates here that while a person who hears the Law of Moses can evoke different attitudes among people, have a different status, yet near God, the mere hearing of the Law is insufficient for righteousness, but it is necessary to fulfil it, to do it (as poets write poems), so as to become more righteous, to act more justly, to go towards justification.

Romans II, 15 — 'τὸ ἔργον τοῦ νόμου γραπτὸν ἐν ταῖς καρδίαις αὐτῶν' — 'to ergon tou nomou grapton en tais kardiais auton' — the work of the Law written in the hearts of them. Where.

Romans II, 16 — 'ἐν ἡμέρᾳ' — 'en hemera' — in a day. When.

God is Righteous, and righteous people gladden Him. At the same time, God does not look upon the face of a person, but onto the heart, onto the works, onto the person themselves, and not onto their status or position in society, origin, and other similar characteristics.

Also about this, already in application to people themselves, writes the apostle James:

Also I quote:

In Romans II, 15 we have: 'τῆς συνειδήσεως καὶ μεταξὺ ἀλλήλων τῶν λογισμῶν κατηγορούντων ἢ καὶ ἀπολογουμένων' — 'tes syneideseos kai metaxy allelon ton logismon kategorounton e kai apologoumenon' — conscience (another translation might be 'consciousness') and the reasonings (thoughts) that among themselves (in the process) at one time accuse, at another time defend one another. What is spoken of is thoughts specifically — as the grammatical agreement in gender (masculine in Koine, confirmed also by Google Gemini Flash-3.5) attests.

The first thing worth noting: the apostle Paul says that with regard to ethics, conscience, and reasonings — the accusation or defence concerns the reasonings themselves and not the people. That is, thoughts and reasonings judge and defend not people but one another. This is similar to the classic model of sandboxing, where security is achieved by working not with the original but with something that represents it. Thoughts and conscience represent people — but to accuse or defend them is not at all the same as accusing or defending people.

Secondly, the apostle here on the one hand praises Gentiles' ethics and philosophy when they lead to the same conclusions as God's Law. But on the other hand, the very mechanism by which this occurs — through contradiction, accusation, and defence — points to the fragility of such a state, even though it is praiseworthy here.

Thirdly, what the apostle praises here is not so much the result as the process of moral actions in the Gentiles. That is, a person does not merely perform moral actions but acquires moral experience, which is comparably as valuable as the actions themselves.

(Matthew IV, 18-23)
Matthew IV, 18 — 'παρὰ τὴν θάλασσαν τῆς Γαλιλαίας' — 'para ten thalassan tes Galilaias' - to the proximity with the sea of Galilee. The preposition 'para' here is with the accusative and indicates drawing near to the shore, that is, here coming near to the shore. 'εἰς τὴν θάλασσαν' - 'eis ten thalassan' - into the sea. Where to.

Matthew IV, 21 — 'ἐν τῷ πλοίῳ' — 'en to ploio' - in the boat; in the ship; on the ship. Where.

Matthew IV, 23 — 'ἐν ὅλῃ τῇ Γαλιλαίᾳ' — 'en hole te Galilaia' - in all Galilee; throughout all Galilee. Where. 'ἐν ταῖς συναγωγαῖς αὐτῶν' - 'en tais synagogais auton' - in the synagogues of them. Where. 'ἐν τῷ λαῷ' - 'en to lao' - in the person [people]. Where. Here is the singular, which conveys two meanings — first, that the Lord healed people, was turned towards people, sought the salvation and healing of people, and not something else; second — that Christ also by Himself, by His Incarnation saves the person, restores human nature, and healing is a sign of such salvation.

The second calling of the disciples of Jesus. About the first one John the Theologian writes in the Gospel in chapter I. And about the final one — the Synoptics, describing the calling of these same fishermen-apostles after the miraculous catch of fish.

The trust of the disciples in Jesus, Whom they already know, is important.
