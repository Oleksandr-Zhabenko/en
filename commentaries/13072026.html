Liturgy:
(1 Corinthians V, 9-VI, 11)
1 Corinthians V, 9, 10 — 'ἐν τῇ ἐπιστολῇ' — 'en te epistole' - in the Epistle; with (by means of) letter. 'πόρνοις' - 'pornois' - to fornicators. This word originates from the one used by Christ when speaking about the possibility of divorce. It differs from adultery. See more and in detail at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/04062026.html

1 Corinthians V, 10 — 'ἐκ τοῦ κόσμου' — 'ek tou kosmou' - out of the world; from the world. The preposition 'ek' indicates the initial location — in the world, in the midst of the world and the exit therefrom.

1 Corinthians V, 13 — 'τοὺς δὲ ἔξω' — 'tous de exo' - those who are outside; but the outsiders. 'ἐξ ὑμῶν' - 'ex hymon' - out of [from among] you. Here is a certain thought in the similarity of words — after separation from the community of the faithful, a person becomes like the outsiders, i.e., unbelievers. Such an action is a call to repentance for unrepentant sinners among Christians. See also at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/10072026.html

1 Corinthians VI, 2, 5 — 'ἐν ὑμῖн' — 'en hymin' - in you; among you. Where, among whom.

1 Corinthians VI, 4 — 'ἐν τῇ ἐκκλησίᾳ' — 'en te ekklesia' - in the Church. Where.

1 Corinthians VI, 11 — 'ἐν τῷ ὀνόμαти τοῦ κυρίου Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ' — 'en to onomati tou kyriou Iesou Christou' - in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ. It indicates that life in Christ makes a person righteous. 'ἐν τῷ πνεύματι τοῦ θεοῦ ἡμῶν' - 'en to pneumati tou theou hemon' - in the Spirit of our God. Analogously to the previous, concerning the Holy Spirit.

Paul speaks about the relationships between Christians within the community, emphasizing that God judges those who are outside.

He reproaches for going to law before the Gentiles, because then the sinful Gentiles get the impression that Christians are not only no better than these Gentiles in virtue, but even worse, since they need the judgment of (sinful) Gentiles (to judge and sort things out). Actually, it is about the humiliation of Christians through such behavior before the Gentiles themselves, which not all of the Corinthians realized.

Further, Paul says that the faithful (saints) will judge the world (and even angels). This is the only place in Scripture where this is spoken of, so it remains mysterious. Mostly, it is thought that the saints, by their virtues and example of life, will reveal the sins of people at the Judgment of God, thereby becoming participants in the Judgment of God as witnesses (and not actually as judges). At the same time, there is a promise of Jesus to the 12 apostles to judge Israel. This, again, is understood as that the words, deeds, and virtues of the apostles will reveal the good and the bad among people in general; they will be a kind of measure for people.

Also, Paul considers that humiliation before the Gentiles, who were sinners, is worse than suffering something from brothers and sisters, forgiving them (and he considers it best and advises to turn to a wise believer who can resolve the matter), if there is no one who could wisely judge among the faithful.

Paul also reminds that sinful people who have not repented do not inherit the Kingdom of God. Still, there are many nuances here. For example, the thief who repented on the cross entered Paradise first, but not as a son of God, but as a forgiven sinner. Those who inherit are children, heirs, and not just people. Therefore, the words of the apostle directly mean that unrepentant sinful people will not be God's children and do not deserve the Kingdom of God. Nevertheless, this is not an absolute and final verdict; there still remains hope for the unutterable mercy of God. Nonetheless, such a hope must in no way stop a person on the path of repentance; one must not put off repentance, thinking that God will save the person anyway out of mercy. Such a thought is deceitful (misleading), because it teaches to despise God and His holy will.

I will also say that not to associate with Christian sinners means not to build good relations with them, not to create for them an illusion of well-being, so that they do not lose the awareness of the need for repentance. If the faithful created an appearance or actually built good relations with unrepentant Christian sinners, the latter would lose the social call to conversion and repentance. This would be a sinful deed.

Also, Paul speaks about the forgiveness and cleansing of sins in Christ. Were, but were cleansed.

(Matthew XIII, 54-58)
Matthew XIII, 54 — 'εἰς τὴν πατρίδα αὐτοῦ' — 'eis ten patrida autou' - into His own country. Where to. The preposition 'eis' indicates onto movement and the end point. 'ἐν τῇ συναγωγῇ αὐτῶν' - 'en te synagoge auton' - in their synagogue. Where.

Matthew XIII, 57 — 'ἐν αὐτῷ' — 'en auto' - at Him; in Him. That is, it indicates that temptations were in the eyewitnesses even before that; they spiritually suffered from respect of persons — they judged people by origin. Christ did not tempt them, in Him there was actually no such reason, but those people were prone to such thoughts; this was their way of thinking, their life position. Had someone else been in Christ's place, they would have reasoned the same way; Christ Himself here has nothing to do with it. 'ἐν τῇ ἰδίᾳ πατρίδι' - 'en te idia patridi' - in His own country. Where. 'ἐν τῇ οἰκίᾳ αὐτοῦ' - 'en te oikia autou' - in His own house. Where.

It must be said that brothers and sisters, as is known, were called among the Jews not only biological brothers and sisters, but also more distant relatives, in particular cousins, second cousins, and third cousins. Christ had no biological brothers and sisters of His own according to the flesh, since the Virgin Mary remained a Virgin always, even being the Mother of God.

Christ did not perform many miracles in His homeland then because the people did not believe. Christ does not want people to found and build faith (only) on miracles. Instead, one can think that faith primarily must be built on relationships. Before the compatriots of Jesus was He Himself, but they did not seek relationships with Him. They did not know God in Him (even if they had seen miracles, they would not have seen God in Him); they did not build these relationships with Christ, with God, on the basis of trust and faith, of knowing. Therefore, such an attitude is superficial, and in perspective they would have become like the seed from the parable of the sower, which did not fall onto good ground, but somewhere else. If Christ, through their desire for miracles, had awakened in them an appearance of faith in Him, then these people would always expect… miracles. This would mean that the meaning of their faith would be not God, not Christ, not relationships with Him, but miracles. This is actually a deception, which even the evil one can take advantage of, if the Lord permits.

Therefore, the Lord did not perform many miracles (perhaps He did not perform even a single one there, in contrast to the many miracles He performed in other places). Instead, He builds faith on Who Christ is. He is like the prophets here.

(Hebrews VII, 1-6; Luke X, 16-21)
For the reading from the Apostle, see the links:
Hebrews VII, 1 — 'ἀπὸ τῆς κοπῆς τῶν βασιλέων' — 'apo tes kopes ton basileon' - from the slaughter of the kings. The preposition 'apo' indicates here onto the complete return; Abraham left those kings and the place of their slaughter completely.

Hebrews VII, 2 — 'δεκάτην ἀπὸ πάντων' — 'dekaten apo panton' - a tenth part of all. The preposition 'apo' indicates here onto that the tithe was separated completely from the rest of the property and wealth, to be given to Melchizedek.

Hebrews VII, 3 — 'ἱεреὺς εἰς τὸ διηνεκές' — 'hiereus eis to dienekes' - a priest continually; a priest always; a priest without interruption; a priest forever. That is, it concerns the continuity of the priesthood, its constancy, factually its unchangeability and eternity.

Hebrews VII, 4 — 'ἐκ τῶν ἀκροθινίων' — 'ek ton akrothinion' - out of the spoils; from the best part; from the best spoils. That is, he gave the best of what was obtained. The preposition 'ek' indicates here onto that Abraham chose indeed the best, the choice part, which was completely sincere and essential, because this is also a prototype.

Hebrews VII, 5 — 'οἱ μὲν ἐκ τῶν υἱῶν Λευεὶ τὴν ἱεраτείαν λαμβάνοντες' — 'hoi men ek ton hyion Leuei ten hierateian lambanontes' - they that are of the sons of Levi, who receive the office of the priesthood. The preposition 'ek' indicates here onto that these are representatives of the group of the sons of Levi (in the same sense as the concept of primates). 'ἐκ τῆς ὀσφύος Ἀβραάμ' - 'ek tes osphyos Abraam' - out of the loins of Abraham. The preposition 'ek' indicates here onto the origin along the father's line.

Hebrews VII, 6 — 'ἐξ αὐτῶν' — 'ex auton' - from them. The preposition 'ek' in its form before a subsequent vowel here indicates onto the origin and representation of a group of people (which here is denied, since the phrase is negative).

It concerns that Christ's high priesthood lasts forever, always, without interruption; His ministry does not end and exceeds all Old Testament prototypes.
