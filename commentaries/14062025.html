(Matthew V, 42-48 )
Matthew V, 42 ‚Äî '·ºÄœÄ·Ω∏ œÉŒø·ø¶' - 'apo sou' - from you. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that that person wants to take it away completely (here it is not clear whether they intend to return it later or not).

Matthew V, 45 ‚Äî 'œÖ·º±Œø·Ω∂ œÑŒø·ø¶ œÄŒ±œÑœÅ·Ω∏œÇ ·ΩëŒº·ø∂ŒΩ œÑŒø·ø¶ ·ºêŒΩ Œø·ΩêœÅŒ±ŒΩŒø·øñœÇ' - 'hyioi tou patros hymon tou en ouranois' - sons of your Father, Who is in heavens (heavenly). Where. Those who love even their enemies become like the heavenly Father, become God's children. They become God's children not only through likeness, and it is wrong to think that they simply repeat similar actions of the Father as if without His participation, separately from Him, but in reality such believers participate in God's love, and precisely this participation makes them similar to the relationship of the Father and the Son, about which Christ witnesses more in the Gospel of John. Also here is an important moment ‚Äî if such active love towards enemies leads to the conversion of the latter, that is, to the spreading of God's love and the grace of the Holy Spirit, which indeed often happens, then one can see clearly here the entire Trinity ‚Äî the Father, the Son and the Holy Spirit, whereas if the enemies do not convert, then one sees more the Father and the Son clearly, and the Spirit ‚Äî implicitly (for the Spirit is inseparable from the Father and the Son). In such a case, so that no one would think that he or she failed to accomplish God's will, Christ says that such believers will be God's children, emphasising the relationship of the Father and the Son. One must remember that in God there is never truly love without response. God's love is never completely without response. If God's creation does not respond with their own love to His love, then to the Father's love the Son and the Holy Spirit respond, to the Son's ‚Äî the Father and the Holy Spirit, and to the Spirit's ‚Äî the Father and the Son. Christ speaks about this implicitly when he tells the apostles that if that person is a son of peace, then your peace will rest upon that house, and if not ‚Äî it will return to you. Why the sun when speaking about good, and rain ‚Äî when about righteousness? Why not the other way around? Or in some other way? Because the sun, like good, a person feels more compared to rain, from which they often hide, although they know how necessary the latter is. So it is simpler to call good that which you feel more, better, and righteousness that which you need more than you feel (although this can be imperceptible).
And also: if you want to feel God's love more, rejoice in the sun when it shines and warms, and when you want to be more satisfied, rejoice in the necessary rain. One can even do this as a spiritual exercise during appropriate weather.
