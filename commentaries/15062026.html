Liturgy:
(Romans VII, 1-13)
One of the most difficult semantic passages of the New Testament Scriptures, nevertheless, has a deep internal logic and a richness of meaning.

Romans VII, 2 — 'ἀπὸ τοῦ νόμου τοῦ ἀνδρός' — 'apo tou nomou tou andros' - from the law of the husband. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that the liberation is complete. The Law, and accordingly the commandment, binds a person with the good not through themselves, not in their nature, but in an additional way, as a model and pattern of action, as an image of what is pleasing to God and intended by Him for the person. The Law is not necessary for the existence of a person, their nature, but is given additionally. Therefore, a person can be under the action of the Law, or can also not be, which depends on various additional factors, specifically here — whether the husband is alive (for the wife).

Romans VII, 3 — 'ἐλευθέρα ἐστὶν ἀπὸ τοῦ νόμου' — 'eleuthera estin apo tou nomou' - [she] is free from the Law. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that freedom from the Law is complete, total, meaning it is not something cosmetic. According to what has just been written above, a person can fully exist without the Law if there are appropriate conditions. The word freedom here has a more natural meaning; without freedom, a person cannot exist, it is embedded in the very nature of a person, being a component of the image of God.

Romans VII, 4 — 'εἰς τὸ γενέσθαι ὑμᾶς ἑτέρῳ' — 'eis to genesthai hymas hetero' - unto your becoming [joined] to another. That is, to make the faithful such as live in drawing near to the Risen Christ, approaching Him by their existence and life. 'τῷ ἐκ νекρῶν ἐγερθέντι' - 'to ek nekron egerthenti' - to the One having been raised out of [the] dead. The preposition 'ek' indicates here from where the transition took place — from the dead to the living.

Romans VII, 5 — 'ἦμεν ἐν τῇ σαρκί' — 'emen en te sarki' - we were in the flesh. 'τὰ διὰ τοῦ νόμου ἐνηργεῖτο ἐν τοῖς μέλεσιν ἡμῶν' - 'ta dia tou nomou energeito en tois melesin emon' - the [passions] through the Law (as a channel of action or state) were working in the members of us. In some translations stands the word originate [stem from], but as is visible, in the original it is not there. Sinful passions do not originate from the Law or the commandment. But their manifestations are closely connected with the Law and the commandment, which is further discussed in more detail. One cannot say that sins, passions, just as the Law, use freedom. This is incorrect, because the freedom of a person is not literally the very thing through which commandments and passions, sins, operate, although it is very important here. That which the Law manifests — how it is intended by God, what is needed — the very same thing sin and passions, on the contrary, use against the person. Therefore, here is a visible and manifest confrontation between sin and the Law. It is also obviously important to emphasise that it is precisely about the human, and not about the Divine. For example, sin cannot use Divine grace, but it can distort and pervert the attitude of a person, the relationship of a person and grace. The Law, in its turn, also does not limit grace and does not determine it. 'εἰς τὸ καρποφορῆσαι τῷ θανάτῳ' - 'eis to karpophoresai to thanato' - unto the bearing of fruit to death. That is, the passions were working for what, or to put it another way — where they led. Recent readings and commentaries upon them reveal what the apostle means regarding death. See at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/11062026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/12062026.html

Romans VII, 6 — 'ἀπὸ τοῦ νόμου' — 'apo tou nomou' - from the Law. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that the liberation is complete. See the analogy above. 'ἀποθανόντες ἐν ᾧ κατειχόμεθα' - 'apothanontes en ho kateikhometha' - having died in that in which we were held fast. 'ἐν καινότητι πνεύματος καὶ οὐ παλαιότητι γράμματος' - 'en kainoteti pneumatos kai ou palaioteti grammatos' - in newness of Spirit and not in oldness of letter; by newness of Spirit and not by oldness of letter. The Spirit renews, and the letter makes old. Both variants of translation are possible; the first indicates that the renewal of nature, life, and the being filled with the Spirit are direct signs and characteristics of the life of the faithful, and the second — that they also share this with others.

Romans VII, 8, 11, 17, 18 — 'διὰ τῆς ἐντολῆς' — 'dia tes entoles' - through the commandment (as a channel of action). 'ἐν ἐμοὶ' - 'en emoi' - in me. Where. 'χωρὶς γὰр νόμου' - 'khoris gar nomou' - for apart from Law.

Romans VII, 9 — 'χωρὶς νόμου' — 'khoris nomou' - apart from Law. If there is no pattern, no model with which the life of a person is compared, then sin cannot distort relationships with the pattern, which do not exist. But when there is a model, then a comparison is possible, and both conformity to it and non-conformity are possible. One can put it this way: sin violated not the Law, but unity with God; God then granted the Law to manifest what exactly sin violates; sin now tries to deepen the fall of man and separation from God not only as the absence of a desired or even proper unity, but also as a violation additionally of God's Law, the commandments. Sin does not turn to God, but a person can. Therefore, sin opposes God's care for the salvation of people.

Romans VII, 10 — ‘ευρεθη’ — ‘heurethe’ — having been found (discovered, taught) (the commandment). 'ἡ ἐντολὴ ἡ εἰς ζωὴν αὕτη εἰς θάνατον' — 'he entole he eis zoen aute eis thanaton' - this commandment, the [one] into life, this [was found to be] into death. What is meant is that a person in their freedom is capable of both variants, and by sinning, chooses the second, the worse one, and sin becomes deeper when there is not only a violation of unity with God, but also a violation and transgression of God's commandment. For more on freedom, see the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/DialogueOnWordsChristFear.html
And also beautifully about this it is said in the 1st Preparatory Prayer of Saint Basil the Great before Communion: … for I have provoked Thy goodness, transgressed Thy commandments and disobeyed Thy commands... and I, though unworthy of heaven and earth and of this temporary life, because I have subjected the whole of myself to sin, given myself into slavery to passions and defiled Thy image.... Here we see that the heavenly state corresponds to unity with God as God's child, likeness to the angels, non-susceptibility to sins; the earthly paradisiacal state corresponds to the fulfilment of the Gospel commandments and freedom from sinful passions (dispassionateness as complete liberation from sinful passions); and a pious temporary life corresponds to the fulfilment of the Old Testament commandments and the preservation of God's image.

Romans VII, 11 — 'καὶ δι’ αὐτῆς ἀπέκτεινεν' — 'kai di' autes apekteinen' - and through it killed [me] (as a channel of action). God did not create death. When a person distances themselves from God, they die spiritually, and then also physically.

Romans VII, 13 — 'ἀλλὰ ἡ ἁμαρτία, ἵνα φανῇ ἁμαρτία, διὰ τοῦ ἀγαθοῦ μοι καтеργαζομένη θάνατον· ἵνα γένηται καθ’ ὑπερβολὴν ἁμαρτωλὸς ἡ ἁμαρτία διὰ τῆς ἐνтоλῆς' — 'alla e amartia ina phane amartia dia tou agathou moi katergazomene thanaton ina genetai kath' yperbolen amartolos e amaria dia tes entoles' - but sin, that it might appear sin, through the good to me was working out death, that sin might become exceeding sinful through the commandment (as a channel or means of action). A complex explanation of several important thoughts, specifically of why sin actually is incorrect, bad, sin. The ideal granted by God to people is a good, but sin inflicts death upon people through this very good.

A difficult passage for understanding, which consists of two parts.

In the first part, Paul speaks about the sphere of application of the Law of Moses (as long as a person lives).

When a person dies, their freedom changes. More about that (with certain theological and philosophical terminology) is in the work at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/DialogueOnWordsChristFear.html

Therefore, Paul says that for a true liberation from being under the Law, one needs to die to sin together with Christ. Then that for which the Law is given will be accomplished through Christ, and therefore, there will be no need to be under the Law. This does not mean that a person will become lawless (both further on, earlier, and in other epistles Paul says this), but instead of a literal understanding of the Law of Moses with its rituals, prototypes, and other things, a person will live by truth and freedom from sin in Christ, to which they are actually called.

And if they do not do this, then for such people the Law is given, as a schoolmaster unto Christ, which to a certain extent warns, cautions, teaches, makes wise, and so on — everything to preserve a weak person from falls. But therein lies the weakness of a person, that without Christ they cannot be saved by the Law.

Very vividly Paul speaks about how sin becomes exceedingly sinful when there is already the Law. But, as written earlier, to overcome a true temptation, it must first be manifested as such, from secret become manifest, and then overcome. If, however, a temptation can be overcome without this, then it is not truly real. Hence the reason why the Lord gave the Law — although people are not saved through the Law, yet it manifests sins and temptations (the first and necessary step to victory).

(Matthew IX, 36-X, 8 )
Matthew IX, 36 — ‘εσπλαγχνισθη’ — ‘esplanchnisthe’ — having been moved with compassion [having felt sympathy].

Matthew IX, 38 — 'εἰς τὸν θερισμὸν αὐτοῦ' — 'eis ton therismon autou' - into His harvest. Where to.

Matthew X, 5 — 'εἰς ὁδὸν ἐθνῶν' — 'eis hodon ethnon' - into a way of Gentiles. Where to. 'εἰς πόλιν Σαμαριτῶν' - 'eis polin Samariton' - into a city of Samaritans. Where to.

The choosing of the 12 apostles and the instructions to them. Some apostles in the list had two names, therefore the lists in the Synoptics differ somewhat (one evangelist calls by one name, and another — by another).

The instructions speak about the fact that the apostles have a special mission, a task, and therefore, besides all the commandments, they have additionally special commandments regarding their work. What is important is that in this way more people should be brought to salvation and the knowledge of the truth.
