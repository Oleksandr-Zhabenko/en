Liturgy:
(1 Corinthians III, 18-23)
1 Corinthians III, 18 — 'εἴ τις δοκεῖ σοφὸς εἶναι ἐν ὑμῖν ἐν τῷ αἰῶνι τούτῳ' — 'ei tis dokei sophos einai en hymin en to aioni touto' - if any man among you seems [thinks himself; has an opinion about himself] to be wise in this world. See the explanation further regarding the next verse.

1 Corinthians III, 19 — 'παρὰ τῷ θεῷ' — 'para to theo' - in the vicinity of God; near God. The preposition 'para' here is with the dative case, indicating here onto a certain comparison: If you place this in the vicinity of God and compare, then…. That is, this does not mean that God considers all human wisdom as foolishness, but it means that all human wisdom absolutely in no way can be compared with God and His Wisdom (such a comparison is, generally speaking, unwise — for what kind of mark does a person wish to receive by comparing their wisdom with God and God's Wisdom?). 'ἐν τῇ πανουργίᾳ' - 'en te panourgia' - in craftiness; in inventiveness. The preposition 'en' emphasizes that the wise people mentioned here, when they are in a state of ignorance, searching, inventing, creating, solving, and the like, easily err, by no means always find the truth, often do not offer effective solutions, and similar. Of course, God does not catch people, but the imagery quite vividly shows how unwise people can be in their confidence that they are truly wise, whereas God is always All-Wise. One can understand the words about catches somewhat otherwise. A person who lives in a world that has distanced itself from God, who is creative, skillful, will not escape sin, and will not be able to hide sins from God. Therefore, the advice to become fools in this world (in a world dominated by sin) is a manifestation of wisdom. And the key question is to what extent the world is dominated by sin, how much it has distanced itself from God. If extraordinarily strongly, then such a world must be avoided. If, however, there are many springs which the Lord gave, much like once through Moses in the wilderness — then by God's mercy this is, on the contrary — a field for labor and Christian mission. It is necessary to remember that the appearance of a spring in the wilderness is a miracle; in the midst of a wilderness there might be no springs, but one can appear by the action of God. Also, this depends on the person themselves: if they are filled with God's grace, like the apostles, and have zeal, then they will go with faith into the world, if there is at least a little hope to save at least some. If, however, a person is infirm, then they need to be cautious. And further I will add that the apostles did not consider themselves wise in this world.

1 Corinthians III, 21 — 'καυχάσθω ἐν ἀνθρώποις' — 'kaukhastho en anthropois' - glory in people.

1 Corinthians III, 22 — Cephas — this in the Aramaic language is a stone, in Greek Peter — the apostle Peter. Apollos — an apostle out of the 70, active in preaching and talented, filled with the gifts of the Holy Spirit. See:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/20052026.html

I will add that among the Corinthians there were many educated, in modern language intelligent people, and also among the Christians there were many spiritual gifts. This it is necessary to remember when we read the Epistle.

(Matthew XIII, 36-43)
Matthew XIII, 36 — 'εἰς τὴν οἰκίαν' — 'eis ten oikian' — into the house; home. Where to.

Matthew XIII, 40 — 'ἐν τῇ συντελείᾳ τοῦ αἰῶνος' — 'en te synteleia tou aionos' - in the end of the world; in the completion of the age. When. That is, with the glorious Second Coming of Christ.

Matthew XIII, 41 — 'ἐκ τῆς βασιλείας' — 'ek tes basileias' - out of the Kingdom. The preposition 'ek' shows that this is an answer onto the question wherefrom?.

Matthew XIII, 42 — 'εἰς τὴν κάμινον τοῦ πυρός' — 'eis ten kaminon tou pyros' - into the furnace of fire. The preposition 'eis' indicates onto the direction of the action, that is, where to. In general, the construction indicates onto that this is a certain environment of sufferings, of losing life.

Matthew XIII, 43 — 'ἐν τῇ βασιλείᾳ τοῦ πατρὸς αὐτῶν' — 'en te basileia tou patros auton' - in the Kingdom of the Father of them. Where.

The explanation of the parable of the wheat and the tares by Jesus Himself (consequently, the main thought in it, which, nevertheless, does not exclude additional ones beforehand). As researchers note, the plot of the parable is built onto that wheat and tares, until they grow up and bring forth fruits, are visually very similar, which is why it is spoken that it can be difficult to discern them. From here a conclusion is made that even angels do not have all knowledge regarding people and their life until the Judgement of God takes place, because God knows all.

It is interesting that this text is read at the matins service before the Synaxis of the Archangel Michael and all the Heavenly Bodiless Powers.
