Liturgy:
(Romans IX, 6-19)
Romans IX, 6 — 'ἐξ Ἰσραήλ' — 'ex Israel' - out of Israel. That is, descendants according to the flesh. The preposition 'ek' in its form before a subsequent vowel indicates here that it concerns origin.

Romans IX, 7 — 'ἐν Ἰσαὰκ' — 'en Isaak' - in Isaac. In whom.

Romans IX, 8 — 'εἰς σπέρμα' — 'eis sperma' - into seed. That is, they are accounted into descendants with all the consequences.

Romans IX, 8-9 verses speak about the meaning of the promise. It is spoken in these words about the children of God. God promised an heir to Abraham — and precisely that child who was born out of this promise, through it, as its accomplishment from God — precisely that one also is God's, because it is truly from Him. And of course, the Divine is higher than the human, therefore also higher than the fleshly.

Romans IX, 10 — 'ἐξ ἑνὸς κοίτην' — 'ex henos koiten' - out of one bed. That is, these are the biological children of Isaac.

Romans IX, 11 — 'προθεσις' — 'prothesis' - a setting before purpose (in particular, the loaves of showing forth in the temple were so named). It is often understood such that God chose Jacob, and therefore he truly became holy. But the matter is in that God knew that precisely Jacob would be holy, knew how he would realize and accomplish his freedom, therefore, in no way limiting the freedom of the man, God foresaw whom it was necessary to call and establish. Therefore also the promises are given precisely to those people for whom they were important. See:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/30032026.html
'οὐκ ἐξ ἔργων ἀλλ’ ἐκ τοῦ καλοῦντος' — 'ouk ex ergon all ek tou kalountos' — not out of works but out of the [One] calling (that is, out of God). It concerns the calling. The cause of the calling of people unto the dignity of children of God after their fall is not their works, but God's will to save them, God's calling and the response into it.

This is the general thought.

Romans IX, 13 — 'εμισησα' — 'emisesa' - [I] hated — from 'μισεω' - 'miseo' - to hate (that is, manifested less love). Here it is spoken about [I] loved less rather than about hatred, because God does not hate His creation, but takes care out of it. Nonetheless, the blessing was given more precisely to Jacob, and not to the elder Esau.

Romans IX, 14 — 'παρὰ τῷ θεῷ' — 'para to theo' - in the vicinity of God. The preposition 'para' here is with the dative case and indicates here that it concerns that drawing near to God means drawing near to the truth, righteousness, but one can say it also thus, that drawing near to righteousness is drawing near to God.

Romans IX, 15 — a difficult passage for an exact translation. Closest of all in meaning to: I will manifest mercy to whom it is possible, and I will manifest compassion to whom it is possible to manifest compassion. See also about that more:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/06062023.html

It concerns the promises of God. God promises something important to people not because they wish it, or strive about it, but because He is Merciful and Loving; God promises not through the merits of people, but as a free gift, nonetheless, to those who can preserve these promises and for whom they truly will be important.

Romans IX, 17 — 'ὅτι εἰς αὐτὸ τοῦτο ἐξήγειρά σε ὅπως ἐνδείξωμαι ἐν σοὶ τὴν δύναμίν μου' — 'hoti eis auto touto exegeira se hopos endeixomai en soi ten dynamin mou' - for into this very thing I raised you up, so that I might show forth in you My power. These are God's words regarding the pharaoh who oppressed the Israelites before their exit out of Egypt. As I wrote recently, see in particular at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/1806025.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/renewal-of-Peter.html
it concerns God's permission, which in the Old Testament, and here also in the quoting by the New Testament, people then did not distinguish out of God's will. Therefore, that God allowed the pharaoh to be cruel is conveyed here by the words that He raised him up such. The translation raised up, exalted here is more exact, because it indicates that the Lord granted glory and honour to the pharaoh and Egypt earlier, so as later to show His power so that henceforth people might have an example and believe into God's power. Instead, the translation set up [appointed] is unsuccessful — it leads onto the thought as if the Lord prompted the pharaoh into cruelty, which, of course, could not be. The Merciful Lord is never cruel, much less does He prompt others into cruelty. 'ἐν πάσῃ τῇ γῇ' — 'en pase te ge' — in all the earth. Where. That is, everywhere among men.

Romans IX, 19 — 'βουλήματι' — to the counsel; to the purpose; to the intent. This word differs out of that which properly signifies desire and will. Therefore here it is expedient to speak not about the will of God, but about His permission.

(Matthew X, 32-36; XI, 1)
Concerning the beginning of the reading, it is well written at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/07062026.html
There, in particular, is analyzed the content of the expression to confess in Me, which Christ uses here, and the translation of which often sounds otherwise.

Here it is important to note that, as Fedir Raichynets points out in his article *War, Peacemaking, and the Peace that Divides: Three Contradictory Statements of Jesus in the Gospel of Matthew*, published on pages 11-22 of the journal *Church and Society* (No. 1 (15)) 2026, by sword one must understand not war, but a division on the basis of values — to be participants of the Kingdom of God with Christ or to follow after this world. That is, Christ does not call into enmity or the like, but into faithfulness and the keeping of values. For details, see in the article itself.

John XIV, 21 — 'ἀγαπηθήσεται ὑπὸ τοῦ πατρός μου' — 'agapethesetai hypo tou patros mou' - shall be loved by the Father of Me. This is literal, though the form is little expanded. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates here that this is the passive state of the participle will be loved.

John XIV, 23 — 'μονὴν παρ’ αὐτῷ ποιησόμεθα' — 'monen par auto poiesometha' - an abode in the vicinity of him We will make. The preposition 'para' in its form before a subsequent vowel here is with the dative case and indicates here that the Trinity will be as if Neighbours, close Friends of such a person, similarly as have such relationships people who move so as to live closer to someone whom they love.

Here we see that Christ, in response to the question about the manifestation not to the world but to the disciples, answers so that one can understand several important thoughts. Christ wishes to manifest Himself to the disciples, and through them — to the world; He wishes that the faithful should participate in His manifestation to the world, that the faithful should be a part and participants of this manifestation. Christ is manifested not only to open the truth, but also to make people its companions and participants through union with God. For it to happen so, it is necessary to love Christ, to fulfil His will, in particular the commandments, to be in unity with God.
