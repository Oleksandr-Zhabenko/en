Liturgy:
(Romans XI, 13-24)
Romans XI, 14 — 'σώσω τινὰς ἐξ αὐτῶν' — 'soso tinas ex auton' - I will save some out of them. The preposition 'ek' in its form before a subsequent vowel indicates here that it concerns representatives of those people, the Jews. Here the apostle wishes to provoke in the Jews a desire to become faithful in Christ, similarly as earlier already such became the Gentiles who after the preaching of Paul converted. Factually in modern language we would sooner say that Paul here advertises Christianity, is a marketer and a PR manager. But it is worth noting also the ambiguity of the concept of jealousy [envy]. The tenth commandment forbids to covet that which is with neighbours. That is factually, it forbids to envy, that is, to desire to appropriate to oneself something that is not one's own. But here is an essential difference — special relationships with God and salvation are not something foreign for the Jews, since they are descendants of Abraham according to the flesh and the Chosen people. That is, the apostle Paul wishes to provoke not a desire for something that is with the converted Gentiles and is not with the Jews, but a natural desire for one's own for the Jews — salvation and God. One could say that this is not envy, but zeal, but also regarding zeal there is a remark of no less weight. Zeal signifies that a person considers something their own and especially cares about it, also so that it further should be one's own. But salvation and God are not purely Jewish, about which the apostle Paul writes much right here. Therefore also the word zeal he does not use. One can see that he wishes to provoke a desire for a common good for all. Actually, from here one can see God's design, for what in a person there is a feeling of envy and zeal — so as to provoke a desire for a common good. The fall damaged these feelings, as well as others, but here we see their Divine meaning.

Romans XI, 15 — 'ζωὴ ἐκ νεκρῶν' — 'zoe ek nekron' - life out of [the] dead. An expression which stood as a parallel into the Paschal words of greeting and faith. The preposition 'ek' here indicates onto the initial state of death and the transition away from it into the opposite state — into life. Not that life originates out of death, but that people shall pass away from death into life.

Romans XI, 17 — 'ἐνεκεντρίσθης ἐν αὐτοῖς' — 'enekentristhes en autois' - you were grafted in them; you were grafted among them. Where.

Romans XI, 24 — 'σὺ ἐκ τῆς κατὰ φύσιν ἐξεκόπης ἀγριελαίου καὶ παρὰ φύσιν ἐνεκεντρίσθης εἰς καλλιέλαιον' — 'sy ek tes kata physin exekopes agrielaiou kai para physin enekentristhes eis kallielaion' - here several different variants of translation are possible: you out of the according into nature wild olive tree were cut off and to the vicinity of nature were grafted into a good olive tree; you out of the according into nature wild olive tree were cut off and regarding nature were grafted in a good olive tree; you out of the according into nature wild olive tree were cut off and contrary nature were grafted in a good olive tree. 'para' here is with the accusative, which precisely allows all these variants of translation. See regarding a similar use at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/10062026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/03062026.html
It concerns that the grafting into a good olive tree (cultivated, and in this context — into God's people, the Church) happens in the context of origin, but not according to the flesh, but according into the promise. Through faith and union with Christ, the faithful out of different nations, including until now frequently former Gentiles, become participants of God's promises and gifts of grace away from the Holy Root, Which is Christ. Also a little I will say about nature here. Nature in this verse is connected with life and values, with the character of behavior, the manner of thoughts, feelings, generally the life of people, describing all this generally and by one word. The meaning of this is in that former Gentiles, and now Christians, through Christ distanced themselves and left gentileship and its life, and became Christians with their life. They turned out to be close into Christ, and not into gentileship.

A comparison of different communities of faithful and unfaithful people with a fruitful olive tree or with other trees. The Gentiles were not Israelites (about Israelites who became Gentiles it is not spoken here) by physical origin, by nature, but now have become a New Israel — not by fleshly origin, not as nations, but by grace, through the gift of power to become children of God according into God's promise to all the faithful in Christ. The Root actually is Christ. And Christ shall remain Himself without the converted Gentiles, whereas the converted Gentiles without Christ in such a manner by no means shall remain.

God is compared with a person in the dimension of virtues — the demandingness of a leader and goodness at the same time. This is called anthropomorphism and is a device that allows to convey something high in the form of something comprehensible and ordinary. The word severe here is worth replacing onto demanding. God is not properly severe, but is demanding (as also every good leader).

Here generally is vividly manifested that for God people at the same time are those who are called to be His children by the gift of grace, but also are a creation.

(Matthew XI, 27-30)
Matthew XI, 27 — 'Πάντα μοι παρεδόθη ὑπὸ τοῦ πατρός μου' — 'panta moi paredothe hypo tou patros mou' - all things into Me were delivered by the Father of Me. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates here onto the passive voice were delivered. It is necessary to say that if a person knows the Son of God with love, then by this they are made similar to the Father, Who knows and loves the Son, Who sent Him into the world. To accept the Son can a person who similarly to the Father loves Him, also they accept God's design regarding the salvation of people and other similar things. And this signifies that they are similar into the Father in this, and this is possible only then if the Father gives this into them. See more at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/04052023.html

Matthew XI, 29 — 'μάθετε ἀπ’ ἐμοῦ' — 'mathete ap emou' - learn away from Me. The use of the preposition 'apo' in its form 'ap' before a vowel of the next word indicates onto the individual characteristics of the faithful in the fulfillment of this commandment. Christ does not call all literally to come into a fleshly crucifixion, for example, but each faithful person must in their case learn, in their place, in their circumstances, into the measure of their capabilities, according into their personality, individuality, etc. In this sense all the faithful are not similar in full measure onto Christ, separated in this season away from Him without sin (wherefrom also the preposition), but by His foresight, calling and permission, for the diversity of ministries and general richness and good.
