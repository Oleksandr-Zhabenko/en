(Matthew XVIII, 10-20)
Matthew XVIII, 10, 14, 19 — 'ἐν οὐρανοῖς' — 'en ouranois' - in the heavens. Where.

Matthew XVIII, 12 — 'ἓν ἐξ αὐτῶν' — 'hen ex auton' - one of them. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that that sheep is similar to the others — at least in its ovine (by the meaning of the parable: human) nature.

Matthew XVIII, 18 — 'ἐν οὐρανῷ' — 'en ourano' - in heaven; with heaven. More likely the first, since the second would mean using heaven as an instrument or means for binding and loosing. An important detail here: unlike verses 10, 14, 19 — where the plural is used throughout — here it is the singular. One may think that the apostles (and subsequently their successors) are here in a certain sense heavenly people, heavenly — but not all-heavenly. See more concerning heavenly people at the link:
https://www.instagram.com/p/C8Iav6ssKOI/
One may think that people arrive by God's design (apart from the Mother of God, possibly John the Baptist, and might be also some others) into one of God's dwellings (of which there are many in the Father's house). They will not enjoy all of them. And the forgiveness of a sin by an apostle (or his successor) opens a place, grants a place in one of those dwellings, if previously that place was inaccessible on account of sin. Analogously the reverse is also true — if an apostle (or his successor) binds something, then sin continues to hinder access to that heavenly dwelling. But God reserves for Himself the Supreme Judgement — and He may show mercy to a person (taking all things into account, and above all from His boundless mercy). Similarly these rules of which Christ speaks here carry greater weight and universal significance if they are violated by people (including apostles — hypothetically, since the apostles took care to observe them — or their successors): in that case such a violation is known to all the heavens — that is, it requires unconditional repentance. For example, if a clergyman, a successor of the apostles, shows contempt for any person (see Matthew V, 22), they fall under the Supreme divine Judgement (not the judgement of the Church, but God's — or at minimum that of an Ecumenical Council). Therefore apostolic succession enables one to join people to heaven, but does not permit the execution of a final judgement or the determination of people's destinies. On the other hand it places great responsibility upon the successors of the apostles. It must also be said that if someone acts according to God's will, then this concerns all the heavens — for it concerns God's will.

Matthew XVIII, 19 — 'ἐξ ὑμῶν' — 'ex hymon' - from among you. The preposition 'ex' (the form of 'ek' before the following vowel) indicates whence those two come. 'παρὰ τοῦ πατρός' - 'para tou patros' - from beside the Father. This construction is used concerning the procession of the Holy Spirit from the Father, concerning the Most Holy Trinity and the entire God's design, and also concerning the human nature of Jesus Christ. See more at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/uk/commentaries/Pryjmennyky.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/02082025.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/28052026.html
Here what is spoken of is that this will also come to pass from the Holy Spirit, and one may also understand the participation of the entire Trinity — Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. At the same time this emphasises that for this it is necessary to ask in the Holy Spirit. 'ἐν οὐρανοῖς' — 'en ouranois' — in the heavens. Here in the plural — which, in addition to what has been said above, also indicates that the Father can answer prayers in a very generous manner. See more on this at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/PowerOfHuman.html

Matthew XVIII, 20 — 'εἰς τὸ ἐμὸν ὄνομα' — 'eis to emon onoma' - into My name. Here the preposition 'eis' means that those gathered are for Christ by His will. 'ἐν μέσῳ' - 'en meso' - in the midst. Common expression.

Concerning the commandment If your brother sins against you… I shall dwell in more detail here.

For this commandment to be fulfilled in the form in which it is given by the Lord Jesus, one needs… to have good brothers and sisters in Christ — and better still, friends as well — so that they may be able to offer support. Tell it to the Church... — manifold in meaning; historically it has most of all been referred to the decisions of Ecumenical Councils, when the testimony of the entire Church determined what is received and what is rejected. But in the sphere of personal life there is no need to bring a matter concerning sin to an Ecumenical Council. Often it is understood that by the Church one means the pastors common to the participants of the situation — for example, the bishop. But this requires that all participants genuinely respect the judgement of the pastors. In any case, we see how strong the relationships of the faithful must be according to the commandment — strong enough that willing supporters are to be found. But what is to be done if no one comes forward (for example, through faint-heartedness, or lack of confidence, or for other reasons)? How is a person themselves to relate inwardly to the sins of others — including sins committed against them?

If we look at the general tradition, and more specifically at the commandments to the apostles and the faithful — for example, not to think beforehand what to answer persecutors at a trial for the faith, about which I wrote earlier — then it is evident that these words together with others mean that one must not harbour resentment within oneself, must not become attached to others' sins. When it is necessary to think through what and how something must be said — one must think it through, and then say it.

One must struggle against sins — but must not become attached to them.
