Liturgy:
(Romans X, 11-XI, 2)
Romans X, 12 — 'πλουτῶν εἰς πάντας τοὺς ἐπικαλουμένους αὐτόν' — 'plouton eis pantas tous epikaloumenous auton' - rich into (for, to) all those calling upon Him. Points onto the gradualness of the opening, revelation, manifestation of God's generosity for people. That is, rich in the understanding both of generosity (God grants extraordinarily much and generously), and of the duration of granting (God endows eternally).

Romans X, 14 — 'εἰς ὃν οὐκ ἐπίστευσαν' — 'eis hon ouk episteusan' - into Whom they did not believe. The preposition 'eis' indicates here that faith leads to God, He is the final Goal of faith. An important thought here is that a human being cannot address God properly unless they believe in Him as a Personal Living God. That is, precisely such an addressing, turning (that is, prayer) is possible in consequence of faith in a Personal God.

In verse 15 there are no prepositions commented upon by me, but there is a good non-obvious thought about feet. Truly, preachers describe their calling into the preaching of the Gospel as one of the most beautiful events of their life — for the Lord manifests trust to them, grants a mission for others, manifests a part of the meaning of their life and history, and the listeners of the Gospel see in this preaching true good. The apostle speaks about that the logic of the spiritual calling into the Glad Tidings of Christ is verified, harmonious, beautiful.

Romans X, 17 — 'ἡ πίστις ἐξ ἀκοῆς' — 'he pistis ex akoes' - the faith [is] out of hearing; the faith [is] out of the capability to hear. A preacher of good gives an impulse of faith, a push of faith, a movement of faith, and not the human being themselves creates this movement of faith. But it depends from the listeners whether they extinguish the light of faith, or accept, multiply, and pass on.

Romans X, 18 — 'εἰς πᾶσαν τὴν γῆн' — 'eis pasan ten gen' - into all the earth. 'εἰς τὰ πέρατα τῆς οἰκουμένης' - 'eis ta perata tes oikoumenes' - into the ends of the world (the then known world inhabited by people). The words are relevant also in our time.

Romans XI, 1 — 'ἐк σπέρματος Ἀβραάμ' — 'ek spermatos Abraam' - out of seed of Abraham. The preposition 'ek' indicates here origin according to the flesh.

Romans XI, 2 — 'ἐν Ἠλείᾳ' — 'en Heleia' - in Elijah. That is, here — in the story about the prophet Elijah.

I wrote earlier, quoting:
In today's Apostle, Saint Paul speaks about the meaning of the calling of the Gentiles into the Kingdom of God, into the Church for the Israelites themselves, speaking about that all subjected themselves to sin, but also all are called into salvation.

End of quote.

Romans X, 17, 18 — ‘ρηματος’ — ‘rhematos’ — a spoken word; the core of the matter under discussion, the content of a conversation (in the 18th verse in the plural). That is, here it is spoken not even about the very words of Jesus, but about the understanding of them, about that which the Lord wishes to bring to us through His Word.

The Old Testament quotes Paul takes according to the Septuagint, the well-known Greek translation.

In general, the Old Testament quotes testify about the drama of Sacred History — God opens Himself to people who distance themselves away from Him. This leads into tension — and this tension — is through people.

But the Lord is Faithful, and calls all into salvation through Christ, expanding the preaching of the Gospel in all the world.

(Matthew XI, 16-20)
Matthew XI, 16 — 'ἐν ταῖς ἀγοραῖς' — 'en tais agorais' - in the marketplaces. That is, in places of gatherings for the decision of matters and trade.

Matthew XI, 19 — 'ἀπὸ τῶν ἔργων' — 'apo ton ergon' - away from the works. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that it concerns something objective that can be considered to a significant degree separately — that is, to evaluate works, fruits, is significantly easier (although it happens to be not easy) than the person themselves (which is possible in full measure only exclusively to God). Christ calls here to look upon the fruits of His activity and also of John.

Matthew XI, 20 — 'ἐν αἷς' — 'en hais' - in which. Where.

I wrote also earlier, quoting:
The words of Christ about children — a comparison of the simple people (predominantly in the words of Jesus, but they relate into the elders of the people also), who did not accept Jesus and did not obey John the Baptist regarding repentance. Not having the capability (grounds) to reproach Both in sin, people accused Them in something else, and worst — accused Them in origin not from God (=You are not from God, for you are not such as we).

The words about pre-eminence of wisdom are relevant also now, especially for those who wish by their individual inclinations and characteristics, psychology, to justify the non-conversion, unrepentance of their own and of others. Jesus and John were in everyday life completely different, opposite, but single in the call into conversion, repentance. Who had an open mind and heart, converted at least through One. And others cannot justify themselves by their individual qualities, for for some the example of John was eloquent, for others — of Jesus, and for many — of Both. So also now, in our times, the faithful are people with completely different qualities.

This passage eloquently speaks about diversity in the Church.

To a person there is no need to step over themselves real (such as the Lord calls them to the fulfillment of God's will), intended by God, so as to fulfill the will of God. Another matter — sins, precisely they harm everyone, with them it is necessary to fight.

End of quote.

Also it is necessary to note that which is more frequently not noticed. Together with great freedom, people can see also many of their own sins. To the reproach that one does not wish to see in themselves much bad, one can say: But every archer wishes to hit into the ten, into the bullseye, not into 9, not into 8, not into 7, 6, 5, nor all the less less or generally not to hit into the target… But the archer does not complain onto that the target is small, rather more rejoices when it turns out to hit.

Researchers note that there was such a game among children which depicted the world — some had to do as people do when they are happy, and others — as people do when they are unhappy. Together the groups show the state of all mankind.

In the words of Jesus, which are a continuation of yesterday's reading, it is spoken about repentance — many people, the majority of that time, did not wish to repent, although the call into repentance was not something in modern language marginal, something exceptional — very many people converted and repented. But in comparison with the majority — that was still a noticeable minority.

And so the majority invented for themselves a justification on the basis of that about which they heard — heard about possession — ascribed it into John, because he was an ascetic, not similar by the style of life onto the majority. Knew about people who spend time in revelry, accused in that Jesus. Actually, these accusations were built upon certain purely external, superficial signs, and to deepen into the core people did not wish, therefore were these accusations untrue.

But the Lord shows that diversity in ministries shows God's wisdom — the Lord strives to save those who can [be saved].

(Romans XI, 2-12)
Romans XI, 2 — 'ἐν Ἠλείᾳ' — 'en Heleia' - in Elijah. That is, here — in the story about the prophet Elijah.

Romans XI, 5 — 'ἐν τῷ νῦν καιρῷ' — 'en to nyn kairo' - in the now season; at the present time. A set expression.

Romans XI, 6 — 'ἐξ ἔργων' — 'ex ergon' - out of works. The preposition 'ek' in its form before a subsequent vowel indicates here that God grants grace to those who can accept it.

Romans XI, 9 — 'εἰς παγίδα καὶ εἰсь θήραν καὶ εἰς σκάνδαλον καὶ εἰς ἀνταπόδομα αὐτοῖς' — 'eis pagida kai eis theran kai eis skandalon kai eis antapodoma autois' - into a snare, and into a trap, and into a stumbling block, and into a recompense. The preposition 'eis' indicates here into what material contentment shall become for people who distanced themselves away from grace. See also at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/Do-you-see-birds-lilies-divine-beauty-and-care.pdf

Here again Paul, following the Old Testament people, does not distinguish God's permission and will, since he quotes the Old Testament where this is visible. God allowed them to be in unbelief, and did not give this according to His desire. Therefore here is not a wish, but a permission for it to be so, for this is a sequential consequence of the rejection of grace by them. See more at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/22062026.html

Romans XI, 11 — 'εἰς τὸ παραζηλῶσαι αὐτούς' — 'eis to parazelosai autous' - for to provoke them to jealousy. See analogously above.

(Matthew XI, 20-26)
Matthew XI, 20 — 'ἐν αἷς' — 'en hais' - in which. Where.

Matthew XI, 21 — 'ἐν Τύρῳ καὶ Σιδῶνι' — 'en Tyro kai Sidoni' - in Tyre and Sidon. Where. 'ἐν ὑμῖν' - 'en hymin' - in you. Where. 'ἐν σάκκῳ καὶ σποδῷ' - 'en sakko kai spodo' - in sackcloth and ashes. That is, with great humiliation. How.

Matthew XI, 22, 24 — 'ἐν ἡμέρᾳ κρίσεως' — 'en hemera kriseos' - in a day of judgement. When.

Matthew XI, 23 — 'ἐν Σοδόμοις' — 'en Sodomois' - in Sodom. Where. 'ἐν σοί' - 'en soi' - in you. Where.

Matthew XI, 25 — 'Ἐν ἐκείνῳ τῷ καιрῷ' — 'en ekeino to kairo' - in that season; then. A set expression. 'ἀπὸ σοφῶν καὶ συνετῶν' - 'apo sophon kai syneton' - away from wise and understanding [ones]. Wise here are named those who sought understanding and knowledge as a self-value, and understanding [ones] — those who sought the same for certain applications, with an applied goal. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that the Father hid true pre-eminence of wisdom and knowledge away from proud people (predominantly by their mind). Precisely pride prevented the mentioned Israeli cities to convert massly, completely. It concerns not that God does not wish their knowledge and conversion, but they themselves do not wish. The Father allows this, for He values freedom. See more at the links:
