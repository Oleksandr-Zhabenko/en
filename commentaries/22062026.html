(Romans IX, 18-33)
Romans IX, 19 — 'βουλήματι' — 'boulemati' - to the counsel; to the purpose; to the intent. This word differs out of that which properly signifies desire and will. Therefore here it is expedient to speak not about the will of God, but about His permission. See also:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/renewal-of-Peter.html

Here it is important to note that in this passage the apostle Paul means that the creator completely understands how the creation should act, work. Paul here compares God with the men-craftsmen of that time. The creation of men of that time did not have its own consciousness, was not endowed with sufficient its own intellect and freedom, therefore also could not ask such questions. Now, with the appearance of artificial intelligence, genetic engineering, and similar modern technologies, men create something such that they themselves do not until the end understand. But men are intelligent [rational], free beings, created according to the image of God, therefore the comparison is very incomplete, only a reflection of the very core of the matter (recall 1 Corinthians XIII). The apostle, by the question Why from a man, means that the man calls into question God's design, or even better to say, calls God to render an account why He created them thus. And God then must be accountable… to the man, His creation. If the modern creators of technologies, who often distributedly create (no one ensures, for example, the full creation and maintenance of AI, since a group of people designs, another group implements, yet another works upon electricity, water, etc.), were made accountable to their creation, in particular AI, what would they say regarding such a perspective? Would they agree? But if they themselves are capable of comprehending their actions, their responsibility, then why additionally AI? And if they are not capable (whilst God is capable), then why should they trust their incomprehensible creation more than themselves and that which they know? The apostle Paul speaks not about the comprehension of the causes of the creation of the world by God, but about the demand to render an account to a man who simply wishes this. How then did God create the man? He created them free and intelligent. Saint Maximus the Confessor writes that a man is so intelligent that they are free, and so free that they are intelligent. Reason and freedom are inseparably connected in a man. See more about that at the links:

God gave the pharaoh freedom, but gave the capability to obey, as well as also the capability not to obey. God knew what the pharaoh would choose, and spoke about this to Moses beforehand. But God did not determine what the pharaoh would do, but pre-eminently and freely foresaw. The question whether God is responsible for the deed and choice of the pharaoh is connected with the question whether, giving to men freedom, God is responsible for all that they will do after this. But if precisely God answers for the use of freedom by men, then in what consists the very freedom of men? Are they truly free, capable of determining something in life and eternity? But if the freedom of creation makes the Creator responsible for it, for its use by the creation which is free, then God becomes dependent from His creation, truly accountable to the man (or to the devil, for example), and that only because He generally endowed the creation with such capabilities and powers. But the Creator in the case of God (and not of a man) is incomparably higher than the creation, is the Most High and does not fall under changes through the creation. He can freely manifest Himself, and to no one is He accountable, except the Other Hypostases of the Trinity and Himself. Consequently, the question whether God is responsible for the use of freedom by the creation is analogous to the question whether God has sufficient freedom to create free beings, being God and further. Yes, God truly has sufficient freedom to create free beings, in particular angels and people. Moreover, He has sufficient freedom to become human being, to suffer, to die upon a cross and rise out of [the] dead, at the same time forever remaining in unity with human nature. One can still set the question in such a manner: why should God answer before His creation, and not the creation before its Creator? Did God create for Himself judges, advisors, leaders, etc. (and for what is this to God?)?
The apostle Paul further reasons regarding this, true, in other words, using the images of Scripture.

Romans IX, 21 — 'ἐκ τοῦ αὐτοῦ φυράματος ποιῆσαι ὃ μὲν εἰς τιμὴν σκεῦος, ὃ δὲ εἰς ἀτιμίαν' — 'ek tou autou phyramatos poiesai o men eis timen skeuos o de eis atimian' - out of the same lump to make the [one] into honour a vessel, and the [other] into dishonour (that is, for special esteem or on the contrary, the absence of some special esteem in comparison with other vessels). Here is a continuation of the preceding thought: whether God is responsible for the fact that men are different, each with their own capabilities and predispositions. Whether God is sufficiently free to create different, unequal men. And the answer is analogous. Moreover, many differences of men are determined not by God, but by people, or originate out of them, and also in connection with this and out of the damagedness of nature in particular. That people are different and have unequal glory is intended by God, but is this bad? But that people often incorrectly seek and accomplish their glory originates out of themselves, not out of God.

Romans IX, 22 — 'ἤνεγκεν ἐν πολλῇ μακροθυμίᾳ σκεύη ὀργῆς κατηρτισμένα εἰς ἀπώλειαν' — 'enenken en polle makrothymia skeue orges katertismena eis apoleian' - [He] endured in much long-suffering vessels of wrath fitted into destruction; [He] held in much long-suffering vessels of wrath ready into destruction. Different variants of translation are possible. The key thought, which an unsuccessful translation can distort and pervert, is that the Lord in no way created men or angels into destruction, but is long-suffering and endures (holds) them, although they are ready into destruction. Mortal sin makes a man or entire communities that commit it ready for destruction and only God's long-suffering withholds them away from immediate death.

Romans IX, 23 — 'ἃ προητοίμασεν εἰς δόξαν' — 'ha proetoimasen eis doxan' - which He prepared beforehand into glory; which He destined beforehand into glory. Here it is spoken not about that God chooses who shall be saved even earlier than salvation, but that through cooperation with God's grace, which calls people, all people, into salvation, people are prepared into the acceptance of salvation, and this happens earlier than the actual conversion and salvation itself. This process itself is named here preparation beforehand or destination beforehand, because God prepares for the people glory through their participation in His glory, into which all are called. People receive from God (and not otherwise) God's grace and glory not outside God's preparation beforehand, but during and as its consequence, for it is impossible to rise unto God's glory without being brought there by God. Romans IX, 24 — 'ἐξ Ἰουδαίων ἀλλὰ καὶ ἐξ ἐθνῶν' — 'ex ioudaion alla kai ex ethnon' - out of Jews as well as also out of Gentiles. The preposition 'ek' in its form before a subsequent vowel indicates here that these people are representatives of nations.

Romans IX, 25 — 'ἐν τῷ Ὡσηὲ' — 'en to Hosee' - in Hosea. That is, in the book of the prophet Hosea. Where.

Romans IX, 26 — 'ἐν τῷ τόπῳ' — 'en to topo' - in the place. Where.

Romans IX, 30, 32 — 'δικαιοσύνην δὲ τὴν ἐκ πίστεως' — 'dikaiosynen de ten ek pisteos' - and righteousness, the [one] out of faith. 'ἐξ ἔργων' - 'ex ergon' - out of works. The preposition 'ek' in different forms indicates here that it concerns righteousness which acts as an organic continuation and manifestation of faith, and also about that which acts as a consequence of performed deeds (and may not necessarily have faith).

Romans IX, 31 — 'Ἰσραὴл δὲ διώκων νόμον δικαιοσύνης εἰς νόμον οὐκ ἔφθασεν' — 'israel de diokon nomon dikaiosynes eis nomon ouk ephthasen' - literally: but Israel, pursuing a law, into righteousness in Law did not arrive (that is, did not attain in the journey as the final point of destination). This translation also is more logical than many others where there appears a law of righteousness alongside a Law. The problem is not in the unattainability of a law of righteousness (actually, what is it? Where does Paul speak about it else?), but in that righteousness remained unattainable after the Law outside Christ.

Romans IX, 33 — 'ἐν Σιὼν' — 'en Sion' - in Zion. Where.

If one looks upon the very examples that Paul brings, upon their internal logic, then it becomes clear that the thought about that God determines everything is not here.

Let us look upon the clay and the potter: the potter has a design of what he wishes to obtain as a result out of his clay. He forms it so as to obtain the desired result. So also God — He has a design regarding the creation; upon this design the creation itself by itself did not influence, for it was not there yet then when the Lord was pondering it. But upon this design influenced God's desire to include into the free blessed eternal life in God also the creation. Hence there is in the creation a goal — it is determined by God, but God does not force the free creation to accomplish this goal. Yes, He contributes into this and does that which depends from Him (like that potter), but unlike the clay, men and angels have freedom and can not become similar to God.

Another example: the history of the exit of the Hebrews out of Egypt from the slavery of the pharaoh. Paul quotes Exodus IX, 16. If one reads carefully that passage completely, then God reproaches the pharaoh for unbelief, saying also that He preserved him and the people, who already earlier deserved punishment, even until the time of the exit of the Hebrews out of Egypt, so as to show His glory in this. Not God made the pharaoh cruel, but God restrained His wrath upon the cruel pharaoh even until the time of the exit of the Hebrews.

Moreover, when Paul quotes Old Testament texts, then it is necessary to remember that the Old Testament people almost did not distinguish God's will and God's permission (about which I wrote earlier frequently, in particular regarding the book of the prophet Isaiah in Great Lent). Therefore, that God allowed the pharaoh and the Egyptians to rule over the Hebrews until a certain time does not mean that God wished this. And does not justify the Egyptians.

Still one can look upon this out of another side — God granted to people capabilities into different and at times in something opposite virtues and perfections — for example, to multiply, but also the capability to be in purity. Such Divine generosity in gifts creates the preconditions for the freedom of people, but also requires from them an skillful management of the forces and capabilities of their nature — a human being must take care with God's help which capabilities of nature and when are more necessary than others. To a human being can be necessary firmness in many cases, but also is necessary softness in many other cases. Both are necessary, but they are mutually exclusive (at least to a certain degree). God's will and God's permission regarding the manifestations of different qualities are for our freedom, but in no way release men away from responsibility for how they accomplish their freedom. And the Old Testament texts simply do not distinguish in God the one and the other.

(Matthew XI, 2-15)
Matthew XI, 2 — 'ἐν τῷ δεσμωτηρίῳ' — 'en to desmoterio' - in the prison. Where.

Matthew XI, 6 — 'μακάриός ἐστιν ὃς ἐὰν μὴ σκανδαλισθῇ ἐν ἐμοί' — 'makarios estin hos hean me skandalisthe en emoi' - blessed is he whosoever shall not be caused to stumble in Me. That is, he who shall be able to accept Christ such as He is, entirely, completely, unconditionally, indubitably. This is possible only for a man who by faith is capable of accepting Christ and who in many things is similar to Him.

Matthew XI, 7 — 'εἰς τὴν ἔρημον' — 'eis ten eremon' - into the wilderness. Where to. 'ὑπὸ ἀνέμου σαλευόμενον' - 'hypo anemou saleuomenon' - shaken by wind. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates here that this is the passive voice of the participle that translates as shaken.

Matthew XI, 8 — 'ἄνθρωπον ἐν μαλακοῖς ἠμφιεσμένον;' — 'anthropon en malakois emphiesmenon?' - a man in soft [garments] clothed?. 'ἐν τοῖς οἴκοις τῶν βασιλέων' - 'en to iois oikois ton basileon' - in the houses (palaces) of the kings. Where.

Matthew XI, 11 — 'ἐν γεννητοῖς γυναικῶν' — 'en gennetois gynaikon' - among [the] born of women. Where, among whom. 'ἐν τῇ βασιλείᾳ τῶν οὐρανῶν' - 'en te basileia ton ouranon' - in the kingdom of the heavens. Where.

Matthew XI, 12 — 'ἀπὸ δὲ τῶν ἡμερῶν Ἰωάννου' — 'apo de ton hemeron Ioannou' - and from the days of John. The preposition 'apo' indicates here the beginning of counting, that is, the glad tidings of the Kingdom of God that came in the Person of Jesus Christ began with the beginning of the preaching of John.

John after the Baptism of Jesus testified about Him to others and had a Revelation from God regarding Jesus. Therefore it is considered that his disciples he sent so that they also might be convinced who Jesus is.

The answer of Jesus to them is closely connected with the fact that John, being a great prophet, nevertheless, did not perform any miracle. And Christ, after him, performed many wonderful miracles with many meanings — for the salvation and instruction of people, into the glory of God and other things. Thus, the absence of miracles of John during his life served the greater glory of Christ, God. An example of how with the Lord the absence in a man of something glorious can serve God's glory, and therefore also the glory of this man.

The testimony of Jesus confirms the fulfillment of many prophecies in the Old Testament about John the Baptist.

Concerning the reed and the wind, there is an explanation at the link:
https://www.facebook.com/Oleksandr.S.Zhabenko/posts/
Quoting:
What went ye out into the wilderness to see? A reed shaken with the wind? (Matthew XI, 7). Further the Lord speaks such that it becomes clear that a prophet precisely is not a reed shaken by the wind. This image, this metaphor testifies about that in spiritual life there is a wind, our fluctuations, uncertainty and that which is connected with them. Then for a prophet such is not characteristic: he or she is not an uncertain person, in them there is no uncertainty, fluctuation. Still in the Old Testament it was said: if the words of a prophet shall not be accomplished, it is not necessary to fear them (to consider a prophet or prophetess). Fluctuations, doubts, uncertainty — to a prophet or prophetess are not characteristic.

End of quote.
