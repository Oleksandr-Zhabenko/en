Liturgy:
(Romans XII, 1-3)
Romans XII, 1 — in the original, there is no comparison as a reasonable service, but simply a continuation of the phrase: a reasonable service. A living, holy, acceptable sacrifice to God — emphasizes complete, total devotion to God (hence sacrifice), and at the same time — the preservation of life in the abundance of its non-sinful manifestations, renewal up to holiness, and the elevation of relations with God to the level of pleasing the All-Holy God. Such a combination requires reason, wisdom, grace, as well as readiness for service, and the service to God itself.

Romans XII, 2 — 'μὴ συνσχηματίζεσθε τῷ αἰῶνι τούτῳ' — 'me synakhematizesthe to aioni touto' - do not be conformed [those who take the form, yielding] to this age. That is, do not adapt to the shifting temporal conventions of the transient world. 'μεταμορφοῦσθε τῇ ἀνακαινώσει τοῦ νοός' - 'metamorphousthe te anakainosei tou voos' - be transformed by the renewing of the mind — in this context, it can be seen that the pagans proposed to be fully corresponding to the world, to adapt well to the world and its changes, to be entirely modern, immersed in the fluidity and variability of time and the world, and for this, they used the mind and considered such changes a transformation of the form of the mind. In contrast, Paul proposes a different renewal of the mind for the faithful — not in adaptation, but in sanctification. 'εἰς τὸ δοκιμάζειν ὑμᾶς' - 'eis to dokimazein hymas' - so that you may cognize [experientially know, test, witness to others]. 'τί τὸ θέλημα τοῦ θεοῦ, τὸ ἀγαθὸν καὶ εὐάρεστον καὶ τέλειον' - 'ti to thelema tou theou, to agathon kai euareston kai teleion' - what is the will [that which God truly wants, and not just permits] of God, good, and acceptable, and perfect [complete] — here it concerns precisely God's will, meaning what God truly wants for people, and more generally — for creation. Living according to Paul's words leads to cognition in one's own experience God's will as good, acceptable, and perfect.

Romans XII, 3 — 'ἐν ὑμῖν μὴ ὑπερφρονεῖν παρ’ ὃ δεῖ φρονεῖν' — 'en hymin me hyperphronein par ho dei phronein' - among you not to think highly of oneself [not to be arrogant], in comparison with what is necessary to think — that is, not to be arrogant, conceited, boastful, proud, etc., in comparison with how one ought to think. The preposition 'para' in its form before the subsequent 'ho' here is with the accusative case and indicates that it concerns a juxtaposition, a comparison. It is necessary to juxtapose the state of the mind with how it ought to be. 'φρονεῖν εἰς τὸ σωφρονεῖν ἑκάστῳ ὡς ὁ θεὸς ἐμέρισεν μέτροн πίστεως' - 'phronein eis to sophronein ekasto os o theos emerisen metron pisteos' - to think [coming to] in sobriety, prudence, wisdom each one, as God has dealt a measure of faith; to think for sobriety, prudence, wisdom each one, as God has dealt a measure of faith — polysemantic words that are difficult to translate aptly. It must be remembered that through faith a person can be elevated to God, see the work about that at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/PowerOfHuman.html
therefore, Paul says here that we should combine prudence and, at the same time, true faith, so that humbly, meaning according to the correct measure (the word humility in some Slavic languages originates from the word measure; to measure), we act and live as God gives as His gift. That is, a person who by faith according to the words of Jesus Christ (see the link above) moves mountains while maintaining sobriety and humility — fully fulfills these words of Paul, violating them in no way.

A theologically rich passage, which is why the reading is concise and short.

Paul teaches a reasonable service to God, which, besides the mind, integrates the whole person in their life. In particular, the body is directly mentioned.

The apostle also calls God's will good, bringing joyful satisfaction, and perfect, complete. If a person performs this reasonable service, they discern God's will, God's grace, and God. Cognizes [knows] also means unites with. Here it generally concerns the blessed life of the faithful in Christ.

Paul also teaches humble wisdom, to have a sober and true opinion regarding oneself and everything.

According to the measure of faith — a humble person has a more or less stable opinion of themselves, knows themselves well, particularly through self-knowledge. But most of all, their perception changes… through the knowledge of faith, through the knowledge of the truth of faith, through the knowledge of God and His action in life. These are unique, most significant degrees of freedom in the language of physics. And therefore, the measure of a person's thought about themselves becomes their faith, the degree of their knowledge of this faith, its depth, its assimilation in their own life and the lives of others. In a similar way, in true love, a person begins to associate themselves, to connect themselves with this love; this kind of becomes their predominant measure of themselves.

(Matthew X, 37-XI, 1)
Matthew X, 39 — 'ὁ εὑρὼν τὴν ψυχὴν αὐτοῦ' — 'ho heuron ten psykhen autou' - he who finds [hence the word Eureka!] his soul. 'ὁ ἀπολέσας τὴν ψυχὴν αὐτοῦ ἕνεκεν ἐμοῦ εὑρήσει αὐτήν' - 'o apolesas ten psykhen autou heneken Emou heuresei auten' - he who loses his soul for My sake will find it. Here it concerns a person's life as a find, meaning a creative, actualized, rich life — a person who does not co-create life with Christ will waste and lose it, but the one who co-creates with Christ, for His sake, so that they are even ready to lose — will in fact find what they seek — a full, creative, actualized, as well as eternal life.

Matthew X, 40 — 'ἀποστείλαντά με' — 'aposteilanta Me' - sent Me. The prefix 'apo' emphasizes that the Son of God entered the world, as if leaving the Father and the Spirit, meaning He became as if distant, took the form of a servant, as Paul says about it in another place. At the same time, He did not cease to be God, nor did He leave the Father and the Spirit, being inseparable from Them.

Matthew X, 41, 42 — 'εἰς ὄνομα' — 'eis onoma' - into the name of; for the name of. Both translation variants are possible, which are identical in meaning. A set expression that emphasizes the movement toward recognition.

Matthew XI, 1 — 'ἐν ταῖς πόλεσιν αὐτῶν' — 'en tais polesin auton' - in their cities. Where.

Here Christ says that whoever seeks to have (obtain, find, build, etc.) their life outside the Gospel, like the prodigal son in the parable, such a person will lose their life. If, however, for the sake of fulfilling God's will, for the sake of the Gospel, for the sake of Christ, someone loses something of their own, such a person will preserve it in eternity in the best sense, which is spoken of further.

What a person does for the sake of God, they will never lose. God will repay a hundredfold for every true good done in His name, according to the Gospel. Even if the person later refuses, the good done in God will not disappear and will not be rejected, but God will reward the person in one way or another. This, as I wrote earlier, does not mean that the person will necessarily be saved, but it means that God will not forget and will not despise that actual good. For Matthew, who speaks several times in the Gospel on the topic of heavenly property and reward, treasures, this was personally topical as a former employee of the financial sphere (in modern terms).
