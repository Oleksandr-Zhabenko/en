Acts I, 7 — 'εθετο' — 'etheto' — placed (in a passive or horizontal position). That is, times and epochs do not actively influence the Second Coming of Jesus Christ — only God has actively determined (established) that time. One may also put it thus: we are not to concern ourselves at all with the question of time in this sense — time in itself has no influence whatsoever on the Second Coming.

The story of the Ascension of Jesus Christ to heaven from the perspective of the apostles as earthly witnesses.

At the beginning of the Acts of the Holy Apostles, Luke addresses Theophilus — who is considered to have been a wealthy and educated Christian of Greek origin, a friend of Luke. The first book is the Gospel of Luke, and the second is Acts (some call Acts the Gospel of the Holy Spirit, since it describes how He acts in the Church). Luke, my beloved saint, is the author of both these books.

(Luke XXIV, 36-53)
Luke XXIV, 36 — 'ἐν μέσῳ αὐτῶν' — 'en meso auton' - in their midst. Common expression.

Luke XXIV, 38 — 'ἀναβαίνουσιν ἐν τῇ καρδίᾳ ὑμῶν' — 'anabainousin en te kardia hymon' - they rise (grow, go upward) in your hearts. Where. That is, those thoughts gain greater weight and attract attention — they prompt such thinking and gradually fill the consciousness.

Luke XXIV, 41 — 'ἔτι δὲ ἀπιστούντων αὐτῶν ἀπὸ τῆς χαρᾶς καὶ θαυμαζόντων' — 'eti de apistounton auton apo tes kharas kai thaumazonton' - yet while they disbelieved from joy and wondered. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that joy was not the true cause of the unbelief — the true cause was their prior belief that Christ was to be found dead in the tomb; joy accompanied their unbelief and wonder — that is, here a moment of inertia is described: they could not believe instantly, a transition was needed. The preposition 'apo' indicates here a non-principal, accompanying cause that is not connected with the essence of the matter but with something else. See more at the links:

Luke XXIV, 44 — 'πάντα τὰ γεγραμμένα ἐν τῷ νόμῳ Μωϋσέως καὶ προφήταις καὶ ψαλμοῖς' — 'panta ta gegrammena en to nomo Moyseos kai prophetais kai psalmois' - all things written in the Law of Moses, and in the Prophets, and in the Psalms. Where. In the entire Old Testament, the Scriptures.

Luke XXIV, 46 — 'καὶ ἀναστῆναι ἐκ νεκρῶν τῇ τρίτῃ ἡμέρᾳ' — 'kai anastenai ek nekron te trite hemera' - and to rise (to be resurrected) from the dead on the third day. The preposition 'ek' indicates here whence the transition took place — from the dead to the living.

Luke XXIV, 47 — 'μετάνοιαν εἰς ἄφεσιν ἁμαρτιῶν' — 'metanoian eis aphesin hamartion' - repentance for the forgiveness of sins; a change of mind for the liberation (from) sins. The preposition 'eis' indicates here the purpose of the action. 'εἰς πάντα τὰ ἔθνη' - 'eis panta ta ethne' - into all nations; for all nations. The preposition 'eis' indicates here the spreading among all nations, and also that this is needful and important for all nations — that is, for all people. 'ἀπὸ Ἱερουσαλήμ' - 'apo Hierousalem' - from Jerusalem. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that what is spoken of is the starting point of the reckoning — that is, this preaching and its beginning constitute an entirely new stage in history.

Luke XXIV, 49 — 'ἐν τῇ πόλει' — 'en te polei' - in the city. Where. 'ἕως οὗ ἐνδύσησθε ἐξ ὕψους δύναμιν' - 'heos hou endysesthe ex hypsous dynamin' - until you are clothed from on high with power. The word order indicates the double meaning intended by the author — you will be clothed from on high and with power from on high. That is, the disciples are here between the Height and the Power — between the Father and the Spirit, which points to the possibly maximum accessible closeness to the entire Trinity, just as Christ as the Son of God is in the Father and the Spirit. That is, the disciples will be close to the Father to such a degree that they will be closer to God than those signs and miracles and powers given to them and for others, whilst this will be accomplished by the direct action of the Holy Spirit in order to lead all to the Father. The image of mutual movement from and to, towards one another, has a direct analogy with breathing — exhalation and inhalation — and in general points to the creation of the human being, and therefore to the new creation promised by God. Luke XXIV, 51 — 'ἐν τῷ εὐλογεῖν αὐτὸν αὐτοὺς διέστη ἀπ' αὐτῶν' — 'en to eulogein auton autous dieste ap auton' - whilst He blessed them, He was parted from them (that is, He began to ascend into the heaven). When. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that they ultimately ceased to see Christ as He ascended bodily into the heaven.
