Liturgy:
(Romans I, 1-7, 13-17)
The reading of the Epistles of the holy apostles begins — which will continue until the next Great Lent. The beginning of the Epistle of the apostle Paul to the Romans.

Romans I, 1 — 'ἀφωρισμένος εἰς εὐαγγέλιον θεοῦ' — 'aphorismenos eis euangelion theou' - set apart for the Gospel (Good News) of God. The preposition 'eis' indicates here the purpose. What is meant is one chosen in a particular manner, entirely consecrated to the Gospel.

Romans I, 2 — 'ἐν γραφαῖς ἁγίαις' — 'en graphais hagiais' - in the holy Scriptures. Where.

Romans I, 3 — 'ἐκ σπέρματος Δαυεὶδ κατὰ σάρκα' — 'ek spermatos Daueid kata sarka' - from the seed of David according to the flesh; from the seed of David in accordance with the flesh. Both translations are possible — the first points to the Incarnation of the Son of God as a Descendant of King David (according to God's promise), and the second — that He received a body in full, with our human infirmities, except for sin.

Romans I, 4 — 'ἐν δυνάμει' — 'en dynamei' - in power; with power. Both translations are possible — the first indicates that divine power, including miracles, accompanied the ministry of Jesus, and the second — that divine power manifested the divinity of Christ to a significant degree. 'κατὰ πνεῦμα ἁγιωσύνης ἐξ ἀναστάσεως νεκρῶν' - 'kata pneuma hagiosynes ex anastaseos nekron' - according to the Spirit of holiness, from the resurrection of the dead; in accordance with the Spirit of holiness from the resurrection of the dead. Various translations are possible, but the resurrection of the dead points to the action of Christ not directly concerning Himself but concerning the deceased. That is, what is spoken of is not the Resurrection of Christ Himself, but how that Resurrection affected people — its saving significance. The Resurrection of Christ is also important for people in view of the fact that in a similar manner Christ will raise the dead on the last day — and this resurrection will also clothe people in incorruption, which is connected here with the Spirit of holiness (the Holy Spirit).

Romans I, 5 — 'εἰς ὑπακοὴν πίστεως' — 'eis hypakoen pisteos' - for the obedience of faith; unto the obedience of faith. The preposition 'eis' has two meanings here simultaneously — to make people fulfillers of God's will through faith, and also to bring people to the state of fulfilling God's will according to faith, in accordance with faith, through faith. 'ἐν πᾶσιν τοῖς ἔθνεσιν' - 'en pasin tois ethnesin' - in all nations; among all nations. Where — among whom.

Romans I, 6 — 'ἐν οἷς' — 'en hois' - in whom; among whom. Where — among whom. The faithful are named as being in the nations, among the nations.

Romans I, 7, 15 — 'ἐν Ῥώμῃ' — 'en Rhome' - in Rome. Where. 'εἰρήνη ἀπὸ θεοῦ πατρὸς ἡμῶν καὶ κυρίου Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ' - 'eirene apo theou patros emon kai kyriou Iesou Christou' - peace from (peace as a divine gift, a manifestation of God's righteousness and love, at the same time connected with the life and faith of the faithful themselves) God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ. The use of the preposition 'apo' points to God's gift to people. See more on this at the link:
https://www.instagram.com/oleksandr_zhabenko/p/DDr3toZNcd2/

Romans I, 13 — 'καὶ ἐν ὑμῖν καθὼς καὶ ἐν τοῖς λοιποῖς ἔθνεσιν' — 'kai en hymin kathos kai en tois loipois ethnesin' - and among you, just as among the rest of the nations. Where — among whom.

Romans I, 16 — 'εἰς σωτηρίαν' — 'eis soterian' - unto salvation; for salvation. The preposition 'eis' indicates here the purpose.

Romans I, 17 — 'ἐκ πίστεως εἰς πίστιν, καθὼς γέγραπται, ὁ δὲ δίκαιος ἐκ πίστεως ζήσεται' — 'ek pisteos eis pistin, kathos gegraptai, ho de dikaios ek pisteos zesetai' - from faith unto faith, as it is written: 'But the righteous shall live from faith'. Paul writes about the origin of the life of the righteous — it is a continuation and manifestation of their faith. He does not say that faith here is the source of life in itself as such (which one might think from certain unclear translations), but means that the life of the righteous is an organic continuation and logical consequence of their faith — whilst at the same time being a movement towards the future realisation of faith as the awaited Kingdom of God. In these words faith is the starting point of life, and in the preceding words it is also the destination of the movement — but now accomplished, that is, to a certain degree reached and realised. A person who has fulfilled God's will and the commandments is righteous, whilst their state may fully be called new faith into which they have arrived through fulfilment and life. Two meanings are also visible here: the righteous will live from faith, but also the righteous from faith will live. That is, what is spoken of is both righteousness and simultaneously righteousness from faith — from faith that is an organic continuation, realisation, and development of faith.

Paul, after the solemn greeting and confession of faith in Jesus Christ the Son of God, speaks of His intention in writing the Epistle, which in part compensates for the fact that he had not yet come to the Romans at that time.

The Epistle is in a certain sense like Paul himself — though of course a text cannot replace the person.

Paul also speaks of the significance of the Gospel, the Good News of Christ.

(Matthew IV, 25–V, 13)
The reading of the Gospel of Matthew begins. The events connected with the Nativity and Baptism are omitted, since there will be readings concerning them during the Christmas season.

Matthew IV, 25 — 'ἀπὸ τῆς Γαλιλαίας καὶ Δεκαπόλεως καὶ Ἱεροσολύμων καὶ Ἰουδαίας καὶ πέραν τοῦ Ἰορδάνου' — 'apo tes Galilaias kai Dekapoleos kai Hierosolymon kai Ioudaias kai peran tou Iordanou' - from Galilee, and the Decapolis, and Jerusalem, and Judaea, and from the Transjordan (the lands beyond the territories adjoining the Jordan). The preposition 'apo' indicates that people were leaving their home places.

Matthew V, 1 — 'εἰς τὸ ὄρος' — 'eis to oros' - into the mountain; onto the mountain. Common expression.

Matthew V, 12 — 'ἐν τοῖς οὐρανοῖς' — 'en tois ouranois' - in the heavens. Here in the plural — the spiritual heavens are meant. Also according to what is written at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/01062026.html
one may see that this glory and reward is a manifestation of God's will.

Matthew V, 13 — 'ἐν τίνι ἁλισθήσεται;' — 'en tini alisthesetai?' - with what will it be salted? That is, what else can give it the property of that which it itself is to be the source of. If the source of saltiness and the saltiest of things becomes unsalty, what can make it salty again? 'εἰς οὐδὲν ἰσχύει' - 'eis ouden iskhyei' - it is fit for nothing; it serves no purpose; it is of no use for anything. That is, it no longer has that saltiness, that power of saltiness — and has no other powers either. 'καταπατεῖσθαι ὑπὸ τῶν ἀνθρώπων' - 'katapateisthai hypo ton anthropon' - to be trampled by people. The preposition 'hypo' here with the genitive case indicates the passive voice to be trampled. Salt has a value, a task, a purpose that makes it needed and desired by people — and similarly with the disciples of Christ. But if the disciples lose their state of discipleship to Christ, then that which is valuable in them and needed by others is lost — and in that case their significance will become less than that of those around them. The beginning of the Sermon on the Mount in Matthew — the nine beatitudes.

There are many fine interpretations and explanations of them; some brief ones are at the link:
https://parafia.org.ua/biblioteka/hrystyyanstvo-virovchennya-ta-tradytsiji/zakon-bozhyj/chastyna-tretya-svyaschenna-istoriya-staroho-ta-novoho-zavitu/#toc--24

It is also fitting today to recall that the beatitudes also form a kind of ladder — the later ones presuppose the attainment of the earlier ones. For example, to have a pure heart one must have poverty of spirit and meekness, and must desire righteousness and be merciful. And to purify it, one must repent and walk the path of repentance.

Also, in my view, the nine beatitudes may provisionally be divided into three groups of three (similarly to how the nine known angelic orders are divided into three ranks). In that case the first three correspond most to repentance and purification, the next three to devoutness, and the last three to Christian glory.

I also wish to say that in the Ukrainian language the word 'πραεις' (the third beatitude), as is well known, has no fully satisfactory and precise equivalent. Saint John Climacus in The Ladder explains it as the capacity and virtue of not being irritated oneself and not irritating others through insolence. It is thus very closely related to freedom from anger and absence of malice.

In my view, in contemporary Ukrainian it makes sense to translate it as humane — Blessed are the humane, for they shall inherit the earth — bearing in mind the fact that the Lord said that this virtue and humility are above all what one must learn from him.
