Liturgy:
(Acts XV, 35-41)
Acts XV, 35 — 'ἐν Ἀντιοχείᾳ' — 'en Antiokheia' - in Antioch. Where.

Acts XV, 36 — 'ἐν αἷς' — 'en hais' - in which; where. Common expression. Where.

Acts XV, 38 — 'τὸν ἀποστάντα ἀπ' αὐτῶν ἀπὸ Παμφυλίας' — 'ton apostanta ap auton apo Pamphylias' - the one who had withdrawn from them from Pamphylia. The preposition 'apo' is used twice, indicating that John (Mark) had entirely left them, and similarly had left Pamphylia. 'μὴ συνελθόντα αὐτοῖς εἰς τὸ ἔργον' - 'me synelthonta autois eis to ergon' - who had not accompanied them for the work. The preposition 'eis' indicates here the purpose of the activity.

Acts XV, 39 — 'ἐγένετο δὲ παροξυσμὸς ὥστε ἀποχωρισθῆναι αὐτοὺς ἀπ' ἀλλήλων' — 'egeneto de paroxysmos hoste apokhorithenai autous ap allelon' - but there arose a sharp disagreement, so that they parted from one another. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that they entirely separated from one another, so that subsequently they were no longer together in journeys and apostolic work. The presence of fellowship and ministry, and of the gifts of the Holy Spirit, does not mean complete unanimity in all things among people. At the same time one must recall the words of Christ about what will happen if two or three of the disciples agree to pray about some matter. See at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/PowerOfHuman.html
'εἰς Κύπρον' — 'eis Kypron' — to Cyprus. Whither.

Acts XV, 40 — 'παραδοθεὶς τῇ χάριτι τοῦ κυρίου ὑπὸ τῶν ἀδελφῶν' — 'paradotheis te khariti tou kyriou hypo ton adelphon' - having been committed (entrusted) to the grace of the Lord by the brothers. The preposition 'hypo' here with the genitive case indicates the passive voice of the participle in the expression having been committed.

(John X, 27-38)
John X, 28 — 'εἰς τὸν αἰῶνα' — 'eis ton aiona' - for ever. Common expression. 'ἐκ τῆς χειρός μου' - 'ek tes kheiros mou' - from my hand. Whence. There is no one stronger than Christ.

John X, 29 — 'ἐκ τῆς χειρὸς τοῦ πατρός' — 'ek tes kheiros tou patros' - from the Father's hand. Analogous to verse 28 — whence. There is no one stronger than the Father.

John X, 32 — 'ἐκ τοῦ πατρός' — 'ek tou patros' - from the Father. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that the works of Christ originate from the Father in the sense that they manifest the Father — they occur according to the Council of the Trinity. Christ performs those works, but they reveal and manifest the Father. See more concerning the Council of the Trinity at the links:

John X, 34 — 'ἐν τῷ νόμῳ ὑμῶν' — 'en to nomo hymon' - in your Law. Where.

John X, 35 — 'εγενετο' — 'egeneto' — came into being, began to be, became, arose. That is, in effect Scripture calls gods those to whom God spoke His word. Why so? Above all, God addresses rational beings whom He has endowed with the fact that they are hypostases of a rational nature possessing eternal being after their coming into existence (people and angels). In general Christ here cites Psalm 81 (82), 6. The entire psalm is devoted to the fact that people are described by the word gods and sons (children) of the Most High, whilst at the same time being subject to sin and death as sinful and mortal people. There is also a similar passage where a human being is called god in the book of Exodus: Exodus VII, 1. There God makes Moses a god for Pharaoh, and Aaron his prophet (the prophet of Moses as god). Concerning the thought about gods, Saint Basil the Great said that people are gods who are led into captivity (by sins, passions, and death). From this one may also see why the divinisation of the person by grace, of which theology speaks, is important (on account of the depth of the theme and the limited space I shall not dwell on it in detail now).

John X, 36 — 'εἰς τὸν κόσμον' — 'eis ton kosmon' - into the world. Whither.

John X, 38 — 'ὅτι ἐν ἐμοὶ ὁ πατὴρ κἀγὼ ἐν τῷ πατρί' — 'hoti en emoi ho pater kago en to patri' - that in Me is the Father and I in the Father. Perichoresis in the Trinity. Here I shall note the thought that Christ is defending — that a person can be of God. He is not even so much defending Himself (since He is citing a psalm that concerns people more than the Son of God) as doing this for the sake of the faithful, who also address God's word to other people. The seventh beatitude is also recalled here, which promises to peacemakers the name sons of God (Matthew V, 9). One may also say that called here deeply resonates with these words of Christ about God's word. One may also say that here this is not simply a naming or recognition, but a manifestation of such power of God's word and of the words of those people (but not words alone) that makes them truly like the Son of God. Also implicitly here there is reference to the veneration of the saints.
