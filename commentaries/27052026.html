Liturgy:
(Acts XXIII, 1-11)
Acts XXIII, 6 — 'ἐν τῷ συνεδρίῳ' — 'en to synedrio' - in the council. Where.

Acts XXIII, 9 — 'ἐν τῷ ἀνθρώπῳ τούτῳ' — 'en to anthropo touto' - in this human being; in this person's case. Where — in whom, in whose case. Various translations are possible. The first indicates that Paul was in the opinion of this group innocent, whilst the second suggests they considered he was not the ringleader of troublemakers — that is, he was not inciting the people, but acting purely on his own account. Depending on this understanding, the overall assessment of their position will differ — but one thing is clear: if the support of the Pharisees on the disputed question led them to decide to close their eyes with regard to other earlier accusations, then their position is certainly not impartial, and consequently the trial that took place was in no way just — even from the standpoint of non-Christians.

Acts XXIII, 10 — 'μὴ διασπασθῇ ὁ Παῦλος ὑπ' αὐτῶν' — 'me diaspasthe ho Paulos hyp auton' - lest Paul be torn apart by them. The preposition 'hypo' in its form before the following vowel here with the genitive case indicates the passive voice of the participial phrase (in the original a participle) be torn apart. The prudence of the tribune is also to be noted. 'ἐκ μέσου αὐτῶν' - 'ek mesou auton' - from their midst; from among them. The second variant is more fitting in English. Whence. 'εἰς τὴν παρεμβολήν' - 'eis ten parembolen' - into the fortress; into the location of the military garrison. Whither.

Acts XXIII, 11 — 'ὡς γὰρ διεμαρτύρω τὰ περὶ ἐμοῦ εἰς Ἱερουσαλήμ, οὕτω σε δεῖ καὶ εἰς Ῥώμην μαρτυρῆσαι' — 'hos gar diemartyro ta peri emou eis Hierousalem, houto se dei kai eis Romen martyresai' - for just as you have boldly and willingly testified concerning Me for Jerusalem, so also must you testify for Rome. The preposition 'eis' indicates here for whom Paul was to give his testimony of faith — in effect with a double meaning here: Paul will be judged, but the standing of people at God's true and righteous judgement will also be determined by how they judged Paul — and with him attempted to judge Christ as well. The testimony concerning Christ in court is needed to expose sin — as Christ says in the Gospels — and is the work of the Holy Spirit.

The story of the trial of Paul among the Jews in Jerusalem. The high priest violates the Law, whilst Paul fulfils it — though this costs the apostle greatly.

Paul finds a way to introduce discord among the unjust judges by using the difference in their views, which he had known since his youth. He succeeds in this.

The Lord also praises and strengthens Paul, gives him the promised wisdom through the Holy Spirit, and reveals that Paul is to continue his testimony — not only in Jerusalem but in Rome as well.

(John XVI, 15-23)
John XVI, 15 — 'ἐκ τοῦ ἐμοῦ' — 'ek tou emou' - from My own; from what is Mine. See at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/10042026.html
Here I shall cite in full.
John XVI, 14 — 'ἐκ τοῦ ἐμοῦ λήμψεται' — 'ek tou emou lempsetai' - He will receive from what is Mine; He will take from what is Mine. Various translations are possible here, broadly similar in meaning. A rich significance is implied — on the one hand, what is spoken of is the fact that Christ has prepared on earth and in heaven the descent of the Holy Spirit; on the other — that the Son and the Spirit, as consubstantial with the Father and with One Another, and as all loving One Another, have communion in this. Also, Orthodox Christians understand here that what is spoken of is the common action of the Son and the Spirit for the salvation and divinisation of people. The Spirit will not do something separate from the Son — something other with respect to what the Son does — and similarly the Father. Google Gemini notes that the presence of the neuter article τοῦ transforms the word into an abstract concept or category of possession — 'that which is Mine', 'My possession', 'My truth'. If the emphasis were placed directly on the Person ('from Me'), the text would most likely contain a construction without the definite article — for example, ἐξ ἐμοῦ (ex emou) or παρ' ἐμοῦ (par' emou).

Here one may see that what is Mine does not mean I — the Son of God. In divinity, origin is traced from the hypostasis, not from the nature. The Son is begotten from the Father — not from the nature of the Father. Similarly the Spirit proceeds from the Father — not from the nature of the Father. Therefore this verse vividly testifies to the energetic, active fellowship of the Son and the Spirit, but does not constitute testimony in favour of the well-known Filioque. It is important to note that Google Gemini cites an example from contemporary Catholic theology (including the late Pope John Paul II, who is particularly memorable to me — his visit to Ukraine contributed to and, I believe, was one of the principal reasons for my deep conversion to God in the summer of 2001). Here is the statement of Google Gemini:

Contemporary theological consensus
It is interesting that contemporary Catholic theology has largely acknowledged the insufficiency of using such verses as direct proof of the Filioque. A landmark step in this direction was taken through the efforts of Pope John Paul II, who sought reconciliation between the traditions.
In 1995 the Vatican issued an explanatory document concerning the procession of the Holy Spirit. In it the acknowledgement is made that the Greek word ἐκπόρευσις (procession as from the first source) concerns exclusively the Father. The document affirms that when Jesus says 'He will take from what is Mine', what is spoken of is mutual communion and the transmission of truth within the single divine nature — and not that the Son is the original source (cause) of the Holy Spirit.

John XVI, 17 — 'ἐκ τῶν μαθητῶν αὐτοῦ' — 'ek ton matheton autou' - of His disciples. That is, some of them.

John XVI, 20 — 'ἡ λύπη ὑμῶν εἰς χαρὰν γενήσεται' — 'he lype hymon eis kharan genesetai' - your sorrow will become joy; your sorrow will be turned into joy. An oxymoron. The words signify the particular joy from Christ's victory. And also God's transfiguration of the sorrow of the faithful — God will manifest His action so powerfully that it will, like the Taboric light, transform human sorrow into joy in the Lord.

John XVI, 21 — 'εἰς τὸν κόσμον' — 'eis ton kosmon' - into the world. That is, whither the person was born. The image is connected with prophecies.

John XVI, 22 — 'τὴν χαρὰν ὑμῶν οὐδεὶς αἴρει ἀφ' ὑμῶν' — 'ten kharan hymon oudeis airei aph hymon' - no one takes your joy from you. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following 'hy' indicates here that this joy cannot be taken away — the faithful cannot be deprived of this joy; it will always remain, even though it may be combined with other feelings.

John XVI, 23 — 'ἐν ἐκείνῃ τῇ ἡμέρᾳ' — 'en ekeine te hemera' - on that day; in that day. When. 'ἐν τῷ ὀνόματί μου' - 'en to onomati mou' - in My name; with My name. Various translations are possible, which may either complement one another or stand separately. The first indicates that the disciples will ask precisely as Christians, as disciples of Christ — that is, as bearers of His name. The second — that they will ask on the basis of the fact that they are faithful disciples of Christ, Christians.

The readings are focused on the words of Jesus concerning the coming events — His sufferings, death, Resurrection, and appearance to the disciples. But also in the broader perspective — in the history of the Church there will be many examples entirely similar to these events in terms of their resolution. There is a profound dramatic quality in the meaning of Jesus's words: the disciples will weep, wail, and grieve, whilst the world will rejoice — thinking it has overcome Christ. Whereas Christ will rise, will appear to the disciples, and their sorrow will be transformed into the joy of the disciples themselves — through the Victory of Christ over sin and death. The disciples, being in their understanding of the Kingdom of Christ as earthly dominion, could not understand this correctly whilst remaining in their prior views.
