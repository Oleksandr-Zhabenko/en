Liturgy:
(1 Corinthians IV, 5-8)
1 Corinthians IV, 5 — 'ἀπὸ τοῦ θεοῦ' — 'apo tou theou' - from God. The preposition 'apo' emphasizes that this praise will be a gift of God, and also shows how lively God will rejoice in people whom He will praise, much like a sincere, open, and benevolent person openly and distinctly reacts to the good in friends. See also more about praise, beauty, glory, honor, and similar at the links:

1 Corinthians IV, 6 — 'εἰς ἐμαυτὸν' — 'eis emauton' - into myself [to myself]. Where to. 'ἐν ἡμῖν' - 'en hemin' - in us. Here an important thought is that Apollos is completely like-minded with Paul here, being humble. 'μὴ εἷς ὑπὲр τοῦ ἑνὸς φυσιοῦσθε' - 'me heis hyper tou henos physiousthe' - that no one of you be puffed up for one against another (a part of the phrase describing mutual arrogance, pride, vainglory).

1 Corinthians IV, 8 — 'χωρὶς ἡμῶν' — 'khoris hemon' - without us; separately from us. Both variants are correct, although somewhat different by content.

Paul says that when the Lord comes a second time, the hidden things, particularly in the hearts of people, will be revealed, and also every person will have praise from God. This does not mean that everyone will be saved, but God will praise every person. Whether it concerns Christians only or all people — one can think that this applies to all (since there are very many Christians, a significant part of humanity at least calls themselves Christians).

Also, Paul explains how to think so as not to fall into conceit and pride in the mind. If we always remember that we have everything good from God, received from Him, and do not attribute it to ourselves, then this true and humble thought will preserve us from conceit.

(Matthew XIII, 44-54)
Matthew XIII, 44 — 'ἐν τῷ ἀγρῷ' — 'en to agro' - in the field. Where. 'ἀπὸ τῆς χαρᾶς' - 'apo tes kharas' - from joy. The preposition 'apo' emphasizes that the action caused by joy and the joy itself are not the same thing. That is, the true reason why the person went, sold everything, and bought the field is not the joy itself, but the determination to obtain the treasure along with the field. Joy accompanies the decision, but is not in full measure the cause of the action. Such a use of the preposition can be noticed in several other places of Scripture, for example, regarding the maid Rhoda and regarding the joy of the apostles during the appearance of the Resurrected Christ:

Matthew XIII, 47 — 'εἰς τὴν θάλασσαν' — 'eis ten thalassan' - into the sea. Where to. 'ἐκ παντὸς γένους' - 'ek pantos genous' - from all kinds; from every kind; out of every kind. The preposition 'ek' emphasizes that the gathered things are representatives of different things (kinds, species). Since it concerns the salvation of people in the Kingdom of God, it speaks here about the salvation of different people, from different groups of people, from different nations, and similar. It is not understood to the end whether it means that every kind will have saved people. For we see from Scripture that, in particular, the descendants of Cain are not mentioned as righteous, and they also perished in the waters of the flood. Rather, it concerns people of different origins, occupations, nationalities, from different states, different social status, etc. This resonates with what follows, as well as with the reading from the Apostle today about praise to every person from God. Matthew XIII, 48 — 'εἰς ἄγγη' — 'eis ange' - into vessels. Where to.

Matthew XIII, 49 — 'ἐν τῇ συντελείᾳ τοῦ αἰῶνος' — 'en te synteleia tou aionos' - in the end of the world; in the completion of the age. It concerns the Second Coming of Christ in glory, the resurrection of the dead, and the Judgement of God. It is also used earlier in the chapter. 'ἐκ μέσου' - 'ek mesou' - from the midst [out of the midst]. A set expression. An interesting image mathematically — if the wicked are severed from the midst of the righteous, then the set of the righteous must be such that next to a wicked person there must be a righteous one. That is, the set of the righteous in the topological space of the Kingdom of God is dense (mathematically). See more about topological spaces, in particular, at the link:
Dense set. Encyclopedia of Mathematics
http://encyclopediaofmath.org/index.php?title=Dense_set&oldid=42518
This does not mean that there are approximately as many righteous as wicked, but it means that in one's environment there is complete hope to find a righteous person, at least one (possibly more). This gives the world hope, since it does not matter what time or country it is, but these words of Christ are eternal.

Matthew XIII, 50 — 'εἰς τὴν κάμινον τοῦ πυρός' — 'eis ten kaminon tou pyros' - into the furnace of fire. Where to. The preposition 'eis' indicates onto the direction of the action, that is, where to. In general, the construction indicates onto that this is a certain environment of sufferings, of losing life.

Matthew XIII, 52 — 'ἐκ τοῦ θησαυροῦ' — 'ek tou thesaurou' - out of the treasure. Where from.

Matthew XIII, 54 — 'εἰς τὴν πατρίδα' — 'eis ten patrida' - into His own country [patria]. Where to. 'ἐν τῇ συναγωγῇ' - 'en te synagoge' - in the synagogue. Where.

A few short parables joined into one reading with explanations and a conversation with people. Here Jesus Christ is seen precisely as a Teacher.

The Lord shows by His own example what the principles of successful teaching are (Christian, but also in general). One of them — the new is used together with the old, the known. And also the need to combine the new (which is being studied, researched, known) and the old (already known). It must be noted that it is very rare to achieve a generally good combination in everything — more often there is a certain bias to one side or the other, which affects the efficiency and results of teaching.

The main meaning and question of today's parables — the value of the Kingdom of God and the need to enter there.

If a person perceives this value, then they develop the determination to act so as to enter there.

But not all have such determination, and not all will enter.

Also, Jesus shows that the New Testament continues the Old, particularly in parables.
