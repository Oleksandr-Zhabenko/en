Liturgy:
(Romans IX, 1-5)
Romans IX, 1 — 'ἐν Χριστῷ' — 'en CHristo' - in Christ. In Whom. That is, Paul witnesses that he believes that his words are from Christ, from the Holy Spirit, although Christ did not speak directly about this in exactly such words. 'ἐν πνεύματι ἁγίῳ' - 'en pneumati hagio' - in the Holy Spirit. Analogously to what has just been written.

Romans IX, 3 — 'ἀπὸ τοῦ Χριστοῦ' — 'apo tou CHristou' - (away) from Christ. The preposition 'apo' indicates here onto complete separation, which factually means eternal destruction (for complete separation from the Saviour makes salvation impossible). The verb in the form used here can mean both a prayer about oneself and a desire regarding oneself. That is, this is a deeply personal position of the apostle Paul, yet not unique in Sacred history. See about that in detail further.

Romans IX, 5 — 'ἐξ ὧν ὁ Χριστὸς τὸ κατὰ σάρκα' — 'ex hon ho CHristos to kata sarka' - from whom is Christ according to the flesh. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the subsequent 'ho' indicates here onto the origin according to the flesh, that is, Christ is a Jew by origin, a descendant of Abraham and King David, the Promised Messiah (the Greek Christ properly is a translation of Messiah - Mashiach). 'ὁ ὢν ἐπὶ πάντων θεὸς εὐλογητὸς εἰς τοὺς αἰῶνας, ἀμήν' - 'ho on epi panton theos eulogetos eis tous aionas, amen' - Who is over all, God blessed forever. Amen. Christ factually is named the Most High God (a strong testimony about the Most Holy Trinity).

One of the important places for the understanding of Scripture in general, of faith, of God, and also has significance for modern history. In particular, precisely the misunderstanding, erroneous or incomplete understanding of this reading and the topics raised in it is one of the main prerequisites for the emergence and functioning of the concept of the Russian world (better known in Ukraine as russkiy mir).

Quoting:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/15072023.html

Paul witnesses about his love for the Israelites, from whom he himself also originates. He enumerates God's promises to them concerning themselves, but this does not exclude that the adoption unto God must be inherited by other nations also through Christ. Right here the order indicates onto that it concerns the Old Testament promises — up to Christ. But after Christ, these promises in essence extended to all faithful people in Christ. Recall, for example, Christ's words: He who receives a prophet as a prophet will receive a prophet's reward; he who receives a righteous man as a righteous man will receive a righteous man's reward, — spoken for New Testament people (Matthew X, 41).

The desire to suffer instead of other people is in this sense unfeasible. Paul (as well as anyone except Jesus Christ) cannot by his sufferings redeem others from sufferings. But worthy of attention is the great love of Paul for people, even those who persecuted him personally. Such love Scripture mentions, for example, in Moses.

Jesus Christ is the Only Redeemer. He took upon Himself our sins, sufferings, death, God-forsakenness, to save us — the Only sinless One for sinful men and women. As sinless, as the God-man, He should not have experienced sufferings, but by God's will and permission (here they coincide) the sufferings to which the creation was subjected after the sin of people, He took upon Himself. He should not have died, but death, which entered into the world through sin and spread even to those who had not sinned (Romans V, 12-21), He took upon Himself by God's will and by God's permission (here they coincide). He, being the God-man, felt upon Himself (taking upon Himself by God's will and God's permission) our God-forsakenness because of our sins, and by the soul descended into hades, although as sinless He should not have descended thither. All this He did as a sinful one, being without sin, taking upon Himself our sins and bearing our infirmities (Isaiah LIII, 4-5). Paul also witnesses about that, saying: Was Paul (that is, he speaks about himself from the third person) crucified for you? (1 Corinthians I, 11-13). Even if Paul had given himself to sufferings for people, they would not have redeeming power, for there would be no God's will for that, only God's permission, since it is not the will of God that one of these little ones should perish (by the way, the name Paul translates as little) (Matthew XVIII, 12-14). Christ, however, accepted everything voluntarily, He is the Only One Who is such, He came to find (becoming, experiencing all that is characteristic of) and to save (remaining sinless, faithful to God and resurrecting from the dead) that which was lost (sinful people, the creation which suffers without its sins) (Luke XIX, 10).

This is what concerns the question from the side of God (properly the theological component, Divine economy). But in life, self-sacrifice for the sake of other people is possible, for example, such can be the deed of heroes and saints who give themselves, sacrificing, to preserve life or save other people.

It is also necessary to mention the holy apostles, martyrs. Many of them accepted martyrdom as a gift of God. But as mentioned above, their sufferings, even being voluntary, do not have redeeming power. That is, they are not needed by God. God mercies the saints not because of their deeds, but out of His grace through the Redeeming Sacrifice of Christ. It is Christ Who is the Only Redeemer. It is in this sense that there is no God's will for anyone except Christ to suffer. But there is God's will for the faithful to remain faithful. There is God's will in being an example for others. God wants the martyrs and apostles even in sufferings to be faithful, and also that their patience and love, hope, faith be a model for others to imitate. Therefore, God permits their sufferings. Sufferings are not needed by God, but that the faithful accept them brings them closer to God, witnessing to their faithfulness and establishment in the good.

God can even guide the faithful saints to such sufferings (as, for example, according to Tradition was with the holy apostle Peter), but not because He wants the sufferings of others, but because such a path of salvation can be better.

End of quote.

(Matthew IX, 18-26)
Matthew IX, 21 — 'ἐν ἑαυτῇ' — 'en heaute' - in herself. That is, it concerns internal speech.

Matthew IX, 22 — 'ἀπὸ τῆς ὥρας ἐκείνης' — 'apo tes horas ekeines' - from that hour. The preposition 'apo' indicates here onto the beginning of the time count (from what moment of time did such a change occur?).

Matthew IX, 23 — 'εἰς τὴν οἰκίαν τοῦ ἄρχοντος' — 'eis ten oikian tou arkhontos' - into the house of the ruler. Where to.

Matthew IX, 26 — 'εἰς ὅλην τὴν γῆν ἐκείνην' — 'eis holen ten gen ekeinen' - into all that land. The preposition 'eis' indicates here onto the spreading of rumors (it sometimes is used in such a context, since it emphasizes the movement of information, emotions, and thoughts).

The resurrection of the daughter of the Jewish ruler (ruler of the synagogue, another Gospel preserved the name — Jairus), as well as the healing of the woman with an issue of blood. Examples of the faith of the Jewish ruler and the woman. Both witnessed to their faith by deeds, but also confessed verbally.

In other synoptic Gospels there are more additional details.

About some details that are in other Gospels, I also wrote previously, quoting:

The maid is sleeping, — spoken so as not to attract great attention, a miracle must rejoice, but preserve reason, and also not cause a craving for miracles. Talitha, cumi! — these words of Jesus in Aramaic even became the name of a music group. Twelve years — a kind of peak of childhood, when a girl combines in herself both childish, adolescent, and youth traits, because of which parental love for her is especially tender (in this case, of Jairus and the family).

End of quote.

I will add that the woman also suffered from a discharge of blood for twelve years, that is, these two events are connected in time twice.

Acts XVI, 1 — 'εἰς Δέρβην καὶ εἰς Λύστραν' - 'eis Derben kai eis Lystran' - into Derbe and into Lystra. Where to.

Acts XVI, 2 — 'ὃς ἐμαρτυρεῖτο ὑπὸ τῶν ἐν Λύστροις καὶ Ἰκονіῳ ἀδελφῶν' - 'hos emartyreito hypo ton en Lystrais kai Ikonio adelphon' - who was well reported of by the brethren who were in Lystra and Iconium. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates onto the passive voice of the verb was well reported of (by whom).

Acts XVI, 3 — 'ἐν τοῖς τόποις ἐκείνοις' - 'en tais popois ekeinais' - in those places. Where.

Acts XVI, 4 — 'τὰ δόγματα τὰ κεκριμένα ὑπὸ τῶν ἀποστόλων καὶ πρεσβυτέρων τῶν ἐν Ἱεροσολύμοις' - 'ta dogmata ta kekrimena hypo ton apostolon kai presbyteron ton en Hierosolymois' - the decrees that were ordained by the apostles and elders who were in Jerusalem. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates onto the passive voice of the participle ordained (by whom).

Acts XVI, 6 — 'κωλυθέντες ὑπὸ τοῦ ἁγίου πνεύματος λาลῆσαι τὸν λόγον' - 'kolythentes hypo tou hagiou pneumatos lalesai ton logon' - being forbidden by the Holy Spirit to speak the word. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates onto the passive voice of the participle translated as being forbidden (that is, the Holy Spirit forbade to speak the word) (by Whom). 'ἐν τῇ Ἀσίᾳ' - 'en te Asia' - in Asia. Where.

Acts XVI, 7 — 'εἰς τὴν Βιθυνίαν' - 'eis ten Bithynian' - into Bithynia. Where to.

Acts XVI, 8 — 'εἰς Τρῳάδα' - 'eis Troada' - into Troas. Where to.

Acts XVI, 9, 10 — 'εἰς Μακεδονίαν' - 'eis Makedonian' - into Macedonia. Where to.

Acts XVI, 11 — 'ἀπὸ Τρῳάδος' - 'apo Troados' - from Troas. The preposition 'apo' indicates onto that they completely left Troas. 'εἰς Σαμοθρᾴκην' - 'eis Samothraken' - into Samothrace. Where to. 'εἰς Νέαν πόλιν' - 'eis Nean polin' - into Neapolis. Where to.

Acts XVI, 12 — 'εἰς Φιλіππους' - 'eis PHilippous' - into Philippi (a city in Macedonia). Where to. 'ἐν ταύτῃ τῇ πόλει' - 'en taute te polei' - in this city. Where.

Acts XVI, 13 — 'ἔξω τῆς πύλης παρὰ ποταμὸν' - 'exo tes pyles para potamon' - outside the gate alongside a river. The preposition 'para' here is with the accusative and indicates onto that the gate and the way there were beside the river, along the bank of the latter.

Acts XVI, 14 — 'τοῖς λαλουμένοις ὑπὸ Παύλου' - 'tois laloumenois hypo Paulou' - which were spoken by Paul. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates onto the passive voice of the participle were spoken (by whom).

Acts XVI, 15 — 'εἰς τὸν οἶκόν μου' - 'eis ton oikon mou' - into my house. Where to.
