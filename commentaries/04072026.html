Liturgy:
(Romans VIII, 14-21)
Romans VIII, 15 — 'πάλιν εἰς φόβον' — 'palin eis phobon' - again into fear. The preposition 'eis' indicates here onto the goal. See the explanation regarding fear and being God's children at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/DialogueOnWordsChristFear.html
'πνεῦμα υἱοθεσίας, ἐν ᾧ κράζομεν, Ἀββᾶ ὁ πατήρ' — 'Pneuma hyiothesias en ho krazomen Abba ho Pater' — the Spirit of adoption, in Whom we cry: Abba, Father!; the Spirit of adoption, with (by means of) Whom we cry: Abba, Father!. Both variants of translation are possible, but the first has the advantage since the second implies a certain instrumental character of the participation of the Holy Spirit, which is not sufficiently justified. Here an important moment is that the Holy Spirit, extending His action in the faithful, lets them be God's children, at the same time letting them carry out His address into the Father as properly their own. It is necessary to remember that the Father — is the proper Name of the Father, and not a description or characterisation of relationships with others (in the words of one well-known theologian: the Father is the Father for the Holy Spirit, although the Holy Spirit is not a Son for Him), but in the context of adoption this Name also characterises relationships, the faithful become God's children. See also at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/Adoption.html

Romans VIII, 18 — 'εἰς ἡμᾶς' — 'eis hemas' - into us; to us; for us. Different variants of translation are possible, which can complement each other. The preposition 'eis' indicates here onto the coming, change, onset, arrival of the Kingdom of God in glory.

Romans VIII, 19 — 'ἡ γὰр ἀποκαραδοκία τῆς κτίσεως τὴν ἀποκάλυψιν τῶν υἱῶν τοῦ θεοῦ ἀπεκδέχεται' — 'he gar apokaradokia tes ktiseos ten apokalypsin ton hyion tou theou apekdekhetai' - for the earnest expectation of the creation awaiteth the manifestation of the sons of God. The apostle Paul answers onto the question what (whom) the creation awaits. It awaits because it does not completely realize all that is implanted by God, that is, it is directed into God. Why is it spoken not that it awaits God Himself, but the manifestation of the sons of God? Because the living creation strives for closeness with God through closeness with the creation close into God. One can see a certain analogy with that that for nature it is better to be in an ordered illuminated place, surrounded by various natural components (an integral biogeocenosis), than to receive the richness of sunlight without a biogeocenosis. In other words, it is better to be close into God together with other creation than alone. Here it concerns not the depth of relationships (which the creation, being non-personal here, that is, for example, animals, cannot to the end contain and understand, know), but the fullness of life, that with which living nature is rich.

Romans VIII, 20 — 'τῇ γὰρ ματαιότητι ἡ κτίσις ὑπετάγη, οὐχ ἑκοῦσα ἀλλὰ διὰ τὸν ὑποτάξαντα, ἐφ’ ἐλπίδι' — 'te gar mataioteti he ktesis hypetage oukh ekousa alla dia ton hypotaxanta eph elpidi' - for into vanity (aimlessness, that is, a state when high and great development, progress, advancement, movement upward is not attained, when a high goal is not attained) the creation was made subject, not willingly (not from its own desire, not as a manifestation of its own will, consciousness, actualisation and realization of that granted and implanted by God, designed by God), but by reason of him who subjected [it] in hope. A very deep passage with many thoughts, onto which I will only briefly dwell. The first and simplest thought — is that the creation became corruptible not because it so wished itself, but through him who subjected it. Here it is completely unclear who subjected the creation into the incompleteness of the manifestation of God's design. One can think — that these are people, for they sinned (Adam and Eve, then their descendants), but one can also see here God, Who subjected the creation (recall the words of God in Paradise after the fall of people) into a state when the full high Divine design is not carried out. God subjected the creation into a human being, but people by sin subjected the creation into corruption, experiencing it also themselves. People did not hope at the same time onto the manifestation of the glory of the children of God (see the next verse 21), but God assured, promised such a hope into people and creation. This God-granted, Divine hope is a part of God's design which remained to operate also after the sin of people. It leads to the union of people and creation (the sanctification of the latter) with God.

Romans VIII, 21 — 'διότι καὶ αὐτὴ ἡ κτίσις ἐλευθερωθήσεται ἀπὸ τῆς δουλίας τῆς φθорᾶς εἰς τὴν ἐλευθερίαν τῆς δόξης τῶν τέκνων τοῦ θεοῦ' — 'dioti kai aute he ktesis eleutherothesetai apo tes doulias tes phthoras eis ten eleutherian tes doxes ton teknon tou theou' - that also the creation itself shall be delivered from the bondage of corruption into the liberty of the glory of the children of God. Here an important non-obvious moment is present, in particular about the liberty of the glory of the children of God. This is like a promise to the one who yields now, that they will yet conquer. Creation, you will yet conquer corruption and vanity!. Words and a passage which most explicitly in Scripture prompt to think also about something similar into the resurrection of creation or about the continuity of creation, when it exists all together, so that the victory over corruption of the latest new creation is a common victory of the entire creation — the victory of the latest ones is the victory of all. Regarding the liberty of the glory of the children of God — one of my favourite expressions of Scripture — a very rich and eloquent combination — of glory so rich and deep that it can be named the liberty of glory. (Matthew IX, 9-13)
Matthew IX, 10 — 'ἐν τῇ οἰκίᾳ' — 'en te oikia' - in the house. Where.

Matthew IX, 12 — 'ἰσχύοντες' — 'iskhyontes' - strong, whole, capable. There is emphasized the sinful infirmity of a person, at the same time it signifies that such people of whom Christ speaks desire deliverance away from sin, cleansing, a change of life and similar. In relation to Matthew this signifies that he sought another life than he had until then, which is why he quickly and resolutely responded onto the calling by Jesus.

Matthew IX, 13 — see also at the link:
https://www.instagram.com/p/C5kr6Misfmu/

The calling of the apostle Matthew, the former tax collector for the Romans. Also the explanation of Jesus why He frequently was among sinful people, whereas the Pharisees considered that this makes a person themselves sinful and unclean. The quote out of Hosea, which already was mentioned earlier in the question regarding good deeds on a sabbath, witnesses about that God seeks the repentance of sinners and through this — their salvation in Christ, whereas the Pharisees considered that God seeks the keeping of all the precepts of the Law of Moses. For Christ, people are important, and for the Pharisees here — the Law. One can also see that Christ operates from great love, whereas the Pharisees — from a certain habit or tradition. And, in the words of Paul, the Pharisees attained not unto righteousness, because they sought it not from faith (values, and here the value — the people themselves, their salvation and conversion), but as it were from works (certain precepts).

For us the Lord presents an example.

Saint Andrew, Archbishop of Crete:
(Hebrews VIII, 3-6; Matthew VII, 12-21)
Hebrews VIII, 3 — 'εἰς τὸ προσφέρειν δῶρά τε καὶ θυσίας' - 'eis to prospherein dora te kai thysias' - for to offer both gifts and sacrifices. The preposition 'eis' indicates onto the goal of the activity.

Hebrews VIII, 5 — 'ἐν τῷ ὄρει' - 'en to orei' - in the mount; on the mount. Where.

The apostle Paul here continues to compare Christ as the High Priest after the order of Melchizedek and the Old Testament priests by the form of activity. The words about gifts, as well as sacrifices indicate onto the Eucharist — the sacrifice of praise and thanksgiving, the Holy Gifts.

Matthew VII, 13 — 'εἰς τὴν ἀπώλειαν' - 'eis ten apoleian' - into destruction. Where to.

Matthew VII, 14 — 'εἰς τὴν ζωήν' - 'eis ten zoen' - into life. Where to.

The way — is a manner of life. If a person does not align their life with God's will, with the Gospel commandments, then this is a broad way. They then have no truly reliable landmarks, and this makes their way generally broad (they are as if everywhere on the way). Conversely, the need to align life with God's will, with the Gospel, gives very clear landmarks and illuminates the path with true light, and this is a narrow way. Creative attentiveness is necessary — for everything where a person has not aligned their life with God becomes a broad way.
