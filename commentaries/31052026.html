Liturgy:
(Acts II, 1-11)
Acts II, 1 — 'ἐν τῷ συνπληροῦσθαι τὴν ἡμέραν τῆς πεντηκοστῆς' — 'en to synplerousthai ten hemeran tes pentekostes' - in the full completion of the day of Pentecost; when the day of Pentecost was fully completed. That is, after the Jewish feast of Pentecost.

Acts II, 2 — 'ἐγένετο ἄφνω ἐκ τοῦ οὐρανοῦ ἦχος ὥσπερ φερομένης πνοῆς βιαίας καὶ ἐπλήρωσεν ὅλον τὸν οἶκον οὗ ἦσαν καθήμενοι' — 'egeneto aphno ek tou ouranou ekhos hosper pheromenes pnoes biaias kai eplerosen holon ton oikon hou esan kathemenoi' - there came suddenly from heaven a sound (another translation: a noise — see below), as though carried by a mighty wind, and it filled the entire house where they were sitting. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that the sound was truly heavenly — that is, it concerned the spiritual heaven above all, but also the visible physical one as a consequence. A noise filled the house as though of wind — a description of a supernatural phenomenon — that is, approximately: the space where they had gathered began invisibly to sound without evident physical causes, completely filling the entire sound range — but not with physical noise as a somewhat chaotic set of random frequencies, but with the full spectrum of sounds (multi-sound — a divinely ordered totality of all possible sounds) — that is, the Creator of all sounds manifests His presence. Acts II, 5 — 'εἰς Ἱερουσαλὴμ κατοικοῦντες' — 'eis Hierousalem katoikountes' - settled in Jerusalem (for permanent residence). Whither — that is, they had relocated to Jerusalem. 'ἄνδρες εὐλαβεῖς ἀπὸ παντὸς ἔθνους τῶν ὑπὸ τὸν οὐρανόν' - 'andres eulabeis apo pantos ethnous ton hypo ton ouranon' - devout men from every nation under the heaven. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that they had left their peoples for the sake of life in Jerusalem — which points to their dedication to the expectation of the Messiah-Christ. The preposition 'hypo' here with the accusative indicates that under which literally those people were (the sky as the space above their heads).

Acts II, 8 — 'πῶς ἡμεῖς ἀκούομεν ἕκαστος τῇ ἰδίᾳ διαλέκτῳ ἡμῶν ἐν ᾗ ἐγεννήθημεν;' — 'pos hemeis akouomen hekastos te idia dialekto hemon en he egennethemen' - how is it that we each hear our own dialect (our own native language), in which (in which tongue) we were born? People are born in a certain language according to these words. One may understand this as meaning that children, even in the womb, hear the language of their environment and the language of their parents, and are born in it — in the language.

Acts II, 3 — fiery tongues — literally tongues like fire, tongues as it were of fire. See concerning them at the link:
https://www.instagram.com/oleksandr_zhabenko/p/DYtvThnNVWT/

Acts II, 4 — tongues — the same manifold word ('γλωσσα') as tongues above. Here we have a kind of similarity to a play on words.

Acts II, 6 — 'συνηλθεν το πληθος και συνεχυθη' — 'synulthen to plethos kai synechythe' — literally a manifold expression: the multitudes came together and merged / mingled / were in confusion (the last word has two different meanings, all of which are in their own way fitting here) — that is, various peoples united, merged, whilst at the same time being in a state of confusion, in an unusual state of directed and captivated attention and mind. For the word language the word 'διαλεκτω' — 'dialekto' is already used here — from which we have the word dialect.

(John VII, 37-52, VIII, 12)
John VII, 37 — 'Ἐν δὲ τῇ ἐσχάτῃ ἡμέρᾳ τῇ μεγάλῃ τῆς ἑορτῆς' — 'en de te eskhate hemera te megale tes heortes' - but on the last great day of the feast. When.

John VII, 38 — 'ὁ πιστεύων εἰς ἐμέ' — 'ho pisteuon eis eme' - the one who believes in(to) Me. The preposition 'eis' indicates here that true faith leads to Christ — He is the goal and final point of faith. 'ἐκ τῆς κοιλίας αὐτοῦ' - 'ek tes koilias autou' - from their belly; from their inward parts. Whence. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that the belly (inward parts) of such a person will become the source of that water — that is, the person will be so filled with grace that even their body, and even its less honoured parts, will be able to transmit grace to others and manifest the action of the Holy Spirit to the world as well.

John VII, 39 — 'οἱ πιστεύσοντες εἰς αὐτόν' — 'hoi pisteusontes eis auton' - those who believe in(to) Him. See above concerning verse 38.

John VII, 40 — 'Ἐκ τοῦ ὄχλου' — 'ek tou okhlou' - from the crowd. Whence. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that those people fully shared the views and state of the crowd — that is, they had not examined the matter carefully.

John VII, 41 — 'ἐκ τῆς Γαλιλαίας' — 'ek tes Galilaias' - from Galilee. Whence. The preposition 'ek' indicates here origin. Those people did not know that Jesus was born in Bethlehem — for after the flight from Herod into Egypt and the subsequent return to Israel, they lived in Nazareth in Galilee. Christ does not wish to testify to the Jews about His miraculous birth, since they reject Him here with prejudice. This is clearly evident from verse 52, where they deny the possibility of even a prophet (let alone the Messiah) originating from Galilee. In reality such a position testifies to their chauvinism and their non-acceptance of the Galileans as proper Jews — which Scripture exposes: a prophet can originate from Galilee, can be sent to Gentiles, and the like. There is also the view that Christ often makes His testimony concerning the Virgin Mary implicit, in order to protect Her from possible persecutions by the Jews.

John VII, 42 — 'ἐκ τοῦ σπέρματος Δαυεὶδ καὶ ἀπὸ Βηθλεὲμ τῆς κώμης' — 'ek tou spermatos Daueid kai apo Bethleem tes komes' - from the seed of David and from the village of Bethlehem. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that Christ was to be of the lineage of descendants of King David (which He truly was — also from Bethlehem), whilst the preposition 'apo' indicates here that the Messiah, in the understanding of the Jews, was to have left Bethlehem in order to carry out His ministry.

John VII, 44 — 'ἐξ αὐτῶν' — 'ex auton' - from among them. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that those people in general were similar to the others in the crowd — that is, to people who had not examined the matter carefully.

John VII, 48 — 'μή τις ἐκ τῶν ἀρχόντων ἐπίστευσεν εἰς αὐτὸν ἢ ἐκ τῶν Φαρισαίων;' — 'me tis ek ton arkhonton episteusen eis auton e ek ton Pharisaion' - has any of the rulers believed in(to) Him, or any of the Pharisees? Concerning 'eis' see above concerning verse 38. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that those people were to have been generally representative of the rulers or of the Pharisees — that is, fully sharing the views of the latter.

John VII, 50 — 'ἐξ αὐτῶν' — 'ex auton' - from among them. Nicodemus was one of the teachers (elders, rabbis) of Israel, but did not share the views of many others among them concerning Jesus — and moreover, after the secret nocturnal conversation (John III), became a secret disciple of Jesus. He is one of my beloved saints.

John VII, 51 — 'ἐὰν μὴ ἀκούσῃ πρῶτον παρ' αὐτοῦ' — 'hean me akouse proton par autou' - if it does not first hear from beside them. The preposition 'para' in its form before the following vowel here with the genitive case. This construction is used concerning the procession of the Holy Spirit from the Father, concerning the Most Holy Trinity, concerning the entire God's design, and also concerning the human nature of Jesus Christ.
