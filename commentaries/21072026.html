Liturgy:
(1 Corinthians X, 5-12)
1 Corinthians X, 5 — 'ἐν τοῖς πλείοσιν αὐτῶν' — 'en tois pleiosin auton' - with many of them. A peculiarity of using the verb 'εὐδόκησεν' with the preposition 'en', one can say — He was not well pleased with them (though there were many of them). 'κατεστρώθησαν' - 'katestrothesan' - scattered, strewn across the ground. 'ἐν τῇ ἐρήμῳ' - 'en te eremo' - in the wilderness. Where.

1 Corinthians X, 6, 11 — 'εἰς τὸ μὴ εἶναι ἡμᾶς ἐπιθυμητὰς κακῶν' — 'eis to me einai hemas epithymetas kakon' - so that we should not be desirers of evil. The preposition 'eis' here indicates purpose, goal.

1 Corinthians X, 8 — 'πορνεύωμεν' — 'porneuomen' - commit sexual immorality. Google Gemini Flash-3.6 gives the following commentary on lexics here:

The Gap in English Renderings
In English, translators are forced to choose between dynamic accuracy and etymological depth, usually splitting the concept across different words:
1. 'Sexual Immorality' (ESV, NASB, CSB)
Strengths: Accurately mirrors the legal/categorical distinction in Matthew 5:32 (porneia as a broad category vs. moicheia as specific covenant adultery).
Weaknesses: Highly abstract and modern. It completely loses the vivid root sense of prostitution, commodification, or trading away sacred boundaries for transient gain.
2. 'Fornication' (KJV, NKJV)
Strengths: Preserves a distinct traditional term separate from 'adultery.'
Weaknesses: In modern English, 'fornication' has narrowed almost exclusively to premarital sex between unmarried persons. It fails to convey the broader spiritual apostasy or the bartering/selling off of grace that Paul describes in 1 Corinthians 10.
3. 'Unchastity' / 'Marital Unfaithfulness' (NRSV, NIV)
Strengths: Captures the relational breakdown and the casting off of restraint (розпуста).
Weaknesses: Too tame or functional; it misses the raw, transactional nature of the Greek root πορν- (porn-).
Why English Struggles Where Greek & Ukrainian Succeed
In Greek: πορνεία inherently links physical licentiousness to the root concept of selling/trading (pernemi). Thus, using porneia for both sexual sin and spiritual idolatry immediately evokes the image of prostituting oneself to the material world.
In Ukrainian: 'Розпуста' (from розпускати / loosening boundaries) naturally contrasts with 'перелюб' (breaking marital faith), while carrying a strong moral weight of spiritual unraveling and wild self-gratification.
In English: English separates the legal/moral classification ('immorality') from the transactional/spiritual metaphor ('prostitution' / 'harlotry'). To capture the full theological weight Paul intends in 1 Corinthians 10, English almost always requires explanatory commentary rather than a single translated word.

1 Corinthians X, 9 — 'ὑπὸ τῶν ὄφεων' — 'hypo ton opheon' - by the serpents. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the genitive case and indicates the passive state destroyed (by whom?) in Ancient Greek.

1 Corinthians X, 10 — 'ὑπὸ τοῦ ὀλοθρευτοῦ' — 'hypo tou olothreutou' - by the destroyer. Analogous to the previous genitive case, indicating the passive state. Spiritually, those who are dissatisfied and complain, reproaching unjustly, are destroyers of themselves first of all.

1 Corinthians X, 11 — 'εἰς οὓς τὰ τέλη τῶν αἰώνων' — 'eis ous ta tele ton aionon' - upon whom the end of the ages has come. The end of the age is not within people, but has come to them, is arriving upon them.

Apostle Paul writes that Old Testament events became types (prefigurations) and instruction for his contemporary Christians, and better understood for all Christians — since the end times last from the time of the First Coming of Christ until the Second Coming of Christ.

Many researchers believe that what occurred in the Old Testament physically, in the New Testament occurs predominantly spiritually/soulfully — for example, if someone back then perished physically due to grave sins, in the New Testament this occurs with the soul of such a person — they (i.e. the person) perish spiritually, distancing themselves from God. One can say that this sequence corresponds to how young children learn to sympathize with the pain of other people and animals — first they learn to realize someone's physical pain through visible and noticeable signs, and later they learn to sympathize with spiritual pain, which may not express itself as visibly as physical pain.

Here Paul lists several famous examples from the Old Testament with the thought that they should teach Christians faithfulness to God in its various manifestations (Paul lists some).

I will note that living exclusively for the sake of material pleasures is equated here to idolatry, because such a life puts the material in the place of God. Modern man is often not inclined to think so, but one must understand that if a person lives for the sake of the material, then the meaning of their life, in their view, is reduced solely to this, and such is the goal. They then neglect that for which they were actually predominantly created — to be eternally happy in love with God. They are then to a certain extent ruled (their thoughts, views, feelings, behavior, etc., are determined) by the material world, and one can say that they serve it to a certain extent. Then the idea that this can be called idolatry, according to Paul, becomes closer and more accessible for a modern person to understand.

Christians, however, need to remember their calling.

(Matthew XVI, 6-12)
Matthew XVI, 6 — 'προσέχετε ἀπὸ τῆς ζύμης τῶν Φαρισαίων καὶ Σαδδουκαίων' — 'prosekhete apo tes zymes ton Pharisaion kai Saddoukaion' — beware [keep away] from the leaven of the Pharisees and Sadducees. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that one must be careful to stay away from the position of the Pharisees (blind zealous adherents of human traditions who are convinced of their own righteousness) and the Sadducees (believers who have actually lost faith in the efficacy of God's power and are occupied only with the earthly). When people attach themselves to the earthly, the vain, and sometimes the wrong, while being certain of their own righteousness, then without changing this attitude it leads to decline, and sometimes even to opposition to God, as happened with the Pharisees and Sadducees who became hostile toward Christ. Of course, one must be careful, because a person can be tempted similarly, even while being a Christian.

Matthew XVI, 7, 8 — 'ἐν ἑαυτοῖς' — 'en heautois' - among themselves; internally. A set expression.

Matthew XVI, 11, 12 — 'ἀπὸ τῆς ζύμης' — 'apo tes zymes' - from the leaven. See above regarding verse 6. The preposition 'apo' emphasizes that one should distance oneself in every way from Pharisaism and Sadduceeism (the latter being an image of faithlessness). 'ἀπὸ τῆς διδαχῆς' - 'apo tes didakhes' - from the doctrine; from the teaching — see the explanation just above.

A direct continuation of yesterday's reading, where what enters into that leaven was described in more detail.
