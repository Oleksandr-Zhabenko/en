Liturgy:
(Romans IV, 13-25)
Romans IV, 14 — 'εἰ γὰρ οἱ ἐκ νόμου κληρονόμοι' — 'ei gar hoi ek nomou kleronomoi' - for if those from the Law are heirs. The preposition 'ek' indicates here the cause of inheriting — if the Law were the cause, then not the promise and not the faith of Abraham would be the determining factor, but the Law itself.

Romans IV, 16 — 'ἐκ πίστεως' — 'ek pisteos' - from faith; of faith. That is, what is spoken of here is that it is precisely faith that is the cause of inheriting. 'εἰς τὸ εἶναι βεβαίαν τὴν ἐπαγγελίαν παντὶ τῷ σπέρματι' - 'eis to einai bebaian ten epangelian panti to spermati' - so that the promise might be firm (certain, reliable) to all the seed (that is, to all the heirs). The preposition 'eis' indicates here the purpose. 'οὐ τῷ ἐκ τοῦ νόμου μόνον ἀλλὰ καὶ τῷ ἐκ πίστεως' - 'ou to ek tou nomou monon alla kai to ek pisteos' - not only to those who are of the Law, but also to those who are of faith. That is, not only to Old Testament Israel, but also to faithful Christians through faith.

Romans IV, 17 — I shall cite:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/03072024.html
Romans IV, 17 — 'ζῳοποιοῦντος τοὺς νεκροὺς' — 'zoopoiountos tous nekrous' - gives life to those who are dead. What is meant is moving, active life — that is, not simply existence as a living being, but full life. The word from which the related term entered the Creed concerning the Holy Spirit concerns all three Hypostases of the Most Holy Trinity. An important point: the souls of the departed and their state are also to a certain degree alive — that is, the departed prior to the resurrection exist as such, but do not have full life. Abraham believed precisely in the full resurrection of the dead — and we Christians believe the same. It must be said that when speaking of the state of the departed prior to the resurrection, the Lord says in the Gospel that in relation to God they are also actively alive to Him (this was not revealed to Abraham, but was revealed to the faithful by Jesus Christ — see, for example, Matthew XXII). This means that God continues to care for them, builds the history of their salvation, draws them near, and will ultimately accomplish their resurrection from the dead. From the human perspective this is an incomplete existence of people — but God cares for the departed similarly to how He cares for the living, and therefore in His eyes they are actively alive: He remembers them, fulfils His promises, remains in relationship with them, does not refuse to call Himself their God if such they were during their life. Therefore the souls of the departed, even before the resurrection of the dead and the Redemption by Christ, could intercede for the living (recall, for example, the appearance of the soul of the prophet Jeremiah in the times of the Maccabees, or the soul of Samuel which announced God's will) — and all the more can they do so after the Resurrection of Jesus Christ.

End of citation.

I cite (here it is translated from the Ukrainian original):
Reflecting on the death of your dog 🐕 — your friend and beloved companion — and on the words of Luke 20:38, «πάντες γὰρ αὐτῷ ζῶσιν» (pantes gar autō zōsin / for all live to Him), I consulted Google Gemini Pro. His response prompted further searching.

God cannot be the God of non-existence. If he is in relationship with the patriarchs now, they must therefore exist. The use of αὐτῷ (to him / in relation to him) is the final chord of this argument: regardless of what human biology says, the relationship with the Creator guarantees the continuity of life. Life is defined not by the presence of breath in the lungs, but by remaining in connection with the Source of Life.

But then one may also say of little birds that falling to the ground, they live… (Matthew 10:29). And the Holy Spirit does not disdain to appear in the form of a dove 🕊️ at the Baptism of Jesus — recalling the dove that brought an olive leaf 🌱 to Noah after the flood ended. Therefore they live... to God.

But they live differently from people, who have an immortal soul and the image of God. After the first return of the dove with the leaf 🌱, Noah subsequently released it a second time, and it did not return. When a beloved pet lives, it is as though it is bringing an olive leaf 🌱. And when it dies, it is as though the dove 🕊️ flies away — but lives… to God. We do not know whether animals will be resurrected, but we know God's love and mercy. And I pray for God's comfort for you.

Romans IV, 18 — 'ὃς παρ' ἐλπίδα ἐπ' ἐλπίδι ἐπίστευσεν εἰς τὸ γενέσθαι αὐτὸν πατέρα πολλῶν ἐθνῶν' — 'hos par elpida ep elpidi episteusen eis to genesthai auton patera pollon ethnon' - who to the proximity with hope, upon hope, believed that he would become the father of many nations; who alongside hope, upon hope, believed that he would become the father of many nations; who contrary to hope, upon hope, believed that he would become the father of many nations. Various translations of the expression with the preposition 'para' are possible. The preposition 'para' in its form before the following vowel here with the accusative indicates that faith and hope interacted in a particular way that is difficult to describe in words. The first translation emphasises that Abraham wished to believe that he would become a father (without thinking of many nations) and therefore believed — that is, faith drew close to hope. The second indicates that faith and hope went alongside, together. The third indicates that human reasonings and expectations yielded to faith in God's revelation, which revived his hope now entirely in God and in His promise — and this hope together with faith was fulfilled.

Romans IV, 20 — 'εἰς δὲ τὴν ἐπαγγελίαν τοῦ θεοῦ οὐ διεκρίθη τῇ ἀπιστίᾳ' — 'eis de ten epangelian tou theou ou diekrithe te apistia' - but he did not consciously separate himself (did not doubt, did not yield) to unbelief in God's promise. The preposition 'eis' indicates here what faith is in — in God's promise.

Romans IV, 22 — 'ἐλογίσθη αὐτῷ εἰς δικαιοσύνην' — 'elogisthe auto eis dikaiosynen' - it was reckoned to him as righteousness. The preposition 'eis' indicates here what his faith became.

Romans IV, 24 — 'τοῖς πιστεύουσιν ἐπὶ τὸν ἐγείραντα Ἰησοῦν τὸν κύριον ἡμῶν ἐκ νεκρῶν' — 'tois pisteuousin epi ton egeiranta Iesoun ton kyrion hemon ek nekron' - who believe upon the One Who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead. The preposition 'ek' indicates here whence the transition took place — from the dead to the living. The preposition 'epi' concerning faith indicates here that the Father is the Foundation for faith, and is also a parallel to the faith of Abraham described just above in Romans IV, 18, where it is used similarly regarding hope.

Concerning the body of Abraham — what is spoken of is the birth to him and Sarah of Isaac, the son and heir of the promise.

Verse 14 means that if the Law, and not faith, has precedence regarding the promise, then the subsequent ordinances of the Law are of greater significance than the promise and faith — and therefore the latter are as though unnecessary. But it is not so: faith and the promise are more important than the Law and precede it.

Nevertheless, the Law too is from God — and therefore Abraham is called the father of many nations throughout, and God's generosity is visible here as well.

Concerning the comparison of the faith of Abraham and the faith of Christians — in the time of the apostle Paul, Christianity was not widespread and was not traditional, and each person believed in their own freedom — just as Abraham also believed, even though by human reasoning and standards he should not have believed in that. But even now the deep faith of Christians is not simply a tradition or something common to many. Therefore the comparison, even though times have changed, remains entirely in force. Indeed, faith in the Resurrection of Jesus Christ and the faith of Abraham are similar.

(Matthew VII, 15-21)
Matthew VII, 21 — 'εἰς τὴν βασιλείαν τῶν οὐρανῶν' — 'eis ten basileian ton ouranon' - into the Kingdom of Heaven. Whither. One of the meanings of this frequent expression in the Gospels is: already in this life to live in such a way as to fulfil God's will entirely — as will be the case in the eternal Kingdom of God after the general resurrection of the dead. 'ὁ ποιῶν τὸ θέλημα τοῦ πατρός μου τοῦ ἐν τοῖς οὐρανοῖς' - 'ho poion to thelema tou patros mou tou en tois ouranois' - the one who does the will of My Father Who is in the heavens. Where.

Matthew VII, 22 — 'ἐν ἐκείνῃ τῇ ἡμέρᾳ' — 'en ekeine te hemera' - on that day; that day. When.

Matthew VII, 23 — 'ἀποχωρεῖτε ἀπ' ἐμοῦ' — 'apokhoreite ap emou' - depart from Me. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following vowel indicates that the departure will be complete, total, and entire.

Matthew VII, 22 — in the original Your name stands in the dative case (to Your name). In ancient Greek there was no instrumental case — its functions were performed by others, including the dative. The literal translation was it not to Your name? therefore remains manifold in meaning. It may carry the meaning of the traditional instrumental translation, but may also indicate that people thought their deeds were bringing glory and honour to the name of Christ. If at the same time they were (in parallel, impenitently) practising lawlessness — that is, through their deeds going against God's will, in particular and above all against God's openly revealed and known will — then they brought no glory to Christ, and neither are they similar to Moses (of whom it is said in the Law of Moses that the Lord knew him by name).
