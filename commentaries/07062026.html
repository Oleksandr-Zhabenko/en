Liturgy:
(Hebrews XI, 33–XII, 2)
Hebrews XI, 34 — 'ἐδυναμώθησαν ἀπὸ ἀσθενείας' — 'edynamothesan apo astheneias' — were strengthened from infirmities. The preposition 'apo' here indicates complete healing and liberation from infirmities. 'ἰσχυροὶ ἐν πολέμῳ' - 'iskhyroi en polemo' - strong in war.

Hebrews XI, 35 — 'ἐξ ἀναστάσεως' — 'ex anastaseos' — from resurrection. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following vowel indicates that the resurrection served as the cause and source of life for the dead — without it their new life would not have come about at all.

Hebrews XI, 37 — 'ἐν φόνῳ μαχαίρης ἀπέθανον' — 'en phono makhaires apethanon' — were slain with the sword (as an instrument). 'ἐν μηλωταῖς, ἐν αἰγείοις δέρμασιν' - 'en melotais, en aigeiois dermasin' - in sheepskins, in goatskins.

Hebrews XII, 2 — 'ἀφορῶντες εἰς τὸν τῆς πίστεως ἀρχηγὸν καὶ τελειωτὴν Ἰησοῦν' — 'aphorontes eis ton tes pisteos arkhegon kai teleioten Iesoun' — looking intently to Jesus the Author and Finisher (Accomplisher; the One Who has accomplished, made full, perfect, complete) of faith. The preposition 'eis' indicates whither one is to look — that is, upon Whom. 'ἐν δεξιᾷ τε τοῦ θρόνου τοῦ θεοῦ' - 'en dexia te tou thronou tou theou' - at the right hand of the throne of God (the Father); to the right of the throne of God. That is, in the highest glory.

because he looked to the reward — in the original he was looking intently at the reward, looking at nothing else — that is, the translation (not only the Ukrainian one) omits an important point. Indeed, if Moses had been thinking of something other than the reward — something other than justice, compassion, and solidarity with his oppressed kindred people — he would at least have hesitated whether to remain with the Egyptians; we would have had a quite different Moses, and it is even possible that the Lord would have sent someone else to the Israelites. Recall what happened with Peter when, walking on the water towards Jesus Christ, he began to look at the stormy waves and the tempest.

Why did Moses look to Jesus Christ? For at that time the Son of God had not yet appeared in this world in the flesh, and Moses was not yet a prophet called by God. But let us recall the words of Christ: Whatever you did (or did not do) to one of the least of these, you did (or did not do) to Me (Matthew XXV, 31-46). Moses did this for the sake of the Jewish people, for the sake of God's justice — and therefore did it looking to Jesus Christ, the Power of God and the Wisdom of God. The same may be said of every person who acts as Moses did.

Further Paul enumerates other Old Testament faithful, using twice the word better — in the original stronger — that is, that which surpasses, that which is more glorious.

The Old Testament saints and faithful received completeness (perfection) in Christ. Indeed, what God promised them was fulfilled precisely in Christ. The train of thought means that God granted fulfilment to the faithful in the time of Jesus Christ, and not earlier — even though people had been expecting it before. Paul does not explain why so much time had passed, but simply states the very fact of the fulfilment.

The reading concludes with a call to faithfulness and patience in following Christ, and with His glorification.

(Matthew X, 32-33, 37-38; XIX, 27-30)
Matthew X, 32, 33 — 'Πᾶς οὖν ὅστις ὁμολογήσει ἐν ἐμοὶ ἔμπροσθεν τῶν ἀνθρώπων, ὁμολογήσω κἀγὼ ἐν αὐτῷ ἔμπροσθεν τοῦ πατρός μου τοῦ ἐν οὐρανοῖς' — 'pas oun hostis homologesei en emoi emprosthen ton anthropon, homologeso kago en auto emprosthen tou patros mou tou en ouranois' — Therefore everyone who acknowledges (confesses, glorifies) me before people, I also will acknowledge them before my Father who is in the heavens. Here is a passage whose grammar and content attract particular attention. Google Gemini Flash-3.5 explains it thus (the translation of this passage is by it itself):

πᾶς οὖν ὅστις ὁμολογήσει ἐν ἐμοὶ ἔμπροσθεν τῶν ἀνθρώπων, ὁμολογήσω κἀγὼ ἐν αὐτῷ ἔμπροσθεν τοῦ πατρός μου τοῦ ἐν [τοῖς] οὐρανοῖς·
(Whosoever therefore shall confess in Me before men, him will I confess also in him before My Father which is in heaven)
From the standpoint of Classical Greek, the verb ὁμολογέω (homologeō — to confess, acknowledge, agree) requires a direct object in the accusative case (Accusativus) or a dative (Dativus) without a preposition. Thus, one would standardly expect simply ὁμολογήσει ἐμέ (shall confess Me).
Yet, what we find here is ὁμολογήσει ἐν ἐμοί (homologēsei en emoi). Why so? This is the result of two powerful factors converging: Semitic influence (Hebraism/Aramaic idiom) and a profound theological subtext.
1. The Semitic Substratum (The Aramaic Influence)
Koine (the common Greek language), in which the New Testament was written, frequently bears the imprint of Aramaic—the language in which Jesus preached—or Hebrew.
In Semitic languages, the verb meaning to confess, to bear witness to someone, or to declare oneself for someone is constructed using a verb followed by the preposition בְּ־ (be-), which denotes in, within, or by means of.
In Aramaic, this corresponds to the idiom ודהי בְּ־ (wadeh be-).
When the early Christians and the Evangelists rendered this expression into Greek, they translated the construction literally, adopting the preposition ἐν as the direct equivalent of the Semitic be-.
Therefore, the first and most immediate layer is a literal idiomatic translation from Aramaic, where to confess in someone means to testify in someone's favour or to take someone's side.
2. The Theological Nuance: Mystical Oneness
However, as is frequently the case in the New Testament, a linguistic peculiarity aligns perfectly with the underlying theology. Had Jesus simply said whosoever confesses Me (ὁμολογήσει ἐμέ), it could imply a purely external, intellectual acknowledgement—as if a person were merely confirming a fact: Yes, I know who Jesus is, and I recognize His status.
The deployment of ἐν ἐμοί (in Me) and ἐν αὐτῷ (in him) shifts the emphasis from an external profession to an internal, existential bond.
To confess in Me: This implies that a person acknowledges God whilst abiding inside a covenant with Christ, being deeply rooted in Him. The witness borne before men is the outward fruit of Christ dwelling within that person.
Reciprocity (Symmetry): Notice the immaculate symmetry of the verse. Whosoever confesses in Me, him will I also confess in him. This is not merely a legal defence at a tribunal (I shall intercede for this person before the Father). It is a mystical interpenetration. Christ recognises and acknowledges His own indwelling presence in that person before the Father.
An Important Parallelism:
This very concept of mutual indwelling is later fully developed in the Gospel of John through the allegory of the vine and the branches: Abide in Me, and I in you (μείνατε ἐν ἐμοί, κἀγὼ ἐν ὑμῖν — John 15:4).
How is this Reflected in Translations?
Because the construction ὁμολογεῖν ἐν is alien to most European languages, translators have generally been forced to sacrifice this literalism for the sake of natural phrasing:
The Latin Vulgate attempted to preserve it: “omnis qui confitebitur me in homine… confitebor et ego in illo (though the preposition faded out in certain later manuscripts).
English Translations: The King James Version (KJV) famously retained the Semitic idiom, rendering it as confess me… confess him in standard modern editions, though older variants closer to the Greek reads confess in me... confess in him. Most modern English versions (such as the ESV, NIV, or NRSV) opt for a smoother idiomatic approach: acknowledges me... acknowledge him, as confessing in someone sounds unnatural in modern English or alters the meaning entirely.
In conclusion, the literal en emoi is a classic example of how an Aramaic linguistic habit (a Semitic idiom) became the perfect vehicle for conveying a revolutionary Christian concept: acknowledging Christ is not a matter of mere words, but a testament arising from a profound internal oneness with Him.

Matthew XIX, 28 — 'ἐν τῇ παλινγενεσίᾳ' — 'en te palingenesia' - in the (spiritual) regeneration; in the (spiritual) renewal; in Christ's renewal. A rare word indicating that creation will be renewed and in it God's design will be restored — everything that is to take place according to God's design concerning the saved, and also the natural world, will take place and be fulfilled. The Lord permits the sufferings of people and does not wish to deprive them of the divine freedom He has granted them.

The apostle Paul mentions in the Epistles that: Do you not know that we shall judge angels? (1 Corinthians VI, 2-3). See on this at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/21072025.html
I cite:
Paul speaks of relations among Christians within the community, emphasising that God judges those outside. He reproaches them for going to law before Gentiles — since in that case sinful Gentiles form the impression that Christians are not only no better in virtue than those Gentiles, but even worse, since they require the judgement (that the sinful pagans might adjudicate, sort things out) of sinful Gentiles. What is spoken of is in fact the humiliation of Christians through such behaviour before the Gentiles themselves — which not all the Corinthians were aware of.

Paul then says that the faithful (saints) will judge the world (and even angels). This is the only place in Scripture where this is spoken of, and it therefore remains somewhat mysterious. The predominant view is that the saints, through their virtues and the example of their lives, will reveal at God's Judgement the sins of people — thereby becoming participants in God's Judgement as witnesses (and not judges as such). At the same time there is the promise of Jesus to the twelve apostles to judge Israel. This again is understood as meaning that the words and deeds, the virtues of the apostles, will reveal what is good and evil among people in general — they will be a kind of measure for people.

Paul also holds that humiliation before the Gentiles, who were sinners, is worse than enduring something from brothers and sisters and forgiving them (and considers it best and advises turning to a wise believer who can resolve the matter) — if there is no one capable of judging wisely among the faithful.

(end of citation)

In general in today's reading concerning judgement there are two meanings. The first concerns the revelation of God's will — against which the angels who fell away with the devil sinned — whilst the apostles are an example of faithfulness to God. This will be revealed — and will thus become to a certain degree a judgement upon those angels by the apostles and the faithful. The second meaning implies the Biblical sense of judging — see in particular the book of Judges — the resolution of matters and governance without dominion or lordship. That is, through the apostles God's will will also be revealed to the angels, who will obey it. Thus the judging of the apostles with respect to the angels will be accomplished. This may also concern the faithful.

It may seem strange that a human being can reveal God's will to an angel. But a human being — being in particular a spiritual-bodily creature — can act with regard to bodily things differently from the way angels do. For example, people can create. Therefore they can also reveal God's will to angels, being similar to them in purity and holiness in the Kingdom of God.
