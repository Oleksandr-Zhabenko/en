Liturgy:
(Romans II, 14-29)
Romans II, 15 — 'τὸ ἔργον τοῦ νόμου γραπτὸν ἐν ταῖς καρδίαις αὐτῶν' — 'to ergon tou nomou grapton en tais kardiais auton' - the work of the Law written in their hearts. Where.

Romans II, 16 — 'ἐν ἡμέρᾳ' — 'en hemera' - on the day. When.

Romans II, 17 — 'καυχᾶσαι ἐν θεῷ' — 'kaukhasai en theo' - you boast in God; you boast with God. Both translations are possible, but the first has the advantage in terms of meaning. The first indicates that the boasting has its foundation and grounds in God — He is its cause; whilst the second suggests that God as it were serves as a means to the person's desire to boast — which is a sin before God.

Romans II, 18 — 'ἐκ τοῦ νόμου' — 'ek tou nomou' - from the Law. From what — whence. The preposition 'ek' indicates here the origin of what the person learns — the source.

Romans II, 19 — 'φῶς τῶν ἐν σκότει' — 'phos ton en skotei' - the light of those in darkness. Where. The light of whom — not for whom. The genitive case indicates that the person thus thinks they are serving others in this way.

Romans II, 20 — 'ἐν τῷ νόμῳ' — 'en to nomo' - in the Law. Where.

Romans II, 23 — 'ὃς ἐν νόμῳ καυχᾶσαι' — 'hos en nomo kaukhasai' - you who boast with the Law. Here only this translation is fitting — boasting with what. Comparison with verse 17 indicates that such a person uses the Law merely as an instrument of their own boasting — as a value they supposedly possess but do not share.

Romans II, 24 — 'ἐν τοῖς ἔθνεσιν' — 'en tois ethnesin' - among the nations; in the nations; with the nations; among the Gentiles; with the Gentiles. Various translations are possible and mutually complementary.

Romans II, 26 — 'εἰς περιτομὴν' — 'eis peritomen' - as circumcision; for circumcision. The preposition 'eis' indicates here what the uncircumcision of such devout Gentiles will be counted as.

Romans II, 27 — 'ἐκ φύσεως ἀκροβυστία' — 'ek physeos akrobystia' - uncircumcised by nature. That is, a person who was not a Jew by faith or an Israelite by origin. Paul here addresses ostensibly the Jews, but in general and more precisely — Christians who were adopting Jewish rites. By nature here in the sense of a person who has not made a conscious choice regarding their faith or lack of faith.

Romans II, 28 — 'οὐ γὰρ ὁ ἐν τῷ φανερῷ Ἰουδαῖός ἐστιν, οὐδὲ ἡ ἐν τῷ φανερῷ ἐν σαρκὶ περιτομή' — 'ou gar ho en to phanero Ioudaios estin, oude he en to phanero en sarki peritome' - for it is not the outwardly apparent Jew who is one, nor is circumcision the outwardly apparent one in the flesh. Here Jewishness (here a synonym for true faith and devoutness — children of the faithful Abraham, of the fathers, and so on) and circumcision — signs and symbols of true faithfulness to God and of true children of God, as it were seals of belonging to God. Paul says that the true seals of belonging to God are not outward manifestations, but the wholly faithful person — in body, soul, and spirit.

Romans II, 29 — 'ἀλλ' ὁ ἐν τῷ κρυπτῷ Ἰουδαῖος, καὶ περιτομὴ καρδίας ἐν πνεύματι οὐ γράμματι' — 'all ho en to krypto Ioudaios, kai peritome kardias en pneumati ou grammati' - but he is a Jew who is one inwardly, and circumcision is that of the heart — in spirit, not in letter (with spirit, not with letter). A continuation of the thought of verse 28 — the true seals are the presence of the Holy Spirit and grace, not of symbols and outward form.

Romans II, 20 — 'μορφωσιν' — 'morphosin' — the form, the outward appearance (of knowledge and truth).

Romans II, 29 — literally: in spirit (with spirit), not in letter (not with letter). The phrase has become proverbial (spirit and letter — in Ukraine, for example, there is a publisher of spiritual literature by this name); here in Paul, in this verse, one may see that spirit concerns the nature of the person themselves, whilst letter concerns the cultural forms to which they belong.

Paul says what the work of the Law is — in what the action of the Law upon a person (upon people) manifests itself — and then explains why Jewishness as a ritual understanding of the Law of Moses is insufficient.

Concerning the work of the Law — Paul uses words with the prefix συν-, which may be translated as together with, co-. Here we see the Law as a helper to the person in living according to God's will and in knowing God.

And this is the value of the Law in this its work. If it is present (for example, in devout Gentiles), then they themselves are by their own lives an example of the Law. But if it is absent — if there is a violation of the Law and of God's will — then the outward forms of the Law, including circumcision among the Jews, have no real significance; and accordingly, their boasting in that case becomes the very grounds for their condemnation.

The passage is in general very substantial and many-layered. I shall dwell on several important and very timely questions today.

In Romans II, 15 we have: 'τῆς συνειδήσεως καὶ μεταξὺ ἀλλήλων τῶν λογισμῶν κατηγορούντων ἢ καὶ ἀπολογουμένων' — 'tes syneideseos kai metaxy allelon ton logismon kategorounton e kai apologoumenon' - conscience (another translation might be 'consciousness') and the reasonings (thoughts) that among themselves (in the process) at one time accuse, at another time defend one another. What is spoken of is thoughts specifically — as the grammatical agreement in gender (masculine in Koine, confirmed also by Google Gemini Flash-3.5) attests.

The first thing worth noting: the apostle Paul says that with regard to ethics, conscience, and reasonings — the accusation or defence concerns the reasonings themselves and not the people. That is, thoughts and reasonings judge and defend not people but one another. This is similar to the classic model of sandboxing, where security is achieved by working not with the original but with something that represents it. Thoughts and conscience represent people — but to accuse or defend them is not at all the same as accusing or defending people.

Secondly, the apostle here on the one hand praises Gentiles' ethics and philosophy when they lead to the same conclusions as God's Law. But on the other hand, the very mechanism by which this occurs — through contradiction, accusation, and defence — points to the fragility of such a state, even though it is praiseworthy here.

Thirdly, what the apostle praises here is not so much the result as the process of moral actions in the Gentiles. That is, a person does not merely perform moral actions but acquires moral experience, which is comparably as valuable as the actions themselves.

(Matthew V, 33-41)
Matthew V, 34 — 'μήτε ἐν τῷ οὐρανῷ' — 'mete en to ourano' - nor by heaven. That is, do not swear also by heaven. By what. Perplexity AI notes that these prepositions are connected with Aramaic and Hebrew, where the object of an oath is used in the expressions to swear by something with prepositions meaning on, over — indicating place and direction. A detailed explanation follows after the verse comments.

Matthew V, 35 — 'μήτε ἐν τῇ γῇ' — 'mete en te ge' - nor by the earth. Analogous to verse 34 — by what — that is, do not swear also by the earth. 'μήτε εἰς Ἱεροσόλυμα' - 'mete eis Hierosolyma' - nor by Jerusalem. That is, do not try to artificially add weight and significance to your words.

Matthew V, 36 — 'μήτε ἐν τῇ κεφαλῇ σου' — 'mete en te kephale sou' - nor by your head. Analogous to verses 34-35 — be aware of your real strengths and possibilities. See more at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/PowerOfHuman.html

Matthew V, 37 — 'τὸ δὲ περισσὸν τούτων ἐκ τοῦ πονηροῦ ἐστιν' — 'to de perisson touton ek tou ponerou estin' - but what is more than these is from the evil one (the evil one in general, by results, in the end, in the broad sense). Analogously — see the link just cited, where this question is examined in detail along with many other important matters.

Matthew V, 39 — 'εἰς τὴν δεξιὰν σιαγόνα' — 'eis ten dexian siagona' - on the right cheek. Whither.

The reading is in fact closely connected with the following — which is read on Saturday — so that throughout the week, by Sunday, the entire chapter V is read, which reveals much of the teaching and commandments of the Saviour.

Matthew V, 36 — 'λευκην' — 'leuken' — white — from 'λυκε' — 'luke' — light, bright (whence the name of my beloved saint, the apostle and evangelist Luke).

Matthew V, 37, 39 — the same word 'πονηρος' — 'poneros' (in different forms) is used in both verses, rendered as the evil one, evil — a derivative of the word harmful — evil by its effect, evil in the end. In the first case what is indeed meant is the evil one — that is, the devil, whilst in the second — evil.
