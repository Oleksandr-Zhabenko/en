Liturgy:
(Romans X, 1-10)
Romans X, 1, 10 — 'εἰς σωτηρίαν' — 'eis soterian' - into salvation; for salvation; unto salvation. The preposition 'eis' emphasizes movement and the goal of destination.

For God's design regarding zeal and envy, see the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/25062026.html

Romans X, 4, 10 — 'εἰς δικαιοσύνην' — 'eis dikaiosynen' - into righteousness. The preposition 'eis' emphasizes movement and the final goal.

Romans X, 5 — 'ἐκ νόμου' — 'ek nomou' - from Law. 'ἐν αὐτῇ' - 'en aute' - in it; with (by means of) it. Here both variants of translation are possible and carry different meaning. The first means that a person shall remain in the Law as an environment of existence, life, and the second means that the Law gives life as good, full, filled, righteous.

Romans X, 6 — 'ἐκ πίστεως' — 'ek pisteos' - from faith; out of faith; of faith. Righteousness which is an organic continuation and manifestation, development of faith. See more at the links:

Romans X, 6, 8, 9 — 'ἐν τῇ καρδίᾳ' — 'en te kardia' - in the heart. 'εἰς τὸν οὐρανόν' - 'eis ton ouranon' - into the heaven. Where to. There is meant the spiritual, invisible heaven.

Romans X, 7, 9 — 'εἰς τὴν ἄβυσσον' — 'eis ten abysson' - into the abyss. Where to. There is meant the place of torments for demons. 'ἐκ νεκρῶν' - 'ek nekron' - out of [the] dead. The preposition 'ek' here means not that Christ originates out of the dead, but that He out of them came [out], having resurrected. That is, it indicates onto the transition away from the dead into the living (wherefrom).

Romans X, 8, 9 — 'ἐν τῷ στόματί' — 'en to stomati' - in the mouth; in speaking.

I wrote earlier, here to quote:
It is necessary to dwell at least on the verses Romans X, 9-10, which especially frequently various Christians understand partially and incorrectly, adducing them for the justification of the image of their own piety (about which prophetically the apostle cautions at the beginning of the chapter). Key is the understanding of faith of the heart and confession with the mouth. Faith of the heart here is a synonym of righteousness, and therefore, of the Gospel purity of the (entire) heart, and the mouth — properly not only the mouth, but all in which is manifested outwardly the life of a person. This passage does not have the meaning of the sufficiency merely of a narrowed understanding of the heart and mouth, it does not allow that which do not allow also all other passages of Scripture, but uses key concepts to emphasize accessibility and expressive imagery — all people can be saved, Jewish righteousness by nothing fundamentally differs away from Hellenic or Scythian, because all people are similar, and righteousness is a gift of God to all people in Christ.

End of quote.

In other words, Paul in no manner diminishes, does not shorten all that is spoken in Scripture, but emphasizes what relates to what (that faith relates to the heart, and confession — to the mouth in a broad understanding), that there is needed a combination of faith and the confession of faith, sincerity and righteousness, integrity and openness.

One can draw a parallel regarding the words of Christ: Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh. I quote:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/04082025.html
Returning to the words of Christ about the heart and comparing with our state, we see that frequently the heart is under-filled. A filled heart is manifested outwardly (in particular through the mouth), and if it has not yet manifested itself, then it is partially filled, or one can still say that in it there is both the one and the other.

End of quote.
One could say so: If you will be genuine in your righteousness up to the deepest spheres of the soul, and will manifest your true sincerity in the good, in deeds, in words, then you will be saved. One can still thus: If you will have integrity in Christ, being righteous both externally and internally, and will this integrity manifest, being open to people and to their salvation, manifesting by this apostolic love and Christ's Gospel, then you will be saved.

Similar examples can be continued, they simply by other expressions and a little more complex and modern phrases convey that same meaning.

(Matthew VIII, 28-IX, 1)
Matthew VIII, 28 — 'εἰς τὸ πέραν εἰς τὴν χώραν τῶν Γαδαρηνῶν' — 'eis to peran eis ten khoran ton Gadarenon' - into the other side into the country of the Gadarenes. Where to. 'ἐκ τῶν μνημείων' - 'ek ton mnemeion' - out of the tombs; out of the places of burial. The preposition 'ek' emphasizes that they did not leave the tombs, they came out as if remaining their inhabitants. In modern language — like zombies.

Matthew VIII, 29, 31 — the phrase What [is] to us and to thee? many times is met in the Gospels, indicates onto an absence of connection (here it means that the demons do not wish to be together with God).

Matthew VIII, 30 — 'ἀπ’ αὐτῶν' — 'ap auton' - from them. The preposition 'ap' as the form of 'apo' before a subsequent vowel indicates onto that the demoniacs did not fit into the general picture of the pasture — rich and numerous herds of swine and on their background as if dead demoniacs.

Matthew VIII, 31 — 'εἰς τὴν ἀγέλην' — 'eis ten agelen' - into the herd. The preposition 'eis' emphasizes movement inside. Where to.

Matthew VIII, 32 — 'εἰς τοὺς χοίρους' — 'eis tous khoirous' - into the swine. Where to. 'εἰς τὴν θάλασσαν, καὶ ἀπέθανον ἐν τοῖς ὕδασιν' - 'eis ten thalassan, kai apethanon en tois hydasin' - into the sea, and perished in the waters. Whereinto it rushed and where it perished.

Matthew VIII, 33 — 'εἰς τὴν πόλιν' — 'eis ten polin' - into the city. Where to.

Matthew VIII, 34 — 'εἰς ὑπάντησιν τοῦ Ἰησοῦ' — 'eis hypantesin tou Iesou' - into meeting of Jesus; for the meeting of Jesus. The preposition 'eis' is a component of a set expression, indicates onto the goal and character of movement. 'ἀπὸ τῶν ὁρίων' - 'apo ton horion' - away from their borders, territory. The preposition 'apo' emphasizes the attempt of the inhabitants to drive away Jesus out of there.

Matthew IX, 1 — 'εἰς πλοῖον' — 'eis ploion' - into a boat. Where to. 'εἰς τὴν ἰδίαν πόλιν' - 'eis ten idian polin' - into His own city.

The miracle of casting out demons [away] from the Gadarene demoniacs. A well-known passage, I will dwell on that that the demons confessed Who Jesus was. This can be understood so that they knew Who He was (the Lord opened it to them), and one can so that they guessed (the Lord did not open it to them), or even tried to deceive the listeners, pretending that they knew (although this in general is unlikely). The conclusion away from that begs to be the following: it is not necessary to trust deceitful spirits, even if they spoke at some time also the truth.

Also many people pay attention that the Lord allowed the demons to enter into the herd of swine, and consequently, the demons cannot harm even animals (and much less — people) without Divine permission.

It may appear strange that the Lord allows the herd to drown. But here precisely important is that people know thus Who He is; in another Gospel it is described that later the preaching of the person healed away from demons created good ground for the later acceptance of Jesus.

Also the question whether it was one demoniac or two — rather that two, simply one of the evangelists recorded the story from the lips of a witness who described this event in a simplified manner (this frequently happens in the stories of witnesses).
