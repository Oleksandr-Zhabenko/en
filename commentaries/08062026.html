(Romans II, 28–III, 18)
Romans II, 28 — 'οὐ γὰρ ὁ ἐν τῷ φανερῷ Ἰουδαῖός ἐστιν, οὐδὲ ἡ ἐν τῷ φανερῷ ἐν σαρκὶ περιτομή' — 'ou gar ho en to phanero Ioudaios estin, oude he en to phanero en sarki peritome' - for it is not Jew who is the one outwardly apparent, nor is circumcision the one outwardly apparent in the flesh. Here Jewishness (here a synonym for true faith and devoutness — children of the faithful Abraham, of the fathers, and so on) and circumcision — signs and symbols of true faithfulness to God and of true children of God, as it were seals of belonging to God. Paul says that the true seals of belonging to God are not outward manifestations, but the wholly faithful person — in body, soul, and spirit.

Romans II, 29 — 'ἀλλ' ὁ ἐν τῷ κρυπτῷ Ἰουδαῖος, καὶ περιτομὴ καρδίας ἐν πνεύματι οὐ γράμματι' — 'all ho en to krypto Ioudaios, kai peritome kardias en pneumati ou grammati' - but he is a Jew who is one inwardly, and circumcision is that of the heart — in spirit, not in letter (with spirit, not with letter). A continuation of the thought of verse 28 — the true seals are the presence of the Holy Spirit and grace, not of symbols and outward form.

Romans III, 4 — 'δικαιωθῇς ἐν τοῖς λόγοις σου καὶ νικήσεις ἐν τῷ κρίνεσθαί σε' — 'dikaiothes en tois logois sou kai nikeseis en to krinesthai se' - You were justified in Your words and prevailed when You were judged. Here the passive voice indicates that it is not the Lord Who wishes to judge creation, but creation that wishes to judge God. The Lord did not create the world in order to judge it afterwards — He has no need of this. Creation, however, through its sins as it were reproaches God — blaming Him for its own falls and sins. And the Lord will prevail in such a judgement and will be vindicated from all accusations. See more at the links:

Romans III, 7 — 'εἰ δὲ ἡ ἀλήθεια τοῦ θεοῦ ἐν τῷ ἐμῷ ψεύσματι ἐπερίσσευσεν εἰς τὴν δόξαν αὐτοῦ' — 'ei de he aletheia tou theou en to emo pseusmati eperisseusen eis ten doxan autou' - but if the truth of God in my lie (in my falsification, my counterfeit) abounded exceedingly for His glory; but if the truth of God through my lie (through my falsification, my counterfeit) abounded exceedingly for His glory. Both translations are possible. The apostle Paul speaks as a human being — that is, from the perspective of a clever but unwise person: such a person might think that God benefits from human falls and sins, since through them He manifests His justice. But in reality — as Paul goes on to say — God has no need to increase His boundless and immeasurable glory, including His righteousness and justice, through judgement upon creation. No — the reason is different: people through their fall distance themselves from God, which serves as the basis for their decline. God's glory is neither increased nor diminished, but people either turn away from life and light or, on the contrary, are converted.

Romans III, 9 — 'πάντας ὑφ' ἁμαρτίαν εἶναι' — 'pantas hyph hamartian einai' - all are under sin. The preposition 'hypo' in its form before the following 'ha' here with the accusative indicates that people have become weaker than sin, have yielded in the confrontation, have surrendered into captivity to sin through the fall.

Romans III, 13 — 'ὑπὸ τὰ χείλη αὐτῶν' — 'hypo ta kheile auton' - under their lips. The preposition 'hypo' here with the accusative indicates that the serpent's venom is concealed behind soft lips. The lips are soft, whilst the venom is dangerous. What is spoken of is the fact that people conceal behind an outward decency and sociability and politeness (they kiss with their mouths and speak) their sinfulness and vices.

Romans III, 16 — 'ἐν ταῖς ὁδοῖς αὐτῶν' — 'en tais hodois auton' - in their ways; on their ways. Where.

Paul speaks of the fact that the words of God in the Old Testament were entrusted to Israel, but then says that all are sinful — both Israelites and Greeks (and in general all people, apart from Jesus; the Mother of God is Most Pure, but not sinless).

Paul also cites several psalms in one and another passage — in particular the well-known penitential psalm.

The meaning is that it is a great gain for a righteous person to have God's words, whilst for a sinful person they are not used as they ought to be — and therefore that person becomes subject to God's judgement.

The citations are taken in an absolute sense, not a partial one — this means that if a person even slightly turns away from the fullness of God's will, they violate it; if they unwillingly or rarely seek it, they are called one who does not seek; if they are not faithful in everything somewhere, they are called one who has turned aside. All of this is correct in the sense of the presence of a fall in each sin — though more often we soften our words regarding the sins of ourselves and of others, so as not to wound people's feelings or our own. This may be compared to the absolute necessity of hitting the target: if one misses even slightly, that is… still a miss and not hitting the target. Whether a marksman is good can be seen by how close he comes to the ten — but if the question is ten or something else, then even a nine (in other circumstances hardly the worst result) will be a miss. In the same sense all the words cited are to be understood.

This may initially appear as something excessively demanding of the person. But if one imagines the alternative situation — that not all sins are a fall, that there are harmless, insignificant, not worth attending to sins (the logic and train of thought are evident further) — then the very concept of sin loses its real meaning, and a person may easily make compromises with conscience and with God.

No — even a small sin is a sin and matters: one cannot simply disregard it. Even a small untruth is an untruth, and moreover, from many small untruths a great deception can arise under certain conditions. And so on. Hence such exactingness — but it is necessary.

One may also say that such exactingness sharpens every moment of human existence — and this can give enormous depth of meaning to every moment, or in a less favourable case, at least to some of them.

(Matthew VI, 31-34; VII, 9-11)
Matthew VI, 32: 'πάντα γὰρ ταῦτα τὰ ἔθνη ἐπιζητοῦσιν· οἶδεν γὰρ ὁ πατὴρ ὑμῶν ὁ οὐράνιος ὅτι χρῄζετε τούτων ἁπάντων' — For all these things the Gentiles (nations) seek; but your Heavenly Father knows that you need all these things. It is important to say that it can be seen that it is good to satisfy needs not through the desire to satisfy them, but through knowledge about their existence. For example, it is good to eat not through the feeling of hunger, but through knowledge that the time has come to eat. One can ask oneself the question: Do I want my need in something to be satisfied without my felt desire? Do I want to feel this desire?. It is necessary to say that the knowledge about that God knows about our needs, does not deny turning to Him in prayer. On the contrary, prayer is encouraged by that God is near and knows more than we ourselves. Also this does not mean that the person renounces labour for needs, but their search goes further than them, to God.

Matthew VI, 34 — 'μὴ οὖν μεριμνήσητε εἰς τὴν αὔριον, ἡ γὰρ αὔριον μεριμνήσει ἑαυτῆς' — 'me oun merimnesete eis ten aurion, he gar aurion merimnesei heautes' - therefore do not be anxious (troubled, preoccupied, distressed) about tomorrow (the morning of the next day, the day whose arrival is announced by the fresh morning breeze), for tomorrow (the morning of the next day, the day heralded by the fresh morning breeze) will be anxious (concerned, pondering) about itself ('tomorrow' in Greek is feminine — she). There is a fine poem on these words of Christ — a somewhat expanded translation in terms of meaning of that verse:
https://www.instagram.com/oleksandr_zhabenko/p/C7cEETLs_Qe/

I cite the poem:
Therefore, do not be anxious, immersed or disturbed
in that tomorrow, which is perceptible and observable
as the fresh breeze of the morning.

For that tomorrow is like the fresh breeze in the morning
and brings a feeling of coolness on that new day when it comes,
and she's as variable as the wind.
And she will be anxious, disturbed in herself – that is morrow.

'Sufficient unto the day is the evil thereof.' (Matthew VI, 34)

You don't have to add the cares of morrow to the ones of today,
as they all have the solutions to their own.

(end of citation)

Indeed this passage in verse 34 evokes such poetic associations. Tomorrow brings with it its own logic for the resolution of questions, concerns, and troubles — a logic that may be inaccessible at present, that is difficult to reason about today, or that it is wrong to do so for another reason the commandment implies. The question then arises: how is one to distinguish tomorrow from today? The first thing that comes to mind is the biblical reckoning of time, which begins with the evening. And there was evening, and there was morning, a day… (Genesis I). Commentators often explain that the words evening and morning are manifold in meaning in Hebrew — they can also signify disorder and order. That is, after a certain disorder there follows a new order. Another linguistic pointer is the already-mentioned morning breeze. And yet another is the feminine gender of tomorrow-as-day. Women and girls differ from one another, whilst being similar in many respects. So too today and tomorrow differ in a similar way, being similar in many respects.

But what is important is that concerning days, almost all people unfailingly understand when a new day has arrived. How does one understand that the new day has brought a new logic of resolution, and that it is the right one — that it genuinely resolves what it is called to resolve?

But in order to understand that a new day has arrived, one must have something of that day — something that testifies to its arrival. If a person wishes to understand how one day will differ from another, this is in effect the same as understanding the logic of tomorrow. And the Lord is precisely warning against this today. Why so? It seems entirely natural to wish to understand the future…

But perhaps just because the attempt to understand the future tomorrow inevitably leads to transferring it already into today — with the impossibility of grasping or reaching it, since it is not yet tomorrow? This is precisely what transferring the perception of tomorrow's trouble into today means — against which the commandment warns. It is also important to see that the commandment also teaches clearly to distinguish between thoughts about resolution and resolution itself.

Another important question touched upon here is that which the apostle addresses in the words of Romans XIII, 14: 'ἀλλὰ ἐνδύσασθε τὸν κύριον Ἰησοῦν Χριστόν, καὶ τῆς σαρκὸς πρόνοιαν μὴ ποιεῖσθε εἰς ἐπιθυμίας' — but put on the Lord Jesus Christ, and the fleshly thinking ahead (that is cares concerning the flesh ahead, beforehand) do not make (that is do not transform) into lusts!.
I cite:
https://www.facebook.com/Oleksandr.S.Zhabenko/posts/
Here is seen that fleshly thinking ahead is not sin, but it can be made (done, that is to cause by it lusts) into lusts (and not one!).
Here it is spoken about bodily needs, but one can also understand also the soul's ones in connection with bodily. Again we see a path — between thinking about bodily needs ahead, that is awareness of bodiliness, its consequences and needs, and between lusts. And again the person is not obliged to pass it!

(end of citation)

We see that thinking beforehand — in particular about bodily needs (which is largely the theme of today's reading) — is not a sin. Also visible here is this golden rule of formation. One must think beforehand in such a way that it does not become transformed into lusts.

In general this prompts the thought that the person is not a static being existing only now, today, or only tomorrow — but they extend into the future and even into all eternity. And it is important that they orient themselves correctly with respect to time and eternity.

One may also say that the person is not called to grasp the logic of the future in the present, is not called to experience the future in the present, but is called to carry out decisions that reach the future and eternity in a good way. Through actions tomorrow arrives. We are called more to actions than to anxieties — for the changes of day and night we can discern, but we are not called to understand the logic of the future today. But it must be remembered that thoughts, feelings, and experiences too can be actions. Therefore one must not devalue them — but one must take care for decisions.
