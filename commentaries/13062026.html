Liturgy:
(Romans III, 19-26)
Romans III, 19 — 'τοῖς ἐν τῷ νόμῳ' — 'tois en to nomo' - to those in the Law. That is, to those who have accepted the Law of Moses as Divine Revelation and try to live according to this faith.

Romans III, 20 — 'ἐξ ἔργων νόμου' — 'ex ergon nomou' - from works of Law. The preposition 'ek' in its form before a subsequent vowel indicates that a person is not justified before God by works done by virtue of obedience to the Law of Moses. This is also evident from the fact that in the Old Testament there is no doctrine of justification before God from sins if a person fulfils the Law. The Law nowhere explicitly promises that its fulfilment will justify a person from their sins in God's eyes. Indeed, the Law has a different purpose.

Romans III, 21 — 'μαρτυρουμένη ὑπὸ τοῦ νόμου καὶ τῶν προφητῶν' — 'martyroumene hypo tou nomou kai ton propheton' - being witnessed by the Law and the Prophets. That is, both by people and by the testimony of the Old Testament Scriptures. The preposition 'hypo' is used here with the genitive case and indicates the passive voice of the participle witnessed (by whom).

Romans III, 22 — 'εἰς πάντας τοὺς πιστεύοντας' — 'eis pantas tous pisteuontas' - into all those who believe; for all those who believe. Both variants of translation are possible. The first indicates that God's righteousness is turned towards, directed into the faithful, meaning that in regards to them it is most fully manifested in the gift of salvation and glorification; the second indicates that God's righteousness is bestowed upon the faithful as God's gift for their growth as children of God.

Regarding the verse Romans III, 23, I wrote earlier at the link:

'υστερονται' — 'hysterontai' — is used there — (all) fall short [lack] (the glory of God). It can be put this way, as it concerns the self-perception of the person themselves (I lack…), rather than being deprived of God's glory (the loss of it). See also at the link:

Romans III, 25 — 'ἐν τῷ αὐтоῦ αἵματι' — 'en to autou haimati' - in His Blood; with (by means of) His Blood. Both variants of translation are possible, they both point to the salvific nature of Christ's Sacrifice for people. 'εἰς ἔνδειξιν τῆς δικαιοσύνης αὐτοῦ' - 'eis endeixin tes dikaiosynes autou' - unto the showing forth (proving, manifest declaration) of His righteousness. This confirmation is not needed by God, but it is important so that the faithful trust God, important for a trusting and deep relationship with Him.

Romans III, 26 — 'ἐν τῇ ἀνοχῇ τοῦ θεοῦ' — 'en te anokhe tou theou' - in the forbearance (self-restraint, long-suffering) of God. There are two meanings here — regarding the long-suffering of God for the sake of repentance and conversion of sinful people, and also regarding the kenosis, the humility of God — primarily of Jesus Christ. 'ἐν τῷ νῦν καιρῷ' - 'en to nyn kairo' - in the now season; in the present time. This refers to the period of the present life of each person, as well as the history of the world in general. 'εἰς τὸ εἶναι αὐτὸн δίκαιον καὶ δικαιοῦντα τὸν ἐκ πίστεως Ἰησοῦ' - 'eis to einai auton dikion kai dikaiounta ton ek pisteos Iesou' - unto His being righteous and justifying the [one] out of the faith of Jesus. That is, the faithful Christians. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that a person lives on the basis of their faith in Christ (i.e., righteous out of faith, which is used throughout the Epistle). That is, if a person is renewed through the faith of Christ, the faith of Christ, if their life has become an organic continuation of their faith in Christ, and faith, in its turn, is the real beginning of their life, then the Lord is the One Who justifies them. For more on this non-obvious meaning, see the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/25062024.html

Generally, a difficult passage. Paul speaks once again about the fact that the Law of Moses, in particular the rituals, do not save or justify a person, but the Law is given to make sins manifest, visible. Paul does not say it here, but it should also be borne in mind that to overcome an unmanifest but real temptation, it is important for it to become manifest. Then, seeing the real temptation as manifest, a person becomes aware of it and can truly overcome it (this requires God's help and certain efforts of the person).

Paul does NOT say that works are not needed for salvation, but only faith. No, but in fact he says here that people receive salvation as a free gift, whilst by the works of the Law (primarily meaning rituals here) they cannot earn it.

(Matthew VII, 1-8 )
Matthew VII, 2 — 'ἐν ᾧ γὰρ κρίμαти κρίνετε κριθήσεσθε, καὶ ἐν ᾧ μέτρῳ μετρεῖτε μετρηθήσεται ὑμῖн' — 'en ho gar krimati krinete krithesesthe, kai en ho metro metreiete metrethesetai hymin' - for in what judgement you judge, you shall be judged, and in what measure you measure, it shall be measured to you. Also, about judgement and measure, it is well written at the link:
https://www.instagram.com/p/BhjBm9bg0EM/
I quote:

The Lord Jesus Christ says: Do not judge, that you may not be judged; do not condemn, so that you may not be condemned. Forgive, and it shall be forgiven to you; give, and it shall be given to you; a good measure, pressed down, shaken together and overflowing shall they give into your bosom. For in what measure you measure, by the same it shall be measured back to you (Luke VI, 37-38). Let us consider how often we measure. We say or just even think, perhaps without even realising it, that a house is large and spacious — and we are already measuring it by the standards of size and the possibility to move and look around. Where lies the problem then? Our measures are rarely truly accurate, which is evident from the fact that we can measure in completely different, arbitrary ways. By a good measure is meant one that is correct, that is, just as a kilogram weight should be 1 kg, and so it is — the situation described here itself is correct, and so it is created by God. Non-uniform mixtures or raw materials are pressed down and shaken so that they are more uniform, the same in different directions, meaning that the mutual measuring out will not happen at once, but after many attempts to measure, and it will be more or less statistically correct. Overflowing means that the response will be weightier and perhaps more intensive than the action itself, the measure itself. The bosom [lap] is the lower part of the clothing, the one that is usually not put on display, which is somewhere on the periphery of attention and consciousness. And very often we have our measures, as well as the measuring from others, in our bosoms, limiting ourselves. This response is often unmanifest unless one pays attention to it, just as the bosom is often ignored.

End of quotation.

Matthew VII, 4, 5 — 'ἐκ τοῦ ὀφθαλμοῦ σου' — 'ek tou ophthalmou sou' - out of your eye. Where from. 'ἐν τῷ ὀφθαλμῷ σοῦ' - 'en to ophthalmo sou' - in your eye. Where.

Matthew VII, 5 — 'ἐκ τοῦ ὀφθαλμοῦ τοῦ ἀδελфоῦ σου' — 'ek tou ophthalmou tou adelphou sou' - out of the eye of your brother. Where from.

Matthew VII, 6 — 'ἐν τοῖς ποσὶν αὐτῶν' — 'en tois posin auton' - with their feet. By (means of) what.

I wrote about the reading earlier, quoting:
I will dwell on verses Matthew VII, 4-5. The well-known verses about the speck in the brother's eye and the log in one's own eye. A passage with many meanings, I will dwell here on one of them.

Only a person who has been able to overcome at least one sin in themselves can be a mentor in spiritual life. Otherwise, the words about the log apply directly to them. If, however, a person who has not conquered a single one of their own sins becomes a mentor in the struggle against sin, the danger lies not so much in the proportionality of their sins and the sins of the one they want to teach, but in the fact that such a person simply will not know how to extract the foreign body without damaging the eye. The advice of such a person is very approximate, much like the advice on navigating a terrain given by someone who does not know it. And, usually, such advice is of little use to anyone. Or they will give advice which, when followed, will damage the eye. The latter (which is worse) often happens, for example, with non-believing people who take it upon themselves to criticise the faith of Christ. They advise things that will harm a person. The Lord warns us against all of this.

End of quotation.

Regarding dogs, pigs, and pearls, soberness and discernment are needed: one must not think that people will necessarily believe, repent, or know the truth just because it is told or shown to them. Therefore, one must act purposefully, prudently, and wisely. Nonetheless, it is wrong to judge people and mentally decide for them — whether they will believe or not, repent or not, etc. History, in particular, knows numerous examples of sudden conversion of even seemingly completely incorrigible people, but it also knows examples of unrepentance when everything prompts repentance.
