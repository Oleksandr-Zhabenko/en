(Luke I, 1-25, 57-68, 76, 80)
Luke I, 1 — 'ἐν ἡμῖν' — 'en hemin' - in us; among us. Where.

Luke I, 2 — 'ἀπ’ ἀρχῆς' — 'ap arkhes' - from [the] beginning. A set expression. The preposition 'apo' in its form before a subsequent vowel indicates here the beginning of a counting of time.

Luke I, 4 — ‘ἀσφάλειαν’ — ‘asphaleian’ — safety, protection, reliability, establishment, firmness.

Luke I, 5 — 'ἐν ταῖς ἡμέραις Ἡρῴδου βασιλέως τῆς Ἰουδαίας' — 'en tais hemerais Herodou basileos tes Ioudaias' - in the days of Herod, king of Judea. When. 'ἐξ ἐφημερίας Ἀβιά' - 'ex ephemerias Abia' - out of the daily course of Abijah. That is, a participant of which course Zacharias was. 'ἐκ τῶν θυγατέρων Ἀαρών' - 'ek ton thygateron Aaron' - out of the daughters of Aaron. That is, a representative of which women Elizabeth was (of a priestly line).

Luke I, 6 — 'ἐν πάσαις ταῖς ἐντολαῖς' — 'en pasais tais entolais' - in all the commandments. That is, they lived always fulfilling the Law of Moses, all the commandments.

Luke I, 7 — 'προβεβηκότες ἐν ταῖς ἡμέραις αὐτῶν ἦσαν' — 'probebekotes en tais hemerais auton esan' - were advanced in their days; were of an advanced age. A set expression which literally is translated by the first method, but according into meaning — by the second. That is, they were already truly old.

Luke I, 8 — 'ἐν τῷ ἱερατεύειν αὐτὸν ἐν τῇ τάξει τῆς ἐφημερίας αὐτοῦ' — 'en to hierateuein auton en te taxei tes ephemerias autou' - in his performing priestly service in the order of his course. How.

Luke I, 9 — 'εἰς τὸν ναὸн τοῦ κυρίου' — 'eis ton naon tou kyriou' - into the temple of the Lord. Where to.

Luke I, 11 — 'ἐκ δεξιῶν τοῦ θυσιαστηρίου τοῦ θυμιάματος' — 'ek dexion tou thysiasteriou tou thymiamatos' - out of [the] right side of the altar of the incense. Where, out of which side. The preposition 'ek' enters here into a set expression.

Luke I, 15 — 'ἐκ κοιλίας μητρὸς αὐτοῦ' — 'ek koilias metros autou' - from [the] womb of his mother. The preposition 'ek' indicates here that this happened not at some moment after conception, but that it was always, that is, as far as can be understood — away from the moment of appearance, away from conception. See an analogous use in John IX, 1:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/17052026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/25052025.html
Such a distinction of John is a foretelling of the Annunciation, but the Church also believes that the Virgin Mary also has the Holy Spirit away from conception (although about this it is not spoken explicitly in the Gospels).

Luke I, 17 — 'ἐν πνεύματι καὶ δυνάμει Ἡλεία' — 'en pneumati kai dynamei Heleia' - in [the] Spirit and power of Elijah. That is, he shall have similar spiritual endowments into the prophet Elijah the Tishbite, who for his zeal was taken alive into heaven (and therefore is not John). 'ἀπειθεῖς ἐν φρονήσει δικαίων' - 'apeitheis en phronesei dikaion' - disobedient [ones] in [the] understanding of righteous [ones]. That is, so that the disobedient might convert through the understanding of the righteous, that is, so that those who were disobedient, consequently, unfaithful, sinful, might become intelligent and righteous — it concerns repentance.

Luke I, 18 — 'προβεβηκυῖα ἐν ταῖς ἡμέραις αὐτῆς' — 'probebekyia en tais hemerais autes' - advanced in her days; became of an advanced old age. A set expression with the meaning of the second translation.

Luke I, 20 — 'οἵτινες πληρωθήσονται εἰς τὸν καιρὸν αὐτῶν' — 'hoitines plerothesontai eis ton kairon auton' - which shall be fulfilled (accomplished) into their season. The Archangel Gabriel prophesies in the Holy Spirit, for angels do not know the future by themselves, but God knows.

Luke I, 21 — 'ἐθαύμαζον ἐν τῷ χρονίζειν αὐτὸν ἐν τῷ ναῷ' — 'ebaumazon en to khronizein auton en to nao' - marveled in his tarrying in the sanctuary. That is, he was punctual, humble, therefore never was late in the service.

Luke I, 22 — 'ἐν τῷ ναῷ' — 'en to nao' - in the temple. Where.

Luke I, 23 — ‘λειτουργίας’ — ‘leitourgias’ — a charitable gift, public service in the widest meaning of the word; the service of a priest or Levite as a ritual. Also this word in Christian tradition became understood subsequently as liturgy. 'εἰς τὸν οἶκον αὐτοῦ' — 'eis ton oikon autou' - into his house. Where to.

Luke I, 25 — 'ἐν ἡμέραις' — 'en hemerais' - in days. 'ἐν ἀνθρώποις' - 'en anthropois' - in people; among people. That is, where.

Luke I, 59 — 'ἐν τῇ ἡμέρᾳ τῇ ὀγδόῃ' — 'en te hemera te ogdoe' - in the day eighth; on the eighth day. When.

Luke I, 61 — 'ἐκ τῆς συγγενείας σου' — 'ek tes syngeneias sou' - out of your kindred. That is, out of those who originate out of your kindred (that is, relatives).

Luke I, 65 — 'ἐν ὅλῃ τῇ ὀρεινῇ τῆς Ἰουδαίας' — 'en hole te oreine tes Ioudaias' - in all the hill country of Judea. Where. That is, in a mountainous region, above.

Luke I, 66 — 'ἐν τῇ καρδίᾳ αὐτῶν' — 'en te kardia auton' - in the heart of them. Where. The singular here indicates onto that the thoughts united people in this.

Luke I, 80 — 'ἐν ταῖς ἐρήμοις' — 'en tais eremois' - in the wildernesses. Where.

If one compares the question of Zacharias (Luke I, 18 ) into Gabriel and of the Virgin Mary (Luke I, 34), they are similar by structure, but Gabriel regarding Zacharias speaks about unbelief, whilst regarding Mary only explains. Here it is not easy to see the factual difference, since both conceptions are miraculous, and especially the conception by the Virgin of Christ. But still I think there is one important difference here which essentially influenced the answer. Zacharias wished that he might have a son, likewise also Elizabeth, but they were barren. They had bodily marital relations, but had until that time no children. Instead, Mary did not think about becoming a mother, for She had given a vow into God to be a virgin. When She asks Gabriel, She implicitly says into God: O God, I gave into Thee a vow of virginity. Dost Thou wish that I should not disregard it? (that is, think that into Thee the vow is displeasing, or that Thou hast not accepted it). Mary does not express an objection on the basis of nature, but speaks exclusively about the spiritual component — about Her vow, which the Lord in reality accepted, blessed and sanctified. Zacharias, instead, expresses a certain skepticism precisely because of the natural state. Mary believes that God can perform as He wishes, if only He truly desires, whereas Zacharias does not realize his imperfect state, answers sincerely, but with a certain distrust. Therefore also the answer of Gabriel is different. One can also note that the interrogative words are different, but it is difficult to find a more well-founded explanation precisely through grammar, and not according into the meaning of the matter. The meaning here, obviously, is more important. Google Gemini Flash-3.5 confirms such a thought and adduces such important details:

Substantiation of your thought about the vow and the spiritual dimension
Your hypothesis that Mary was concerned precisely with the spiritual component — her dedication into God (purity/virginity) — finds strong confirmation in Church tradition (in particular in Saint Gregory of Nyssa and Blessed Augustine).
A girl betrothed into Joseph by the logic of the world of that time should have perceived the news about the future birth of a son as something natural: 'I am getting married, consequently, soon I will have a child.' But Mary asks: 'How shall this be, when I know not a husband?' (in the original the present tense is used, which often is interpreted as a constant disposition or a vow — 'I do not have and do not plan to have intimate relations').
Mary defends Her spiritual covenant with God: She does not say 'this is impossible', She asks 'how exactly wilt Thou realize this, for I am completely devoted into Thee in my virginity?'. She seeks the will of God so as not to violate that fidelity which She already promised into Him.
Zacharias, however, looks from a position of deficit: he and Elizabeth for years strived for this by a natural path, prayed, but when the answer came, he feared old age. His 'how' — is a demand for proof through disbelief in that God can operate contrary into the biological clock.
Saint John Chrysostom thus explained this difference: Zacharias, having heard news that agreed with the laws of nature (for birth from elderly parents, though rare, still happened in the history of Israel), manifested distrust. Mary, however, having heard about a thing absolutely unprecedented — a husbandless conception — did not object, but only asked about the method of fulfillment, manifesting full obedience into the Lord.
Therefore Gabriel acts as a righteous judge of human hearts. He sees where behind the words stands natural rationalism and a hidden reproach (in Zacharias), and where — holy simplicity, purity and devotion into God (in Mary)… the difference consisted not in the grammatical form of the question, but in the system of coordinates through which they looked onto God's miracle. Zacharias looked through the earth and the laws of nature, and Mary — through Heaven and fidelity into her Creator.

Regarding Thy prayer is heard — many commentators consider that Zacharias already was not praying about his descendant, had reconciled himself with that most likely there would be none (compare his question into the angel), but prayed for the people, being righteous, wishing the conversion of people into the faith and God. And precisely about this his desire and this prayer Gabriel says that it is heard (here this means that it shall be accomplished). Together with God's care for people there will be manifested also a special care for the righteous Zacharias and Elizabeth in the miraculous birth of a son in old age. God here adds the fulfillment of personal desires into the fulfillment of general [ones], important for all. Therefore one can consider Zacharias as an example of blessed intercession for the people. The beginning of the magnifying of the Lord in Zacharias also testifies regarding this.
