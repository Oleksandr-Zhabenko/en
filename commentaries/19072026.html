Liturgy:
(Romans XV, 1-7)
Romans XV, 2 — 'ἕκαστος ἡμῶν' — 'hekastos hemon' - each of us. 'εἰς τὸ ἀγαθὸн' - 'eis to agathon' - [so that what happens turns] to good. The preposition 'eis' emphasizes that good must be the result, the outcome, indicating the purpose of the action.

Romans XV, 4, 5 — 'παρακλήσεως' — 'parakleseos' - of comfort, encouragement, consolation, the granting of inspiration and courage. From here comes the name of the Holy Spirit — the Comforter [Paraclete].

Romans XV, 4 — 'εἰς τὴν ἡμετέραν διδασκαλίαν' — 'eis ten hemeteran didaskalian' - for our learning. The preposition 'eis' here shows the purpose and ultimate destination for which it was written.

Romans XV, 5 — 'τὸ αὐτὸ φρονεῖν ἐν ἀλλήλοις' — 'to auto phronein en allelois' - to think the same thing in one another. A complex phrase that means approximately to be of one mind, but the form itself can also indicate how like-mindedness can be achieved — by thinking in one another (a parallel to perichoresis in the Trinity) — and thinking the same thing. The key to this is thinking primarily in one another — meaning that the person must be a higher value than the thinking itself; the thought must move from the person and seek a path to unity. And if someone does not think in one another (is far from accepting perichoresis), then the thought of such a person can hardly be of one mind (most likely — only until the first non-trivial question or problem). See also the verse at the link:
https://www.instagram.com/p/DDr3toZNcd2/

Romans XV, 6 — 'ὁμοθυμαδὸν ἐν ἑνὶ στόματι' — 'homothymadon en heni stomati' - with one accord in one mouth; with one accord with one mouth. Rather the second, because the mouth here is not the main thing; it merely manifests here the single-mindedness spoken of. The first variant might be misunderstood as meaning that single-mindedness is only on the lips, only in words, and not in deeds. An important detail — with one accord — is one word in the original Ancient Greek, while with one mouth is two (three together with the preposition). This means that in the Gentile culture of that time (Greek, Hellenistic culture), there was a clear concept of single-mindedness as a state, a phenomenon, an event, so there was a separate compound word to designate the concept. But for one mouth there was no established concept — it was something new. Indeed, if an unbelieving person repeats something, they try to put it in their own words, or even look for a way to be original in words. In contrast, Paul speaks of something else — if the faithful in Christ have one faith, then they can also formulate it with the exact same words, and this formulation is good; it witnesses to this unity, which is deeper than just like-mindedness and single-mindedness.

Paul writes that Christ, being God, served and serves people for their salvation. By this ministry of His, He received people into the glory of God. God serves people! This is the glory of God!

And thus Christ is an Example and Model for the faithful and for everyone in this. Therefore, if a person is capable in something (in a very broad sense — good, strong, wise, rich, influential, etc.), they can and are called by this to also serve for the salvation of others, thereby becoming like Christ God Himself.

Romans XV, 7 — 'εἰς δόξαν τοῦ θεοῦ' — 'eis doxan tou theou' - into the glory of God; to the glory of God. Where to. The preposition 'eis' emphasizes the movement toward glory and the state, the moment of entering into it, that is, glorification.

Matthew IX, 35 — 'ἐν ταῖς συναγωγαῖς' — 'en tais synagogais' - in the synagogues. Where.

The healing of two blind men and a demoniac, as well as a summary account of the preaching, healing, and general ministry of Jesus Christ.

Once, earlier, I wrote that the prayer of the blind men is the first Jesus Prayer known in various versions. For example, a common variant of the Jesus Prayer is Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on us!. If we juxtapose the prayer of the blind men and the Jesus Prayer, and also remember that Son of David is a synonym for Messiah in the Old Testament faith, which in Greek sounds as Christ, then the parallel becomes direct and obvious. Yes, the Jesus Prayer is the prayer of the healed blind men in the Gospels, joined with Peter's confession, the prayer of the publican, and the Canaanite woman.

Jesus strictly forbade the healed blind men to speak at that time, but they still spoke everywhere. The general view is that the prohibition to speak was designed so that people would not believe merely because of miracles, not building their faith on miracles. I wrote about this recently:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/17072023.html
