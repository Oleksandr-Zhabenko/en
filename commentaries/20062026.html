Liturgy:
(Romans III, 28-IV, 3)
Romans III, 28 — 'δικαιοῦσθαι πίστει' — 'dikaiousthai pistei' - [a man] to be justified to faith [with faith]. 'χωρὶς ἔργων νόμου' - 'khoris ergon nomou' - apart from works of Law. Rather the first variant, since the second would mean literally that the works of the Law are placed alongside the person apart from them, and the person is justified apart, while the works for some reason are present separately and do not play a role (which logically is incomprehensible and strange in itself) at the same time.

Romans III, 30 — 'δικαιώσει περιτομὴν ἐκ πίστεως καὶ ἀκροβυστίαν διὰ τῆς πίστεως' — 'dikaiousei peritomen ek pisteos kai akrobystian dia tes pisteos' - [He] shall justify circumcision out of faith and uncircumcision through the faith. That is, faith serves justification in both cases — in the case of circumcision (that is, Judaism or the Judeo-Christianity widespread then) — the justification of circumcision happens because it accomplishes faith, is a manifestation of faith, and in the case of uncircumcision — the Lord does not look onto uncircumcision if there is faith, that is, He looks onto faith, onto the heart, but also grants grace through faith, and not through uncircumcision or circumcision. One can still see another understanding of the words of the apostle — a view from the point of view of faith adds heirs to Israel — for now by faith significantly more people belong into it than biological descendants. Faith accomplishes God's promise and serves as the foundation of the Law, therefore the next verse is logical.

Romans III, 31 — 'διὰ τῆς πίστεως' — 'dia tes pisteos' - through the faith.

Romans IV, 2 — 'ἐξ ἔργων' — 'ex ergon' - out of works. 'πρὸς θεόν' - 'pros theon' - (not) towards God [in drawing near into God]. The preposition 'pros' here is with the accusative and has the meaning of the greatest possible drawing near even unto the deepest personal relationships. Here the apostle means that faith to God, trust to God is capable of leading into the closest relationships with God, whereas the placing of hope onto one's works does not develop a person, does not lead into the closest relationships with God. A parallel to the well-known parable from the Gospel about the publican and the pharisee. To Abraham were accounted into righteousness not the works of the Law of Moses, but faith, trust in deep personal relationships with God.

Romans IV, 3 — 'ἐλογίσθη αὐτῷ εἰς δικαιοσύνην' — 'elogisthe auto eis dikaiosynen' - it was accounted to him into righteousness. That is, what it became.

One can still add the following.

Here Paul speaks about the salvation of different people through faith. According to faith means that people have an experience of life with God, already had a certain faith, and now it has been accomplished, they have awaited the Promised Messiah, Jesus Christ. Instead, through faith means that people before the Coming of Jesus Christ did not have faith in God, but through Jesus Christ came into faith and the knowledge of God.

In such a sense, faith is central.

Abraham, who believed God, is set as an example for all.

…has whereof to glory — in the proper sense — a ground of boasting [a matter of praise, something to boast of], but can also mean the very action of boasting. It was faith that made Abraham righteous, because works can have other explanations and causes than God's will. For example, Abraham's hospitality (an active manifestation of love to different people, even strangers) was praised also by men, but his faith was praised only by God (put somewhat simplified, but faith is something greater than works in this sense). For hospitality had visible advantages which men also understood, whereas faith in that Abraham would have a son in old age, that he would become the progenitor of many numerous nations, that his descendants would inherit the land in which he lives with a few people as a stranger and a sojourner, that Isaac would be alive even if he were offered into sacrifice, and other things — this has no other foundations than faith, than relationships with God, and therefore faith has here an advantage before works. Yes, now very many people praise Abraham, because his faith has been accomplished, at least partially and in many essential moments, but then Abraham's faith almost no one of men (except Melchizedek, perhaps Sarah, Lot) praised, but God accounted such faith into righteousness.

(Matthew VII, 24-VIII, 4)
Matthew VIII, 1 — 'ἀπὸ τοῦ ὄρους' — 'apo tou orous' - away from the mountain. Where from. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that He came down altogether, completely, which indicates the completion of the Sermon on the Mount itself.

Matthew VIII, 4 — 'εἰς μαρτύριον αὐτοῖς' — 'eis martyrion autois' - unto a testimony to them. The preposition 'eis' indicates here the goal of the action.

Concerning the building upon the rock and the sand in the Sermon on the Mount, its completion, and the deeply impressed crowd, also concerning the healing of the man sick with leprosy.

The foundation of life in Christ is the fulfillment of His will, of the commandments. Hearing is necessary for knowledge and faith, but the manifestation of the entire Divine purpose regarding people in their life is accomplished through the fulfillment of God's will.

Regarding the comparison — the scribes and pharisees taught as experts (to a certain extent confidently where there were no doubts or disputes), but Christ taught as One having authority, which in the Biblical understanding means here as the Doer Whose words do not diverge out of life (which was not entirely characteristic for the scribes and pharisees), and also as the Lawgiver. That is, He taught not with a demand to obey, but by His own example, accomplishing everything and even working miracles so as to fulfill His words, a Man of word and deed, as many people understood (including and at least some disciples, see, for example, Cleopas on the way into Emmaus: Luke XXIV, 18-24).

Regarding the healing of the leper — he rightly says that if Christ wishes, He will heal, in this way the leper manifests the necessary faith, and Christ immediately also heals, showing His mercy and love for mankind. And as a fulfiller of the Law, He says what to do next.
