Liturgy:
(Romans XVI, 17-24)
Romans XVI, 17 — 'παρὰ τὴν διδαχὴν' — 'para ten didakhen' - concerning the teaching. Here 'para' with the accusative is used in a sense that in modern language is connected with the ideas of dynamical systems and chaos theory. Namely: even minor deviations for a dynamical systems in the final result lead to essential changes in its behaviour. Well-known examples are the state of weather and the Lorenz attractor. 'ἐκκλίνετε ἀπ’ αὐτῶν' - 'ekklinete ap auton' - turn away from them. The preposition 'apo' in its form before a subsequent vowel indicates onto that it is necessary to avoid such completely, altogether.

Romans XVI, 19 — 'εἰς πάντας' — 'eis pantas' - into all [people]. The preposition 'eis' emphasizes a movement. 'εἰς τὸ ἀγαθόν', 'εἰς τὸ κακόν' - 'eis to agathon', 'eis to kakon' - into the good, into the evil. That is, it concerns the application of reason — for the doing of good, it is good and fruitful to operate and think reasonably, wisely, whereas the avoidance of evil thoughts leads to cleansing also from evil deeds.

Romans XVI, 20 — 'ὑπὸ τοὺς πόδας' — 'hypo tous podas' - under the soles [of the feet]. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the accusative and indicates onto that the Lord shall conquer and make vain the designs of the devil regarding the faithful. 'ἐν τάχει' - 'en takhei' - shortly; soon. A set expression.

Romans XVI, 22 — 'ἐν κυρίῳ' — 'en kyrio' - in the Lord.

The 24th verse is absent in many ancient codices of the Epistle.

The first part of the reading cautions away from false teachers, from discord, divisions, people who do not seek the knowledge of truth but are guided by their own other motives. An important distinction — for if people disagree because they seek the truth which they do not know in full measure, then this can have (although does not always truly have, and onto this also it is necessary to pay proper attention) also a good meaning — that those who are approved may be made manifest among you, as Paul says in another place. But when people do not seek the truth as such, then it is necessary to note this and take care so as not to be tempted and so that others might not be tempted.

The second part — also greetings, just like the beginning of the chapter. Twice Paul through Tertius wishes the grace of the Lord Jesus Christ. Also here is a reminder about the Second Coming.

(Matthew XIII, 10-23)
Matthew XIII, 10, 13 — 'ἐν παραβολαῖς' — 'en parabolais' - in parables; with (by means of) parables. Both variants of translation are correct.

Matthew XIII, 12 — 'ἀρθήσεται ἀπ’ αὐτοῦ' — 'arthesetai ap autou' - shall be taken away from him. The preposition 'apo' in its form before a subsequent vowel indicates onto that it shall be taken away completely, fully, altogether. Here Christ encourages to attain integrity, non-double-mindedness. For only about an integral person can it be said that they truly have what they possess.

Matthew XIII, 19 — 'ἐν τῇ καρδίᾳ αὐτοῦ' — 'en te kardia autou' - in the heart of him. Where. 'παρὰ τὴν ὁδὸν' - 'para ten odon' - to the proximity with the road [alongside the road]. The preposition 'para' here is with the accusative and indicates onto that the sower sows everywhere, and here it concerns the seed that fell close into the way.

Matthew XIII, 21 — 'ἐν ἑαυτῷ' — 'en heauto' - in his own self. A set expression. Where.

Matthew XIII, 22 — 'εἰς τὰς ἀκάνθας' — 'eis tas akanthas' - into the thorns. Here the preposition 'eis' does not mean that the sower had such an intention — to sow precisely into the thorns — but that the seeds got precisely thither.

Matthew XIII, 13 — literally because seeing they do not see, and hearing they do not hear, neither do they understand.

Matthew XIII, 14 — literally approximately the following: by hearing you will hear, but no, you would not understand, and seeing you will see, but no, you would not clearly and distinctly perceive. Visible is God's hope onto that people will understand, know. But extraordinarily frequently people know only partially or insufficiently.

Matthew XIII, 15 — literally approximately the following: with difficulty [heavily] they heard and their eyes they closed, lest at any time they should see with their eyes, and hear with their ears, and understand with their heart, and should convert — and I shall heal them. Important is that the Lord speaks in the future tense regarding Himself — this gives hope not only into those who have already converted, but also into those who are on the way to that.

One could say so, that Christ wishes that all should understand, all should see, know, learn, convert, and He heals people, but people do not understand, do not see and do not hear. Therefore for Christ all is manifest, and He opens into people, and for people — all is to a certain degree a parable, all requires thinking, knowing.

Further Christ notes that His listeners already have the happiness to see and hear Him.

And then He explains the parable in its immediate and direct meaning and design (which does not exclude other meanings).

I will add further that onto the apostles and into many faithful the Lord granted to know the truth immediately, openly, manifestly (for example, the beatitudes, love for enemies, dogmas of faith, etc.), but many people did not prepare themselves into this, therefore they know God in parables, which prompts them into searches. A search — is the beginning of knowing God, for it is impossible to know God involuntarily, without wishing that. Yes, the Lord can open Himself (and indeed opens Himself) into those who sought Him not, but for the knowing of God His Revelation alone is insufficient (not all who saw Christ accepted Him as the Saviour), it is necessary that a person should approach into God freely.

Galatians IV, 2 — 'ὑπο ἐπιτρόπους ἐστὶν καὶ οἰκονόμους' - 'hypo epitropous estin kai oikonomous' - is under guardians and stewards; is under guardians and managers; is under guardians and householders (who care for family welfare, and not build something — note of the translator). Possible is each of the three variants, but generally speaking not all three at once. That is, children have freedom — under the supervision of elders. The tasks of the named elders are the welfare of the family and of these children themselves.

Galatians IV, 3 — 'ὑπὸ τὰ στοιχεῖα τοῦ κόσμου ἤμεθα δεδουλωμένοι' - 'hypo ta stoikheia tou kosmou emetha dedoulomenoi' - under the elements of the world were made servants. That is, they had to perform what these principles point onto — and precisely with an educational and developing goal. Under the principles (elements, rudiments, etc.) of the world one can understand almost everything connected with the creation itself, in particular the Old Testament Law (in ordinances regarding creation), folk experience, customs, traditions and similar, possibly also authority (in modern conditions, here can also be included science, AI, technologies, diplomacy, jurisprudence, medicine, sports, leisure, tourism, ecology and all other similar things). Performance and obedience were necessary not because this is the best, saving and similar, but because this is directed into general welfare. Becoming, however, children of God through Christ in the Holy Spirit, the faithful cease to be a part of the world and become those for whom this world precisely is (heirs of this and the future age and Kingdom).
