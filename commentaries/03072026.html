Liturgy:
(Romans XVI, 1-16)
Paul completes the Epistle with personal greetings to chosen Christians, many of whom are saints (the majority in the rank of the 70 apostles).

With a holy kiss — by that which does not provoke sinful temptations or passions. See at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/Kiss.html

Romans XVI, 1 — 'διάκονον τῆς ἐκκλησίας τῆς ἐν Κενχρεαῖς' — 'diakonon tes ekklesias tes en Kenkhrea' - a deaconess of the Church in Cenchreae; a servant of the Church in Cenchreae. Where. One of the mentions of the rank of deaconesses as Church servants. The mentioned Phoebe is a saint.

Romans XVI, 2 — 'ἵνα αὐτὴν προσδέξησθε ἐν κυρίῳ' — 'hina auten prosdexesthe en kyrio' - that you might receive her in the Lord. How. 'ἐν ᾧ' - 'en ho' - in what. That is, in what she will have need.

Romans XVI, 3 — 'Πρίσκαν καὶ Ἀκύλαν τοὺς συνεργούς μου ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ' — 'Priskan kai Akylan tous synergous mou en CHristo Iesou' - Prisca and Aquila, my fellow-workers in Christ Jesus. Also well-known saints.

Romans XVI, 4 — 'ἐκκλησίαι τῶν ἐθνῶν' — 'ekklesiai ton ethnon' - the Churches of the Gentiles. There are meant the Churches in various localities whose members are former Gentiles, and not Jews. The wide activity of this holy Christian married couple is reflected by such words.

Romans XVI, 5 — 'ἀπαρχὴ τῆς Ἀσίας εἰς Χριστόν' — 'aparkhe tes Asias eis CHriston' - the first-fruits [first-fruit] of Asia into Christ. The preposition 'eis' emphasizes the renewal of Epaenetus in Christ Jesus, through Whom he is named first-fruits.

Romans XVI, 6 — 'εἰς ὑμᾶς' — 'eis hymas' - for you. Some consider that this is Mary Magdalene, my beloved saint.

Romans XVI, 7, 9, 10 — 'οἵτιнές εἰσιν ἐπίσημοι ἐν τοῖς ἀποστόλοις' — 'hoitines eisin episemoi en tois apostolois' - who are of note among the apostles; who stand out (by labor and fruits) among the apostles. Also saints. 'ἐν Χριστῷ' - 'en CHristo' - in Christ. Here only such a variant of translation. 'τοὺς ἐκ τῶν Ἀριστοβούλου' - 'tous ek ton Aristoboulou' - those out of the household of Aristobulus (originate from him, most likely either household members or people converted by him personally).

Romans XVI, 8, 11-13 — 'ἐν κυρίῳ' — 'en Kyrio' - in the Lord. A synonym of the previous, In Christ. 'τοὺς ἐκ τῶν Ναρκίσσου τοὺς ὄντας ἐν κυρίῳ' - 'tous ek ton Narkissou tous ontas en Kyrio' - those out of the household of Narcissus, who are in the Lord. Possibly, not all of Narcissus' household were Christians.

Romans XVI, 16 — 'ἐν φιλήματι ἁγίῳ' — 'en philemati hagio' - with a holy kiss; in a holy kiss. Mostly the first variant is used, but the presence of the second only emphasizes that the goal of the kiss is precisely the greeting (greet in a kiss).

(Matthew XIII, 3-9)
Matthew XIII, 3 — 'ἐν παραβολαῖς' — 'en parabolais' — in parables; with (by means of) parables. Both variants of translation are correct. At the same time, the first emphasizes that the truth has the form of parables, and the second — that a parable as such is suitable to teach well. See in particular regarding the parallel passage in Luke at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/02112025.html

Matthew XIII, 4 — 'ἐν τῷ σπείρειν' — 'en to speirein' - as he sowed (in the process of sowing, during the sowing). 'παρὰ τὴν ὁδόν' - 'para ten hodon' - to the proximity with the way. The preposition 'para' here is with the accusative and indicates onto that the sower cast the seed so that it fell into every ground, including also into the ground beside the way, by the way. In the context of the parable it indicates onto that all are called, into all the Word of God is preached.

The parable itself further is explicitly explained by the Saviour, but this does not signify that His explanation does not require a deep investigation, knowledge, and also that there do not exist additional meanings which are in this parable by the will of God.

Luke VIII, 14 — 'εἰς τὰς ἀκάνθας' — 'eis tas akanthas' - into the thorns. Where to. 'ὑπὸ μεριμνῶν καὶ πλούτου καὶ ἡδονῶν τοῦ βίου πορευόμενοι συνπνίγοντα' - 'hypo merimnon kai ploutou kai hedonon tou biou poreuomenoi synpnigonta' - by cares, and riches, and pleasures of life going on are choked. The preposition 'hypo' here is used with the genitive case and indicates onto that which causes that choking in people, the weakening of spiritual life and efforts — cares, riches, life desires; indirectly by the context here it also indicates onto that these latter stand out frequently as obstacles, empty thorns which wound but do not feed the soul.

Luke VIII, 15 — 'ἐν τῇ καλῇ γῇ' — 'en te kale ge' - in the beautiful, in the highest degree good ground. 'ἐν καρδίᾳ καλῇ καὶ ἀγαθῇ' - 'en te kardia kale kai agathe' - in a heart beautiful, in the highest degree good and good by results, generally, as a whole. Here the combination of good in different degrees of height and perfection indicates onto that a person first cleaves with the heart strongly into the Word of God, and then continues in the good even at a time when there is no such same enthusiasm and alertness, energy, vigour and inspiration. The Lord it is necessary to love strongly, and then to preserve this love not necessarily in that same elevated and high state, but in a good one. 'ἐν ὑπομονῇ' - 'en hypomone' - in patience [patient endurance]. A continuation of the previous thought — patience is necessary in the time of 'ἀγαθῇ'.

The well-known parable of the sower with the explanations of Jesus Himself. I will say here that in Luke the very text of the parable is somewhat shortened, but it is noticeable that he understands this parable as a description of the process of ripening and bringing forth fruits. Not all successfully pass this process to the end, although all at least in a small measure, but were included into it, all are called to that ripening in the Lord. The description shows what are the possible widespread obstacles for a successful completion and the bringing forth of many fruits. Onto such an understanding point, in particular, the words of Luke VIII, 14-15, where there are the words 'τελεσφοροῦσιν' — 'telesphorousin' — (do not) bring fruit to maturity, 'ὑπομονή' - 'hypomone' - patient endurance which requires time, constancy.

End of quote.

Wording from previous notes, to quote:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/Motivation.html

If a seed sprouts, it brings forth much fruit, multiplying at least 30 times, or even 100. For example, if a person studies something necessary, good and beneficial (which contributes to a good, prosperous and full life), then not much time is spent on this, for example, to hear a good teaching requires… from a few seconds to, perhaps, an hour or two. The measure of time is quite conditional, but still approximately so. But if a person perceives this well and then makes it a part of their life, then in life it brings multiple fruits. A person can even all their life, and this can be up to 100 or more years, that is, 876,600 hours (if we take away a third of them for sleep, approximately 584,400 hours remain). If the probability that a person within the nearest hour will use what they have learned is at least 2/584,400, which is approximately equal to 3.4223*10^(-6), that is, slightly more than three millionths, then the spent two hours have justified themselves. For motivation to study... it is enough just to realize this, perhaps, to clearly imagine it.

The sower from the parable casts the seed, and it falls onto different ground and accordingly, depending on where it fell, so it happens with it. Only the part that fell onto good ground, only that brings multiple fruits — 30, 60 and 100 times. Christ Himself explains the meaning of this parable. But it is necessary to pay attention that the fruit is brought forth not by all the seed that fell, but by that which fell into the good ground — that brings multiple fruits. Thus the labor of preachers, people who try to bring others to Christ, does not everywhere find a response, does not everywhere give good fruits, sometimes it gives none. But at the same time, where it finds a response, where it is accepted, there it gives good fruits. These fruits far exceed the invested labor in that direction. This is precisely why Christianity spreads in the end, precisely due to this it achieved such a successful development over many centuries and even now for almost two millennia. Those who bring fruit cover the expenses, and society develops.

Here it is necessary to mention also another principle, that every civilisation, every society develops to the extent that it lives in harmony with the Gospel commandments: if people fulfill them, then well-being awaits them in this, if they violate them, then there will be a decline in this — and accordingly there is no society, there is no civilisation in the history of mankind that would completely fulfill the Gospel commandments and live according to them. On the other hand, this speaks about that there is a principle of resonance: they can fulfill those commandments — let them fulfill them — and this will resonate, and this will give a multiple fruit, this will contribute to their development and well-being.

The Russian world fundamentally forgets about that other cultures, in particular the Western European one, have their own resonant Gospel commandments — those which are easier for them to fulfill by virtue of their organization, experience, level of development, in particular this is the protection of human rights, this is the expansion of human freedoms, this is the development of education. And there they achieve significant progress, there these civilisations, these cultures develop, grow, have flourished — this is known also to the Russians themselves.

But what do the leaders of the concept of the Russian world do? They do not look onto that the Lord takes care of all, but look onto that they have much bad.

And regarding the good — in particular larger territories, economic development and others — they manifest envy.

End of quote.
