Liturgy:
(Romans I, 7-12)
Romans I, 7 — 'ἐν Ῥώμῃ' — 'en Rhome' — in Rome. Where. 'εἰρήνη ἀπὸ θεοῦ πατρὸς ἡμῶν καὶ κυρίου Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ' - 'eirene apo theou patros emon kai kyriou Iesou Christou' - peace from (peace as a divine gift, a manifestation of God's righteousness and love, at the same time connected with the life and faith of the faithful themselves) God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ. The use of the preposition 'apo' points to God's gift to people. See more on this at the link:
https://www.instagram.com/oleksandr_zhabenko/p/DDr3toZNcd2/

Romans I, 8 — 'ἐν ὅλῳ τῷ κόσμῳ' — 'en holo to kosmo' - in the whole world. Where.

Romans I, 9 — 'ἐν τῷ πνεύματί μου ἐν τῷ εὐαγγελίῳ τοῦ υἱοῦ αὐτοῦ' — 'en to pneumati mou en to euangelio tou hyiou autou' - in my spirit in the preaching the Good News of His Son. Paul serves with what — in what. Spirit here is more likely not a component of human nature (for the apostle serves also with soul and body) but a testimony to the fact that Paul's ministry is united with the action of the Holy Spirit.

Romans I, 10 — 'ἐν τῷ θελήματι τοῦ θεοῦ' — 'en to thelemati tou theou' - in the God's will; with the God's will. Both translations are possible. The first indicates that Paul strives to fulfil God's will and indeed does fulfil it, travelling as an apostle to preach the Gospel. The second indicates that every genuine good is given to people — in particular to Paul — through God's will, that is, because such is God's will.

Romans I, 11 — 'εἰς τὸ στηριχθῆναι ὑμᾶς' — 'eis to styrikhthenai hymas' - for your resolute direction and strengthening. The preposition 'eis' indicates here the purpose of the action.

Romans I, 12 — 'συνπαρακληθῆναι ἐν ὑμῖν διὰ τῆς ἐν ἀλλήλοις πίστεως ὑμῶν τε καὶ ἐμοῦ' — 'synparaklethenai en hymin dia tes en allelois pisteos hymon te kai emou' - to be comforted together with you through the faith that is in one another — both yours and mine. That is, to be comforted through the shared faith, through the sharing of faith. See also below on this, in particular in the poem at the link.

Paul writes of his sincere desire to come to the Romans, and also praises their faith and their labour in spreading the Gospel of Jesus Christ, and gives thanks to God.

Verses Romans I, 11-12 reveal spiritual gifting more deeply. 'συνπαρακληθῆναι' has as its root a word related to the name of the Holy Spirit — the Comforter. The apostle can and wishes to transmit a spiritual gift, which means to be comforted together with the community through faith that is in one another. The gift is a gift of the Holy Spirit, but its transmission means that (it takes place when) people become mutually comforted by the Holy Spirit through shared faith, through fellowship, communion in Christ, and so on. The shared comfort here is not merely psychological but spiritual, ontological, and substantial — it is the fellowship, communion of the grace of the Holy Spirit, His communion, His gifting, and also that which grace bestows along with itself. It is important to emphasise that it is not the apostle who determines what gift to transmit to the faithful, but he expresses faith that he will transmit. What is important here is faith that the Holy Spirit Himself — God — will determine what to transmit and to whom. What is meant here is not ordination or the Mystery of the priesthood, but shared participation in the life of the Church — in particular at the divine services, fellowship, communion in worship, prayer, in actions — help and support, in life — fellowship, community, and joy, and so on. The gifts are thus many here, and the Holy Spirit Himself determines what to give to whom. See also in the poem at the link:

(Matthew V, 42-48)
Matthew V, 42 — 'ἀπὸ σοῦ' — 'apo sou' - from you. The preposition 'apo' indicates here that that person wants to take it away entirely (it is not clear here whether they intend to return it subsequently or not).

Matthew V, 45 — 'υἱοὶ τοῦ πατρὸς ὑμῶν τοῦ ἐν οὐρανοῖς' — 'hyioi tou patros hymon tou en ouranois' - sons of your Father Who is in the heavens (the heavenly Father). Where. Those who love even their enemies become like the heavenly Father and become children of God. They become children of God not merely through resemblance, and it is incorrect to think that they are simply repeating similar actions of the Father as it were without His participation, separately from Him — in reality such faithful participate in God's love, and it is precisely this participation that makes them similar to the relationship of Father and Son, about which Christ testifies more in the Gospel of John. Also important here is the following point: if such active love for enemies leads to the conversion of the latter — that is, to the spreading of God's love and the grace of the Holy Spirit, which indeed happens — then one may see the entire Trinity clearly here: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit; whereas if enemies do not convert, the Father and Son are visible more explicitly whilst the Spirit is implicit (for the Spirit is inseparable from the Father and Son). In such a case, so that no one thinks it did not succeed in accomplishing God's will, Christ says that such faithful will be children of God — emphasising the relationship of Father and Son. It must be remembered that with God there is never truly love without a response. God's love is never entirely without response. If creation does not respond with its love to God's love, then to the Father's love the Son and Holy Spirit respond, to the Son's — the Father and Holy Spirit, and to the Spirit's — the Father and Son. Christ speaks of this implicitly when he tells the apostles that if that person is a son of peace, your peace will rest upon that house, but if not — it will return to you.

Why is the sun spoken of — for the evil and the good — and rain for the righteous and the unrighteous? It is well written at the links — I shall cite it below:
https://www.facebook.com/Oleksandr.S.Zhabenko/posts/
https://churchandsociety.org.ua/pdf/projects/zbirnyk.pdf
Why the sun when goodness is spoken of, and rain when righteousness? Why not the other way around? Because the sun, like goodness, a person feels more in comparison with rain — from which one often shelters, even though one knows how necessary it is. So it is easier to call that which one feels more and better goodness, and that which one needs more than feels (even if it passes unnoticed) righteousness.

And additionally: if you wish to feel God's love more, rejoice in the sun when it shines and warms; and when you wish to be more content, rejoice in the needful rain. One may even do this as a spiritual exercise in the appropriate weather.

It must also be remembered that Christians do not struggle against people, but against sins and unclean spirits. The apostle Paul says this in the Epistle to the Ephesians. If a person has so united themselves with sin that the struggle against the latter affects them, then the fault is not with the faithful but with the sinful person themselves.

I cite:
On loving enemies

Does a person have the capacity to become a source?
To become someone who was never there before?
Can a person initiate a beginning,
To manifest goodness where evil is shown?

I believe this ability resides deep within.
For their Creator — God, the Heavenly Father,
Has given humanity the capacity for perfection,
Just as He is perfect in His love.

If we lacked such capacity, such power within,
We would be unable to bring forth a beginning,
And even if we did, we could never truly be
Those brimming, abundant sources.

We would be defined entirely by our environment,
By our surroundings and our circumstances,
Becoming ourselves, to a certain degree,
Merely an event in the life of the world around us.

But I believe in the human whom God has made.
And when I truly perceive them with understanding, beloved by God,
The world arises as that divine utterance,
As a sign and manifestation of His profound love.

And then I do not look to myself as the cause,
But see it and the beginning in God,
Who has gloriously clothed the gift of His love
With sun, with rain, and the path to us, for us.

In one recent commentary on social media (8 February) I wrote that Christ says that love for enemies has its grounds — the example of the heavenly Father and his perfection (Matthew V, 44-48 ). That is, the cause and incentive for love of enemies must be the example of the heavenly Father and His perfection. And the sun and rain can help us to love. I came to this thought during a discussion of the translation of my commentary with Google Gemini. Also, Proverbs XV, 3 — a parallel with the New Testament words about love for enemies — the heavenly Father makes His sun to rise on the evil and on the good. Concerning the latter there is a fine work at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/06022026.html

One may also add that Christ speaks not simply of love for enemies, but of such love as resembles the perfect love of the perfect heavenly Father. This connection is often forgotten when people seek to understand love for enemies outside of the resemblance to the perfect Father. If a person cannot manifest love for enemies resembling the love of the perfect heavenly Father, then they cannot claim that their love is precisely the fulfilment of this commandment of Christ. But the sun and rain help to build bridges between the highest divine perfection and imperfect people. If a person can manifest love in a similar manner, then even in this imperfect love they are conformed to God, to the Father.

I shall also add the following.

When the Lord says do not resist the evil one (literal translation), and then continues concerning love for enemies, He means that the driving force of our actions, thoughts, and life — inner and outer — must be love for God and for people, not hatred of anyone. It is fundamentally impossible to attain perfection and overcome sins on any basis other than genuine love — outside of it.

That is, love must prevail — this is the state of perfection.

One must dwell on the words do not resist the evil one. This does not mean ceasing to struggle against the sins of other people. But one must struggle not to their harm but for their salvation, conversion, repentance, purification, and so on.

Also the Lord does not wish us to compete as to who is more persistent and tenacious in their convictions — faithful Christians or embittered enemies. Such a comparison may arise, but one must not make it the goal or the content of the struggle.

Any similar concentration upon evil (even with the intention of overcoming it) does not bring a person closer to the perfect God — a person, instead of moving towards God and growing, transforms their life into a struggle with evil (this is still not the same thing).

Let us look at God: if the Lord considered the destruction and eradication of evil to be the main thing in the world, He would immediately stop all evil — and moreover, before it began, would not have allowed it to appear at all. But God… is long-suffering for the sake of the salvation of people. He gives freedom, so that it may develop in the direction towards Him and blessed eternity. God judges evil, overcomes evil, restrains evil, destroys evil, stops evil, and directs its consequences towards good — but above all He gives creation freedom to draw near to Him.

One may thus see in this the positivity of Christianity. No — the meaning of the life of Christians is not in the struggle against sins, but in drawing near to God, in love. One must not transpose the principal and the important.
