Liturgy:
(Romans VI, 18-23)
Romans VI, 18, 22 — 'ἀπὸ τῆς ἁμαρτίας' — 'apo tes hamartias' - from the sin. The preposition 'apo' emphasizes distancing away from sin, the leaving of sin.

Romans VI, 19, 22 — 'εἰς τὴν ἀνομίαν' — 'eis ten anomian' - for the iniquity. 'εἰς ἁγιασμόν' - 'eis agiasmon' - for sanctification. Here the preposition 'eis' signifies the goal, as well as an answer to the question For what?.

Romans VI, 23 — 'ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ τῷ κυρίῳ ἡμῶν' — 'en CHristo Iesou to kyrio hemon' - in Christ Jesus the Lord of us. That is, eternal life which opened in Christ, the Source of which He is.

(Matthew VIII, 5-13)
Matthew VIII, 5 — 'εἰς Καφαρναοὺμ' — 'eis Kapharnaoum' - into Capernaum. Where to.

Matthew VIII, 6 — 'ἐν τῇ οἰκίᾳ' — 'en to oikia' - in the house. Where.

Matthew VIII, 8 — 'ὑπὸ τὴν στέγην' — 'hypo ten stegen' - under the roof. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the accusative.

Matthew VIII, 9 — 'ὑπὸ ἐξουσίαν' — 'hypo exousian' - under authority. 'ὑπ’ ἐμαυτὸν στρατιώτας' - 'hyp emauton stratiotas' - under myself soldiers. In verse 9 is used the accusative with the preposition 'hypo' or its form before a vowel, which indicates here onto subordination, and more exactly in the context of the centurion's words — onto a certain functional consistency regardless of desires. Let me explain the last statement in more detail. The centurion being under authority — means that he performs a part of the functions of authority, in particular protection and control, at the same time the higher meanings of human existence, for example, freedom, are not revealed here. Likewise also regarding the soldiers of the centurion — they perform his orders, realizing to a certain degree his functions; the higher meanings of the existence of people here also are not shown. Precisely this also is the cause of the humility of the centurion. Factually, he means that he recognizes the honour to accept Jesus to himself, and recognizes that his life does not pass in the higher meanings of human existence. And Jesus in his eyes is the One Who came for their sake.

Matthew VIII, 10 — 'οὐδὲ ἐν τῷ Ἰσραὴλ τοσαύτην πίστιν εὗρον' — 'oude en to Israel tosauten pistin heuron' - not even in Israel so great faith have I found. Where.

Matthew VIII, 11 — 'ἀπὸ ἀνατολῶν καὶ δυσμῶν' — 'apo anatolon kai dysmon' - from east and west. The preposition 'apo' indicates here onto that these people shall leave their lands — judging out of everything they shall convert or turn out to be not such as the surrounding Gentiles and non-believing people. Matthew VIII, 12 — 'εἰς τὸ σκότος τὸ ἐξώτερον' — 'eis to skotos to exoteron' - into the outer darkness. That is, into the space and state outside the Kingdom of God, alienation away from God, away from Light and Life.

Concerning the faith of the Roman centurion and the healing of his sick servant.

This passage is well known, I will only emphasize certain important moments.

Jesus marveled — here one can translate in different ways, there will be theological nuances regarding interpretation — for example, if one translates marveled, then it concerns a human feeling, for as God He knows all things and does not marvel; if one translates was delighted, became significantly more animated, then God also extraordinarily rejoices at special human virtues — and then these are both Divine and human feelings (in the dimension of natures) — in general, however, this is a feeling of the Son of God in consequence of the hypostatic union of two natures in Him (His human feelings — are also His personal feelings).

The sons of the Kingdom — the Jews, in general those who know the Law, the Scripture, who are instructed in that. It concerns not all, but that (to wonder) there shall be such among them.

So great faith — we would say that the centurion first manifested faith, and then — humility, when he did not consider himself worthy, but the Lord in general says faith. Why so? Because the humility of the centurion originates out of his faith, is an organic continuation and component of his faith, and the Lord looks onto the heart and onto the core of the matter, therefore speaks about faith.

In Israel — among those whom Christ saw during the preaching. One can say that the case with the centurion is similar to the praise by Jesus of John the Baptist. It concerns not a comparison of both people whom the Lord glorifies, but about a special honour which is manifested by Him into both. The apostle Paul (at least about the saved) says: And then shall every person have praise away from God (1 Corinthians IV, 5), — that is, the Lord can endow with special gifts and praises as much as He shall wish; one can say in the language of mathematics, here is a non-linear structure, these gifts differ among themselves, it is difficult (and certainly, not necessary) to compare them, but it is necessary to rejoice at them all.
