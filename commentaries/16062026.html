Liturgy:
(Romans VII, 14-VIII, 2)
Romans VII, 14 — 'ὑπὸ τὴν ἁμαρτίαν' — 'hypo ten hamartian' - under the sin. The preposition 'hypo' here is with the accusative and indicates here that a person finds themselves lower, as if weaker. Here and in verse 15 it is important to see that spirit and spiritual indicate that the spirit is not limited by bodily limitations, therefore having more freedom. Also right here it is visible that the angels, whose angelic spiritual nature is here indirectly mentioned through the general spirit — they act as they wish, that is, angels are not slaves; it is also visible why those angels that fell do not repent — because they fell entirely freely, and now do not want to repent. Regarding creation, spirit manifests all the possibilities of creation, all powers, all gifts and endowments. Therefore, we also see in giftedness more manifestations of the spirit, more freedom.

Romans VII, 15 — not what I wish, I practice (frequently, repeatedly, constantly do), but what I do not wish, this I do.

Romans VII, 16 — 'σύμφημι' — 'symphemi' - I consent unto.

Romans VII, 18, 21 — 'ἐν τῇ σαρκί μου' — 'en te sarki mou' - in the flesh of me. Where. 'ἀγαθόν' - 'agathon' - good (in a broad sense, good generally, as a whole, in the result). 'καλὸн' - 'kalon' - excellent, good in a higher sense, worthy. Paul says that if a person does not reach unto the performance of the excellent, worthy, then it cannot be said that the good constantly dwells in them.

Romans VII, 19, 21 — 'κακὸν' — 'kakon' - evil; something truly bad (evil in the proper sense). In the 21st verse, the opposites are manifested the most: the wish for the excellent good and the presence of the true evil. Here is a strengthening of the thought of verse 15 — not what I wish, I do [of] good, but that evil (truly evil, properly bad) which I do not wish, this I practice (repeatedly, frequently, constantly do).

Romans VII, 23 — 'ἕτερον νόμον ἐν τοῖς μέλεσін μου' — 'heteron nomon en tois melesin mou' - another law in the members of me. Where. 'ἐν τῷ νόμῳ τῆς ἁμαρτίας' - 'en to nomo tes hamartias' - in the law of the sin; with the law of the sin. Both variants of translation are possible, and they are significantly different in meaning. The first indicates that the so-called another law, that is, a certain force which captivates, operating according to a certain logic, causes the remaining of a person under the influence and action of sin. The second indicates that the very influence and action of sin cause that force which captivates. One can say that here is a certain closed circle of mutual reinforcement of both laws.

Romans VII, 24 — 'τίς με ῥύσεται ἐκ τοῦ σώματος τοῦ θανάτου τούτου;' — 'tis me rysetai ek tou somatos tou thanatou toutou?' - who shall deliver me out of the body of this death?. Here it does not necessarily mean the physical body that has subjected itself to sin and death, corruption. The apostle in other places also uses the words flesh and body in the context of sin. See at the link:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/29102025.html
Quoting:
Colossians I, 22 — 'ἐν τῷ σώματι τῆς σαρκὸς αὐτοῦ' — 'en to somati tes sarkos autou' - in the body of the flesh of Him; with the body of His flesh. Both variants of translation are possible. The phrase is mysterious, since at times body and flesh act as synonyms, or predominantly only one of these two words is used. One can think that such a combination indicates how God through the Incarnation saves human nature, damaged by sin. Body is then called the state of nature when it is full of Divine grace, divinised, lives according to God's will, and flesh — the human damagedness and weakness assumed upon Himself by God. In Christ, thus, human nature is simultaneously in both senses during His earthly life — and the salvation of the flesh is accomplished through the divinised body. And in a broader sense, the salvation of mankind is accomplished through the Body of Christ — the Church. Here in the verse it is also spoken literally holy, unblemished, and irreproachable. These are certain degrees of holiness and drawing near to God — the holy can have shortcomings, instead there are such as can hardly be reproached for their shortcomings, and there are also such as truly cannot be reproached for their shortcomings, for example, the Mother of God, the All-Pure Virgin Mary.

End of quote.

The body here in the verse Romans VII, 24 indicates something that, unlike the spirit, is more inert, slow, which does not change easily through repentance. This is something akin to sinful habits that do not disappear at once, or to the general state of nature after the fall of people, through which people die.

Romans VIII, 1 — 'ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ' — 'en CHristo Iesou' - in Christ Jesus.

Romans VIII, 2 — 'ἠλευθέρωσέν σε ἀπὸ τοῦ νόμου τῆς ἁμαρτίας καὶ τοῦ θανάτου' — 'eleutherosen se apo tou nomou tes amartias kai tou thanatou' - freed you from the law of the sin and of the death. The apostle begins to use the word law constantly; here in the passage we have the law of the mind, the Law of God, the law of the Spirit, the law of the sin, and the law of the death. And previously there was simply the law. Actually, here it is that which is more durable, more enduring, stronger than all that can be changed by a simple effort of a person. The image of the desired good, named the law of the mind, cannot simply be arbitrarily changed by a person, for it attracts them, reflecting the depth of their personality; the Law of God is established by God; the law of the Spirit of the life in Christ — is a gift of grace, which is able to operate upon all; the law of the sin and of the death — inclinations to sin, passions, the capability to die, and death itself.

A difficult passage, where Paul speaks rather on behalf of another person (that I might gain some, according to his words, whilst he himself through the grace of the Lord is found in a better state). Thus Paul wishes that the listeners (readers) would sooner agree with his deep reflections.

In general, it can be said that by body (flesh) here is not meant properly a component of human nature created by God, but the state of damagedness of human nature (similar to the skins of animals in which the Lord granted Adam and Eve to be clothed when they were expelled from Paradise because of the fall and unrepentance). And so because of this damagedness, it is not easy for a person to be constant in the good, even when they realise it, know it, and wish to do it. The susceptibility and inclination to sin, sin itself, and sinful habits and passions (dependencies on sin) — all of this together is named the law of the sin.

At the beginning of the VIII chapter, Paul repeats the thought that only through Christ can a person truly be freed from that (be saved).

(Matthew X, 9-15)
Matthew X, 9 — 'εἰς τὰς ζώνας ὑμῶν' — 'eis tas zonas hymon' - into the belts of you. Where to.

Matthew X, 10 — 'εἰς ὁδὸν' — 'eis hodon' - into a way. Where to.

Matthew X, 11 — 'εἰς ἣν δ’ ἂν πόλιν ἢ κώμην εἰσέλθητε' — 'eis hen d an polin e komen eiselthete' - and into whatsoever city or village you may enter. Where to. 'τίς ἐν αὐτῇ ἄξιός ἐστιν' - 'tis en aute axios estin' - who in it worthy is. Where.

Matthew X, 12 — 'εἰς τὴν οἰκίαν' — 'eis ten oikian' - into the house. Where to.

Matthew X, 14 — 'κονιορτὸν ἐκ τῶν ποδῶν ὑμῶν' — 'koniorton ek ton podon ymon' - dust out of the feet of you. The dust does not originate out of the feet of the apostles, but adhered there earlier than anyone external would notice the shaking of it off the feet. Therefore, this is said from the point of view of an observer and witness of the events — how people are to see it. This is a sign for the people, the meaning of which is to convey the testimony to them.

Matthew X, 15 — 'ἐν ἡμέρᾳ κρίσεως' — 'en hemera kriseos' - in a day of judgement. When. At the Last Judgement.

I wrote earlier, quoting:

From the Gospel today, I will dwell on two verses — Matthew X, 14-15.

About Sodom and Gomorrah we learn from the book of Genesis; there lived Lot with his family, the nephew of Abraham. The inhabitants of the city (all except Lot and partially his family) were so dissolute and corrupted that instead of hospitality, they wanted to rape Lot's guests in an unnatural way, scorning even his daughters. Abraham with his slaves defeated the kings of those cities by God's help, and the Lord delivered Lot and his family from those cities, and turned them with fire from heaven into the Dead Sea, in which nothing living lives (only recently can tourists bathe), so that the pestilence and contagiousness of the inhabitants of those cities would not spread further.

But why such harsh words about the rejection of the apostles?

If anyone did not receive the apostles who preached the way to salvation, it was only because of the sin of those inhabitants, to which they had become more accustomed, so that neither preaching, nor miracles, nor example — nothing influenced the people. Sin in them turned out to be stronger than the good, and, therefore, as it was with Sodom and Gomorrah, nothing restrains them anymore. And then, in the course of history, nothing will be able, without their repentance, to stop them on the path of fall and the loss of even humanity.

Therefore, a warning to all, why the Lord forewarns everyone.

End of quote.
