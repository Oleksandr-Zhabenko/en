Liturgy:
(Acts XVII, 1-15)
Acts XVII, 1 — 'εἰς Θεσσαλονίκην' — 'eis Thessaloniken' - into Thessalonica. Whither.

Acts XVII, 2 — 'ἀπὸ τῶν γραφῶν' — 'apo ton graphon' - from the Scriptures (of the Old Testament). The preposition 'apo' indicates here that the Scriptures were regarded to a certain degree as something objective — something that can be examined and studied as a text.

Acts XVII, 3 — 'ἀναστῆναι ἐκ νεκρῶν' — 'anastenai ek nekron' - to rise from the dead. The preposition 'ek' indicates here whence the transition took place — from the dead to the living.

Acts XVII, 4 — 'τινες ἐξ αὐτῶν' — 'tines ex auton' - some of them. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that those were representatives of those Jews — that is, before their conversion they were similar to the others.

Acts XVII, 5 — 'εἰς τὸν δῆμον' — 'eis ton demon' - into the crowd; to the people; for the crowd. That is, to please the crowd.

Acts XVII, 9 — 'τὸ ἱκανὸν παρὰ τοῦ Ἰάσονος καὶ τῶν λοιπῶν' — 'to ikanon para tou Iasonos kai ton loipon' - surety (pledge, something of value) from beside Jason and the rest. The preposition 'para' here with the genitive case. This construction is used concerning the procession of the Holy Spirit from the Father and also concerning the human nature of Jesus Christ. See more at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/uk/commentaries/Pryjmennyky.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/02082025.html
Here this indicates that the self-sacrifice of Jason and the rest furthered the preaching of the apostles — that is, it was prompted by the action of the Holy Spirit.

Acts XVII, 10 — 'εἰς Βέροιαν' — 'eis Beroian' - into Beroea. Whither. 'εἰς τὴν συναγωγὴν τῶν Ἰουδαίων' - 'eis ten synagogen ton Ioudaion' - into the Jewish synagogue. Whither.

Acts XVII, 11 — 'τῶν ἐν Θεσσαλονίκῃ' — 'ton en Thessalonike' - those in Thessalonica; the Thessalonians.

Acts XVII, 12 — 'πολλοὶ μὲν οὖν ἐξ αὐτῶν ἐπίστευσαν' — 'polloi men oun ex auton episteusan' - many then indeed of them believed. That is, their believing was decisive and the faith was firm. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that those people became representatives of that community.

Acts XVII, 13 — 'οἱ ἀπὸ τῆς Θεσσαλονίκης Ἰουδαῖοι' — 'hoi apo tes Thessalonikes Ioudaioi' - the Jews from Thessalonica. The preposition 'apo' indicates here either that those Jews had at that time left Thessalonica, or that Thessalonica was the city whence their mothers came (that is, the father was a Jew and the mother a Greek). 'ἐν τῇ Βεροίᾳ' - 'en te Beroia' - in Beroea. Where. 'κατηγγέλη ὑπὸ τοῦ Παύλου ὁ λόγος τοῦ θεοῦ' - 'katengele hypo tou Paulou ho logos tou theou' - the Word of God is proclaimed openly by Paul. The preposition 'hypo' here with the genitive case indicates the passive voice of the participle, rendered here as is proclaimed openly.

The reading concerning Paul's preaching in Europe continues. Despite the varying attitudes on the part of the hearers and other people, Paul labours actively and continues the preaching — and it is also worth noting the help given to him and his fellow workers by the faithful.

(John XI, 47-57)
John XI, 48 — 'πάντες πιστεύσουσιν εἰς αὐτόν' — 'pantes pisteusousin eis auton' - all will believe in(to) Him. The preposition 'eis' indicates here that true faith leads to Christ — he is its goal and final point.

John XI, 49 — 'εἷς δέ τις ἐξ αὐτῶν Καϊάφας' — 'heis de tis ex auton Kaiaphas' - but one of them, Caiaphas. The preposition 'ek' in its form before the following vowel indicates here that Caiaphas generally shared their common views.

John XI, 51 — 'ἀφ' ἑαυτοῦ' — 'aph heautou' - from himself. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following 'he' indicates here that these words could be regarded as something independent of Caiaphas himself — as an involuntary testimony concerning Christ. See more on this at the links:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/10042026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/06052026.html
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/22042026.html

John XI, 52 — 'συναγάγῃ εἰς ἕν' — 'synagage eis hen' - to gather together into one. That is, to gather so that they might be united, in unity.

John XI, 53 — 'ἀπ' ἐκείνης οὖν τῆς ἡμέρας' — 'ap ekeines oun tes hemeras' - from that day, therefore. The preposition 'apo' in its form before the following vowel indicates here the starting point of the reckoning — when they passed from deliberation to malicious action.

John XI, 54 — 'ἐν τοῖς Ἰουδαίοις' — 'en tois Ioudaiois' - among the Jews. Where. 'εἰς τὴν χώραν ἐγγὺς τῆς ἐρήμου, εἰς Ἐφραὶμ λεγομένην πόλιν' - 'eis ten khoran engys tes eremou, eis Ephraim legomenen polin' - into the land near the desert, into a city called Ephraim. Whither.

John XI, 55 — 'ἀνέβησαν πολλοὶ εἰς Ἱεροσόλυμα ἐκ τῆς χώρας πρὸ τοῦ πάσχα' — 'anebesan polloi eis Hierosolyma ek tes khoras pro tou paskha' - many went up into Jerusalem from that region before the Passover (the Jewish one). Whither.

John XI, 56 — 'ἐν τῷ ἱερῷ' — 'en to hiero' - in the temple. Where. 'εἰς τὴν ἑορτήν' - 'eis ten eorten' - to the feast. Whither and for what purpose.

The preceding context is the reading for Lazarus Saturday:
https://oleksandr-zhabenko.github.io/en/commentaries/04042026.html

The reading concerns the conspiracy of the Pharisees, priests, and elders of the Jewish people to seize and kill Jesus Christ. It is evident that all of them saw in Jesus a political figure supposedly planning to seize political power on the territory of Israel, even though Jesus had shown many times that this was not so.

The words of the high priest Caiaphas were a prophecy — but one must not think that this somehow exalts him. The matter is that God foreknew the evil designs of their hearts and wishes in this way to save people, whilst Caiaphas is in no way a prophet — but knowing his evil will, God also manifested a sign through him (through His omniscience) in this manner. Nevertheless, it is shown that the high priest, by virtue of the nature of his activity and the significance of his person, was thinking about the people — and therefore his words became not merely personal thoughts but also concerned (though contrary to his wishes) the children of God.

Jesus, as God, knew what was happening and therefore acted so that the time might be fitting.
